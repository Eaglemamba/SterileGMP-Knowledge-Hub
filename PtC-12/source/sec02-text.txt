Q 1.9. What construction design considerations are required for materials and 
maintenance? 
Recommendation 
RABS surfaces should be manufactured in materials compatible with the environmental classification 
conditions required for the proposed use.   
Construction Materials Considerations: 
•
Interior metallic surfaces in direct or indirect contact with the primary packing should be
constructed in 316 stainless steel or better and, for the rest of the line, metallic components could be
constructed of low-carbon 304 stainless steel or better (1, 6).
•
Interior transparent surfaces made of polycarbonate or glass.
•
Seals joining the RABS with upstream and downstream equipment should take temperature and
differential pressures into consideration (e.g., depyrogenation tunnel to filler RABS or freeze
dryer). 
•
Cleanable with detergents commonly used in the cleanroom with no absorbing or desorbing effect
when in contact with them (10).
All RABS surfaces should be resistant to the following conditions and agents to avoid the release of 
particles:  
•
Scratches and abrasions.
•
Detergents and sporicidals used for CIP and cleaning-out-of-place, or disinfection (e.g., turntables,
guides) (11).
•
Sterilization process as SIP for fixed-system components (e.g., filling path). 
•
External sterilization processes with moist heat or radiation, or chemical sterilization processes for
complementary components removable from RABS (e.g., stopper bowl, feeding lanes), or ready to
use.
Maintenance Considerations 
The internal RABS working areas should be designed to minimize the risk of contamination in critical zones 
for troubleshooting and repair activities.   
The access to maintenance panels and/or mechanical spaces should be outside the RABS and below the 
operating/processing level (product height) as much as possible. Even if outside of the machine, these areas 
should not be opened without additional controls being evaluated and implemented (e.g., requires to 
exchange gowning after those activities).   
All maintenance activities for a RABS and related equipment should be scheduled in a plan to occur at the 
same time, when it is possible, to avoid multiple preventive-maintenance work-order days.   
Rationale 
The RABS and equipment construction materials should be designed to meet such production requirements 
as cleanability, sanitization, and practicality of use, with the scope to reduce the contamination risk by 
particles that could be released from routine use or from eventual damages and abrasion occurred during 
routine production or maintenance activities.

Q 1.10. What unique design provisions are required for highly potent compounds 
exposed within closed restricted access barrier systems (cRABS)? 
Recommendation 
Environmental Health Safety vs. Good Manufacturing Practices 
When designing a RABS for aseptic highly potent compounds, both the good manufacturing practice 
(GMP) and environmental health and safety (EHS) aspects are very important. While overpressure of the 
containment is necessary from a GMP perspective, negative pressure is required from the EHS standpoint to 
ensure operator safety (6).   
Therefore, the pressure differential between the inside of the RABS and the outside environment must be 
positive. To avoid contamination from the most highly potent exposure area (e.g., filling zone), this zone is 
operated in negative pressure towards adjacent RABS sections. To make this physically and technically 
possible, a cRABS is needed. To allow for the creation and maintenance of the pressure differences during 
operation, it needs at least a cRABS, if recirculating air is used, a dedicated air handling unit (AHU) must be 
present.  
Additional Considerations:  
•
Ability to safely change out terminal HEPAs by a safe-change filter system or one-pass aeration
systems (with bag-in/bag-out exhaust HEPAs) (See Figure 1.10-1.)
•
Interlocked door sensors and other mechanical devices to ensure operator safety
•
Vacuum systems should be avoided where possible; otherwise, the integration of filters that prevent
contamination at the base of the machine should be considered
•
Transfer ports should be present to manage the removal of contained material used for spillage
clean-up without having to open the RABS.*
•
Pressure differential-monitoring systems to alarm in case of loss of pressure differential or RABS
leakage.
•
Double glass doors are to be avoided (See Figure 1.10-1.)
•
Important for the commissioning of such products is the correct protective equipment of the
employees and their behavior in case of an accidental breach.
*Note: This consideration does not apply for monitoring equipment using vacuum, such as total-particle
counters or active viable monitoring. The monitoring equipment must be located in the most critical
positions according to the EM risk analysis. A strategy to prevent contamination of the vacuum lines and the
monitoring equipment must be evaluated (e.g., the particulate filter above the machine plate in the aseptic
area) immediately after the sampling head for active viable (6). The sampling head must be treated as any
other exposed equipment surface within the RABS and needs to be appropriately cleaned before the
sterilization cycle. For particulate measurement systems, a different strategy must be considered. Total
particle counters should be dedicated to certain areas and changed in situ to reduce the movement of the
equipment and to limit exposure. Particulate filter can be placed after the device to ensure contamination
prevention of the vacuum lines and pumps.

Cleaning 
A highly preferred method for cleaning a cRABS contaminated with toxic substances is the automatic wash-
in-place (WIP) process. The WIP process can either be executed with spray guns mounted inside the RABS 
for manual wash-down or with a spray ball to achieve an automatic wash down. This means that even hard-
to-reach areas can be cleaned more easily while the doors remain closed and, therefore, the risk of operator 
exposure is minimized. The handling of the wastewater must be considered with a special focus on the 
draining systems (i.e., EU Annex 1: Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal Products excludes drains in Grade A 
or Grade B environments) and toxic water separation. Depending on toxicity, measuring residuals in 
wastewater is recommended to ensure the RABS environment is free from potential potent residuals. A risk 
assessment should be performed to determine the proper neutralization of the wastewater from the toxic 
process and proper design. Nevertheless, the machine and the containment must, of course, be designed in 
such a way that all parts can be easily cleaned and equipped in a hygienic design.  
Rationale 
Potent compounds are hazardous to operators and, while product sterility must be maintained, it is equally 
important to ensure that operators and the environment they operate in can be protected from exposure. 
Utilization of cRABS, with negative pressure and WIP systems, allows for the operation and cleaning of the 
system, without ever exposing the operator or the environment outside of the RABS to the compound.

Q 1.11. What are the best engineering design considerations for in-line environmental 
monitoring (EM)? 
Recommendation 
Monitoring should be performed at locations posing the highest risk of contamination of the sterile 
equipment surfaces, containers, closures, and product. The selection of monitoring locations and the 
orientation and positioning of sampling devices should be justified in an EM risk analysis and appropriate to 
obtain reliable data from the critical zones (1, 4, 12).   
In-line Viable Monitoring: 
•
Viable air samplers located near critical process zones (e.g., accumulation table, filling station,
stoppering section). 
•
Consider location near glove ports for ease of plate placement, removal, and storage.
•
Consider installing an EM plate holder for staging multiple EM plates as well as holding the lid of
the air sampler, if applicable. This stand should be installed near to the air sampler and within reach
via glove ports.
•
Verify active air samplers are part of the calibration program (airflow). 
•
Consider First Air requirements for critical surfaces (e.g., initial load of agar plates, replacement of
settle plates).
In-line Total Particulate Monitoring: 
•
Total particle air sampler located near critical process zones within a one foot radius of the working
position (e.g., accumulation table, filling, stoppering) (4).
•
If air samplers have covers, verify sampler is near glove ports for ease of cover placement or
removal. 
•
Verify air sampler is part of the calibration program (airflow, particulates typically 0.5 µm and 5.0
µm). 
•
Ensure that the tube between the sampling probe (isokinetic funnel) and particle counter is <1 m
minimizing bends or radii. If deviating from this requirement, particle loss studies need to
demonstrate the functionality (1).
If considering the use of an alternate rapid microbiological methods (real-time 
monitoring via biofluorescent particle-counting), considerations should include, but not 
be limited to, the following (1, 6, 12, 13):  
•
Sampling locations must be representative and close to conventional settle plates to show
equivalency of the system for validation and data collection during production.
•
Suitable installation location of the counter to ensure accessibility for retraction of gelatine
filter/agar plate for possibility of identifying the autofluorescence unit.
•
Tube length from sampling point to counter needs to be considered and validated by particle loss
studies if >1 m.
•
Alarm management for autofluorescence unit hit must be in place and validated.
Temp/RH/DP/Airflow Velocity (6): 
•
Sensors should be placed in representative locations—located downstream of UDAF/HEPA filters
but above product pathway—so as not to interfere with equipment operation and potential
interventions.

Q 1.12. What are the criteria for retrofitting and design considerations for restricted 
access barrier systems (RABS), including restricted access barrier system (RABS) 
interfaces? 
Recommendation 
Retrofitting a line into a RABS is normally a temporary solution to maintain compliance with GMP. 
However, retrofitting can be beneficial by increasing sterility assurance while the company works on the 
longer-term implementation of a full RABS or isolator design.   
Typical retrofit scenarios, include:  
•
Conventional A/B filling to oRABS
•
oRABS to cRABS
Note: Retrofitting a cRABS could be challenging.
•
Adding transfer interfaces (e.g. RTPs) to existing RABs
Retrofitting a RABS or transferring system interfaces along the filling line is recommended to address the 
following needs:   
•
Create a barrier system for the RABS critical zone and interfaces
•
Compliance with First Air concepts (i.e., modifications to the design of the manufacturing line may
be needed)
•
Reduced open-door interventions 
•
Improved CCS for the manufacturing line
•
Requirements from relevant health authorities (international and local)
Note: Retrofitting a RABS could be difficult.

First Air Principle Maintained

components and materials that can be transferred into the RABS via rapid transfer ports (e.g. RTPs) to avoid

1.3 for further details on RABS design considerations.
