Q 7.3. What considerations are required for qualification of the restricted access 
barrier system (RABS) cleaning, bio-decontamination, disinfection, and sporicidal 
disinfection process, and validation of the bio-decontamination and decontamination 
process? 
Recommendation 
The qualification of the RABS cleaning and disinfection process is critical to ensuring a Grade A 
environment within the RABS. The planning of the qualification should begin as early as possible prior to 
implementation. A key output of the design phase of a RABS installation is a quality risk assessment 
detailing the RABS physical attributes and usage scenarios that may have an impact on product quality. This 
risk assessment then feeds into a validation plan of which cleaning and disinfection validation is a major 
component.   
There are different facets to the RABS cleaning and disinfection (bio-decontamination) and 
decontamination validation process:   
•
Establishing Grade A conditions for aseptic manufacturing
•
Applying cleaning validation for drug-product residue removal as a cross-contamination control
measure
Decontamination may be applied as a cross-contamination control measure and/or a measure to manage 
operator exposure to toxic or biologically hazardous products. Where aseptic-containment is required to 
process toxic or biohazard products, it may be more appropriate to apply isolator barrier technology because 
of the inherent limitations of containment measures (e.g., air-overspill to the surrounding environment) in 
RABS.   
RABS Cleaning Process  
The qualification of the RABS cleaning process should provide confidence of the: 
•
Removal of any residue (chemical, including lubricants) or debris that would detrimentally impact
the effectiveness of the disinfecting agent to be used. The target should be visibly clean (1).
•
Reduction of bioburden before the disinfection process step, as determined by the end-user.
RABS Disinfection Process  
The qualification and validation of the RABS disinfection process should provide confidence that the 
disinfection procedure demonstrates its effectiveness in reducing any microbiological contamination within 
the RABS to an acceptable level. The disinfection process should include:  
1.
Use of a sporicidal agent, possibly in rotation with other disinfectant agents that together provide
broad spectrum efficacy (7). The selection and frequency of use should be based on EM data
(quantitative/qualitive), trends, cleanroom materials, and the efficacy of the disinfectant procedures
(2).
Note: Rotation of the disinfectants is not considered due to increasing microbial resistance but
because different disinfectants have different modes of action, and their combination can increase
the spectrum of efficacy. Rotation may also reduce occurrence of material damage.
2.
Use of sterile disinfectant agents for Grade A and Grade B areas.
3.
A target log reduction for bacteria, spores, and viruses (if applicable) (6, 20).

Topic 8. Aseptic Process Simulation 
Q 8.1. What are the special considerations for aseptic process simulation (APS) in a 
restricted access barrier system (RABS)? 
Recommendation 
A risk-based analysis and approach taken for APS are recommended for all aseptic processing technologies, 
including RABS systems. By design, the APS should imitate routine manufacturing conditions and should 
ensure that all critical steps are challenged. A RABS relies on the surrounding cleanroom environment to 
maintain Grade A conditions for sterile processing. To maintain Grade A, the RABS routinely includes 
cleaning and disinfection processes, in addition to sterilization processes for processing accessories and 
equipment. By design, the RABS should only provide for open-door intervention during setup and, by 
exception, during operations. The cleanroom qualification, operator training, cleaning, and disinfection 
programs should all be qualified.  
There are several key considerations that need to be established in support of APS, and they should include, 
but are not limited to:  
•
Cleanroom and clean air equipment, such as UDAF units, must be qualified according to the
required characteristics of the environment.
•
The RABS must be qualified to meet all requirements for aseptic processing including HEPA-filter
integrity-testing, pressure differential across the filters, air velocity testing, and AVS. AVS should
confirm unidirectional airflow that sweeps over and away from exposed products during processing,
without increased risk of contamination through turbulent airflow. Airflow visualization
qualification and monitoring should be applied at open-door interventions as a prerequisite to all
APS designs to ensure environmental-control conditions are qualified in readiness for an APS.
Note: APS cannot be used to justify poor design of equipment and processes.
•
Operator training and qualification extending to Grade A and Grade B aseptic gowning and aseptic
behavior training must be complete. Operators undertaking RABS cleaning and disinfection
programs should be qualified and operations should be implemented procedurally.
•
A list of authorized inherent and corrective interventions should be designed, defined, and
appropriately assessed and documented in the CCS to minimize the risk of contamination. Open-
door interventions should be a rare and exceptional occurrence. When required, the need to take
representative sterility samples should be considered, and the intervention should be effectively
demonstrated in an APS.
•
Qualified methods are in place for the transfer of materials that enter the cleanroom, and
subsequently the RABS, that do not compromise the cleanroom-surrounding environment or the
RABS Grade A processing zone.

•
Risk assessments support the establishment of a comprehensive EM program, which should be
based on detailed knowledge of the process, facility, and equipment. Sampling locations, sampling
frequency and type of EM monitoring (e.g. active air, passive, contact plate) should be defined
based on risk assessment output.
The use of a “piggyback” APS can be considered as part of the overall APS design. A piggyback APS is 
where an APS run is undertaken at the end of a routine single or campaign filling process and can be used in 
support of validating an extended run or campaign length/duration. There are specific limitations associated 
with a piggyback APS which, by design, does not provide for an appropriate simulation of activities and

also needs to be given to potential interferences of residual product with the growth-promoting properties of 
the media used in the APS. Consequently, a piggyback APS alone will not fulfill the validation requirements 
to demonstrate an effective APS program. Rather, its use can be considered as part of an overall effective 
APS program, which also must include an APS strategy that covers setup and other potential worst-case 
scenarios.  
The scheduling of piggyback APS in conjunction with traditional APS should be supported by a formal risk 
assessment, for which the limitations of the piggyback approach and its use are considered.

The planning of open- and closed-door interventions and simulation of worst-case criteria should be based 
on risk management principles. The same criteria for the design of an APS as stated in PDA Technical 
Report No. 22 (Revised 2011): Process Simulation for Aseptically Filled Products are also appropriate for 
all RABS.  
After establishing Grade A conditions inside a RABS, the barrier technology provides a physical and 
aerodynamic barrier to contamination ingress to Grade A when the barrier is closed. The barrier is open at 
setup where presterilized materials are transferred and are installed. At this time, both Grade A conditions 
and the sterilized parts are at an increased risk for contamination and, thus, can compromise sterility.   
If rare open-door interventions take place during aseptic manufacturing, there is also a risk escalation that 
needs mitigation by design, qualifications, and procedural controls, and is subject to additional qualification 
in an APS. Such in-process significant interventions require consideration for sterility samples to be taken 
and should be included in the APS.   
Simulation of the production activities is a necessity for designing the APS studies.

Q 8.2. What are the special considerations of the aseptic process simulation (APS) for 
multiple shifts and multiple-day campaign production in the restricted access barrier 
system (RABS)? 
Recommendation 
The duration of the APS should be based on risk assessment and sufficient to adequately challenge the 
aseptic process. The duration should be long enough to allow simulation of the predetermined interventions 
and activities and to fill the required units.  
Where there are no risk-based time-related effects and where longer duration does not pose a risk to the 
routine production process, there is no requirement that the APS should be equal to or longer than the 
maximum production duration.  
The following points need to be considered when creating an APS for campaign manufacturing: 
•
Setup
•
Number and duration of interventions based on risk management principles to ensure that the APS
is representative of the routine manufacturing process and qualified interventions (type, frequency,
and duration)
•
Indirect product-contact parts remain installed for the duration for requalification of the campaign
and are subject to an aseptic hold time (the duration may need to be shortened based on the risk
assessment evaluation)
•
Replacement or leaving in place of direct product-contact parts (i.e., product filling needles), should
be representative of the routine manufacturing process exposure time
•
RABS gloves remain installed for the duration of the campaign
•
Cleaning/disinfection practices occurring inside the RABS during routine campaign manufacturing
should be simulated
•
Certain circumstances when performing an APS related to a production hold and/or interruptions
•
Taking of sterility samples after significant critical interventions, if justified
The approach to APS with a “piggyback” qualification is not considered a best practice as setup is missing 
in the final APS and not representative of the full campaign.  
Rationale 
Contamination of an aseptic process is primarily an outcome of events rather than time. Therefore, the 
duration of the APS should be sufficient to assess the performance of those activities identified in the risk 
assessment as having the potential to introduce contamination.   
The APS is not sensitive enough to uncover the flaws, weaknesses, or failures in the integrity of the RABS, 
the environmental quality, or the ability of the personnel to perform activities over time.   
The APS should challenge and confirm the effectiveness of these contamination control measures. This 
means that the maintenance of environmental conditions should be assured and assessed by the RABS 
system design and EM. Good process design and well-controlled activities required between the batches 
within a campaign (e.g., fill needles or glove replacement), RABS gloves ergonomics, training and 
qualification of personnel, assessment of human factors, and scheduling of breaks and rest periods for 
operators are better tools for controlling the complexity of RABS campaign operations than extended APS 
runs.

Q 8.3. What are the special considerations for determining worst-case conditions in a 
restricted access barrier system (RABS) aseptic process simulation (APS)?  
Recommendation 
Based on the features of the operation, the worst-case scenarios chosen for inclusion in an APS should be 
predefined. The best way to determine the suitable worst-case scenarios is to evaluate the APS, taking into 
account the pertinent elements of the process and the aseptic process risk assessment. The worst-case 
condition should be considered within the expected operational boundary. Worst-case does not mean 
creation of artificial conditions or environments that exceed allowed operating conditions and can force a 
system failure.  
Note: APS is not used to validate bad practices.  
A few examples of worst-case conditions include, but are not limited to: 
•
Open-door interventions (if required) in addition to the setup
•
Closed-door interventions (inherent/corrective)
The following parameters generally have a lower impact on the product-contamination risk, since the Grade 
A RABS interior environment with exposed product/critical surfaces are separated from the lower-classified 
environment and operators:  
•
Hold time of sterile materials (e.g., filtration and filling equipment, filtered media solution, sterile
garments/goggles)
•
Filling duration
•
Simulation of the maximum or minimum number of personnel
•
Speed of the filling machine (i.e., slowest speed for largest container considering the maximum-
duration vial remains open online and the highest speed for the smallest container considering
difficulties in handling)
Any intervention required via opening the door shall be considered along with the frequency and duration 
for the simulation in the APS.   
Interventions that do not require handling via an open door but need manual adjustment via RABS gloves 
and tools shall be simulated in a similar way.   
A risk-based approach shall be followed in order to determine the frequency, duration, and criticality of the 
intervention.  
Rationale 
Open-door interventions represent the highest risk for product contamination and therefore should be the 
focus of the predefined worst-case conditions in the APS. In addition, the design, barrier properties, and the 
closed-door interventions of RABS might provide other worst-case conditions, which should be considered 
in the design of APS. Companies should conduct thoughtful risk assessments to help determine which 
conditions and configurations represent the most useful challenge to the process and how to most effectively 
include them in the APS.

Q 8.4. What is the best practice when performing the setup and assembly of a 
restricted access barrier system (RABS) filling line?  
Recommendation 
There are two setup conditions for RABS: Initially, with an open barrier door, and then, readiness for 
production operations with the barrier door closed where full EM is implemented and critical tasks 
involving sterilized parts are completed, for example, positioning of filling needles (if single-use). As much 
as possible, set-up should be performed with the doors closed so careful design and understanding of the 
equipment is essential.  
The open-door set-up process presents the highest risk of contamination when transferring in or assembling 
sterilized parts or components.  
Based on risk knowledge and the role First Air takes in preventing contamination ingress into the Grade A 
zone, operators need to position themselves so protective airflow passes over body parts inserted into the 
RABS (such as hands and sleeves) and into the surrounding cleanroom. Positioning of materials ready for 
transfer should also consider protective airflow exiting the RABS Grade A zone and from the overhead or 
over-open-door UDAF. AVS should demonstrate the appropriate airflow from the interior of the RABS to 
the surrounding area.  
Materials and parts should be presented for transfer at working position, typically on carts. 
A secondary operator could support positioning parts and materials provided they do not compromise the 
protective airflow.   
Good aseptic technique (e.g., transfer of inner bag at the RABS interface by double-bag methods) is 
required on open-barrier-door entry, and the operator’s reach should be limited to the required task without 
touching other surfaces, critical or otherwise. Practices of hand disinfection prior to open-door interventions 
should be based on the contact time recommended and qualified practices. (See PDA TR-62 for additional 
information.)  
During the intervention, EM sampling locations in the proximity of the intervention should be subject to 
continuous EM (i.e., viable and nonviable). The surrounding cleanroom should also be monitored at the 
door opening/operator interface. (See Topic 5.) Installation of sterilized RABS gloves after open-door setup 
is commonly done as the final procedure. (See Topic 9.)  
Before and at the time barrier doors are closed, the inside of the barrier door should be disinfected. The 
RABS gloves should be drawn back so only the RABS glove port entry interface should require disinfection 
(in some cases, covers may be inserted to prevent RABS glove contamination at open-door interventions).  
With the barrier door closed, full EM should be implemented and should be ready for monitoring 
throughout the production-readiness setup.   
Installation setup of all critical parts requires good aseptic technique, possibly with the use of tools (e.g., 
tweezers, tongs) and throughout the procedure. First Air protection should not be compromised.   
Rationale 
As interventions are such a high-risk activity, best practice is needed in all cases to mitigate risks to an 
acceptable level.

To ensure product sterility, operators performing aseptic operations must adhere to aseptic techniques for

Overall contamination risk to product (e.g., open units, exposed components), based on personnel

•
Maintenance of First Air
•
Including the accessibility of the area to perform the intervention
•
Supported with AVS
•
Consideration of line clearance (e.g., removal of compromised units)
•
Disinfection
•
Area where the intervention takes place needs to be accessible for disinfection, if required

•
Monitoring the intervention
•
Viable (e.g., surface(s), operator(s),) and total particulates

Start and end time of intervention (i.e., duration)

Q 9.5. What should be performed after open-door interventions? 
Recommendation 
RABS should be designed and operated with doors closed during aseptic processing; however, the process

•
Additional disinfection and/or sporicidal disinfection of nonproduct-contact surfaces, including the

Identification of the appropriate units that are to be manually removed (i.e., discarded) as part of the

•

•
Start and end time of intervention
•
Description of the qualified intervention
•
Participants
•
Documentation of units removed (i.e., discarded)
•
Documentation of required EM
Note: The firm is responsible for determining the appropriate risk-based monitoring related to the

Monitoring of operator gloves and additional risk-based locations (e.g., forearms, chest, head, etc.),
immediately after completion of the open-door intervention. (See Topic 2 for additional EM

personnel and the external environment and exposed product or product-contact surfaces, which helps 
prevent microbiological contamination of the sterile product by personnel and the environment.   
In the case of open-door interventions, there is no longer a physical separation; therefore, additional control 
measures are required, as described in this section.

Q 9.6. How should interventions be performed? 
Recommendation 
Prior to performing an intervention, all product and empty containers are to be cleared, as applicable, then 
the filling process is stopped. The operator(s) will then don the RABS glove in a slow and deliberate 
manner, which should immediately be followed by a qualified visual inspection process. The operator(s) 
then executes the intervention, as described in an approved procedure, following good aseptic practices and 
the use of a sterilized tool, where appropriate, based upon the intervention.   
Upon completion of the intervention, the operator(s) returns the sterile tool, where appropriate, to its 
designated stand or location within the Grade A RABS. Prior to retracting the hands from the RABS glove, 
the operator(s) will perform a second qualified visual inspection of the glove. The operator(s) then ensures 
the glove is correctly placed to minimize stress and ensures the glove does not activate any light curtain, 
present. The filler can then be reactivated to continue the manufacturing process. When open-door RABS 
intervention is required, operators should work as a team, with a primary operator performing the task under 
Grade A environment and a secondary operator assisting the primary operator in the intervention and to 
ensure proper segregation.  
This collaboration between primary and secondary operator(s) during Grade A open-door intervention aims 
to prevent any contact between the primary operator's gloves and any surfaces exposed to the surrounding 
environment (i.e., RABS door opening, material transfer). Intrusion of the primary operator and the duration 
of the intervention into the Grade A RABS should be minimized.  
Rationale 
Each intervention needs to be performed using a sterile tool to minimize the likelihood of the RABS glove 
contacting critical equipment and/or damaging the glove. Minimizing the impact to sterile components, 
equipment, and sterile product, along with the RABS environment, is critical when executing an 
intervention. Each firm needs to have a robust discard strategy, based on approved airflow visualization, to 
discard product and open primary packaging components that have potentially been impacted when an 
intervention is required. It is important to ensure the operator(s) understands and follows good aseptic 
practices, as they can be a significant source of contamination. The integrity of the RABS gloves is an 
important part of the comprehensive CCS to minimize hazard ingress; therefore, the RABS gloves need to 
be inspected prior to and after every use. This process must be proceduralized, and each operator is to be 
qualified to perform the inspection process. Each intervention needs to be performed using a sterile tool to 
minimize the likelihood of the RABS glove contacting critical equipment and/or damage the glove.

Q 9.7. How and when should restricted access barrier system (RABS) gloves be 
installed? 
Recommendation 
RABS gloves are sterilized before installation in the RABS barrier wall (glove port), and subsequently, they 
must be wrapped and sterilized within an integral bag for protection against contamination through transfer 
within the Grade B area. Prior to installation, RABS gloves are required to be sterile. Typically, this is 
accomplished via autoclave or gamma-irradiation. There are multiple ways to install RABS gloves and, 
while this document outlines three industry approaches, other options may be considered based on the 
company’s equipment and risk assessment.  
Note: In all the approaches listed below, there should be no contact of the RABS glove to any Grade A 
surface with the exception of sterile tools.  
Approach 1: RABS Door Closed 
1.
RABS door is closed.
2.
Glove port inside is decontaminated by spraying and/or wiping with validated agents (e.g.,
sporicidals).
3.
Wrapped glove is introduced through the glove port; the glove remains covered.
Note: The wrapped RABS glove does not leave the UDAF (e.g., within cart) or UDAF zone of
protection using a UDAF cart, and it is transferred into the RABS under the UDAF zone of
protection.
4.
Upper part of the glove bag is opened using sterile tools (outside of RABS).
5.
RABS glove is connected by the attachment of the o-ring on the outside of the RABS door.
6.
Glove wrapping is aseptically removed inside the RABS.
7.
Glove remains inverted (i.e., turned “inside-out”).
8.
Glove wrapping is then taken out of the RABS by using the opening below the RABS doors for
oRABS. For cRABS another method must be used.

Q 9.8. Are restricted access barrier system (RABS) gloves treated with sporicidal agents 
and/or otherwise disinfected during operations? 
Recommendation 
The use of disinfection, including sporicidal disinfection, should be defined within a CCS. In general, there 
is no need to conduct a sporicidal disinfection of RABS glove(s) during aseptic operations.   
Note: Disinfection is only required if the RABS gloves come in contact with nonsterilized surfaces. 
RABS gloves should be installed, operated, and maintained in a manner in which they will not become 
contaminated by exposure to the zone of protection outside the RABS (Grade B environment), by contact 
with personnel during door opening, or by contact with nonsterilized surfaces within the RABS.   
This requires that the RABS gloves be inverted (turned “inside out”) to the RABS outside when leaving the 
RABS gloves with the operator hands (See Figure 1.4-1 for visual example of inversion). Leaving the 
RABS gloves inserted and hanging down inside the RABS should be prevented as they may be 
contaminated by direct contact with nonsterilized surfaces.   
When there are door openings as part of setup or as a qualified intervention during a filling operation, the 
RABS gloves should also be inverted and should not be directly exposed to the zone of protection outside 
the RABS (Grade B environment).   
RABS glove disinfection or sporicidal disinfection is only required if the RABS glove has come in contact 
with potentially contaminated surfaces, which are locations below the working level, for example, for 
interventions where an equipment- part must be fixed. The disinfection or sporicidal disinfection should be 
performed by wiping; spraying disinfectants inside the Grade A RABS during filling should be prevented.  
Rationale 
RABS gloves are sterilized before installation and should be installed in a manner that prevents 
contamination. (See Question 9.7.) Proper aseptic techniques should always be used when working with 
RABS gloves during closed-door conditions. If the door is not opened, the RABS glove is not exposed to 
the Grade B environment, and there is no contact with nonsterilized surfaces. There is no requirement for an 
additional wiping, via disinfection or sporicidal disinfection, during operations. If a contamination risk 
results as described above (e.g., RABS glove exposure in Grade B, contact with non-sterilized surfaces, etc.) 
mitigation should follow the recommendations from Questions 9.4 and 9.5.
