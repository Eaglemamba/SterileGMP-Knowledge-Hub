Topic 1. Restricted Access Barrier System Design 
Q 1.1. What are the different types of restricted access barrier systems (RABS)? 
Recommendation 
A Restricted Access Barrier System (RABS) is a series of components and processes that enclose and 
provide a Grade A environment for aseptic processing (e.g., dispensing, filling and closing, lyophilization 
loading). Due to the protection offered by the barrier system, a RABS can provide a higher level of product 
and personnel protection than conventional Grade A or Grade B filling operations. See Question 2.3.  
All RABS have rigid barriers, glove ports for operator access, a unidirectional airflow (UDAF) unit source 
providing H14 high efficiency particulate air filter (HEPA) filtered air (i.e., First Air), and a method to 
transfer materials and supplies in and out of it. Having these features in common, RABS differentiate in 
three different types of air handling: passive open RABS (oRABS), active oRABS, and closed RABS 
(cRABS), each offering an increasing level of protection for product and personnel. See Topic 3.  
Note: This document does not cover isolator design. (See PDA’s Point to Consider for the Aseptic 
Processing of Sterile Pharmaceuticals in Isolators for additional information.)  
Figures 1.1-1, 1.1-2, and 1.1-3 depict the general layout of each RABS type highlighting the UDAF source, 
rigid barrier coverage, and typical environmental classifications (4, 5).  
Note: Diagrams are provided to illustrate the ways that air is supplied to and exits from the different types 
of RABS systems. Diagrams are not intended to inform readers how operators should interact with the 
equipment enclosed; that detail follows in later sections.

All types of RABS have a barrier system in place (e.g., translucent rigid barriers) that surrounds the aseptic 
operations. In oRABS (i.e., passive and active), the barrier system is open to the room via openings below 
the product level that allow the unidirectional airflow to vent to the surrounding space. This type of oRABS 
is not sufficiently sealed during operations, therefore the RABS cannot be subjected to a Leak Rate Test to 
verify enclosure integrity while in operation.

In cRABS, the barrier system is closed to the room such that the unidirectional airflow is recirculated inside 
the cRABS. The barrier system is sealed, and the cRABS may be subjected to a Leak Rate Test to verify 
closure integrity even during operations. However, the lack of inflatable gaskets can lead to leaking into the 
room, and the Leak Rate Test should not be conducted without additional precautionary measures.  
Unidirectional Airflow Source 
Non-barrier systems and passive oRABS have unidirectional airflow supplied by the surrounding 
space/room via a ceiling-mounted HVAC system with HEPA-grade filters. Active oRABS and cRABS have 
unidirectional airflow supplied by a dedicated RABS HVAC system with HEPA-grade filters.  
Glove Ports 
Non-barrier systems do not have glove ports because the process is open to the surrounding space/room. All 
types of RABS have glove port(s) installed on the rigid barrier; number and placement will vary. Glove 
ports allow for aseptic interventions to occur without the need for the operator to enter the RABS (i.e., 
aseptic processing area).  
Rapid Transfer Ports (RTPs)  
Non-barrier systems have no material transfer ports because supplies and samples that are transferred into 
and out of the processing area are done so directly. (See Topic 6 for additional information.) All types of 
RABS have material transfer port(s) that are integrated into RABS. These ports allow for the aseptic transfer 
of materials and samples in and out of the RABS. 
Note: Although RABS doors can be opened, in the absence of appropriate controls this would potentially 
allow the introduction of lower-grade air into the Grade A space, potentially contaminating the Grade A 
processing area environment with lower-grade air. Open-door interventions should be extremely rare 
occurrences, and proper risk mitigation precautions should be implemented (1).  
Environmental Control 
Factors such as temperature, humidity, and differential pressure are controlled by the HVAC unit mounted 
in the surrounding room/space for non-barrier systems and passive oRABS. These factors are controlled by 
the dedicated RABS HVAC unit for active oRABS and cRABS.  
Typically, no positive pressure is present over the processing area compared to the surrounding space. 
Minimal to no positive pressure is present in passive oRABS over the processing area compared to the 
surrounding space. In active oRABS and cRABS, positive pressure is established in the processing area 
compared to the surrounding space. However, in some designs there is the capability to selectively control 
the air volume in specific areas to enable a positive air flow.  
Pressure Cascade 
No pressure cascade is possible in non-barrier systems, or oRABS (i.e., active or passive), but this is 
possible in cRABS. 
Bio-decontamination  
Bio-decontamination (by gassing or fogging) is possible for non-barrier systems or oRABS only if the room 
is also bio-decontaminated. In the case of cRABS, it is possible to perform bio-decontamination without the 
room, however, if the cRABS does not utilize inflatable gaskets, there could be leakage around the gaskets 
leading to health safety concerns and adequate precautions are needed to ensure personnel safety.

Product  
Only nontoxic products are able to be manufactured in non-barrier systems and in oRABS (i.e., active or 
passive). In cRABS, it is possible to manufacture nontoxic or toxic drug products.  
Additional design features should be considered including, but not limited to the following:  
•
Design must ensure that all processes (i.e., with the exception of setup) will be designed to avoid
open-door interventions.
•
The barrier walls should be rigid, resistant to chemical cleaning and disinfecting agents, and also
resistant to mechanical stresses (e.g., scratches/cracking) from cleaning tools. Typical barrier
materials are polycarbonate or tempered/safety glass.
•
The positioning of equipment, components, and sensors within the critical zone (e.g., filling
needles, open vials) should be considered to ensure that First Air is not disrupted. For fixed machine
components (i.e., transport mechanisms), having an effective disinfection program in place is
required.
•
The glove positions inside the RABS barrier wall should be appropriate to allow operator arm entry
into RABS glove(s) without disruption of First Air above critical surfaces, for example, open vials
at the accumulation turntable, above fill needles, above stopper bowl, or above environmental
monitoring (EM) equipment. It is key that these locations be determined early in the design process
and tested during the mock-up phase of the project to verify operator ergonomics for all intended
operations. Having support from experienced operators during this design process is beneficial and
recommended.
Note: Any tools that are stored inside the RABS for operator use must be sterilized.
•
To protect the product from particulate contamination, it is recommended that RABS parts and
components in the aseptic processing area be constructed of non-shedding, non-leaching, non-
corrosive, chemical-resistant materials. It is recommended that all metallic components be
constructed of low-carbon 304 stainless steel or better. In the case of metallic product contact and
indirect product-contact components, low-carbon 316 stainless steel or better should be used (5). All
nonmetallic materials, including RABS gloves, gasketing, seals, and barrier walls and doors need to
withstand exposure to various cleaning and disinfecting agents and be suitable for the process.
The following are some points to consider in design and operations of RABS that impact achieving 
specified environmental control conditions within the RABS:  
•
Appropriate lighting to see the equipment, product, and components without degradation of light
sensitive product. 
•
Rounded corners and smooth materials to minimize the risk of compromising the glove integrity
during changes or line interventions.
•
Safety curtain (i.e., light curtain) to detect operator glove entry into the RABS.
•
Alarms for environmental parameters (Temperature/Humidity/UDAF velocity) and door status
(door open or closed). 
•
EM system viable and total particles in continuous operation during setup and filling operations. 
•
EM system (settle plates and active air-monitoring locations) readily accessible via glove ports. 
Rationale 
The RABS design attributes as described in Table 1.2-1 are beneficial in assuring that environmental 
conditions are maintained and minimizing microbial contamination associated with direct human 
interventions in the critical zones.
