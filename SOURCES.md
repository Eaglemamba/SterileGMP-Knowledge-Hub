## PDA — Parenteral Drug Association

**What it is:** Industry association for injectable drug manufacturers and their suppliers. Publishes Technical Reports (TR) and Points to Consider (PtC).

**What it covers:**
- Aseptic processing: TR22 (APS/media fill), PtC-12 (RABS), PtC-Isolators
- Contamination control: TR90 (CCS), TR70 (C&D programs), TR88 (microbial deviation)
- Container/closure: TR27 (CCI), TR73 (PFS), TR85 (visible particles), TR87 (glass)
- Sterilization: TR26 (filtration), TR48 (moist heat)
- Supply chain: TR39, TR46, TR52, TR68 (GDP, cold chain, serialization)
- Quality systems: TR60 (process validation), TR84 (data integrity), TR91 (PAC)
- Biologics/ATMP: TR49, TR86, PtC-13, PtC-14

**When to use it:** For operational "how to" questions — how to design a media fill, how to write a CCI strategy, how to select a sterilization method. PDA TRs represent industry consensus from hundreds of practitioners.

**Limitations:** Not regulatory documents. Regulators (FDA, EMA) may disagree with specific recommendations, but they are rarely cited negatively. They are often submitted as part of regulatory dossiers as supporting evidence.

## ISPE — International Society for Pharmaceutical Engineering

**What it is:** Engineering-focused society for pharmaceutical facility design, equipment, and systems. Publishes Baseline Guides, Good Practice Guides, and GAMP.

**What it covers:**
- Facility design: Vol.3 (Sterile Manufacturing), Vol.4 (Water & Steam), HVAC
- Commissioning & Qualification: Vol.5 (C&Q) — the industry standard for IQ/OQ/PQ
- Risk management: Vol.7 (Risk-Based Manufacture of Pharmaceutical Products)
- Data integrity & computerized systems: GAMP 5 (2nd Ed.)
- Single-use technology: SUT Guide
- Specialized topics: Tech Transfer, Process Gas, Quality Culture

**When to use it:** For engineering design decisions — ISO class selection, HVAC cascade design, water system qualification, equipment commissioning strategy. ISPE Vol.5 is the definitive C&Q reference globally.

**Compared to PDA:** ISPE is more engineering/systems-focused; PDA is more process/microbiology-focused. Both are complementary.

## PIC/S — Pharmaceutical Inspection Co-operation Scheme

**What it is:** International network of 55+ regulatory authorities sharing GMP inspection standards. PIC/S Annex 1 (2022) is the most important sterile manufacturing regulatory document globally.

**What it covers (in this hub):**
- Annex 1 (2022): Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal Products — the complete rewrite covering CCS, facility design, EM, personnel, sterilization, aseptic processing

**Regulatory weight:** PIC/S guidance is directly adopted into national GMP regulations in 55+ countries including the EU, UK, Australia, Canada, Singapore, Japan, and more. In practice, if you pass an inspection against Annex 1, you pass most global audits.

**When to use it:** When you need to know what inspectors will ask. Annex 1 defines the minimum acceptable standard for sterile manufacturing in most markets outside the US.

## FDA — US Food and Drug Administration

**What it is:** The US federal agency responsible for drug approval and GMP enforcement. FDA guidances represent the agency's current thinking on how to comply with US cGMP regulations (21 CFR Parts 210/211).

**What it covers (in this hub):**
- Aseptic Processing (2004): The definitive US sterile manufacturing guidance — facility, personnel, EM, process, testing
- Process Validation (2011): Lifecycle approach to validation (Stages 1/2/3)
- Process Inspection (CPGM 7356.002A): How FDA investigators conduct sterile manufacturing inspections

**Regulatory weight:** FDA guidances are not legally binding, but non-compliance will trigger 483 observations and Warning Letters. In practice, they define the minimum for US market access.

**Compared to PIC/S Annex 1:** FDA guidance is older (2004 vs 2022 Annex 1) and less prescriptive on specifics like CCS. Annex 1 is now more detailed. However, FDA has unique requirements (e.g., specific EM action level philosophy, PUPSIT expectations) that differ from Annex 1.

## ICH — International Council for Harmonisation

**What it is:** Joint body of regulators and industry from US (FDA), EU (EMA), and Japan (PMDA) that harmonizes pharmaceutical technical requirements for global regulatory submissions.

**What it covers (in this hub):**
- Q8(R2): Pharmaceutical Development — design space, QbD, QTPP/CQA/CPP framework
- Q9(R1): Quality Risk Management — ICH's risk assessment methodology (FMEA, HACCP, FTA)
- Q10: Pharmaceutical Quality System — the PQS framework that connects all other Q guidelines

**Regulatory weight:** ICH guidelines are formally adopted by FDA, EMA, and PMDA. They are referenced in regulatory submissions globally and are foundational to dossier preparation (CTD format).

**When to use it:** ICH Q8/Q9/Q10 form the theoretical foundation that all other documents sit on top of. When PDA TR60, TR90, or ISPE Vol.7 reference "QRM" or "design space," they mean Q9 and Q8. Read these first if you're new to pharmaceutical quality systems.

## USP — United States Pharmacopeia

**What it is:** Non-governmental scientific organization that publishes compendial standards for medicines, excipients, and test methods. The USP-NF (National Formulary) is incorporated by reference into US law (21 CFR).

**What it covers (in this hub — 56 chapters):**

| Category | Key Chapters |
|----------|-------------|
| **Core sterility & endotoxin** | 〈71〉 Sterility Tests, 〈85〉 Bacterial Endotoxins, 〈151〉 Pyrogen Test, 〈161〉 |
| **Microbial control** | 〈1116〉 Microbial Control in Aseptic Environments, 〈1117〉 Sampling Plans |
| **Sterilization** | 〈1211〉 Sterilization & Sterility Assurance, 〈1229〉 series (13 chapters) |
| **Depyrogenation** | 〈1228〉 series (5 chapters): overview, dry heat, filtration, rinsing, indicators |
| **Particulate matter** | 〈787〉 〈788〉 〈789〉 〈790〉 〈1788〉 〈1790〉 |
| **Container/closure** | 〈381〉 〈660〉 〈661〉 〈670〉 〈671〉 〈1207〉 〈1660〉 |
| **Compounding** | 〈797〉 Pharmaceutical Compounding—Sterile Preparations |
| **Quality tests** | 〈55〉 〈71〉 〈729〉 〈755〉 〈771〉 〈785〉 〈791〉 |

**Regulatory weight:** In the US, USP chapters with numbers below 1000 are **legally enforceable** for products marketed in the US — if your label says "sterile" and your product fails USP 〈71〉, you are non-compliant with US law. Chapters ≥ 1000 are informational/guidance.

**Compared to FDA:** USP tells you *how to run the test*; FDA guidance tells you *why and when*. They are complementary — FDA says "perform sterility testing"; USP 〈71〉 tells you exactly how.

## ISO — International Organization for Standardization

**What it is:** International standards body publishing consensus standards for cleanrooms, sterilization, medical devices, and quality management systems.

**What it covers (planned for this hub):**
- ISO 14644 series: Cleanroom classification (Part 1/2), testing & monitoring (Part 3), design (Part 4)
- ISO 17665: Moist heat sterilization of health care products
- ISO 11608: Needle-based injection systems (for combination products/autoinjectors)
- ISO 14971: Medical device risk management (required for combination products)
- ISO 13485: Medical device quality management systems

**Regulatory weight:** ISO standards are referenced by FDA and EMA for specific applications. ISO 14644-1 defines cleanroom classes that are now written into PIC/S Annex 1 and FDA aseptic guidance. For combination products (prefilled syringes, autoinjectors), ISO 11608 and 14971 compliance is required for the device constituent part.

**When to use it:** When making capital investment decisions for cleanroom design, when filing combination products, or when conducting device constituent part qualification.

### EU GMP Annexes — European Commission

**What it is:** The binding GMP regulations for the EU market, published by the European Commission under Directive 2003/94/EC. Structured as a main guide (Part I/II) plus annexes covering specific topics.

**Key annexes not yet in this hub:**
- **Annex 15** — Qualification and Validation: the EU regulatory companion to ISPE Vol.5 C&Q. Defines lifecycle validation, process validation stages, and revalidation triggers. Referenced in every EU inspection covering IQ/OQ/PQ.
- **Annex 2** — Biological/ATMPs: GMP requirements for biological drug substances and ATMPs. Complements ISPE Vol.6 (which covers facility engineering only).
- **Annex 20** — Quality Risk Management: EU's formal adoption of ICH Q9 into GMP regulations.

**Regulatory weight:** Legally binding for all EU-market pharmaceutical products. EMA inspectors use these as the primary inspection standard.

**When to use it:** When your product has an EU Marketing Authorisation Application (MAA), when preparing for an EMA or EU national authority inspection, or when a client requires EU GMP compliance.

**Why it's not here yet:** Annex 15 (2015) and Annex 2 (2012) are freely available PDFs from the European Commission website — no licensing barrier. Priority addition.

### Ph. Eur. — European Pharmacopoeia

**What it is:** The European equivalent of USP. Published by EDQM (European Directorate for the Quality of Medicines), legally adopted by 39 European countries. For EU-marketed products, Ph. Eur. test methods take precedence over USP.

**Key chapters relevant to sterile manufacturing:**

| Ph. Eur. Chapter | Topic | USP Equivalent |
|-----------------|-------|----------------|
| 5.1.1 | Methods of Preparation of Sterile Products | USP 〈1211〉 |
| 5.1.9 | Guidelines for Using the Test for Sterility | USP 〈71〉 informational |
| 2.6.1 | Sterility (test method) | USP 〈71〉 |
| 2.6.14 | Bacterial Endotoxins | USP 〈85〉 |
| 2.9.19 | Particulate Contamination (subvisible) | USP 〈788〉 |
| 2.9.20 | Particulate Contamination (visible) | USP 〈790〉 |

**Regulatory weight:** Legally enforceable for EU-marketed products. Ph. Eur. and USP requirements are largely harmonized (via Pharmacopoeial Discussion Group) but differ on some limits and methods — notably endotoxin limits for certain product types.

**When to use it:** When your label claims Ph. Eur. compliance, when your EU client's specification references Ph. Eur. monographs, or when there is a USP/Ph.Eur. harmonization gap affecting your release testing.

### WHO GMP — World Health Organization

**What it is:** WHO's GMP guidelines for pharmaceutical manufacturers, primarily designed for countries that do not have their own mature regulatory framework. Used by national regulatory authorities in Asia, Africa, Latin America, and the Middle East.

**Key documents for sterile manufacturing:**
- **WHO TRS 961 Annex 6** — Good Manufacturing Practices for sterile pharmaceutical products
- **WHO TRS 1010** — Good Manufacturing Practices for biological products
- **WHO TRS 1019** — Good Manufacturing Practices: Water for pharmaceutical use

**Regulatory weight:** Not legally binding internationally, but adopted by 100+ countries via the WHO Prequalification Programme. If a CDMO supplies WHO-tendered products (e.g., vaccines, ARVs, injectables for global health programs), WHO GMP compliance is a customer requirement. WHO inspectors audit manufacturing sites for prequalification.

**When to use it:** When serving clients with WHO prequalification products, when a customer's supply agreement references WHO GMP, or when entering emerging markets (Africa, Southeast Asia) where national regulations adopt WHO standards.

## How to Choose the Right Source

| Question type | Start with |
|---------------|-----------|
| "What does the inspector expect (US)?" | FDA Guidance |
| "What does the inspector expect (EU/global)?" | PIC/S Annex 1, EU GMP Annexes |
| "What does the inspector expect (WHO prequalification)?" | WHO TRS 961 Annex 6 |
| "How do I design this process/facility?" | ISPE Baseline Guides, PDA TR |
| "How do I run this test / what are the limits (US)?" | USP General Chapters |
| "How do I run this test / what are the limits (EU)?" | Ph. Eur. Chapters |
| "How do I structure my submission?" | ICH Q8/Q9/Q10 |
| "What is industry best practice?" | PDA TR, ISPE GPG |
| "How do I qualify this equipment/system (EU regulatory)?" | EU GMP Annex 15, ISPE Vol.5 |
| "How do I qualify this equipment/system (industry practice)?" | ISPE Vol.5 |
| "What counts as compliant for this device component?" | ISO 14971, ISO 11608 |
| "What are the cleanroom classification requirements?" | ISO 14644-1/2, PIC/S Annex 1 §4 |
