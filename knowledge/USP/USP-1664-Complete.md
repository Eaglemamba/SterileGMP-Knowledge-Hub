# USP General Chapter 〰1664〱: Assessment of Drug Product Leachables Associated with Pharmaceutical Packaging/Delivery Systems

USP General Chapter <1664>
ASSESSMENT OF DRUG PRODUCT LEACHABLES ASSOCIATED WITH PHARMACEUTICAL PACKAGING/DELIVERY SYSTEMS
Official Date: 01-Dec-2024 | Currently Official: 12-Feb-2025
DOI: https://doi.org/10.31003/USPNF_M7127_04_01

PURPOSE

This general chapter presents a framework for the design, justification, and implementation of assessments for drug product leachables derived from pharmaceutical packaging and delivery systems. A scientifically sound leachables assessment is important to manufacturers and their various suppliers primarily as a means of establishing the suitability for use of pharmaceutical packaging/delivery systems, as leachables can potentially affect drug product efficacy, safety, and quality.

Designing an individual leachables assessment is a process that strikes a balance between sound science, prudent resource allocation, and effective risk management with an emphasis on patient safety and product quality. Achieving this balance is the responsibility and obligation of the drug product manufacturer.

KEY TERMS

Packaging Systems: The sum of packaging components that together contain and protect the dosage form. Also referred to as Container Closure Systems; may include primary, secondary, and tertiary packaging.

Container: A receptacle that holds an intermediate compound, active pharmaceutical ingredient, excipient, or dosage form and is in direct contact with the product.

Closure: A material that seals an otherwise open space on a container and provides protection for the contents.

Packaging Component: Any single part of the package or container-closure system including the container (e.g., ampuls, prefilled syringes, vials, bottles), closures (e.g., screw caps, stoppers), ferrules and overseals, closure liners, inner seals, administration ports, overwraps, administration accessories, labels, cardboard boxes, and shrink wrap.

Primary Packaging Component: In direct contact or may come into direct contact with the product (e.g., IV bag).

Secondary Packaging Component: In direct contact with a primary packaging component and may provide additional protection of the product.

Tertiary Packaging Component: In direct contact with a secondary packaging component and may provide additional protection of the product during transportation and/or storage.

Ancillary Component: A component or entity that may come into contact with a tertiary packaging component during distribution, storage, and transportation (e.g., pallets, skids, shrink wrap, active containers).

Packaging Materials of Construction: Substances used to manufacture packaging components. Also referred to as Raw Materials.

Delivery System: The sum of components and materials that are used to transport a drug product from its packaging to the point of administration into the patient.

Extractables: Organic and inorganic chemical entities that can be released from a pharmaceutical packaging/delivery system, packaging component, or packaging material of construction and into an extraction solvent under laboratory conditions. Extractables are potential leachables.

Leachables: Foreign organic and inorganic chemical entities that are present in a packaged drug product because they have leached into the packaged drug product from a packaging/delivery system, packaging component, or packaging material of construction under normal conditions of storage and use or during accelerated drug product stability studies. Leachables are typically a subset of extractables or are derived from extractables.

Migrants: Foreign organic and inorganic chemical entities that are present in a packaged drug product after the migrant has crossed a physical barrier, such as that provided by primary and secondary packaging. Migrants are derived from secondary and tertiary packaging and ancillary components.

Leachables Studies: Laboratory investigations into the qualitative and quantitative nature of a particular leachables profile(s) over the proposed shelf-life of a particular drug product.

Characterization: The discovery, identification, and quantitation of each individual organic and inorganic leachable present in a drug product formulation above a predetermined level or threshold.

Identification: The process of assigning a molecular structure to an organic leachable, or assigning constituent elements in the case of an inorganic leachable.

Quantitation: The process of measuring the level, or concentration, of an individual organic or inorganic leachable contained in a drug product formulation.

Leachables Profiles: Qualitative and/or quantitative analytical representations of the leachables content of a particular drug product formulation.

Leachables-Extractables Correlations: Established when observed drug product leachables are linked both qualitatively and quantitatively to extractables from associated packaging/delivery systems, packaging components, or materials of construction.

Threshold of Toxicological Concern (TTC): A level of exposure for all chemicals, whether or not there is specific toxicity data, below which there would be no appreciable risk to human health.

Safety Concern Threshold (SCT): The threshold below which a leachable would have a dose so low as to present negligible safety concerns from carcinogenic and noncarcinogenic toxic effects. For OINDP: SCT = 0.15 µg/day TDI.

Qualification Threshold (QT): The threshold below which a given noncarcinogenic leachable is not considered for safety qualification (toxicological assessments) unless the leachable presents structure-activity-relationship (SAR) concerns. For OINDP: QT = 5 µg/day TDI.

Analytical Evaluation Threshold (AET): The threshold at or above which a leachable should be characterized and reported for toxicological assessment. The AET can be mathematically derived from the SCT based on factors that include the dosing parameters of the drug product.

BACKGROUND

Management of leachables is important to pharmaceutical and biotechnology/biologic product manufacturers and regulatory authorities because certain leachables above specific concentrations can present safety concerns for patients and/or compatibility issues for drug product formulations. During the 1980s, the FDA began to formally address leachables in drug products after findings of patient sensitivity induced by leachables and other potential safety concerns.

Table 1 — Modified FDA/CDER/CBER Risk-Based Approach to Consideration of Leachables:

Degree of Concern Associated with Route of Administration:
- Highest risk: Inhalation Aerosols and Sprays (High interaction), Injections and Injectable Suspensions + Inhalation Solutions (Medium), Sterile Powders for Injection + Inhalation Powders (Low)
- High risk: Transdermal Ointments and Patches (High), Ophthalmic Solutions + Nasal Aerosols (Medium)
- Low risk: Topical Solutions + Suspensions + Oral Solutions (High), Oral Tablets + Oral Capsules + Topical Powders (Low)

CONCEPTS

General Concepts for Leachables Assessment

In order to assess these risks and manage the potential issues posed by leachables, it is necessary to know their identities and the levels to which they will accumulate in the finished drug product over its shelf-life.

Leachables studies can be used within the context of an overall leachables assessment to:
- Facilitate development of safe and effective dosage form packaging/delivery systems
- Facilitate establishment of qualitative and quantitative leachables-extractables correlations
- Establish the worst-case drug product leachables profile
- Identify trends in drug product leachables accumulation levels over the shelf-life
- Facilitate change-control processes for drug product packaging/delivery systems
- Facilitate investigations into the origin(s) of identified leachables producing OOS results

Safety Thresholds

Safety thresholds allow for a science- and risk-based determination of acceptable levels of leachables. Because safety thresholds are derived from exposure data they are considered in terms of units of exposure, such as Total Daily Intake (TDI). Any safety threshold must be converted into units of concentration (e.g., µg/mL) to be applied as an analytical threshold in the laboratory.

Examples of safety thresholds:
- TTC (EMA): 1.5 µg/day TDI for genotoxic impurities (risk factor 10^-5)
- PQRI SCT for OINDP: 0.15 µg/day TDI (risk factor 10^-6)
- PQRI QT for OINDP: 5 µg/day TDI

Below the SCT: identification and safety evaluation generally would not be required.
Below the QT: leachables without structure alerts for carcinogenicity or irritation would not require compound-specific safety risk assessment.

Special case compounds for OINDP requiring lower thresholds: PAHs/PNAs, N-nitrosamines, 2-mercaptobenzothiazole.

Information Sharing

To successfully manage leachables throughout the drug product lifecycle, it is critical to establish close and regular communication among stakeholders: chemists, toxicologists, packaging engineers, manufacturing operations, procurement, etc.

LEACHABLES STUDY DESIGN

Ideally, leachables assessment is conducted as follows:
- The assessment is performed on the actual drug product and not simulations thereof
- The assessment is performed with the actual packaging and delivery system in the form it will be commercialized
- The related extractables assessments are accomplished on the same lots of packaging components used to manufacture the drug product lots
- The assessment is performed on a product manufactured under conditions that reflect actual commercial processes; must include real-time assessment (not only accelerated conditions)

Leachables studies can also be performed early in drug product development (preclinical stage) to facilitate selection of packaging components. For high-risk dosage forms, leachables characterization is recommended for any drug product batches used in definitive toxicology or clinical studies.

During later stage development in support of product registration, leachables studies may be accomplished on definitive registration batches during the course of overall product stability studies. For inhalation aerosols and other OINDP, leachables testing should be an integral part of the larger ICH registration stability program.

Post-market leachables assessments may be appropriate after necessary or desired changes in a marketed drug product, including: composition of the drug formulation; manufacturing processes; primary and secondary packaging components or their materials of construction.

The design of a particular leachables study requires:
1. Information on identities and maximum possible accumulation levels of all potential leachables
2. Consideration of physical/chemical properties of potential leachables (polarity, volatility, solubility)
3. Consideration of nature of formulation contact (direct or indirect; transient or continuous)
4. Assessment of all possible sources including primary, secondary, tertiary packaging and manufacturing equipment

LEACHABLES CHARACTERIZATION

The primary goal of any leachables study is leachables characterization: the discovery, identification, and quantitation of leachables present in a particular drug product.

Analytical Thresholds

The AET Formula:
AET (µg/container) = (0.15 µg/day ÷ doses/day) × (labeled doses/container)

For liquid dosage forms:
AET (µg/mL) = AET (µg/container) ÷ mL/container

For solid dosage forms:
AET (µg/g) = AET (µg/container) ÷ g/container

This AET establishes the level at which unknown leachables should be identified and quantified in a particular drug product, and can be used as a basis for analytical method development.

Analytical Method Requirements

Analytical method requirements are based on the determined AET (or alternative valid threshold concept) and information on potential leachables obtained from extractables assessments. Any analytical method for leachables used for drug product stability studies in support of product registration, establishing leachables-extractables correlations for high-risk dosage forms, or developing leachables specifications must be subject to complete validation.

Preparing the Drug Product for Analysis — Sample Preparation

Sample preparation approaches by dosage form:
- Aqueous dosage forms: liquid-liquid extraction with water-immiscible organic solvents (dichloromethane, hexane, petroleum ether); pH manipulation to enhance extraction
- Solid dosage forms: dissolve in aqueous solution, apply liquid-liquid extraction; or headspace GC for volatiles
- Oral liquid dosage forms: dilute in aqueous solution, apply liquid-liquid extraction
- Cream and ointment dosage forms: dissolve in aqueous solution, filter, apply liquid-liquid extraction
- Nonaqueous drug product vehicles (MDI with organic propellants): special sample preparation (see USP 1664.1)

Analytical Techniques

Most useful techniques: GC/MS and HPLC (LC)/MS for discovery, identification, and quantitation of organic leachables. Structural analysis should be accomplished using the same systematic process described in USP 1663 for extractables, to a level of confidence sufficient for safety assessment.

Quantitative Methods — Validation Considerations

Validation parameters may include: accuracy, precision (repeatability, intermediate precision), specificity, limit-of-detection, limit-of-quantitation, linearity and range, and robustness.

Key considerations:
- Accuracy and precision: evaluated using drug product samples spiked with known amounts of target leachables
- Linearity and range: validated linear range should consider the potential maximum accumulation levels of each target leachable
- Limit of detection/quantitation: the limit of quantitation should be at or below the designated AET
- Specificity: evaluated by chromatographic peak purity in spiked and non-spiked drug product samples
- Robustness: design-of-experiments statistical approach considering critical analytical method parameters
- System suitability: evaluated each time the quantitative leachables method is used

ESTABLISHING A LEACHABLES-EXTRACTABLES CORRELATION

A leachables-extractables correlation is established when actual drug product leachables can be linked either qualitatively or quantitatively with extractables from corresponding extractables assessments.

Example of quantitative correlation:
- BHT in drug product formulation: 5 µg/mL
- BHT extracted from primary packaging component: 300 µg/component
- Drug product volume: 50 mL
- BHT extracted: 300 µg; BHT leached: 250 µg (5 µg/mL × 50 mL)
- Conclusion: quantitative correlation established; 50 µg unaccounted for (not leached or lost)

If a leachables-extractables correlation cannot be established, possible explanations include:
- Inadequate extractables assessments of packaging components
- Unreported changes in packaging component composition or manufacturing processes
- Unreported changes in the identity of packaging components

CONSIDERATIONS IN DEVELOPING LEACHABLES SPECIFICATIONS AND ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA

One means by which specifications and acceptance criteria could be developed: testing a minimum of three drug product batches to determine their leachables levels. After thorough chemical and safety evaluation, the test data can be used to establish acceptance criteria:
1. Qualitative and/or quantitative results of leachables studies
2. Capabilities of the drug product's manufacturing process
3. Potential safety, compatibility, and/or drug product quality impact of the leachables

Acceptance criteria can be both qualitative and quantitative. A typical leachables specification could include:
- Quantitative end of shelf-life limits for target leachables
- A quantitative limit for "unspecified" (previously unidentified and uncorrelated) leachables

ADDITIONAL CONSIDERATIONS

Simulation Studies

Occasions may arise in which it is not analytically feasible to successfully discover and identify all actual leachables in a drug product leachables study. In such circumstances, the activities of discovery and identification of probable leachables are accomplished in an extraction study. Key design differences between a simulation study and a drug product leachables study:
1. The drug product formulation has been replaced with a simulating solvent that mimics the formulation
2. The conditions of contact have been accelerated to increase concentrations of probable leachables
3. The test article can be either the complete packaging and delivery system or separate components

Inorganic (Elemental) Leachables

Elemental impurities leached from packaging or delivery systems represent only one source of elemental impurities in a drug product. Testing of plastic packaging systems establishes extractable elemental impurities relevant to a particular packaging system. Note that atomic spectroscopy does not establish the compound, or form, that the detected element is present in (e.g., sulfur as elemental sulfur, sulfate, or organic sulfur compound; silicon as silicone oil or silicon dioxide).

SUMMARY

The requirement for, and completeness of, a leachables assessment for any particular drug product can only be determined by the drug product applicant with reference to appropriate regulatory guidance documents.
