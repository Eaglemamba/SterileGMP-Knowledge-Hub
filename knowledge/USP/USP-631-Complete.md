# USP General Chapter 〰631〱: Color and Achromicity

USP General Chapter <631> COLOR AND ACHROMICITY
Status: Currently Official on 12-Feb-2025
Official Date: Official as of 01-Dec-2023
Document Type: General Chapter

INTRODUCTION

The purpose of this chapter is to provide well-controlled methods for the assessment of color and achromicity of uniform liquid samples. The methods described in this chapter may be applied to the liquid phase of dispersed systems in cases where the dispersed phase can be removed prior to color measurements. Color is an organoleptic characteristic, which means that the value is based on the human response to stimuli. To reliably evaluate color, it is important to control both the illumination and observation parameters. By using a characteristic response for a standard observer, it is possible to perform precise instrumental evaluations of color. This chapter provides guidance for both an organoleptic assessment (Method I) and an instrumental assessment (Method II) of color and achromicity in liquid samples.

Color may be defined as an observer's perception of or subjective response to a stimulus of radiant energy in the visible spectrum extending over the wavelength range of 400-700 nm. Perceived color is a function of three variables: spectral properties of the object, spectral power distribution of the source of illumination, and visual perception of the observer.

Two objects are said to have a color match for a particular source of illumination when an observer cannot detect a color difference. Where a pair of objects exhibit a color match for one source of illumination and not another, they constitute a metameric pair. Color matches of two objects occur for all sources of illumination if the absorption and reflectance spectra of the two objects are identical.

Achromicity or colorlessness is one extreme of any color scale for transmission of light. It implies the complete absence of color; therefore, the visible spectrum of the sample lacks absorbances. For practical purposes, the observer in this case perceives little if any absorption taking place in the visible spectrum and is unable to discern the difference between an almost colorless sample and a colorless reference.

COLOR ATTRIBUTES AND COLOR SPACE COORDINATES

Three attributes are commonly used to identify a color:
1) hue (angle), or the color dimension dominant in its purest form, such as red, yellow, blue, green, and intermediate terms
2) lightness, or the quality that distinguishes a light color from a dark one
3) chroma, or the quality that distinguishes an intense color from a weak one

CIELAB Color Space:
L* is the lightness coordinate (0 = black, 100 = colorless/white)
a* is the red/green coordinate (+a* = red, -a* = green)
b* is the yellow/blue coordinate (+b* = yellow, -b* = blue)
C*ab is the chroma = sqrt((a*)^2 + (b*)^2)
h*ab is the hue angle (0-360 degrees)

Delta E* = sqrt(deltaL*^2 + deltaa*^2 + deltab*^2)
Human eye can differentiate colors with delta E* of about 1; difficult to reliably differentiate when delta E* < 3.

COLOR DETERMINATION AND STANDARDS

Determinations should be made using diffuse, uniform illumination. Liquids should be compared in matched color-comparison containers transparent to illumination light.

Containers for Liquid Samples and Matching Fluids:
- Non-absorbing in 400-700 nm range
- Identical for sample and matching fluids
- Round or square containers
- Recommended path length: ~10 mm cuvettes or glass tubes

Turbid and Opaque Samples:
- Remove dispersed phase by centrifugation or filtration before color evaluation
- Remove air bubbles or foam by centrifugation

Matching Fluids (Table 1):
Prepared from Cobaltous Chloride CS, Ferric Chloride CS, Cupric Sulfate CS, and water in prescribed volumes.
Fluids A through T defined with specific ratios.
Freshly prepared on day of use unless stability data supports longer use.

METHOD I: ORGANOLEPTIC (QUALITATIVE) ASSESSMENT OF COLOR AND COLOR MATCHES

Illuminant:
- Daylight illuminant with CCT of 6000-7000 K
- Filtered tungsten halogen lamps recommended
- D65 illuminant conforming to ISO 23603/CIE S 012, grade B/C or better
- Illumination should be diffuse; recommended geometries: 0:45 and 45:0

Observer:
- Viewing angle of 2-10 degrees
- Compare 2.0 mL sample vs 2.0 mL water or matching fluid
- Use identical tubes of colorless, transparent, neutral glass (12-mm external diameter)
- Surrounding field: white, black, or neutral gray

Three approaches for colored samples:
1. Establish minimum level of color (passes if sample has same or more color)
2. Establish maximum level of color (passes if sample has same or less color)
3. Indiscernible from color standard (sample must match)

Achromicity Evaluations:
- Special case of maximum level of color evaluation
- Use Indiscernible from Color Standard test with Purified Water, OR
- Maximum Level of Color test with standard showing delta E* of 3-6 relative to Purified Water
- Recommended matching fluids: A, P, Q, S (from this chapter)

METHOD II: INSTRUMENTAL (QUANTITATIVE) ASSESSMENT OF COLOR AND COLOR MATCHES

Instrumentation:
- Spectrometer or colorimeter in transmission mode
- Qualified per USP <857> Ultraviolet-Visible Spectroscopy
- Operating range: 400-700 nm
- Bandwidth: 10 nm or less

Calibration:
- Black tile for zero end of lightness scale
- Purified Water for 100% transmission
- See Analytical Instrument Qualification <1058>

Recording of Spectroscopic Data:
- Record from 400-700 nm at 10-nm intervals
- Clarify turbid samples before loading into cuvette
- Check for air bubbles or foam in light transmission path

Calculation of CIELAB Values:
- Use illuminant C with 2-degree observer (C/2) OR
- Illuminant D65 with 10-degree observer (D65/10)
- Weighting factors provided in Tables 4 and 5

METHOD IIA: COMPARATIVE TEST OF COLORS USING CIELAB VALUES

Almost Colorless: delta E* vs Purified Water; requirement is delta E* < 1
Maximum Level of Color: delta E* < delta E0* (of matching fluid of similar hue angle, delta h* < 15)
Minimum Level of Color: delta E* > delta E0*
Indiscernible from Color Standard: delta E* < 3

METHOD IIB: INSTRUMENTAL COLOR ASSESSMENT

CIELAB values used directly as quantitative measure of color attributes.
Specification forms:
- L* NLT XX, NMT XX; a* NMT XX, NLT XX; and b* NMT XX, NLT XX
- delta E* versus a single point in 3-dimensional space

Reporting of Results:
CIELAB ([illuminant]/[observer]) = (L*, a*, b*)
Example: CIELAB (C/2) = (50.70, -11.30, -33.30)
