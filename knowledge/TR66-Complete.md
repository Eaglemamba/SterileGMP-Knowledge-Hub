### **1.0 Introduction**

Single-use technology, often described as single-use systems (SUSs) or single-use equipment, has the
potential to transform pharmaceutical manufacturing by offering tremendous opportunities to reduce
cost, improve flexibility or cycle time, and shorten the time needed to build a manufacturing process for
new, lifesaving drugs. This success, however, is very much dependent on how effectively the industry approaches the development and implementation of single-use technology. Ultimately, a new drug can only
be successful if it is effective, safe, and available. Traditionally, only a comprehensive understanding of the
drug product and manufacturing process can achieve these goals. This remains true as SUS is introduced
in place of traditional reusable equipment. Encouraging an open science- and risk-based dialogue during
supplier audits and evaluation of SUS supply chains significantly improves an SUS implementation.

This document is intended to provide the reader with critical concepts or points to consider when
implementing an SUS strategy in a pharmaceutical manufacturing process. These concepts are intended to be valid both for chemically synthesized small molecules and for bioprocesses that produce
large-molecule biopharmaceutical products. However, to be truly effective, many of these critical
concepts must start with the design, supply chain, manufacturing, and distribution of SUSs themselves, as many inherent quality attributes can impact either the product molecule or its production
process. Pharmaceutical manufacturers and single-use technology suppliers have become partners
whose success is dependent on the control strategies implemented.

This document discusses SUSs that are in either direct or indirect contact with the raw materials,
intermediates, and pharmaceutical drug substances or drug products. The document does not intend
to discuss disposable items related to laboratory activities, final delivery system to the patient, transfusion bags, packaging, or medical devices.

Successful SUS implementation needs a comprehensive approach balancing the product and process
goals achieved by using single-use technology. Section 3, Manufacturing Strategy, of this technical report is intended to present an approach that ties together key considerations when evaluating singleuse technology. A well-designed manufacturing strategy will address technical, quality, business, and
implementation considerations. Each topic has a dedicated section in this technical report, providing
a detailed discussion of the associated considerations.

Determining the optimal manufacturing strategy involves concepts from many disciplines. An effective
evaluation will have a balanced viewpoint, with input from engineering, regulatory, quality, project management, and accounting. Balancing risks and rewards of an SUS over a multiple-use system (MUS) will
help determine the most appropriate manufacturing strategy. Thus, a structured science- and risk-based
approach is recommended and should be consistent with principles described in ICH Guidelines Q6, Q7,
Q8, Q9, Q10, and Q11. Primary goals, when developing any manufacturing strategy, should focus on
controlling impacts to patient safety, product availability, and product and process understanding _**(1–6).**_

Only a formal partnership with an SUS supplier can ensure that quality is as good as or better than
what is achieved with traditional systems (e.g., a purchase order is not a partnership). SUS suppliers
provide equipment that includes the outsourced, value-added activities that the end user no longer
performs. These value-added activities are important for the success of both organizations, and a winning control strategy for SUS has elements in both the supplier’s and the end user’s quality systems.

The concepts and recommendations presented in this technical report were developed over several
years of discussion within the task force, at PDA workshops, and at other industry meetings. The authors of this technical report recognize that the conversation regarding how best to implement SUSs
is just beginning. Ultimately the success of these systems will be determined by the decisions suppliers and end users make during implementation, and the hope is that this report provides a foundation
for the industry to build on.

### **2.0 Glossary of Terms**

**Campaigning**
Extending the period of time, or number of cycles a single-use system is operating in a closed
process without breaking the sterile barrier processes. The end user is responsible to evaluate
and determine if appropriate quality requirements are met for their application.

**Closed System**
An isolated system that has no interaction with
its external environment, preventing contamination and release of the material contained.

**Consumables**
This refers to items (e.g., SUS, storage bags, tubing, filters, diaphragms, flasks, etc.) that form or
are a part of process equipment and are used on
a per batch basis.

**Deployment**
Activities involving the hands-on steps required
to successfully assemble and make a system
ready for use for a specific SUS application.

**Extractables**
Chemical substances that can be extracted from
components of material process fluid contact surfaces by exertion of an exaggerated force (e.g., organic solvent, extreme elevated temperature, ionic
strength, pH, contact time, etc.) Extractables may
represent most but not all of the potential leachables that may be seen in process fluids.

**Flexible 2D or 3D Bag**
A flexible-wall container designed with 2 sides
(two dimensional or “pillow” shape) or 6 sides
(three dimensional cuboid shape) designed to
hold process fluids or product.

**In-Process Leachables**
Chemicals substances that are leached, from
product-contact or non-product-contact materials under typical process conditions and could
be cleared or sufficiently diluted by downstream
processes so as to be undetected as leachables
in the final dosage. Alternate Terms: Transient
Leachables, Migrant Leachable.

**Packaged Raw Material for Single-Use**
Procurement of a product such as liquid or powder format culture media or buffer and that has
been supplied in single-use technology

**Leachables**
Chemicals substances that are leached, from
product-contact or non-product-contact materials, under typical process conditions and detected in final dosage. Leachables may be a subset
of Extractables, and can include their reaction or
breakdown products.

**Multi-Use System (MUS)**
An engineered process equipment solution for
process management and unit operations designed for repeated use.

**Opportunity Costs**
This is defined as the value of the next-best
choice available when choosing between several
mutually exclusive choices (e.g., the decision to
expand a facility may result in losing the opportunity to invest the maintenance funds in the
financial markets). Opportunity costs are often
excluded from estimates of fixed operation costs
because they can be difficult for comparative
analyses in the overall decision process

**Pressure Decay Test**
A leak test in which a container or system is pressurized with air to a preset level. After the pressure has stabilized, the decay in pressure over a
preset test time is measured and evaluated to determine if a leak (defect) is present.

**Ready-To-Use**
A marketing term often used to describe the benefits of single-use technology or SUS. This designation has no regulatory or scientific basis supporting
suitability for use and the end user is responsible to
evaluate and determine if appropriate quality requirements are met for their application.

**Single-Use Component (SUC)**
An individual part designed to perform a particular
function when assembled into a single-use system.

**Single-Use System (SUS)**
An engineered process equipment solution,
most commonly assembled from components
made using polymeric materials, which together
create a system or unit operation designed for
one time or campaign use.

**SUS Interchangeability**
Functionally equivalent substitution of an alternative SUS, for an existing SUS design providing a
contingency or process improvement with equivalent process performance and product quality.
The end user is responsible to evaluate and determine if appropriate quality requirements are
met for their application.

This section provides an introduction to and overview of the most important points to consider and
the necessary steps the end user must accomplish in order to implement a successful single-use (SU)
strategy, while a more detailed description of these points and a structured implementation strategy
can be found in the subsequent sections of this document. Additionally, this section offers a guideline
for a structured approach for an initial feasibility evaluation of a single-use system (SUS) manufacturing strategy.

The key to successful SU technology implementation is the development of an effective manufacturing strategy for the molecule or formulation to be produced. Many factors must be considered
when an organization is making strategic decisions, and implementation of SU technology is no different. In this report we suggest developing a high-level manufacturing strategy after a science- and
risk-based approach with consideration given to four main areas that influence the success of SUS
implementation:

        - Technologies and system integration

        - Qualification of assembled products

        - Business drivers

       - Implementation

The resulting manufacturing strategy will then outline the questions for consideration needed for
successful implementation of an SUS. This technical report has a dedicated section for each of these
areas of consideration. An overview of each will be presented in this section.

As depicted in **Figure 3.0-1,** each area plays a crucial role in establishing successful use of SUSs. Questions within these areas of focus must be considered and balanced against an organization’s needs and
competencies when developing a manufacturing strategy (detailed in **Section 7.1).** Only a comprehensive approach ensures that the primary customer-based goals of patient safety, product availability,
product understanding, and process understanding are met when implementing new technology.

****3.1 Single-Use Technologies****

SUSs differ in many ways from conventional MUSs. When examining whether a technically feasible
single-use solution exists, the end user should evaluate process operating parameters and requirements in relation to traditional systems:

        - Process and product are compatible with materials of construction

        - Process volume, temperature, pH, pressure, and flow rate are within specifications

        - Oxygen, moisture, or light barrier properties of the system are adequate for the process

        - Specific process components can be sterilized

        - Process monitoring and sampling strategy exists for the critical operating parameters

        - Access ports and connection technology are available to perform the operation

        - Mixing technology meets the uniformity needs of the process

        - Weight, size, and physical strength of SUS meet the needs of the process and facility

After an organization determines that a technical solution exists for a single-use solution, other considerations to be included in the final decision to implement an SUS are as follows:

        - Does the implementation of SUS impact existing process equipment that will need modification to
optimize use of an SUS?

        - What is the impact on cost and material management of customization, given that custom manufacturing of small quantities is less effective for suppliers?Although there are advantages to using
a supplier’s standard configurations, in some cases the SUS itself may require customization to accommodate an individual organization’s needs.

        - What is the potential impact on facility design and utility requirements?

The end user’s approach to the determination of technical feasibility should be recorded in a riskanalysis document that is consistent with the methods outlined in ICH Q9, and it should include
process-specific considerations such as _**(4)**_ :

        - The stage of the SUS application

        - Process performance or capability

        - Process scale

        - Product quality attributes

        - Batch production rates

        - Factors affecting system design (e.g., process compatibility, component reliability, facility design,
storage and materials handling, and/or transportation)

****3.3 Qualification and Verification of Suppliers, Materials,** **Components, and Completed Assemblies****

Any adverse effect on product quality and, hence, patient safety must be the main concern when
the end user performs a risk assessment of an SUS. An assessment of critical operational parameters
must be performed in order to determine the potential impact of an SUS on product quality. In this
exercise, the end user must take into account the potential for interaction between process fluid and
the materials of construction of the process equipment.

****3.3.1 Product Risk****

The potential impact of the SUS on the product quality is a key area to be assessed through a riskbased approach considering its proximity to the final product, the processing stage, and product requirements. The following are examples of specific considerations for potential interactions between
SUS and product fluids:

        - An SUS is a defined system, which may minimize the risk of both microbial and chemical contamination.

        - SUSs are susceptible to generation of particulates during the fabrication and operation of the system.

        - SUS minimizes risk of cross-contamination when they are used once or in campaign manufacturing
of a single drug substance.

        - An SUS will contribute to the leachables present in a process. An assessment of impact can be made
by 1) Identifying potential leachables through an extractables study and 2) Conducting further studies
should an extractables study identify materials of concern.

Product impact considerations typically include:

         - Sterility

        - Degradation, or binding to the product molecule

        - Adsorption of the product molecule or product pool

        - Adsorption of the functional component of the media or buffer

       - Interactions between leachables or their breakdown products with the product molecule or
formulation or the process materials of construction

        - Technical limitations of the SUS, which could lead to suboptimal processing, poor cell growth, low
yields, or impact to product-critical quality attribute (e.g., limitation of temperature control may impact
MAB glycan profile)

        - Changes in the light, gas, moisture, or other barrier to the surroundings, which could cause oxidation
or chemical reaction of the product molecule, product pool, or media components

In addition to product quality considerations, incorporating an SUS may affect key operating parameters at different process steps and potentially the control strategy for the entire process. An assessment for each process step, as well as the entire process, should include the following considerations:

        - Impact on system integrity (may be positive or negative)

        - Estimation of risk of equipment or system failure for both SUS and MUS, including downtime for
correcting potential failures and backup equipment required (e.g., maintaining an ISO 5/Grade A-B
area for connections if an SU connector fails or designing the connection for backup sterile tube
welding if a connector fails)

        - Size limitations of an SUS as compared to an MUS

       - Productivity impacts when using SUS as compared to an MUS approach

        - Verification activities, such as system integrity or leaks, compared to an MUS approach

        - Supplier and vendor supply-chain transparency, change control, communication, product quality,
and supply security

        - Proximity of the SUS to the final drug product

****3.4 Process Control Strategy Considerations****

The monitoring capability of single-use and multiple-use systems may differ. Some points to keep in
mind include the following:

        - When deciding between reusable and single-use probes, balance the cost per batch to generate the
desired data, control of sensor calibration, cleaning validation requirements, and probe accuracy
and reliability.

        - Consider and assess impact from potential differences in automation level; SUSs typically include
more manual controls dependent on operator training and execution. Some SUSs may incorporate
proprietary support framework and control systems at comparable costs to traditional process skids;
however, this significantly limits SUS interchangeability. To avoid this problem, the end user may
wish to adopt cross-platform supervisory control and data application (SCADA) systems, which
provide greater flexibility and portability.

        - Consider that an SUS may affect how adjustments to the process are made (e.g., adjusting pH,
adding solids, foam control); in some cases these adjustments may not be possible with SUS.

       - Note that qualifying and calibrating probes and the measurement system before use may be
more complicated or not possible if a single-use probe is built into a presterilized system. Batch
documentation practices for recording sensor calibration may be different for SUS.

        - Evaluate dependency on the supplier’s and vendor’s quality system and qualification activities.

        - Account for the possibility of smaller batch sizes with increased frequency or sub-batch or batch
pooling due to smaller container volumes.
****3.5 Implementation of a Single-Use System****

Implementation includes all of the activities required for the realization of the end user’s manufacturing strategy. Since the manufacturing strategy is an assessment of feasibility informed by evaluation
of the considerations from the sections on technology, business drivers, qualification, and implementation in this report, it provides a framework for implementation of the specific situation evaluated.
However, each evaluation performed will be unique.

Furthermore, since time-to-clinic and time-to-market are becoming increasingly important factors
for making implementation decisions, a comparative implementation assessment has become a crucial requirement. A well-thought-out and thorough implementation plan is important for successful
implementation of an SUS. The implementation process is organized as a road map with a series of
gateways between stages. The workflow should be organized around the following themes.
****3.5.1 Stakeholder Management****

Successful implementation of an SUS may require an active analysis of the effects of the implementation on the organization and stakeholder management. Broad implementation of SUS across an
organization can impose certain organizational strains and stresses and problems may arise which
have to be considered and dealt with.
****3.5.2 Risk Management****

The use of formal risk-assessment methods during SUS implementation provides a means for quantitative comparisons of risk related to MUS versus SUS to assist in decision making and stakeholder
management _**(7)**_ **.**

****3.5.3 Process Validation and Verification (PVV)****

The PVV strategy for an SUS differs from that of an MUS. Therefore, the end user must be aware
that traditional qualification activities may have limited value for an SUS implementation without the
confidence that the supplier’s production processes are of a high enough caliber and the end user’s
process is sufficiently robust to tolerate any variations of the SUS by the supplier _**(8)**_ .
****3.5.4 Scoping****

This segment of the implementation plan contains a detailed description of the technical scope of the
SUS project; it complements the manufacturing strategy by providing global guidance and policies to
be applied for SU applications across the project.
****3.5.5 Project Execution Plan (PEP)****

A PEP provides a detailed description of the project, the execution strategy, management, and compliance in order to attain project goals in terms of budget, time, and quality. This includes a policy for
the identification and selection of suppliers, which will encourage the creation of a transparent relationship and help prevent the selection of equipment and suppliers from becoming an ad hoc process.
An example PEP is shown in Section 9, Appendix II: SUS Facility Project Execution Plan Example.
****3.5.6 End-User Requirements****

This section of the plan provides a description of the end user’s requirements for the SUS, including
E&L and standardization in preparation for the equipment procurement process.
****3.5.7 Testing and Documentation****

Following delivery and installation, the user will develop methods and documentation for equipment
SOPs; maintenance; qualification and process validation; the HSE plan; and personnel training.
****3.5.8 Supplier Management****

The effective management of SUS suppliers must include periodic review meetings involving the
analysis of the supplier’s performance during the previous period for quality and punctuality. Business
review meetings should also include collaborative planning and effective forecasting of future demands in order to take effective advantage of the supplier’s resources, lower the costs of maintaining
capacity, and eliminate spikes in demand that may affect lead times. Appropriate supply and quality
agreements must be in place to clearly communicate expectations to critical suppliers.

Drug master files provided by either the polymer compound or film supplier can be referred to, however, they may not exist for all applications and the level of detailed of the information may vary by
submission. The polymer compound formulations (type and levels of additives), for example, may
only list the highest amount of the additives that can be included in the polymer rather than specifying the actual specification range of additives. In any case the information is not available to the end
user so it is preferable to receive the information in the form of a regulatory support file.
****3.5.9 Logistics Control Requirements****

A logistics control strategy is a critical part of the implementation of an SUS because the end user
relies on the supplier’s production and quality systems to a higher degree than for the implementation
of an MUS. The following factors are important for an effective logistics control strategy.

**3.5.9.1 Inventory and Supply Chain Management**

A thorough analysis of the entire supply chain is necessary in order to safeguard the supply of important
materials for undisrupted SUS production. Such an analysis must encompass the supply and inventory
strategy of the vendors who supply components and materials to SUS manufacturers in addition to the
supply and inventory strategies of the SUS suppliers themselves. SUS suppliers have a responsibility to
protect the security and supply of their finished goods by appropriate risk analyses and risk-mitigation
strategies for the materials and components they purchase. It is incumbent on the end user to confirm
that these strategies are adequate to maintain the continuity of supply. If the supplier’s control strategies
fall short of expectations, the end user may mitigate the risk of supply interruption by qualifying secondary sources or putting specific inventory management and supply agreements in place.

**3.5.9.2 Waste**

Operation of an SUS leads to an increased amount of solid waste compared to an MUS. An SUS
waste-management strategy must include:

        - Collecting, storing, and inactivating waste in the facility

        - Avoiding cross-contamination

        - Monitoring the waste-management activities of subcontractors

**3.5.9.3 Transportation**

All aspects of transportation and holding must be understood for their role in maintaining integrity
of the material throughout the supply chain. SU Technology requires additional care be taken during the entire supply chain; transportation, receiving, storage and delivery to the point of use. Some
considerations are:

        - Maintain integrity of material throughout the supply chain.

        - An SUS must have a control strategy (such as supplier qualification, ID tests, or statistical analysis)
of any product contacting materials to assure performance of critical components. Note that
testing incoming components will in many cases be impractical beyond confirming the package
and label, since SUS are typically not fully unpacked until the point of use.

       - Ensure proper warehousing and inventory management.

        - SU processes place additional demands on a warehouse. Consider shelf space and support
functions for a warehouse and ways of improving delivery time, maintaining integrity, and
documenting chain of custody from the vendor

**3.5.9.4 Single Suppliers**

In cases where an SUS component, film, or integrated assembly may be unique and proprietary, making it difficult to find and qualify a second supplier, the risk (including mitigation efforts and additional
equipment needed to ensure uninterrupted supply) must be recorded in a risk-assessment document.

In some cases, sole source situations may exist with intellectual property (IP) protections, which need
to be addressed with special contractual provisions in the event that the supplier is no longer able to
provide the SUS in the future.

**3.5.9.5 Change notifications**

The end user must make sure that a change notification agreement is in place with suppliers (including third-party suppliers) and that such an agreement provides enough time to assess the effects of any
changes on SUS functionally. Predictable, controlled transitions and ongoing operations of an SUS de

pend on strong communication between end users and suppliers. Identifying and qualifying alternative
suppliers may help control costs, inventory, and time needed to plan and implement changes.

**3.5.9.6 Technical Diligence**

Technical diligence is the process by which the SUS end user obtains a thorough understanding of the
SUS product, the supplier, and the compatibility of the SUS with the end user’s processes and facility.
These principles are based on established recommendations and procedures for critical raw materials.
****3.6 Implementation Summary****

Efforts required for implementation of a single-use process solution often differ from what is required for
a reusable system. Thus, the critical path for the fastest implementation of a process solution might also
differ. SUSs may allow for concurrent qualification and validation whereas MUSs require a more sequential approach. Successful implementation of an SUS hinges on a thorough comparative analysis.

Furthermore, the workflow performed in the implementation stages should be supported by best
practices in process validation and verification, risk management, and stakeholder management.

****4.3 SU Components and Assembly****

The primary components for the assembly of an SUS include fittings, tubing, filters, connectors, bags
(film), porting (bags), impellers, spargers, valves, sensors, filling needles, pump heads, docking port
containers, and outer pouches (dust covers). The individual components do not require environmentally controlled storage rooms; they are stored in sealed bags and must be handled with gloves once
opened to avoid contamination during assembly of the SUS. Several of the components can be assembled into a system and supplied presterilized, commonly via gamma irradiation. This is especially
convenient for a completed fluid contact pathway of tubing preattached to a bag with all the tubing
legs either heat-sealed or terminated with aseptic connectors. These systems are called bag assemblies, and they have tubing sets or tubing manifolds attached to them.

MUSs are assembled in classified and environmentally monitored facilities, and the equipment interior

the assembly room is required to maintain area classification and provide a consistent environment for
manufacturing SU solutions. SUS components are manufactured and assembled in controlled environments in order to meet acceptable standards for particle, bioburden, and endotoxin levels. Since there

****4.6 Facility Impact for SUS Setup and Deployment****

Although SUSs can be set up and deployed in existing manufacturing facilities, a new or renovated facility may be more effective if a high level of SUS integration is required. To manage the risk of damage to the SUS, assessment of facility design and layout should include SUS material management,
storage, and handling during operational setup. Including a defined setup and deployment stage, with
standardized activities for each part, is a recognized success factor for SUS implementation and should

****4.8.2 Mixing****

Mixing is the process used to achieve homogeneity of process liquids and solids. The process can
involve liquid to liquid, solids dispersion, powder dissolution, powder to powder, or other combinations. Some mixing processes may require temperature regulation. The choice of mixing technology
depends on the type of mixing required; the solubility, viscosity, or particle size of the constituents;
and the scale of the operation.

**4.8.2.1 Technological Examples**

The particular method of mixing can be used to classify SU mixers into five main categories:

**1. Rocker-style bag mixer —** A rocker bag system is composed of a pillow-style bag attached to the

top of a rocker platform. The bag is partially filled with liquid and inflated to create a pillow-shaped
structure. The liquid is agitated in a back-and-forth or orbital movement to create a waveform on
the liquid surface. The angle and frequency of rocking affects the size and speed of the resulting
wave. Mixing is controlled by the intensity of the rocking action and the length of time the fluid is
mixed. Addition of an electric blanket to the platform is used for temperature regulation.

**28** © 2014 Parenteral Drug Association, Inc. Technical Report No. 66

**2. Stirred-tank mixer —** A stirred-tank mixer is composed of a 3D bag inserted in an open-tank support

structure made from rigid plastic or metal (e.g., aluminum or stainless steel). In this configuration,
mixing is accomplished via an internal impeller driven by electromagnets. The purpose of the
support structure is to support the shape of the bag and to counter the hydrostatic pressure in the
bag. The bags are designed to be similar to the internal geometry of the support structure. An
electric blanket or water jacket is used for temperature regulation. Although cylindrical systems
are the most common, cube-shaped systems are available in various process sizes.

**3. Vibromixer —** A vibromixer is composed of a 3D bag with integrated polymeric disk impellers

containing multiple conical perforations and a shaft, which is inserted into an outer support
container or tank made of rigid plastic or stainless steel. The temperature regulation of a
vibromixer is similar to that of stirred-tank mixers.

**4. Recirculation loop —** A recirculation loop provides a simple method for mixing using, for

example, dip tubes and a peristaltic pump. These can be readily integrated into a bag.

**5. Unique configurations —** Novel mixing methods, including bellows, acoustic, and hybrid

systems, continue to emerge. They rely on specific bag geometry and other unique design aspects
for liquid movement (e.g., collapsible and expandable bellows sections designed into a container).

****4.8.3 Fermenters and Bioreactors****

The fermenter or bioreactor is the system that maintains appropriate growth conditions to support
the drug-product-producing strain.

**4.8.3.1 Technological Examples**

The following are the three main design categories of SU fermenters or bioreactors:

**• Rocker-style bag bioreactor —** A rocker-style bag bioreactor is a rocker mixer that can be used to
monitor and control cell growth–related process parameters like pH and dissolved oxygen and also
allows for product sampling. The rocking action is used to control mixing and dissolved oxygen.
Headspace gas, sparge tubes, or a combination can also be used to regulate dissolved oxygen.
Temperature regulation is achieved via heat transfer to the surface of the rocker.

**• Stirred-tank bioreactor —** An SU stirred-tank bioreactor comprises a tube-shaped 3D bag inserted
into an open-tank support structure made from plastic or stainless steel. The support structure
retains the shape of the bag, counters hydrostatic pressure, and restrains the bag from moving
around. Temperature regulation of the bioreactor is via an electric blanket or water jacket. A
jacketed temperature regulation system allows for cooling of the culture during growth or prior
to harvest and clarification. An agitator can be mounted either above or below the bioreactor. The
impeller, shaft, and shaft cover (if used) and seal (if used) are integrated into the bag.

**• Other SU fermenters and bioreactors —** Hollow fiber systems remain in use for cell culture applications
that are operated in perfusion mode. In addition, scaled-up versions of rigid, plastic cell-culture ware, novel
bioreactor designs with unique mixing techniques (e.g., vibromixers, orbital shakers, paddle, and airlift)
are being produced along with hybrid systems. These emerging systems often rely on bag geometry and
other unique design aspects to achieve mass transfer. Novel SU fermenters and bioreactors are becoming
commercially available. Technological advances in the method of agitation seem to be driving innovations
in the new generation of SU fermenters and bioreactors. SU fermenters require unique strategies for heat
and mass transfer in order to control temperature and the necessary oxygen transfer rates.

****4.10 Freezing****

The end user may use freezing techniques to separate process pools or intermediates and hold them
for a short time at the process temperature and conditions or for a longer duration before another
production step. For long durations, the end user may freeze the intermediate directly in an SUS that
is designed for freezing. Uncontrolled freezing is performed in standard laboratory freezers, purposedesigned blast cells, or nitrogen freezing chambers. Controlled freeze-and-thaw equipment (e.g., a
plate freezer) is used to regulate the rate of freezing and thawing of the contents of each SU bag. To
maintain the integrity of the bag containing the frozen process intermediates or bulk drug substances,
careful attention must be paid to the reusable storage container design and logistics of shipping and
handling.
****4.10.1 Technological Examples****

**• Unprotected small-scale bag** : It is common practice to freeze an unprotected bag (less than 20 L)
that is not specifically designed for the application. Compared to other technologies, the failure
rates of frozen unprotected bags tend to be higher **(Table 4.10.1-1).**

**• Separate bag shell —** The purpose of a bag shell is to maintain the structural integrity of a frozen
bag by protecting it from bumps and drops during handling at low temperatures. Bag shells are
made of either rigid plastics or rigid foam construction.

**• Integrated shell-protected bag —** The shell design includes a tubing and fitting compartment for
security and storage at the most common point of failure. Off-the-shelf International Safe Transit
Association (ISTA) and ASTM-qualified shipping containers can also be used _**(10).**_

**• Controlled freeze-thaw system, bag, frame, and logistics equipment —** A controlled freeze-thaw
system uses contact freezing technology to allow for the reproducible freezing of the contents of
the bag. Contact freezing plates maintain freezing distances by maintaining the filled bag between
two freezing contact plates. In addition, the rate of heat transfer from the bags is increased in a
controlled freeze-thaw system, and this decreases the time required for freezing. Thus, a controlled
freeze-thaw system is part of an integrated refrigeration system for maintaining the integrity of the
bag and the quality of its contents during fill, transport, freezing, and thawing.

****4.12 Centrifugation****

****4.14 Drug Product Final Filling****

The impact of any failures in final fill operations is high, since further processing cannot reduce quality failures at this stage. Common strategies for reducing the risk of failure include performing filling
operations in aseptic environments, use of physical barrier systems to separate operators from the
process, and validating the sterility of process components (refer to **Section 4.15,** Isolators).

SU components are widely used in product final filling operations. For example, flexible plastic tubing is used between the filling pump head and needle. Another example is a peristaltic filling system,
which employs disposable peristaltic tubing inside the pump head. Both types of tubing are normally
replaced between batches.
****4.14.1 Technological Examples****

An SU dosing system comprises a product hold bag, supply tubing, and a filling needle. These components are assembled in a cleanroom and sterilized using gamma irradiation. The complete assembly
is removed from its outer pouch and connected to the filling machine immediately before use. The
filling machine may be as simple as a single pump or may contain multiple pumps to achieve commercial production speeds.

SU filling systems are controlled volumetrically or gravimetrically.

**• Volumetric dosing system —** This type of system involves a volumetric system that consists of a
peristaltic pump and specialized tubing, within which flows the product to be dispensed. Peristaltic
pumps work by mechanically compressing the tubing within the pump head to push a specified
volume of fluid through the dosing path. Accurate dosing depends on factors such as the mechanical
life of the pump tubing. A positive displacement pump-based system relies on the use of either a
rotary-piston or rolling-diaphragm pump design. In both cases, fluid is forced out of the pump by
the action of the piston. Accurate dosing depends on the precision of the piston stroke length.

**• Gravimetric dosing system —** A gravimetric system is normally used in low-throughput applications.
It uses an optical sensor to dose a specified weight of the product based on the calculation of the interior
volume of a given length of tubing or glass. The entire product path comprises SU components. The
accuracy and precision of the system with aqueous solutions is comparable to that of other dosing
systems. The dosing speed and accuracy are directly related to the fluid temperature and viscosity.
Dosing time is the speed at which a liquid will flow through the tubing based on gravity; thus, viscosity
influences dosing time. Relatively small temperature changes over the course of a filling event can affect

****4.20 Supply Chain Integration****

The final SUS may involve a large number of supply-chain integration pathways that are currently
in use. A company with an SU manufacturing strategy should have highly trained procurement and
quality staff with a good understanding of how to manage the several supply chains related to the
SUS products that will be used to produce their drug products.
****4.20.1 Factors Which Affect the Quality of Supply Chain****

The quality of the supply chain has a profound effect on the quality of the SUS. A SUS supplier will
typically have a supply chain encompassing aspects of insourcing (i.e., the manufacture or assembly of
a part in-house) and outsourcing (i.e., the procurement of a part from a subsupplier). Insourcing and
outsourcing both have advantages and disadvantages. A high-quality supply chain ensures that the quality of raw materials and final products is consistent; that the supplier can maintain business viability
and continuity; and that the supplier has access to technical experts for product design and support.

Having ready access to technical design and manufacturing experts ensures that both the design and
the ongoing manufacture of parts will be executed correctly (refer to Section 6, Business Drivers for
the Adoption of Single-Use Systems). If the end user chooses an insourcing model, technical experts
would exist in-house, but they may be too few in number or may be sequestered due to business prioritization. With an outsourcing model, a well-chosen business partner would have core competency
and experts, but the partner might be less accessible and require confidential disclosure agreements to
be executed before substantive discussions can begin. What concerns the end user is the availability
of technical experts to assist in troubleshooting any problems with the SUS.

****5.1 Introduction****

The qualification and verification of suppliers, materials, components, and completed assemblies require attention to several factors. It is important to use a risk-based and science-based approach to qualify both materials and completed assemblies for SUSs in pharmaceutical development and manufacture
_**(18).**_ The assessment of risk must be based on the complexity of the system and its intended use (e.g.,
product contact versus non-product contact, upstream versus downstream use, short-term contact
versus long-term storage). See PDA’s Technical Report No. 54 on Quality Risk Management for more
information _**(7).**_ In addition, scientific principles must be applied to 1) identify and monitor extractable
materials from films and other components; 2) investigate potential interactions with product CQAs
or significant process parameters like cell culture media; and 3) assess the effects of assembly processes
such as welding, fusing, mechanical stress, and sterilization (e.g., gamma irradiation) on the materials
during manufacturing. In assessing the suitability of the SUS with respect to potential leachables in the
final product, extractables may be considered as potential in-process leachables. Finally, the design and
functional integrity of the completed SUS must be verified and maintained by appropriately trained
end users via suitable processes for construction, packaging, shipping, and deployment.

The supplier of the SUS must provide sufficient data to qualify the materials and components used to
assemble the final SUS. In this regard, the end user may use the documents from the supplier to meet
most of the verification criteria required to make sure that the SUS is fit for the intended application
and purpose. This requires a thorough qualification of the supplier by the end user to ascertain an acceptable supplier quality system, documentation, and an appropriate level of technical capability that
extends all the way to the original sources of materials to the supplier. In addition, the suppliers must
be able to provide documented evidence of performance claims for off-the-shelf component designs
for the SUS. A supplier quality agreement must underpin any custom designs that the end user may
need from the supplier.

The first step in a science- and risk-based approach is to identify and assess the risks associated with
using an SUS. The end user must identify that the SUS supplier’s approach to the design and selection
of materials of construction includes components and materials such as:

         - Filters

must fully qualify and audit a number of potential suppliers before embarking on extensive studies of

****5.2 Risks Associated with Using Single-Use Systems****

risk to the process may occur as a result of process failure, but it rarely affects patients because of
process discontinuation. Normal safety precautions for handling large volumes of liquids, which may
also be hazardous, must be maintained. The main focus of this discussion is the risk to the patient.

****5.3 Single-Use System Assembly****

SUSs include fittings, tubing, filters, connectors, bag material (film), and outer packaging. SU components are normally received and stored in areas with minimal environmental control. It is critical for
the components to remain in their sealed packaging, and they should be handled with gloves to avoid
contamination. Transport into the assembly room must follow standard clean-room practices. All the
external packaging of the SU components should be wiped with a suitable antimicrobial or cleansing
agent before moving them into the assembly room. The assembly rooms are typically ISO 7 or 8 with
a combination of particle and microbial sampling. It is important to design manufacturing suites with
HEPA-filtered air, low returns, smooth surfaces, and airlocks for entry and exit. Avoid introducing any
sharp edges or corners into the assembly room because they may perforate the SUS bags. Personnel
must be trained and wear appropriate clothing for an ISO 7 operation and the assembly room must
be cleaned regularly. Where SUSs are preassembled, the supplier takes these steps.

****5.4 Supplier Qualification of Single-use Systems****

Since a typical SUS installation requires the acquisition of several components from various external
suppliers, it is important for the end user to qualify suppliers to ensure that they can deliver SU components that meet or exceed regulatory and customer expectations.

****5.4.1 Supplier Audits and Technical Diligence****

It is important to audit the supplier’s quality systems as part of the supplier qualification process to
ensure delivery of consistent SU components to the end user. An understanding of the supplier’s capabilities may lead to a better understanding of end-user expectations and can help identify any gaps
in the capabilities of the supplier. Therefore, the auditing step must encompass manufacturing and
technical aspects, and both parties must agree on corrective actions as appropriate. Microbiological
control measures for SUSs must be evaluated as part of a supplier audit.

The auditor should review the procedure and records for quarterly dose audits where the SUS is subjected to gamma-irradiation sterilization.

Standard auditing practices, extending to a review of quality systems and complaint handling, should
be followed. Unique to SUSs for inclusion in audits would be the supplier’s definition of change and
how the change-control process functions, including customer notification. The specific particulate
control and reduction schemes in place at the supplier should also be evaluated.

Many SUS suppliers may either be ISO 9001 or medical device manufacturers under FDA and ISO
registration. ISO 9001 registration means a company operates in a quality management framework
that has been audited by a registrar and confirmed as adhering to ISO 9001 guidelines _**(20).**_ The manufacturing facilities of registered medical devices are subject to inspection and follow 21 CFR Part 820
(cGMPs for Medical Devices) and ISO 13485 _**(21,22).**_ SUSs, including bulk drug containers, are considered process equipment rather than medical devices; therefore, the manufacturing facilities of SUS
suppliers are not subject to regulatory registration or inspection. Although SUS manufacturers tend
to design their quality systems along GMP guidelines, the auditor should not expect these systems to
adhere strictly to cGMPs for drug or medical device manufacturing _**(21,23).**_ ISO 9001 is considered a
minimum requirement.

Pertinent factors for a technical diligence assessment and an interpretation of its implementation over
the clinical development lifecycle are reviewed in greater detail in **Section 7.7.3.**

****5.4.2 Supplier Quality Agreements and Responsibilities****

The purpose of a supplier quality agreement is to set quality expectations, identify and develop a
mutual understanding of pertinent regulations and guidance documents, and provide advice on
managing quality concerns such as audits, complaints, and change control. Change control can be
a complex process for an SUS because of the diverse number of suppliers (e.g., compounders, film
producers, and bag assemblers) and materials (e.g., resins and additives) involved in the supply chain.
The supplier must provide full transparency on the full supply chain of the SUS and guarantee quality oversight of the supply chain. The supplier must have a robust supply chain, which may require
improvements, which can be developed with the support of the customer in a partnership mind-set.
The extent to which the primary supplier manages the overall supply chain should be assessed, and
if necessary, audit and technical diligence activities should be extended to the original supplier of
materials and components. It is important to establish a cordial working relationship between the end
user and supplier because an SUS supplier will inevitably become an extended part of the end user’s
manufacturing and quality systems.
****5.5 Qualification of Alternative Suppliers****

The use of SUS permits considerable flexibility in the choice of materials, designs, and suppliers. This
flexibility can be used to minimize risk by ensuring that multiple configurations are viable options for
any given requirement. There are multiple combinations of films, bag designs, tubing, filters, connectors, and so on, that are capable of delivering or receiving a specified volume of process fluids to the
required standard. Therefore, it is advisable for the end user to qualify an alternative supplier as part
of the SUS risk management system _**(7).**_ Risk- and science-based assessment must be used to determine the level of testing and validation required. In a case involving minimal risk, it may be sufficient
to treat an SUS from a secondary supplier as representative of the same class of system or material
from the primary supplier. The data generated for the original SUS can be used and compared to
that from the secondary supplier. With higher-risk cases (e.g., bioreactor bags or product storage),
the secondary supplier’s component must be subjected to the same comprehensive qualification process as that of the primary supplier. Note that different suppliers may adopt different approaches to
tackle a particular issue and still remain within the bounds of an acceptable supplier quality system.
For instance, supplier A may test systems for bioburden from every lot whereas supplier B may test
representative samples from different lots as proof of an overall bioburden management system.
Both approaches may be acceptable and should be reflected in the user specifications **(Table 5.5.2-1).**
An industry effort to standardize SUS testing methods would improve end users’ ability to compare
alternative SUS suppliers and adequately assess testing differences between suppliers.
****5.5.1 Qualification of Alternative Sources****

End users typically have two options when it comes to alternative sources: 1) the same SUS supplier
with alternative sources of raw materials, or 2) an alternative SUS supplier. Regardless of the choice,
the qualification of an alternative source should be part of the drug substance manufacturer’s riskmanagement system. The user should qualify the alternative supplier or raw material source in the
same thorough way as the primary supplier or source.

With each option, the risk- and science-based assessment will continue to determine the level of testing and validation required.

The issues under consideration differ somewhat for each option. Suppliers may qualify alternate rawmaterial sources as part of their risk mitigation strategy at the level of the resins, additives, or the
film itself (if sourced separately). For applications with low potential for process or product impact,

****5.6 Extractables and Leachables (E&L)****

_Extractables_ may be defined as chemical entities that can be extracted from the components of a material by
exertion of an exaggerated force (e.g., organic solvent, extreme temperature, ionic strength, pH, contact time).
_Leachables_ may be defined as chemical entities that can migrate from product-contact or non-product-contact
surfaces into a process stream. Leachables may also include chemical entities that can migrate from contact
surfaces into the bulk drug substance or final product under specified conditions of production, storage, and
use. While leachables are considered a subset of extractables, they can also be derived from product-contact or
non-product-contact materials, under typical process conditions and detected in final dosage. The evaluation
of extractables is an essential step in the accurate prediction of leachables and in the selection of a suitable SUS
dedicated to the manufacture of a given product. When chemical substances are identified that could be cleared
or sufficiently diluted by downstream processes so as to be undetected, these can be termed as in-process leachables for the final dosage and may need to be accounted for during risk assessment. Refer to **Table 4.8-1** for a
summary of common materials of construction and comments regarding their respective E&L properties.

Drug manufacturers are encouraged to perform a risk-assessment analysis as part of an E&L evaluation
within the overall context of enhancing process performance and product safety and efficacy. The accumulation of extractables and leachables in a process fluid is governed by thermodynamic (i.e., the extent to
which the materials would migrate) and kinetic (i.e., the rate at which materials would migrate) factors, as
well as the amount of time during which such migration would occur. Higher temperatures increase the
migration rate of leachables from the bulk of the plastic to the surface in contact with the process stream
or formulation. The user can determine the worst-case extent of the extractables by determining the highest temperature at which extractions cease and the entire additive has been removed from the plastic. E&L
risk factors can be generally categorized as risk to the patient and risk to the process. In evaluating risks, an
extractable or leachable and the level at which it appears will need to be evaluated for impact on process and
product quality and for patient safety. The following risk factors may be considered:

- Proximity of the process steps undergoing a change to the final product

- Storage and processing conditions (e.g., duration of exposure, temperature, pressure, pH extremes,
buffer extraction propensity)

- The type of process fluid (e.g., purification buffer versus formulated drug substance, presence of
solubilizing agents)

- Construction materials (e.g., PVC containers, bags, and tubing are prone to leaching diethylhexyl
phthalate, which has adverse effects on the liver, testis, mammary, nerve, immune system, blood,
and fatty tissue) _**(26,27).**_

- Potential adverse effects, including synergistic and additive affects

- Drug dose, mode, and frequency of administration

- Therapeutic necessity of the drug (included in drug dose, mode, and frequency)

The presence of leachables during any stage of the production process or storage may pose a safety
risk due to their potential to cause toxicity, carcinogenicity, immunogenicity, and/or endocrine dysregulation _**(28,29).**_ In addition, these substances may adversely impact the physicochemical characteristics (e.g., via aggregation, oxidation, degradation, and formation of particulates) of the final protein
product. It has been demonstrated that leachables have adverse effects on cell growth and viability
_**(30).**_ Lastly, the potential for leachables to impact quality control tests should be considered. For these
reasons, proper selection and handling of an SUS that supports the production and processing of safe
and efficacious therapeutic biologic products is of paramount importance.

Potential sources of extractables and leachables include SU materials used in manufacturing, packaging, storage, filtration, and transfer systems. These include but are not limited to SU bioreactors, bags for intermediate
and long-term storage, containers, filters, transfer tubing, valves, fittings, housings, and so on. Extractables and
leachables are chemically diverse compounds that may include but are not limited to phthalates (e.g., plasticizers), organic acids, hydrocarbons, cyclic esters derived from urethane adhesives, antioxidants, residual solvents,
antistatic and cleaning agents, as well as trace metals and degradation products from irradiation.
****5.6.1 Material and Supplier Qualification****

It is important for end users to qualify alternative sources of films to safeguard against supply disruptions and possible shortages. There will be situations where this may be a minimal risk (e.g., bags used
for sampling), so a comparison of extractables data from different suppliers may be acceptable; other
situations may be more demanding (e.g., storage of bulk drug substance). In every case, the approach
taken should be supported by a sound risk- and science-based assessment.

Detection, characterization, identification, and quantitation of leachables may present analytical challenges, since these substances represent diverse chemical classes of organic and/or inorganic compounds
that coexist in complex mixtures in trace amounts _**(31).**_ Therefore, well-designed extractables studies are
important to provide an analytical road map and to identify early warning signs regarding leachables released during upstream or downstream operations or in storage. Emerging analytical technologies with
appropriate sensitivity and specificity may be considered in addition to the currently available methods
during the design of such studies. If an extractable may have an adverse effect on the physicochemical or
biological properties of a protein, performing characterization studies in which the extractable is spiked
into the product may be of value in assessing the potential risk to product quality.

In order to isolate chemicals from plastic components, the study of extractables begins with the selection of a suitable exaggerated condition that is not stipulated for the normal manufacture, storage, or
use of the SUS. It is recommended that the extraction studies be performed with material components
using the exaggerated conditions with regards to pH, ionic strength, contact time, temperature, surface
area–to–volume ratio, and, if applicable, organic solvents of varying polarity and surfactant properties
as the extraction media. If the material component in question is not compatible with organic solvents,
aqueous media may be used. If a prerinse step is used in routine manufacturing and use of material
component, it should be included in the design of the leachables studies but not in the extractables
studies because the latter are intended to represent a worst-case situation. Additional pretreatment of
plastics such as gamma irradiation and steam treatment can also impact the E&L profile.

A representative sample of process fluid (e.g., cell culture media and buffer) can be used as the extraction media in addition to others listed above for accurate prediction of extractables. If a representative
process fluid cannot be obtained (e.g., due to interference with the analytical method), a similar solution to the original may be an acceptable alternative. The extraction study may be performed using a
soaking or a recycling method for a specific contact time, under accelerated temperature conditions
and preselected surface area–to–volume ratio. USP chapters <661> and <381> contain recommenda

**48** © 2014 Parenteral Drug Association, Inc. Technical Report No. 66

tions for extraction conditions with regards to surface area–to–volume ratio, temperature, and time of
extraction _**(32,33).**_ The USP <661> monograph also includes general analytical testing for the content
of nonvolatile residue, residue on ignition, buffering capacity, and heavy metals. However, because
such methods are not equipped to identify specific contaminants, their scope and applicability may not
be sufficient in every instance. Several industry organizations, such as BioPhorum Operators Group
(BPOG) and the Bio-Process Systems Alliance (BPSA), have developed standardized testing approaches. Please see the latest protocols at their websites as they are periodically updated _**(33-36).**_

Testing of E&L substances includes the analysis of nonvolatile, semivolatile, and highly volatile organic
compounds as well as analysis of trace light and heavy metals. Less specific tests such as pH, conductivity,
chemical identity via Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, and analysis of total organic carbon
(TOC) may be performed. TOC analysis cannot be used with extract solvents that contain carbon. Highly selective analytical techniques for the detection, characterization, and quantitation of these chemical
entities include high-performance liquid chromatography coupled with mass spectroscopy (HPLC-MS),
gas chromatography coupled with mass spectroscopy (GC-MS), inductively coupled plasma with mass
spectroscopy (ICP-MS), proton nuclear magnetic resonance (1H-NMR), and atomic spectroscopy (e.g.,
atomic absorption and atomic emission spectroscopy). For instance, nonvolatile compounds may be analyzed with HPLC-MS, whereas highly volatile and semivolatile organic compounds may be resolved with
GC-MS. Other methods such as ICP-MS may be used for the detection of residual metals.

The extractables study must be complemented with an appropriately designed leachables study. In
addition to monitoring the substances that are leaching under the recommended conditions of use
and storage, leachable studies must be designed to identify any interactions and the resulting effect
of such interactions on the in-process material and/or on the product. In many cases, drug substance
and drug product stability studies may be used to support conclusions concerning the effects of these
substances on product quality over time. This is particularly important where there is no downstream
purification process to eliminate impurities (e.g., drug product formulation buffer storage container).

In addition to determining the chemical identity, quantity, and composition of E&L, and the impact
on process fluid or product quality, E&L must be assessed for cytotoxicity and toxicity in animals
in order to preserve patient safety and clinical efficacy _**(24,25).**_ This information may be obtained
from publicly available literature sources such as ICH Q3C (R3) or Product Quality Research Institute
Leachables and Extractables Working Group, which stipulates the safety threshold levels for such impurities _**(37,38).**_ In the absence of specific toxicity data, the threshold of toxicological concern (TTC)
approach may be applied in order to judge the risk associated with a particular leachable.

When an SUS is used to grow cell culture, the end user must determine the effect of cell growth on
both individual components and the complete SUS. This is important because a recent study showed
that the breakdown products of gamma-irradiated antioxidants can have adverse effects on the viability of the cell culture. Spiking studies showed that the phenomenon can occur at levels well below
parts per million in a cell line-specific manner _**(39).**_

Cell-based assays can be used to identify the particular combinations of cell lines and plastic extractables
that may reduce cell culture viability and growth profiles. End users must evaluate the compatibility of
the supplied SUS with their specific application, because certain applications (e.g., media storage, bioreactor bags) have been reported to be sensitive to certain combinations of materials and cell lines _**(30).**_

The mechanism of interaction of the SUS and contact solution can include leaching from the plastic into the
contact solution or the adsorption of the contained solution or its constituents onto the SUS. These interactions can be modeled through science-based assessments of the physicochemical interactions of the solution
and SUS. The use of model solvents for the extractables and leachables is an industry standard that limits the

cost and time required for their assessment _**(40).**_ It is important for the model solvent to be representative of
the contact solutions in the end user’s process or formulation. Common model solvents include:

- WFI aqueous extraction (neutral pH)

- Aqueous extraction (pH <2)

- Aqueous extraction (pH >9)

- Solvent extraction—ethanol/water mixtures

- Phosphate-buffered saline

- Surfactant systems—anion (neutral pH)

The key aspect of the model solvent selection is to closely replicate the contact solution and minimize
interference with qualified analytical methods for detecting the extractable or leachable.

****5.6.2 Toxicity of E&L****

The toxicity of leachables must be evaluated with regards to the effect on the patient or process.
Containers and storage bags are a potential source of leachables that may have adverse effects on the
patient. Although some chemicals may be considered unacceptable at almost any concentration in
pharmaceutical products (e.g., ICH Q3C class 1 solvents), determination of the toxicity of extractables or leachables must be viewed in the broader context of the following criteria _**(37):**_

- Actual concentration of leachables in the final
drug product

- Dose size and regimen

- Mode of delivery

- Patient population

- Risk-benefit assessment

Therefore, toxicity cannot be derived from extractable identification and concentration alone, or from
the amount of leachables in process fluids or drug product intermediates.

The data for the identification and quantitation of extractables is used to determine the concentration of potential leachables in the final drug product after considering further processing—including downstream purification, diafiltration, dilution, and formulation. If an identified leachable in the
final drug product is not covered by existing guidelines and there is limited or no toxicity data, the
threshold of toxicological concern (TTC) may be applied _**(41).**_ The purpose of the TTC is to define
a common acceptable exposure level for unstudied chemicals. Therefore, it is applicable to the risk
assessment of leachables derived from SUSs. The concept of TTC may also be applied to extractables
that may be potential leachables in the final drug product.

Leachables may affect the function of various components of the SUS. For instance, it has been reported that residual silicone from silicone tubing significantly suppressed the post-use bubble points
of sterilizing-grade filters, resulting in false integrity test failures _**(42).**_ Therefore, it is important to
make sure that SUS test samples removed for sampling do not contain leachables that may interfere
with test measurements or present unknown peaks in routine assays.

Leachables may have an adverse effect on the viability of cell cultures without posing a risk to the
patient. Recent presentations from different users have reported that certain cell lines may be more
sensitive to some materials _**(39,43-46).**_

In one case, cell growth media that was warmed overnight in disposable containers produced lower
than expected growth in glass bioreactors. In another case, the effect of prefilters on growth performance was studied, and it was noted that extensive flushing was required for optimal performance.
Differences were observed in the surfaces and texture of films, and certain cell lines showed different
sensitivities to different films _**(39).**_

****5.6.3 Using Supplier Documentation for Extractables****

The end user may rely on extractables data from the supplier as a starting point for general information on E&L components. This kind of data is typically generated using model extraction solvents
under exaggerated conditions. Given the specificity of each product and diversity of extractables and
leachables, as well as production and final use conditions, it would be prudent for the end user to
perform an independent assessment to ascertain that the data are representative of the actual SUS
process. Supplier documentation may be sufficient for certain low-risk applications (e.g., short-term
exposure, no contact with the product, or position in the process stream); but more detailed studies
may be required for high-risk applications (e.g., where long-term contact and extreme conditions exist). This is critical for qualifying alternative sources of materials.

The end user must review the underlying methods in order to conduct an effective comparison of
supplier extractables data for components constructed of like materials. In addition, it is critical for
the end user to use the same components and processes for an extractables study as that used in the
original supplier’s manufacturing process. If the potential for adverse results is high (e.g., a high severity score in a risk assessment), data from extended storage of product and a leachables study may
need to be generated and supported by actual functional testing to ensure that there would be no
adverse effect on either the product or the process (e.g., stability test of the final product container,
cell growth, and the effect of the bioreactor bag on cell viability).
****5.6.4 Extractable Testing Standardization****

To assist in defining common extractable study requirements for biopharma drug substance requirements,
including test conditions, analytical techniques, and format of the results provided to users, some level of
standardization is needed in the industry. Such standardization would facilitate SUS implementation _**(36).**_

****5.7 Bovine Spongiform Encephalopathy (BSE)/Transmissible** **Spongiform Encephalopathy (TSE) Concerns****

SUSs have low-risk BSE/TSE concerns and they do not contain high-risk animal-derived components.
Certain mold release and slip agents and plastics resins (e.g., polypropylene or polycarbonate) may
contain trace levels of additives (e.g., calcium stearate), which are manufactured from bovine tallow
and have low BSE/TSE risk. Tallow is a lubricant sometimes used in pelletizing operations for bulk
resin or as a mold release agent for the manufacture of SU components. Tallow is produced by treating beef fat with steam to create a mixture of fats. The fat (e.g., glyceryl tristearate) is then boiled in a
sodium hydroxide solution, resulting in a mixture of sodium stearate and glycerin.

Tallow-derived additives are not considered as specified BSE risk materials according to the current
revision of the U.S. Code of Federal Regulations, Title 21 Part 189.5 _**(47).**_ Furthermore, the CPMP’s
“Note for Minimizing the Risk of Transmitting Animal Spongiform Encephalopathies via Human
and Veterinary Medicinal Products” (EMA 410/01 rev 3 ), gives specific consideration to tallow derivatives _**(48).**_ It states that they are unlikely to be infectious due to the rigorous processing steps (e.g.,
transesterification, or hydrolysis, at not less than 200°C under pressure for not less than twenty minutes) used during their manufacture. A BSE/TSE risk assessment should be completed and periodically updated to reflect whether an SUS supplier is using components in the fluid pathway that were
fabricated from plastic resins containing bovine-derived additives at trace levels.

****5.8 The Quality Systems of the End User and Supplier****

It is important for the end user to have instituted a robust quality system to receive materials, quarantine unreleased and rejected materials, inspect those materials, and release them to production. Since
the testing and release of an SUS can vary widely from end user to end user, a complete and careful
documentation in the form of a purchase specification and inspection plan should accompany any
off-the-shelf or customized SUS requisition. A supplier quality agreement is another useful way to
manage the requirements and expectations of both supplier and end user.

****5.9 Quality Management for Single-Use System Implementation****
****5.9.1 Determination of Expiration Date and Shelf Life****

The SUS supplier is expected to provide estimates for expiration dates and the shelf life of the SUS
according to validation of maintenance of sterility tests and key functional properties. The expiration

date is normally set post-sterilization. Any extension of the shelf life of the SUS must be supported
by pertinent data. Real-time aging studies provide the best data to ensure that both the materials and
the functional integrity of a sterile barrier system do not degrade with time. Accelerated studies may
be a suitable substitute if real-time aging studies cannot be performed. The ASTM F1980–07(2007)
Standard Guide for Accelerated Aging of Sterile Barrier Systems for Medical Devices is a widely used
test for accelerated aging of the plastic components of SUSs _**(49).**_

The term Ready-To-Use is a marketing term often used to describe the benefits of single-use technology or SUS. This designation has no regulatory or scientific basis supporting suitability for use, and
end users are responsible for evaluating and determining whether appropriate quality requirements
have been met for their application.
****5.9.2 Dealing with Particulates****

The level of particulates found in the SUS depends on the application (fluid path contents) of the
system. Particles embedded in the bulk plastic as well as the presence of loose particles or fibers in an
SUS are undesirable and their limits should be clearly specified in purchase agreements. If the presence of particulates in the fluid path would not have adverse effects on the product or process, then
components used upstream of final filters may not require stipulated particulate levels. However, SUS
components employed downstream of final filters or without particulate filtration may require stringent limits for particulates to ensure that the final product meets pharmacopeia standards. An acceptable solution is to qualify fluid path rinse volumes to meet USP <788> and USP <790> or equivalent
pharmacopeia standards for large-volume injectables _**(10,50).**_ In addition, the frequency of sampling
and analysis may be extended from lot samples to periodic audits of model SUSs—this would be akin
to the bioburden audits that are conducted for the validation of sterilization by irradiation. Applicable
SUS model system design and particulate audit practices should be established between the supplier
and the end user.
****5.9.3 Sanitation and Sterilization****

The use of an SUS in biopharmaceutical manufacturing can vary so widely that in one instance there
may be no need for a specific bioburden control (e.g., as in the mixing of nonsterile buffer formulations or other raw materials) whereas in another, stringent restrictions on the incidence of a bioburden are called for (e.g., validated sterility for final formulation and filling of sterile drug product). It
is critical for the end user to review processes, fluids, contact, and storage conditions for microbial
quality to determine the appropriate measures to be taken.
****5.9.4 Bioburden Control****

The molding and extrusion of SUS components at high temperatures plus their assembly into a functional SUS in a clean and controlled environment confers advantages such as intrinsically low incidence of bioburden on the fluid paths of the SUS. Although this inherently low degree of bioburden
may be adequate for nonsterile processes, many SU components or systems may be autoclaved or
subjected to gamma irradiation for additional control of bioburden. Doses of irradiation as low as
8 to 10 kGy are enough to reduce intrinsic bioburden to zero; however, in practice, doses >25 kGy
are used to achieve a sterility assurance level (SAL) of 10 [-6] . The increased irradiation dosage has the
advantage of attaining a bioburden level comparable to that of validated sterility without conducting
the necessary validation protocols or periodic sterilization audits that would otherwise be required
for a statistical claim of sterility of 10 [-6] _**(51).**_ This may be suitable for bacterial fermentation applications and aseptic processes such as the preparation and storage of downstream chromatography and
ultrafiltration buffers and intermediates.

****5.10 Sterilization****
****5.10.1 Irradiation Sterilization****

SUSs are especially suitable to sterilization by irradiation because of the relative transparency of plastics to ionizing radiation, which allows complex systems to be sterilized without the risk of spreading
the sterilizing agent throughout the fluid path. Irradiation may be used to sterilize plastics or SUS
assemblies that may be unsuitable for steam sterilization. The practice of sterilizing via irradiation is
well established in the medical device industry with transferable standards that can be applied to SU
process equipment _**(51).**_ Although no specific industry standard has been developed for the sterilization of SUSs, the industry and regulatory communities have adopted the ANSI/AAMI/ISO 11137
standard, _Sterilization of Health Care Products—Radiation_, and the closely related AAMI Technical Information Report TIR 33:2005, _Substantiation of a Selected Sterilization Dose—Method VD max_ _**(51,52).**_
Adaptation and implementation of these standards to the implementation of SUS have been discussed in the literature _**(53).**_ The most common alternative methods mentioned in the standards are
the dose substantiation methods (i.e., the verification dose max, or “VD max,” methods).

In practice, a typical audit involves the selection of a single-use product (SUP) as representative of the
SUS. A supplier quality audit must consider how the product family and the representative product design
were derived. In cases where testing the representative product would be impractical (e.g., due to size and
complexity), a portion of the sample item may be used with mathematical scaling between the actual
product and the sample item. A supplier quality audit must include an understanding of the mathematical
scaling technique. Since the standards require that a supplier must perform quarterly dose audits for each
product family, the end-user quality audit of the SU supplier must ascertain that there is a procedure in
place for the supplier to conduct quarterly dose auditing. Validation normally involves the determination
of an average bioburden level in a representative master system, assessment of radiation resistance by
sterility testing of the fluid path of the master system after a low “verification” dose (about 8 to 10 kGy),
and calculation of a suitable dose to provide a sterility assurance level or a probability of nonsterility of
10 [-6] for the model product and its variants. The supplier and the end user should review the applicability
of validation via master system sterilization to the end user’s SUS configuration.
****5.10.2 Sterilization with Moist Heat****

Several methods of sterilization are acceptable for use with SUS. Each is appropriate for different applications and should be evaluated for suitability.

**• Autoclaving —** This technique is often used to sterilize an SUS or SU components assembled by
end users _**(54).**_ After that, most end users may find it more convenient to source preassembled SUSs
that are sterilized by gamma irradiation.

**• Sterilize-in-place (SIP) —** Steam-to-connector configuration is one example of SIP. The physical
limitations of plastics limit the ability of an SUS to be sterilized by steam pressure in situ. Some
SU connectors are designed to be physically attached to a stainless multiple-use port so that the
tank connection can be sterilized with steam. Steam-to or steam-through connectors tend to be
preconnected to tubing with an SUS at one end and are presterilized by autoclaving or irradiation.
A valve or barrier mechanism is used to isolate the sterile system. The steam-to or steam-through
connector is then connected to a tank, and the interface is sterilized with steam _**(55).**_

**• Gas sterilization —** Although it is not a common practice, an SUS may be sterilized using a gas
such as ethylene oxide. Gas sterilization is not as popular as other methods of sterilization for two
reasons: 1) ethylene chlorohydrin and ethylene glycol reaction by-products can form; and 2) some
ethylene oxide may be retained in the filter matrix, when a filter is present. Some of the factors that

affect the use of gas sterilization are preconditioning, concentration, relative humidity, temperature,
and time. The monitoring and validation of gas sterilization is conducted by destroying a suitable
biological indicator such as the spores of _Bacillus subtilis_ . To achieve successful sterilization using
ethylene oxide, the SUS should be dry and wrapped in a way that allows for the influx of water
vapor and gas into the fluid path. The appropriate concentration of gas and other parameters
must be established during development and validation processes. It is important to rid the SUS of
residual gas and other undesirable by-products after gas sterilization.

**• Resterilization —** SU components are not amenable to multiple autoclave cycles. Gamma
irradiation is a cumulative process and most SU components cannot be re-irradiated without
causing significant degradation of physical integrity, creation of extractables, and reduction of shelf
life. In addition, irradiation may reduce the resistance of the SUS to subsequent steam exposure,
which may increase the formation of extractables and leachables. In general, an SUS is not designed
to be resterilized.
****5.10.3 Sensor Technology****

The pace of technological evolution of SU sensors is so rapid that the end user may decide to use
several strategies at the same time. The end user may:

       - Choose a novel method

        - Adapt an existing technology for SU

        - Apply traditional multiple-use sensors in an SUS

The end user must evaluate the technology for suitability according to the accuracy, repeatability,
range, and calibration requirements. Other questions to consider are:

        - How compatible is the SU sensor with the sterilization technique?

        - What is the shelf life of the sensor?

        - What is the in-service life of the sensor?

        - How appropriate is the sensor for the SUS in question?

Early examples of SU sensors for pH and dissolved oxygen in an SU bioreactor (SUB) were based on
optical differences observed on a chemical patch immersed in the process fluid. Optical sensors are
calibrated with a batch-specific calibration standard instead of a zero-and-span calibration technique.
The end user must be aware that optical sensors may be sensitive to photo bleaching and drift. In
general, steam sterilization cycles may be more damaging to a reusable sensor than a single gamma
sterilization cycle. Some SU sensors may be calibrated using a traditional two-point calibration technique prior to use without any adverse effects on sterility. Thus, SU sensors may be inherently more
reliable than reusable sensors. The end user must pay attention to the advantages and limitations of a
chosen SU sensor, including the specific requirements and circumstances of application.
****5.10.4 Qualification and Validation****

The end user is responsible for process validation, and the method for validating an SUS must be
determined using a risk-based approach, which considers the effective integration of the SUS design
into the end user’s quality systems _**(8).**_ Although purchasing a preassembled, sterilized SUS allows the
end user to take advantage of the qualification data generated by the SUS supplier, it is incumbent on
the end user to understand the supplier’s qualification approach and how it applies to the end user’s
specific application. The scope of process validation at the manufacturing site must encompass the
materials for construction, relevant testing procedures (e.g., for heavy metals, particulates, pyrogenic

deficiencies discovered during the validation process may be mitigated by applying additional targeted
checks or other controls. Additional information may include pressure limitations, correct procedures

****5.11 Integrity****
****5.11.1 Testing an SUS for Leaks****

The requirements for testing an SUS will depend on its intended use. The retention of structural integrity (i.e., containers do not leak) is the main concern and is a key concern in process-contact applications. For low-risk applications (e.g., sample and buffer bags), an understanding of supplier testing,
performance, and training in appropriate handling procedures may be sufficient.

SUSs are increasingly used for the production of vaccines, and the egress of a live virus could have
serious consequences;, therefore, specific testing of individual systems is recommended.

When an SUS is used for holding media, the potential for microbial ingress that may compromise sterility becomes a significant concern. Attempts have been made to assess the maximum size of a defect
that will compromise the effectiveness of a flexible bag _**(56)**_ . This is a complex undertaking because
it depends on factors such as the surface tension of the liquid in the bag, time, temperature, and the
method of measurement. In addition, the permeability of individual components (e.g., tubing and
films) may differ according to the test agent (e.g., gas or dye) used.

The structural integrity of the SUS should be carefully evaluated in a collaborative manner by the end
user and the supplier. Post-shipment/transport testing to evaluate the method of packaging the SUS
will need to be incorporated. To test for the integrity of the fluid path during qualification—including tubing manifolds and connectors—a suggested method is to internally pressurize tubing with air
and hold it under water. Provided that testing will not compromise the sterility or cleanliness of the
equipment, manifolds can be inspected by manipulation to check for air bubbles at both tubing connector engagements and along the entire length of tubing. For upstream applications, the user should
confirm the supplier’s claims during verification and accept materials based on their documentation

thereafter. A special case may need to be made for customized SUS. Demands are generally stricter
for product containers; therefore, end users may wish to ask the suppliers what testing is feasible for
larger containers at their sites versus the end user’s site (after installation).

To test a bag container or a SUS for leakage, a suggested method (depending on the size of the SUS)
is to fill the plastic bag or SUS with water inside the bin, drum, or tank and check for leaks at maximum volume. If the location of the leak is not apparent, fill the plastic container with water outside
of the bin, drum, or tank on a flat, absorbent surface such as cardboard or paper. Locate the leak by
identifying wet spots on the absorbent surface corresponding to a position on the container. Using
the mapped location of the leak on the bag, examine the bin, drum, or tank for sharp points that may
have caused the leak. Examine the leak under magnification to determine whether the leakage was
caused by puncture, tear, creasing, pinhole, or another means. Track leak locations to help with root
cause analysis of failure modes (e.g. handling, structural/design considerations, and material failure).

If the end user determines that a leak was caused by a manufacturing defect, the failed sample should
be returned to the supplier for quality investigation and corrective action. The returned sample should
include full information regarding the conditions of use and location of the leak. Suppliers should
maintain records of system failures and their root causes for further analysis.
****5.11.2 Leak Prevention in an SUS****

loss. A thorough investigation must be conducted to determine the cause, identify the source of the
leak, and monitor corrective and preventive actions. The investigation strategy should include a leakinvestigation procedure for generating standard reports. Such a report must include the location of
the leak on the assembly, the surrounding temperature conditions, composition of the fluids, any
adverse mechanical events, and an analysis of the failure mode. This information is particularly useful

****5.12 Campaigning****

Although they are disposable systems designed to be used once and discarded, there are a few exceptional circumstances in which an SUS may be reused. Examples would be the use of a container as a
mixing vessel or reservoir for buffer concentrates, in which there are no broken connections and the
contents of the container are replenished. Extending the period of time or number of cycles an SUS
is operating in a closed process without breaking the sterile barrier processes is called campaigning.
The end user is responsible for evaluating and determining whether appropriate quality requirements
are met for their application.

The end user must also consider whether the reuse of an SUS would compromise the definition of
a batch. Conversely, strict adherence to single usage of a prepacked, presanitized chromatography
column may be prohibitively expensive; therefore, it would be economical for the end user to run
the column for multiple cycles (e.g., for preparing clinical material). The column may then be held
in storage conditions and tested before being reused. The effectiveness of the storage conditions and
tests should be verified as for a brand-new column.

This section provides a detailed description of the key business drivers that inform the evaluation and
implementation of single-use versus traditional MUSs. The business drivers for the introduction of an
SUS are many, varied, and unique to each company’s individual situation and business model. Several
factors must be considered for a successful analysis. These factors include:

        - Supplier-demonstrated technical and quality capabilities

        - Product sustainability

        - Guarantee of business continuity

       - The business model of the company considering an SUS

        - Process design

        - The product and its potential market volume

         - Available facilities

        - Anticipated location

Each company will approach SUS implementation with a different set of objectives developed from
business drivers that are unique to a specific business model.

SUS implementation confers advantages such as reduced capital investment, flexible operation, faster
changeover, and the application of lean manufacturing principles. Since a company’s individual situation and business model will affect the role of pertinent business drivers, it is critical to start the evaluation with an understanding of the business in question. For example, the main concern of a contract
manufacturing company (CMO) may be the elimination of cross-contamination in a multiple-client,
multiple-product facility. A stand-alone manufacturer with a facility dedicated to the production of a
single product may consider a hybrid approach of an SUS and MUS to reduce SU variable costs yet allow for the expansion of production capacity at peak periods. An effective implementation of an SUS
would help the company to meet important business goals in addition to cost savings.
****6.1 Evaluation of Business Drivers****

The decision to implement an SUS tends to be driven by economic incentives such as 1) potential elimination of cleaning and sterilization processes; 2) reduction of dedicated equipment or changeover time; and
3) increased flexibility and capacity of the manufacturing facility. The end user must consider both tangible cost savings and less quantitative benefits such as low risks of cross contamination. A cost-of-goodsmanufactured (COGM) analysis can be used to support claims of economic savings. Other tools, such as
risk assessment, value-stream mapping, CO2 footprint, green manufacturing, and value-added analysis, can
provide invaluable data to inform a business decision on whether to implement SUS or not.
****6.2 Lifecycle Approach****

The importance of specific business drivers will depend on the needs of each individual company.
The scope of the project may be as narrow as using an SUS to boost the capacity of a single process
step or to eliminate cleaning steps. Such a tactical comparison depends on technological, process
compatibility, and economic factors. Conversely, the project scope could be a corporate-wide initiative to evaluate the feasibility of SUS implementation for an entire production process or part of an
overall lifecycle evaluation for a product or facility. A lifecycle approach involves an evaluation of the
costs incurred throughout the lifetime of the project in order to provide a complete economic basis
for making decisions. Costs for evaluation may include the initial capital investments, production and
quality overheads, and decommissioning. The lifecycle approach incorporates time and risk factors as
relevant strategies are applied, and this leads to a more comprehensive economic evaluation.

For instance, the forecast for a new product may include an initial launch phase with a relatively uncertain production demand followed by a full-scale production phase with options for capacity expansion
or additional products. In such a situation, operational flexibility becomes important to the business
case because of the uncertainty in product demand. Such flexibility comes with an increase in capital
investment for traditional MUSs, whereas the implementation of an SUS tends to reduce the cost of
operational flexibility because it is less reliant on investment for utilities and equipment installation.
An SUS is less costly to modify or expand to a new facility when a change in product lifecycle occurs.
****6.3 Opportunity Cost****

Opportunity cost is defined as the value of the next-best choice available when choosing between
several mutually exclusive options. These costs are often excluded from estimates of fixed operation costs because they can be difficult for comparative analyses in the overall decision process. For
example, deciding to expand a facility may result in forgoing the opportunity to invest in other projects, contract batch production at a CMO, or maintain cash reserves. Opportunities can be difficult
to compare; hence, opportunity cost calculations between competing projects may help identify the
best path forward.
****6.4 Cost of Quality****

The cost of quality can be defined as any cost that would not have been expended if quality were
perfect. The inherent advantages of an SUS may lead to reductions in the cost of quality for the end
user. In order for an SUS to be used in a manufacturing process, the end user must be able to demonstrate that it is fit for purpose and meets applicable quality and regulatory requirements. SUS suppliers
may take advantage of automation, economies of scale, and lean manufacturing to improve consistency and reduce the cost of manufacturing. This arrangement makes the end user dependent on the
supplier’s quality system. Therefore, it is critical for the quality system of the supplier to be robust
enough to manage subsuppliers (e.g., resin and component suppliers). In addition, the quality system
of the supplier must be able to monitor and detect the unique failure modes associated with SUS (e.g.,
bag breakage, variations in the profile of extractables and leachables, and effects on cell growth). It is
the responsibility of the end user to ensure that the SUS meets applicable quality standards. Any tests
conducted by the end user to ascertain process compatibility due to changes made by the supplier
may be included in cost-of-quality estimates.

The cost of inactivating solid waste can be significant depending on the degree of containment required. The solid waste may be 1) inactivated on-site, which will lead to increased load on kill autoclaves; or 2) transported off-site for incineration or disposal at a landfill. Both approaches would incur
transport and disposal fees. A third approach involves chemical inactivation. The disposal strategy for
each SUS must comply with the laws and requirements of the municipal authority in charge _**(64–66).**_

affected by SUS implementation, the baseline costs of MU and SU systems tend to be similar. Batch
review and investigation efforts may decrease and result in additional savings because SUS implementation results in reduced cleaning, SIP, and changeover activities. However, certain quality oversight
activities (e.g., quality agreement, supplier audits, SUS documentation review, subsupplier audits, and
QC-receiving inspection) may increase with an SUS implementation. The operation of several quality activities within the supplier’s quality system rather than that of the end user may make process
investigations more complex, and this additional effort should be taken into account.

****6.10.1 Cost of Capital****

The cost of capital represents a fixed cost that is independent of the number of batches produced.

hand. Because fixed costs are added to variable operating costs to obtain the average batch cost, capital costs would affect batch costs through depreciation and amortization.

The rate of depreciation may be faster for an SUS compared to an MUS because new models and

The cost of staff depends on the number of batches produced through direct processing operations and

of the number of batches produced, it is a good simplification to keep staff costs separate. However, if a
production slot is allocated at a CMO, the cost of staff needs to be included as a variable operating cost.
This is because both process suite occupancy and the cost of staff can be viewed as direct variable costs.

direct-production (production staff) and indirect-production related (e.g., administration, management, automation and maintenance). A time study or production modeling may be used to determine the comparative cost of staff between SUSs and MUSs.

****6.11 Project Duration, Time, and Productivity****
****6.11.1 Overall Project Duration****

Overall project duration may be an overarching decision driver that makes all other economic analysis secondary. The comparison of the project duration for a facility that depends heavily on SUS to
one that utilizes primarily MUSs may be compelling enough to make further analysis unnecessary.
In general, SUS-based facilities have shown significantly shorter project durations when compared
to MUS-based facilities. This reduced project duration can be used to delay capital investment or to
speed time-to-clinic, time-to-market, or a combination thereof. The benefit of delay of capital investment may be measured by both the reduced cost of capital and the significantly reduced risk of a
bad investment due to more clinical data being available at the time the decision must be made. For
novel drugs under patent protection, the benefit of reduced time-to-clinic or time-to-market may be
calculated based on the assumed revenue and profits to be realized during the period of exclusivity.
Reduced time-to-clinic and time-to-market effectively extends the period of exclusivity by the difference in the project duration.

****6.11.2 Process Operation Time****

A SUS may increase the capacity of the facility by reducing process downtime for:

       - Equipment setup

       - Product changeover

With an SUS, there is an opportunity to streamline the process by combining process steps because of
better turndown ratios. However, the end user must consider the entire process because limitations
in mass and heat transfer capacities may delay important setup or preparative operations for SUS,
such as hydration, process fluid transfers, temperature adjustments, pH adjustments, and charges and

****6.11.3 Time Constraints****

An SUS may remove time and capacity constraints and improve scheduling because of the reduced

effects on the main process schedule. In this case, an SUS still requires time to bring components to
the facility for setup and deployment. The planned batch time should account for time spent in set
up, deployment, process adjustments, temperature and pH control, and dismantling the systems _**(67).**_

****6.13 Disposal of an SUS****

Despite the recognized benefits in biological and pharmaceutical manufacturing of SU technologies,
the end user must still address issues such as residual solid waste from used components and systems.
Options for used SU equipment include reuse, material repurposing, or solid waste disposal, including landfill, incineration, and energy recovery. Solid waste is typically measured in tonnage units, so
while the implementation of SU manufacturing may appear to introduce a significant increase in solid
plastics waste, it is often not perceived as significant by waste management professionals compared to
other industrial and municipal plastics waste.

Some SU technologies may be amenable to limited reuse in continuous processing or campaign
modes. Examples include nonsterile mixing containers, buffer preparation systems, disposable prepacked preparatory chromatography columns, or membrane chromatography units used in dedicated campaigns.

At the moment, it is not commercially feasible to recycle SU materials because of the potential need to
inactivate biohazard waste or separate SU assemblies with multiple components. Furthermore, there
is often inadequate volume of total waste for most recycling programs. Recycling is more effective
and economically feasible when there is a critical mass of materials that can be recycled. However, the
increasing adoption of SU technologies has led to an increasing concern for the amount of clean polymeric/elastomeric material that is either deposited into landfills or incinerated. Most of the SU materials used in the biopharmaceutical industry are either nonhazardous or rendered nonhazardous before
being shipped out to either a landfill or an incinerator. Although previous technology was restricted to
the recycling of only homopolymers, modern technology now allows for the recycling of mixed plastics. Silicone tubing can be fully converted to base silicone, which can be used to make sealants, gums,
and other products. Although some challenges still remain, biopharmaceutical companies are looking
for cleaner and greener alternatives, and suppliers are starting to provide alternative solutions _**(68,69).**_

Repurposing of mixed-plastic solid waste may require cleaning and sterilization, followed by grinding
into particulate form for use as a filler material. Some companies accept clean mixed-plastic wastes
for repurposing.

Where SU components or systems cannot be reused, recycled, or repurposed, some form of solidwaste disposal must be explored. Burial (landfilling) or incineration may be either controversial or
preferred for a variety of reasons. The waste management professionals of the facility would be familiar with local municipal regulations for the disposal of solid-waste plastics (including wastes from
discarded packaging materials, laboratory and cafeteria use, and maintenance). Grind and autoclave

Cancer Institute (NCI) and the National Institutes of Health (NIH).

According to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), “incineration is a widely-accepted waste
treatment option with many benefits. Combustion reduces the volume of waste that must be disposed

countries and municipalities. In a waste-to-energy process, incineration may result in significant energy
recovery. Waste-to-energy technology is employed throughout much of Europe, and it is increasingly
being implemented in the Americas. Waste-to-energy is used to capture the trapped energy in municipal
and industrial wastes and convert it to heat or electricity. SU waste may be considered for internal wasteto-energy conversion or may be transferred to other municipal or industrial waste-to-energy facilities
(e.g., cement manufacturers who are now using mixed plastics instead of coal or used rubber tires to

****6.15 The Value Added****

Unlike an SUS, an MUS requires several preparative procedures (value-added activities) prior to the
beginning of each manufacturing batch. In an SUS, these steps are outsourced to the supplier and
managed under the supplier’s quality control (QC) systems. The cost of these value-added activities
is shifted from the company’s fixed-cost stream (e.g., labor) to a variable-cost stream (e.g., disposables per batch). This is because in SU manufacturing, high-quality, presterilized, disposable parts
are supplied to the end user by the supplier, and this value-added cost and the cost of quality for any
deficiencies that the end user encounters must be included in the assessment; otherwise, it would
lead to inaccurate results. Capturing the major value-added activities of process design, qualification,
operation, and verification across the supply chain ensures that all the required value-added activities
are included in the assessment.

During a typical MUS operation, the quality unit verifies the activities performed by operations. Adoption of an SUS eliminates many of the preoperational activities (e.g., assembly, cleaning, and sterilization) from the work performed by operations. ****7.2.3 Process Validation and Verification (PVV)****

In pharmaceutical manufacturing, process validation (PV) is the collection and evaluation of data
from the process design stage through commercial production that establishes scientific evidence that
a process is capable of consistently delivering quality product _**(8).**_ The U.S FDA and EMA consider PV
a requirement in both general and specific terms in Good Manufacturing Practice _**(74,75).**_ Verification
is a systematic approach for ensuring that manufacturing systems (acting either singly or in combination) are suitable for their intended use, have been properly installed, and are operating correctly. This
is an umbrella term that encompasses all types of activities to assuring systems are fit for use, such as
qualification, commissioning and qualification, verification, system validation, and others _**(18).**_ Verification (or process verification) is an important concept for SUSs and is a primary control strategy for
sourcing from suppliers. This remains active throughout the lifecycle of the project.

PVV is a tool that requires an integrated project management approach that involves all pertinent
stakeholders (e.g., a cross-company project team) and full support from management. A scienceand risk-based approach to PVV tends to generate a thorough understanding of the manufacturing
process, which in turn enhances product quality and patient safety _**(8,74).**_ ICH guidance documents,
such as ICH Q8, Q9, and Q10, provide useful guidelines for development, risk analysis, and product
control _**(3–5).**_ A draft guideline on process validation for the manufacture of biotechnology-derived
active substances and a guideline on process validation for finished products has been published by
EMA _**(75,76).**_

SUS process validation and verification is normally performed using a lifecycle approach. The FDA
2011 process validation guidance and PDA Technical Report 60: Process Validation - A Lifecycle Approach provide useful models _**(8,74).**_ Within that model, SUS implementation becomes part of process validation stages 1 and 2 (S1 and S2). Productive use of SUS is part of process validation S3. Additional technical guidance is available for this and for a phased approach to quality systems and the
application of GMP as well _**(63,77).**_

Continuous quality verification can also be used. The knowledge obtained as the product lifecycle
progresses will allow a framework to be established in order to attain mitigation of risks, reduction of
process variability, enhancement of process capability, definition of enhancement of process design
space, and improvements in product quality. These may lead to other benefits that address both compliance and operational goals (e.g., continuous process improvement) _**(78).**_ **Figure 7.2.3-1** illustrates
two component elements—validation prerequisites and conformance batches—for process validation

****7.3 Implementation S3: Scoping****

The project scoping process provides a source of information that is complementary to that obtained from a
definition of the manufacturing strategy (Section 3). Scoping identifies pertinent information that may affect
conception (e.g., potential materials, science, and facility operational requirements) and establishes the principles for conceiving, selecting, and operating the SUS. Once the basic strategy is defined, this information is
applied in S4 to define process and facility requirements **(Section 7.4.2).** Technical guides that may assist the
SUS scoping process are available from the website of Bioprocess Systems Alliance (BPSA) _**(79).**_
****7.3.1 SUS Strategy****

The output of the scoping process is the SUS strategy document. This is a primary reference document that
provides overall guidance and policies for SU applications across the entire project. It may be combined with
outputs from S1 and S2 if more elaborate evaluations were performed during those stages. However, if the
organization of the road map is changed, care should be exercised that the implementation process does not
lose structure and that the comprehensive treatment of all of the elements necessary remains in place. The
SUS strategy is normally a short and concise document with a global technical vision outlining how SUS
control strategies would integrate with quality systems and international GMPs.

The strategy also highlights key topics and clarifies their roles within the SUS. This is important because propitious decisions made at the beginning of the implementation process can save considerable time, effort, and money in later stages. For instance, it is critical that the selection of SUS equipment and suppliers is not allowed to become an ad hoc process. This issue can be problematic for
large companies as well as small to medium enterprises. Lack of control over this decision at an early
stage can lead to equipment redundancies and using more suppliers than necessary. Depending on the
circumstances, this can be considered to be a form of technology overdosing (i.e., too many suppliers
or no coordination across company functional areas). Although having a straightforward relationship
with suppliers is essential, dealing with too many suppliers can significantly increase the resources and
supplier qualification effort required at later implementation stages.

Equally important, consider the case in which an SUS is supplied as part of a raw material in a readyto-use format (e.g., a ready-made culture media in which the media container is an SUS). Regardless
of whether this was an occasional order or made via a supply agreement, the end user would need
to make sure that the supplied components adhere to the same quality standards as all the other SUS
components. Such a scenario may represent an SUS-inherited implementation because the end user
acquires the product but does not necessarily have a choice about the technology source.

The following paragraphs describe key subjects that should be addressed in the SUS strategy document.
****7.3.2 Business Drivers****

It is important to review the business drivers for SUS implementation. Sometimes, an SUS may be
used for predevelopment prior to a requirement for GMP (e.g., in a small-scale and disposable multireactor robotic screening system). Much larger SUSs can be deployed when process development
or manufacturing services are required during advanced stages of the product lifecycle. Economic
considerations (i.e., the desire to reduce costs) tend to become the most critical business drivers for
selecting either an MUS or an SUS. Other factors are quality-improvement drivers such as containment or the need to avoid cross contamination. End users may need to adapt the policies described in
this report to their specific situation and review them regularly. This is because it may be necessary to
determine how the quality system, GMP, and PVV fit into the overall process.

****7.3.3 Operating Scenarios and Standardization****

Developing operating scenarios and standardization offers end users the opportunity to define specific rules,
policies, or principles for key issues relevant to the implementation process, for example, the type of multipurpose operations in use or the selection of production by campaign. More examples are described later
in this section. An example of the practical application of standardization can be found in the literature _**(80).**_
****7.3.4 Process Validation Stage****

It is important to define the SUS project within the process validation lifecycle because it affects the
subsequent approach to PVV. Predevelopment systems (prior to PVV S1) would not necessarily be
subject to the same quality requirements as that for later-stage processes involving GMP. Conversely,
the starting materials for some medicinal products, such as those for cell therapy, could be isolated at
a very early stage in the product development lifecycle, and their exploitation may require extensive
PVV and careful handling to ensure process compatibility and regulatory compliance.
****7.3.5 Operating Volumes and Storage Requirements****

The end user should define operating and storage volumes for static and mobile requirements. A
maximum mobile volume (i.e., the maximum allowable volume for manual transportation within the
plant) above 500 L may be difficult to transport and may significantly affect the floor space required in
the facility. In addition, such large equipment may be subject to HSE regulations.
****7.3.6 Hybrid Systems****

The end user should evaluate the requirements for connections that combine SU and MU systems
(e.g., CIP or SIP). The principles that ensure operator safety and product integrity must be defined.
Any MUS parts that require manual cleaning and autoclaving must be defined and specified.
****7.3.7 Connection Principles****

The end user should define the requirements and principles of operation that apply to the various
connection applications. These principles should include the types of connections allowed or employed, according to process circumstances and regulatory requirements.
****7.3.8 Campaigning****

Although there are specific situations where an SUS container might be refilled and used in a single
campaign (e.g., where a large buffer tank is used to refill a single SUS storage vessel without breaking
the sterile barrier), reusing SUS is not recommended. It is preferable to define a position from a riskbased perspective against reusing SU components. Multiple caustic cycles over an SU column may be
used to process several pools from a batch or series of harvests, but SU tubing circuits in processing
skids may not be able to withstand repeated caustic cycles. Therefore, while the SU columns may be
campaigned, the SU tubing circuits may need to be replaced for each cycle.
****7.3.9 Future Deployment****

Significant productivity gains can be achieved by deploying and exploiting SUS over MUS. For example, there can be significant differences in maintenance costs, batch-to-batch turnaround, and staffing requirements _**(81).**_ Standard principles must be reviewed and identified for activities involving the
hands-on steps required to successfully assemble and make a system ready for use for a specific SUS
application. Including a defined setup and deployment stage, with standardized activities for each
part, is a recognized success factor for SUS implementation, and this should be a routine part of the
workflow and planning for each SUS application.

****7.3.10 Sourcing****

The end user should determine whether the SUS would be sourced from exterior suppliers or vendors (i.e., designed, constructed, and delivered) or self-made (i.e., designed, prepared, and operated
on-site). Some applications and business drivers may implicitly require a clear policy, so a risk-based
approach should always be used. A self-made SUS would have to meet the following requirements:

        - Has specifications

        - Has batch process records

         - Meets qualification criteria including sterility

        - Has a change-control process in place

         - Passes stability studies if necessary

It is essential to verify that both internally and externally sourced SUSs meet the same quality standards.
****7.3.11 Testing Strategy****

An SUS strategy should define the principles and aims of any in-house testing program that would be
proposed as part of the implementation process. This may include the collection of supplier testing
data and controls. Testing strategies should be discussed in detail with the supplier, and quality agreements should allow for the exchange of test data between a supplier and the end user.
****7.3.12 Materials****

The strategy should define a position on single- or multiple-film usage. Reducing the number of material specifications reduces confusion and errors. For example, using the same product-contact film
may simplify the subsequent evaluation of extractables and leachables. However, it may also limit
the range of available technical solutions. Although desirable, standardization within a single-film
strategy may not be possible where multiple SUSs from different suppliers are used in an integrated
process. Finally, the materials should be biocompatible and the end user should be aware of supplier
testing procedures. Harmonizing the various testing methods that suppliers use could make it easier
for end users to make comparisons between suppliers (see **Table 5.5.2-1).**
****7.3.13 Shelf-Life Policy****

The expiration and shelf-life policy needs to be defined. This is particularly important if the end user
has a large number of different SUSs (from multiple suppliers or self-made assemblies). Note that
some SUS suppliers may have their own definition of shelf life, and the end user should address and
resolve these issues during policy definition.
****7.3.14 Procurement****

The procurement process should be standardized. If the project deals with several suppliers, it may be
necessary to make this a uniform process. The end user may need different strategies for purchasing
occasional versus routine SUS equipment and components **(Sections 7.4.10, 7.5.3, 7.7.1,** and **7.7.2).**
****7.3.15 Storage****

The strategy document should define how SUS warehousing and logistical operations would be organized. A dedicated clean and controlled storage area is recommended.

****7.3.16 Waste Treatment****

The policy for waste treatment (i.e., how SUS solid waste would be handled and inactivated) should
be defined, and the potential impact of carbon taxes must be taken into account. In addition, the end
user must explore the feasibility of recycling some of the waste from SUSs.
****7.3.17 Non-GxP Issues****

The end user should define any non-GxP issues that must be considered as part of the SUS implementation project. These issues are often quite diverse and can be local or country-specific (e.g., permits,
tax credits, grants or subsidies, imposed supplier preferences, or safety-related concerns).
****7.3.18 Sustainability****

A key point is that in general, SUSs are more sustainable and have less adverse environmental effects
than traditional MUSs. The SUS strategy should define a policy that includes these principles. Some
examples would be fossil fuel depletion and energy use, urban land occupation, pollution, greenhouse gas emissions and ozone depletion, water depletion, and so on _**(82).**_
****7.4 Implementation S4: Business Case****

****7.4.8 Operational-Level Integration Plan****

This describes the parameters for SUS integration (i.e., assembly, connection, and deployment) in readiness for manufacturing. A risk-based approach should be used because of the structural and functional
diversity of SUSs _**(91).**_ Some of the important parameters to consider during deployment include:

**• Packaging and container integrity —** It must be determined whether preuse leak testing of SUS
is feasible. Manufacturing, shipping, handling, and operational procedures should be qualified to
maintain SUS integrity. This can be achieved by implementing control strategies within the end
user’s organization and between supplier and end user.

**• SUS testing and release —** Testing and release vary with use. The same off-the-shelf system
may have different test and release criteria depending on process-, product-, regulatory-, and userimposed requirements.

**• Reuse of components —** In general, reuse of SUSs is not recommended. The policy for
this should have been established in the SUS strategy **(Section 7.2.1.7).** However, there are
circumstances in which the reuse of SUS may be acceptable (e.g., use of a container as a mixing
vessel or reservoir for buffer concentrates in which there are no broken connections). A risk
analysis should be performed to determine if the risks are acceptable.

**• Training —** Appropriate personnel training is essential because the collapse of a bag may lead
to stress cracks that could result in a loss of structural and functional integrity. Training topics
might include the use of different sterile connection and disconnection devices or techniques for
avoiding bag creases or holes during deployment.

**• Shelf life —** A coherent expiration and shelf-life policy should be implemented, especially in
situations in which the end user has a large number of different SUSs from multiple suppliers or
local, self-made assemblies.

**• Quality assurance —** Ensuring quality during deployment should be addressed at an early stage
of implementation. Technical diligence may enhance this goal **(Section 7.4.10** and **7.7.3).**

****7.4.10 Supplier Selection and Supply-Chain Review****

The aim of this review is to determine a policy for the identification and selection of suppliers in order
to complete the PEP and project planning.

The selection of appropriate SUS suppliers can often be an arduous task that can be made less cumbersome with a risk-management approach. For a successful SUS implementation, it is essential to
have a detailed and transparent relationship between suppliers, vendors, and the end user. This requires good communication and understanding of stakeholder management in order to garner the
expectations of both the suppliers and the end users. Being able to share pertinent information with
suppliers at the early stages of the project may speed up the design process. Note that in order to
obtain full benefits from this approach, the exchange of uni- or bilateral confidential disclosure agreements (CDA) may be required. The implementation and management of an end user–supplier relationship via technical diligence is described in **Section 7.7.3.** Suppliers provide material data, advice
on applying typical designs, and the following documents:

- Process flow diagrams (PFDs)

- Piping and Instrumentation Diagrams
(P&IDs)

- Component lists

- Physical layout proposals

- Operation manuals

- Pricing

- Qualification packages, including guidelines
on E&L, for example

For both large companies and SMEs, it is critical to prevent the selection of equipment and suppliers
from becoming an ad hoc process because that can lead to unnecessary and costly redundancies. In addition, working with too many suppliers may divert limited resources that could otherwise be gainfully
employed in other parts of the SUS implementation process. Since the degree of business integration of
a particular SUS supplier may depend on the size, location, and SUS assembly concerned, it is important
to adopt a risk-mitigation approach to SUS supplier qualification and validation **(Section 7.7.3).**

Starting materials such as resins and films should be treated as critical raw materials as part of the
verification process due to the complexity of SUS implementation _**(18).**_ It is important to begin the
supplier technical diligence (i.e., obtaining a thorough understanding of the SUS product, supplier,
and compatibility of the SUS with the end user’s process and facility) at this stage. These principles
are based on established procedures for critical raw materials as recommended by the Association
for Professionals in Infection Control and Epidemiology (APIC) due diligence process, ASTM E250007 vendor technical capability as part of the PVV process **(Figure 7.2.7.1-1),** and quality by design
(QbD)-inspired raw materials qualification processes _**(18,92–94).**_ Full details on how to implement the
technical diligence process are described in Section 7.7.3. Note that this task may require the exchange
of uni- or bilateral CDAs or nondisclosure agreements (NDA).

The quality of the entire supply chain should be assessed, qualified, and implemented via a structured
risk-management process _**(72).**_ All parties in the supply chain should make sure that their activities
maintain business continuity and support the health of patients. SUS equipment that is purchased
occasionally may be done on a contractual basis, but a quality and supply agreement must be used to
buy routine SUS equipment **(Sections 7.5.7** and **7.7.12).**

****7.5 Implementation S5: Development****

During this stage, the SUS user requirements are developed, including layout and design, specifications, and standardization. Complete user requirements should precede the procurement process
(e.g., commercial negotiations between strategic project partners or raising a purchase order).
****7.5.1 User Requirements for Implementation****

User requirements should be prepared at the start of the development process. The principal user and
stakeholders must be identified and their requirements obtained. The user requirements are based on
product and process knowledge, critical quality attributes, and any relevant regulatory requirements.
The principal user should also provide the justifications for the resources required to design, purchase,
qualify, and implement any new process, system, or equipment. These justifications can be developed
prior to selecting an SUS or supplier, or they may be developed for a particular system. Some or all of
the requirements can be based on a QbD approach. In this case, the product and process-control strategy
performed earlier in the QbD process will become a prerequisite for defining the SUS requirements.

User requirements and functional specifications may be recorded together in a single document for
low-risk, small-scale SUS. However, multiple user requirements may be needed for complex systems
or processes. In this case, it would be critical to avoid conflicts across multiple documents. Any necessary conflicts must be identified and documented. The user requirements are created by the end user
or on the end user’s behalf; however, some supplier documentation may be necessary to supplement
this. Supplier documentation is acceptable if the supplier follows GEP _**(90)**_, has an appropriate quality
system, and is technically capable according to ASTM E2500-07 and _PDA Technical Report No. 60: Process_
_Validation — A Lifecycle Approach_ _**(8,18).**_ In some cases, the supplier documentation could replace the
company document. The user requirements are kept current throughout the system’s lifecycle and
should be reevaluated after any change in intended use, user needs, or altered materials specifications
(e.g., the films used, functional specifications, regulatory assessments or supplier quality audits, technical diligence). An example of a user requirements document is included in **Section 8, Appendix 1.**
****7.5.2 Preparing User Requirements****

Brainstorming sessions, workshops, interviews, assessments, and ad hoc discussions may be used
to gather pertinent information about the needs of the end user. Other useful information may be
obtained from suppliers, case histories, and workflow analyses. These requirements may not be fully
defined initially but may be developed further during subsequent phases of the project. It is crucial to
determine the level of interest of each stakeholder has in the project and ensure that stakeholders receive adequate and regular communications throughout the SUS process lifecycle. User requirements
statements must be written to a level of specificity that:

        - Describes the SUS function

        - Allows end users to verify that the system meets their functional requirements

        - Addresses the compatibility of processes and materials (e.g., polymer films)

However, they must be general enough to avoid constraining the system design. If a supplier will provide
specific functionality, then the user requirements must be specific enough to determine that functionality.

****7.5.5 Standardization Policy****

Although the inherent flexibility at the core of SUS design is an advantage, it could degenerate into a
disadvantage by introducing unwelcome variations in the supply chain if left unchecked. Therefore,
introducing a standard design policy across the project may reduce the incidence of variations, especially as the scale of operations becomes more complex. Implement a standardization policy as part
of the user requirements according to the following guidelines:

        - Working volumes may be chosen so that bag ranges cover a series of application ranges.

       - Connections and material on bags and tubing may be chosen so that several possibilities for
connecting are maintained (e.g., providing weldable tubing and a typical connector together).

        - Setting up an SU toolbox consisting of Y pieces, sample manifolds, and lengths of tubing for special
or infrequent applications will allow a more specific design element to be attached to a more
standardized bag design.

        - An active standardization policy may have been defined as part of the SUS strategy **(Section 7.2.1).**
If so, it may not be necessary to implement it at this stage.

****7.5.7 Procurement****

Although procurement procedures will differ from company to company, it is critical to make sure
that the end user requirements and other relevant specifications are correctly used and that the SUSbased equipment adheres to those specifications. It is important to maintain a close partnership between suppliers and end users in order to facilitate the procurement process. Some of the key points
to consider are as follows:

        - The main procurement process will be outlined and planned in the PEP.

        - Key stakeholders must be identified and their roles and responsibilities must be clearly defined. This
is extremely important in cases where procurement precedes the implementation of a quality and
supply agreement.

        - Contractual issues must be clearly defined. The end user’s legal team should have reviewed and,
if necessary, negotiated any modification(s) to the supplier’s general terms and conditions of sale
to ensure that the user’s business, financial, and operational expectations are met (e.g., compliance
with Incoterms 2011) _**(103).**_

        - Good project planning and management principles must be carefully followed so that the project
remains on track throughout its life.

        - A change-notification process should be in place between the equipment supplier and the end user
in order to monitor any design changes that take place at this point.

        - Appropriate verifications must be conducted and the acceptance criteria met on a timely basis in
order to avoid unnecessary delays.

        - A technical diligence and quality audit process should be implemented. By now, the supplier and SUS
should have been evaluated by a site visit. Any design changes should be assessed for their compliance, the
supplier’s ability to adhere to the modifications, and the end user’s process compatibility **(Section 7.7.1).**

        - The delivery and installation process should be agreed to with the supplier. Please note that not all
suppliers include these facilities in their basic offers; hence, they can take time to define properly
and add significant costs to the total investment **(Sections 7.6.1** and **7.6.2).**

        - It may be appropriate at this stage to initiate quality and supply agreements for routine SUS
procurement **(Section 7.7).**

        - Business contingency planning and crisis management should be reviewed for potential problems
or the need to make necessary changes to project planning, construction, and delivery or receiving
because these can have profound impact on the end user’s startup and C&Q processes.
****7.6 Implementation S6: Testing and Validation****

During this stage, methods and documentation must be developed for equipment SOPs, maintenance, qualification and process validation, HSE processes, and personnel training. Some documents
(e.g., the validation master plan) may require updates at this stage to maintain compliance.
****7.6.1 Delivery****

The PEP must cover the delivery process, including responsibilities of the supplier and the end user.
Transport logistics are sometimes complex, and the procedure by which the equipment will be safely
shipped (transported) and unloaded from its delivery vehicle must be considered. Some suppliers do
not take unloading into account, and the end user may need to take particular precautions here or
ensure that the supplier contractually takes responsibility. There may be specific non-GxP issues to
resolve such as documentation (delivery notes), inspection for inventory or damage, and insurance or
the removal and appropriate disposal of crates and other packaging materials _**(103)**_ **.**
****7.6.2 Installation****

The PEP will also cover all aspects of the startup process, including post-delivery storage, handling
prior to installation unpacking, and the responsibilities of the supplier and the end user in a more
general manner. Transferring the equipment to its place of use can be problematic if equipment dimensions or facility access has not been reviewed in advance. Preparing the area, particularly in cases
where the SUS equipment will be used in manufacturing operations, may require specific training.
The overall strategy and approach will depend on the nature of the SUS and the level of risk associated with this process **(Section 7.4.1).** In some cases, non-GxP documentation may be generated or
supplied and work permits (e.g., fire safety) secured during the installation processes (e.g., removals).
****7.6.3 Qualification****

Qualification of an SUS should include lifecycle testing that reproduces the most impactful steps of
the system’s lifecycle (e.g., sterilization, transport, freezing/thawing) followed by a verification of the
key functionalities and performance attributes (e.g., integrity testing). The lifecycle testing must be
designed to cover the intended working space. Please refer to **Sections 5** and **7.7** for a discussion of
the SUS qualification process and supplier qualification, respectively.

****7.6.4 Training****

Training is a GMP requirement and an integral part of SUS technical implementation. The goal of
any training program should be to provide specific knowledge and understanding that will support
successful operations. Although, the specific training needs are normally described in the user requirements, other training requirements (e.g., for project management and risk assessment) may be
critical for the successful implementation of the project. Training programs for SUS should include
concepts and principles of operation that might be unique to SUS and unfamiliar to MUS operators.
****7.6.5 Training Workflow****

Guidelines for a training workflow are as follows:

        - Examine each part of the entire SUS PEP individually and identify the functional areas concerned.

        - Identify the training needs in each functional area and assign stakeholders.

        - Assemble training requirements in a training matrix (example in Section 10, Appendix 3).

        - Determine the scope of each training module. Some subjects may require more than one level of
presentation and may require periodic retraining.

        - Make sure that a trainee assessment or evaluation process is included at the end of the training
module; this is a GMP requirement.

        - Take the resources as well as the subject and complexity of the training requirement into account
when planning training procedures.

       - Some training modules may be delivered by competent team members or operators who are
familiar with SUS implementation. Other training modules may be delivered by suppliers.

        - Add completed training modules to the individual training records of each stakeholder.

Factors that may contribute to the leakage of bags are:

        - Mechanical stress on films or welds

The in-house training program should be based on operational parameters and operator feedback.
Operators should be educated and engaged about:

        - Prior root causes for failure

****7.7 Implementation S7: Launch****

The PEP leads to an initial procurement of equipment for the SUS project in addition to setting out
a secure supply chain for continuous operation of the SUS. Initial supplier evaluations are part of the

with a detailed evaluation of the appropriate tools for the routine management of suppliers.
****7.7.1 Management of Materials****

Since continuous sourcing of supplies is critical to keeping the SUS in a productive state, it is important to maintain an efficient routine supplier management (e.g., technical diligence, quality audits,
quality and supply agreements, and purchasing, logistical, and waste-handling strategies) to create a
robust support for SUS manufacturing operations.
****7.7.2 Routine or Operational Procurement****

Routine procurement for SUS (e.g., single-use bioreactors or chromatography systems) requires a supplier qualification program for each of the SUS suppliers included in the PEP. Whenever the risk and
complexity of the component in question is considered significant, the qualification program should
be implemented using a risk-management approach. Some of the factors to consider are as follows:

- The end user may have to adopt a different approach to the procurement of integrated SUS
equipment versus routine SUS components **(Sections 5.2, 5.4, 5.5, 6.12.1, 6.14, 7.5.7,** and **7.6.2).**

- A backup policy of sourcing the same component from at least two different vendors should be
adopted, and the suitability for the backup to be interchangeable with the original must be defined.

- The end user must evaluate and audit the supplier’s manufacturing capabilities and supply chain to
determine the supplier’s ability to increase output on demand.

- The relationship between the end user and the supplier should be as transparent as possible in order
to enhance the unimpeded operation of the SUS.

- The quality systems of the supplier should be examined as part of a quality audit process, and
quality and supply agreements should be implemented **(Section 7.7.4).**

        - The supplier should be provided with a detailed description of the manufacturing process of the
SUS, film quality, biocompatibility, and testing (e.g., bioburden monitoring of the SUS), particulates,
extractables and leachables, and testing of leakages _**(108)**_ .

        - A contract (supply or manufacturing agreement) describing the commercial conditions that relate
to the supply chain, guarantee of delivery, and transport quality should be implemented _**(109)**_ .

        - Both the supplier and the end user must institute robust change-control processes in order to
prevent ad hoc modifications to the SUS.

        - Time lines from the design to arrival of both MUS and SUS parts should be monitored.

        - Evaluation models (preferably at the end user’s site) that use mock-ups of preliminary SUS designs
(available for training and simulations) should be tested. Testing is an important risk-mitigating
procedure. This process may have been started as part of the SUS strategy **(Section 7.2.3).**

        - The end user should adhere to green and sustainable manufacturing policies throughout the entire
supply chain and evaluate waste disposal for carbon footprint reduction.
****7.7.3 Technical Diligence****

A QbD approach is the best way to qualify suppliers for SUSs. It is based on ICH principles (ICH
Q8) and it embraces the complementary concepts of quality-risk management and pharmaceutical
quality-system management (as detailed in ICH Q9 and Q10), process validation and verification, and
stakeholder management **(Sections 7.2.1, 7.2.2** and **7.2.3).** These approaches lay the foundation for
quality-driven processes built on a science- and risk-based approach and understanding. This is the
case not just for the process for which the SUS will be used but also for the SU technology that will be
deployed, thus supporting patient safety and product availability _**(3–5,18,77,106,110-114).**_ The QbD
approach allows for the identification and management of critical sources of variability in which control strategies can be used to maintain a state of control and facilitate the application of continuous
improvement throughout the SUS product lifecycle _**(114).**_

About PDA Technical Reports

PDA Technical Reports are global consensus documents, prepared by member-driven Task Forces (listed on inside front cover) comprised of content experts, including scientists and engineers working in the pharmaceutical/biopharmaceutical industry, regulatory
authorities and academia. While in development, PDA Technical Reports are subjected to a global review of PDA members and
other topic-specific experts, often including regulatory officials. Comments from the global review are then considered by the authoring Task Force during the preparation of the final working draft. The level of expertise of the Task Force and those participating
in the global review ensure a broad perspective reflecting best thinking and practices currently available.

The final working draft is next reviewed by the PDA Advisory Board or Boards that sanctioned the project. PDA’s Advisory Boards
are: Science Advisory Board, Biotechnology Advisory Board, and Regulatory Affairs and Quality Committee. Following this stage
of review, the PDA Board of Directors conducts the final review and determines whether to publish or not publish the document as
an official PDA Technical Report.
