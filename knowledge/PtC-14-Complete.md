Introduction
Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products (ATMPs) are unique because they are based on cells, genes, or
tissues from animals and human beings. ATMPs are produced either in low-batch volumes, such as products
produced for an individual patient (autologous), or (relatively) larger batch volumes for products produced
for multiple patients (allogeneic).
ATMPs are classified into three main types.
1. Gene Therapy Medicines: These contain genes that lead to a therapeutic, prophylactic, or
diagnostic effect. They work by inserting “recombinant” genes into the body, usually to treat a
variety of diseases, including genetic disorders, cancer, or other long-term diseases. A recombinant
gene is a segment of DNA that is created in the laboratory, bringing together DNA from different
sources.
2. Somatic-Cell Therapy Medicines: These contain cells or tissues that have been manipulated to
change their biological characteristics, or cells or tissues not intended to be used for the same
essential functions in the body. They can be used to cure, diagnose, or prevent diseases.
3. Tissue-Engineered Medicines: These contain cells or tissues that have been modified so they can
be used to repair, regenerate, or replace human tissue.
“Combined ATMPs” are ATMPs that combine with one or more medical device(s) as an integral part of the
medicine.
In general, ATMPs should adhere to the same manufacturing requirements as sterile parenteral drugs for
their upstream, downstream, and fill-and-finish requirements. The minimum expectations for the
manufacture of ATMPs are essentially the same as for all biological process considerations, with some
specific nuances depending on the starting material, end product, intended administration window, and
intended recipient(s). The use of ATMPs for clinical trials must also be manufactured under equivalent
quality standards to the manufacturing of ATMPs with a marketing authorization (1-3).
The basic good manufacturing practice (GMP) principles of all processes include design, control, and
monitoring. For ATMPs, these are:
• Design
̶ An appropriate Pharmaceutical Quality System (PQS)
̶ A detailed design of the process (open, closed, or hybrid, for autologous and/or allogeneic
therapies)
̶ Facilities, utilities, and process equipment that support the process design
̶ Prevention measures to avoid extraneous contamination, cross-contamination, error, or
mix-ups

=== PAGE 8 ===
̶ Material, personnel, and waste flows that support the process and prevention measures
• Control and Mitigation
̶ A formal contamination control strategy (CCS) specific to the process
̶ Control of starting materials and their source/donation and critical raw materials that are
appropriately sourced and controlled for quality
̶ Manufacturing environment controlled in a manner that supports the CCS
̶ Laboratory practices and personnel training that support the PQS
̶ Process (Safety) equipment controlled in a manner that supports both the PQS and CCS
̶ Effective Pharmaceutical Quality System (including a facility risk assessment)
̶ Traceability requirements that support batch identity and traceability from manufacture
through distribution
̶ Warehousing, distribution, and temperature controls that support product quality
throughout all phases of production and distribution
• Monitoring
̶ Process-environment-related attributes in accordance with CCS
̶ Cleanliness of facility and equipment to support fulfillment of CCS
̶ Testing (including microbial, particulate, airflow, and HVAC) to confirm integrity of
environmental controls
̶ Release-testing strategy to ensure fulfillment of the quality target product profile (QTPP)
This document considers the facility, utilities, and process equipment designs necessary to support the
manufacture and testing of ATMPs (autologous and allogeneic). Ideally, the design considerations should be
weighed upfront to ensure the process meets the respective requirements.
ATMP processes are still an emerging science. As a consequence, there are a variety of groups that are
involved in their development and commercialization including academia, research organizations, hospitals,
and biopharmaceutical companies. At the time of compilation of this Points to Consider (PtC) document,
various manufacturing environment technologies are employed in the manufacture of ATMPs. Products are
commonly manipulated in biosafety cabinets (BSCs), isolators, or closed systems with the required
cleanroom surroundings. The stage of commercialization does not always dictate the type of facility or type
of technology through which the products are produced; rather, the environment or technology is often
selected based on the flexibility required for the process.
These technologies all have controls to ensure the environment where product manipulations occur is
maintained in a manner that prevents contamination. These cell-, gene-, or tissue-engineering-based
products are classified from occupational safety requirements to have a working environment determined by
its Biological Safety Level (BSL).
ATMP manufacturing includes some products that are meant to replace, repair, or modify genes in a cell. To
achieve this, certain viral vectors (e.g., adeno-associated virus, retrovirus, lentivirus, herpesvirus) are used
for that purpose. Viral vector-based products can vary in their BSL depending on the type of virus and its
potential to cause disease in humans. These are commonly classified as BSL-1 or BSL-2 and, in rare
circumstances, even higher (e.g., BSL-3), depending on the virus load and type of virus. This Points to
Consider document will focus on BSL-1 and BSL-2 requirements only, as BSL-3 and BSL-4 facilities are
rare for the manufacture of ATMPs.

Product and process characterization is performed using quality risk management (QRM) principles to help
the sponsor understand the importance of each aspect of the developed process/product. This information
includes requirements for environmental conditions, production equipment/systems, product quality, and
occupational safety. These elements have been reflected in GMP guidelines, norms, and practices for
decades. Additional recommendations on these concepts specific to ATMPs are addressed in this technical
document.
Given the novelty of these products and the relative scarcity of comprehensive technical guidance in support
of manufacturing, it can become difficult to find resources to help determine appropriate strategies to ensure
the successful manufacture of ATMPs. This Points to Consider (PtC) is an initiative of the PDA ATMP
Advisory Board and was compiled by a diverse team of PDA subject matter experts to address some of the
common questions within the industry regarding the manufacture of these products.
The manufacture of ATMPs should generally comply with the GMP and regulatory guidance for other
biologic and aseptic products. Therefore, many of the topics that are included in this document may overlap
with the conventional handling of biologics. However, the scarcity of comprehensive technical guidance and
common questions from the industry seeking help to understand how various requirements apply to ATMP
processes necessitate this guidance exhaustively addressing those design, control, and monitoring
requirements, even when those requirements overlap with conventional processing.
As technology advances and regulatory expectations for continuous process improvement continue to grow,
pressures may increase within the industry to further minimize the risk of our processes by moving to
greater containment, greater sterility assurance, and reduction of personnel involved in the process. This is
not always possible with the current state of the art for these novel processes. Hence, this PtC addresses the
common technologies employed today and presents the risk-benefit analysis for each so that manufacturers
can realistically assess their current process while simultaneously understanding alternate designs and
controls that may be available for implementation.
This document is organized into topics and various subtopics that address the questions raised on the subject
of ATMP manufacture. Each question first presents an overall recommendation, which may be followed by
a rationale for the recommendation and/or appropriate references for further reading.

Topic 1. Risk Management
Q 1.1. How can a risk assessment be created for an ATMP facility?
Recommendation
The manufacture of any product, including an ATMP, necessarily entails some degree of risk. The degree of
risk often correlates with such factors as the number of manual interventions, the type of environment in
which those interventions occur, and the open or closed nature of the process. For example, autologous
therapies produced in small batches (small-scale) often have a higher risk per unit produced when manual
manipulations are numerous. The facility design element of a formal facility risk assessment should always
be included as part of the Design Review process in the organization, but it is even more important when
processes have varying levels of risk. Based on the process design principles and nature of the work to be
performed in the facility, the organization can define the risk profile of the facility and work to mitigate
those risks through appropriate design controls associated with the equipment (e.g., isolators, BSCs) and
appropriate process improvements (e.g., single-use systems, closed processing, automation, improved
intervention design). As part of the same facility risk assessment, design controls can also be considered to
manage risks related to biological hazards.
Facility risk assessment should be undertaken by an interdisciplinary team and should be guided by a risk-
assessment facilitator of appropriate experience along with a scribe to record the risk assessment. The scope
of the facility risk assessment should include both facility and equipment design and selection, as well as
product, personnel, and environmental risk categories. Including personnel and environmental safety aspects
in the risk assessment is recommended as it is likely that safety-related controls could also have an impact
on product quality. Control measures for one risk category should be assessed for impact on other risk
categories. For example, the use of a virus in cell therapy manufacturing may pose health risks to personnel
while facilities producing the viral vectors have additional safety considerations due to the active replication
of viral vectors, albeit in a controlled process and environment. Where applicable, specialized safety
personnel, such as those with biosafety, toxicology, microbiology, and virology expertise, should be
involved in the performance of the risk assessment and in the design of the mitigations of the identified
risks.
Facility risk assessments should consider, but are not limited to, the following elements:
1. Material
a. Presence of adventitious agents (e.g., potential presence of replication competent agents).
b. Material handling and transfer into and out of critical process areas (e.g., appropriate design
and classification of pass-through containers, surface decontamination/disinfection during
transfer of frozen materials, transfer of sterile parts/consumables)

=== PAGE 12 ===
c. Controls and material-flow requirements when viruses, cellular materials, or genetically
modified organisms (GMOs) are present in the facility (including raw material, product,
sample, and waste flows)
d. Incoming control of materials for quality, microorganisms, and adventitious agents (e.g.,
vendor qualification, vendor audit, vendor certification, in-house sampling and testing,
controls at loading dock, warehouse)
2. Facility and equipment
a. Equipment, material, and waste flow (e.g., material or equipment moving to a higher-grade
area, cross-contamination due to non-unidirectional flow)
b. Personnel flow (e.g., cross-contamination risk due to personnel working or supporting
different batches or different products at the same time)
c. Cleanability of equipment and facility, including contact materials (e.g., selection of
materials of construction that are nonadditive, non-absorptive, or non-reactive, including
primary equipment, gaskets, filters, and selection of product-/process-compatible
lubricants)
d. Need for dedicated, segregated, self-contained areas and separation by time (e.g.,
manufacturing process using infectious viral vectors)
e. Containment adequacy (e.g., ability to contain potential aerosol release, examination of
open processing for possible routes of contamination)
f. Radiation shielding (e.g., use of x-ray irradiation equipment in process may require
additional consideration directed by local regulations)
g. Aspects of commissioning, qualification, maintenance, and environmental monitoring
(EM) (e.g., handling of pass-through containers, process gases)
3. Multiproduct processing (e.g., handling multiple batches of product and/or multiple products
simultaneously to ensure chain of identity to avoid mix-ups, limit risks of cross-contamination,
prevent processing errors, and ensure process adherence)
a. Pressure-cascade design, which meets the containment design and aseptic processing
requirements for all materials, especially highly potent materials and the appropriate
controls for transitioning from area to area (e.g., air lock and pass-throughs with
appropriate interlocks and purge times before opening to other zones or classifications)
b. Utilities that ensure appropriate quality, reduce risk of contamination, ensure adequacy of
supply and appropriate control (e.g., steam, process gases, power, clean compressed air,
instrument air, heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC), water for injection (WFI)
and/or purified water, liquid nitrogen, carbon dioxide)
4. Cleaning, decontamination, disinfection, sanitization, and sterilization
a. Equipment and facility cleaning/decontamination design (e.g., design of automated
decontamination, cell-virus inactivation process)
b. Waste decontamination (e.g., solid or liquid, in-process or waste treatment system)
c. Accident and spill response, decontamination, and cleaning
d. Equipment and material disinfection, sanitization, and sterilization
5. Airflow design
a. Airflow within the facility design should consider the acceptability of recirculation of air
within the facility or whether single-pass air (i.e., no recirculation) is required
b. Zone control within the HVAC air-handling units should consider separation by cleanliness
and/or by process
c. Differential pressure management to ensure appropriate containment and/or cleanliness

=== PAGE 13 ===
6. External environment
a. Impact of activities performed in the facility on the external environment (e.g., waste
treatment requirement, construction)
b. Seasonal changes of incoming air and source water, where applicable, to ensure that
adequate pretreatment is included in the utility system design
c. Pest control measures
Rationale
A facility risk assessment can help determine the most appropriate design of facilities and selection of
equipment. In particular, it is important to consider not only quality risks but also environmental and safety
risks. Examples of how hazards can affect both safety and quality aspects include, but are not limited to:
1. Certification and qualification test for a BSC covers product, personnel, and environmental risk but
placement and layout of BSC should also be verified according to manufacturer and/or standard
requirements (e.g., NSF/ANSI 49). The layout will then likely impact personnel, material, sample,
and waste flows and potentially affect the cross-contamination control strategy. If the risk cannot be
adequately controlled with the use of a BSC, a closed system or isolators should be used.
2. Waste treatment is part of the biological risk management in the facility, and the type of waste
treatment will impact personnel and material flow and potentially affect the cross-contamination
control strategy.
3. Facility decontamination processes may leave residues that subsequently impact product quality and
patient safety but may also represent a risk to operator safety.
4. Cross-contamination between manufacturing areas of different products (e.g., live vs. inactivated
microorganisms) will impact product quality and patient safety, as well as put operators at risk.
5. Design requirements for BSL containment of GMOs or other highly potent products may conflict
with aseptic processing requirements. For example, a negative-pressure cascade may be
recommended to contain GMO within the facility, but aseptic processes rely on a positive-pressure
cascade to prevent contamination from entering the facility.

Q 1.2. How does the ATMP risk assessment determine the biosafety level
requirement?
Recommendation
A list of biological materials, including GMOs, should be maintained and reviewed regularly against local
requirements as part of a risk management program. The list serves as a basis for the organization to
determine the risk group classification of each material, and the risk group is determined by the hazard
posed by the biological agents and toxins (if any) used in the manufacturing process. Although risk group
classification depends on localities, the definition is generally in line with those from the World Health
Organization (WHO) and U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) (4, 5):
• Risk Group 1 (no or low individual and community risk)
̶ A microorganism that is unlikely to cause human or animal disease.
• Risk Group 2 (moderate individual risk, low community risk)
̶ A pathogen that can cause human or animal disease but is unlikely to be a serious hazard to
laboratory personnel, the community, livestock, or the environment. Laboratory exposures
may cause serious infection, but effective treatment and preventive measures are available,
and the risk of the spread of infection is limited.
• Risk Group 3 (high individual risk, low community risk)
̶ A pathogen that usually causes serious human or animal diseases but does not ordinarily
spread from one infected individual to another. Effective treatment and preventive
measures are available (not considered in this document).
• Risk Group 4 (high individual and community risk)
̶ A pathogen that usually causes serious human or animal disease and that can be readily
transmitted from one individual to another, directly or indirectly. Effective treatment and
preventive measures are not usually available (not considered in this document).
Upon understanding the risk group classification of the materials involved in the product, the organization
should consult with the local environmental health and safety regulatory bodies and the inspecting
regulatory bodies of the countries in which the product is licensed to determine the BSL requirements for
the manufacturing facility. The organization can then determine the controls required to manage the risk
using facility risk assessment. Typically, risk group classification and control requirements are heightened
for the following situations:
• Novel biological materials (e.g., new gene insertion or deletion, novel virus)
• Biological material that is replication competent as opposed to biological material known to be no
longer capable of replication
• Viral vectors and their risk to the operator and cross-contamination
The organization should also consider the requirements related to the use of GMOs. Many countries
maintain specific facility and procedural requirements for facility-handling of GMOs, and the containment
requirements are typically applied in addition to the typical pharmaceutical facility cleanliness requirements.
Risk management principles should be applied when determining the control points. The risk assessment
and mitigations should aim to minimize the risk to as low as is reasonably practical.

=== PAGE 15 ===
Current work related to the cell and gene therapy industry generally uses materials that are classified as Risk
Group 1 or Risk Group 2 and are rarely in the higher classification (e.g., 3 or 4).
Discussing the BSL requirements with the applicable regulatory bodies as early as possible is recommended
to ensure regulatory concerns are addressed in the facility design stage.
Rationale
Process and product knowledge are important factors to enable the application of science and a risk-based
approach to determine what controls to apply to the manufacturing facility.

Topic 2. Facilities
Q 2.1. How do you design facility process flow to minimize extraneous contamination
and cross-contamination and prevent errors in an ATMP facility?
Recommendation
Facility design plays an essential role in minimizing contamination, cross-contamination, and prevention of
errors. For this purpose, the possible risk factors that can lead to contamination and cross-contamination
should be evaluated and defined in the CCS document.
Contamination and cross-contamination occur through several different vectors, which include but are not
limited to:
Physical
1. Contaminated packaging or containers – For example, a packaging format of any raw or starting
materials used in the process that cannot be adequately decontaminated upon transfer for use.
2. Mechanical transfers – For example, spillages of materials, through improperly sealed transfer
devices, or through risks such as the “ring of concern,” which is an area on a rapid transfer port that
cannot be adequately disinfected and may be exposed during transfer procedures if not addressed.
3. Personnel – For example, contamination of clothing from non-process-dedicated clothing regimes.
4. Waste processes.
5. Airborne sources – For example, Aerosols, failure of air-handling units in the Grade A
environment, or volatile constituents of contaminated waste.
In order to minimize these risks, a containment strategy should be developed from the inside-out (from
critical operations to less-critical operations) based on a risk assessment. The containment strategy and CCS
should identify any critical manufacturing processes, paying particular attention to higher-risk operations.
Based on the risk related to the primary protection and containment measures, a secondary means of
protection and containment should be planned, such as the personnel air lock (PAL) and material air lock
(MAL) pressure cascades (e.g., from production room to air lock and from air lock to the corridor) (see
Figure 4.3-1). Personnel, product, sample, and waste paths should also be considered to avoid possible
contamination in other areas and to minimize the risk of cross-contamination.
Important Considerations
1. Is the overall process closed, such as a closed process in a BSC or an open process in a closed
isolator? Does it remain closed throughout the whole procedure? If not, can a fully closed process
be integrated?

=== PAGE 17 ===
a. Any process which can be performed closed should be designed and executed closed to
minimize the risks associated with open processes.
b. Items required within the Grade A space (e.g., tools, equipment) should be sterilized. When
sterilization is not possible, the requirement for the continued use of that item should be
evaluated and, when deemed necessary, the highest possible level of surface disinfection
should be pursued with appropriate controls and monitoring and justified within the CCS to
ensure risk to the process is minimized.
c. Intrinsic sterile connectors should be used, when possible, to avoid multiple aseptic
connections.
2. How are transfers in and out of the BSC or isolator performed? Do they have closed transfers via
rapid transfer ports (RTP) or within a MAL where an automated surface decontamination is
executed?
3. Isolators or BSCs should use appropriate technologies like RTPs for transferring sterilized or
disinfected material to reduce the risk of physical transfer of contaminants.
4. How is the pharmaceutical product handled inside the BSC or isolator? Does the handling pose a
risk of contamination and, if so, how can this risk be eliminated?
a. First-air protection of any open-handled processes inside the BSC or isolator.
b. Greatest possible separation of personnel from the process through the use of barriers and
sterile implements.
5. Can the packaging material for the pharmaceutical product be externally contaminated before
leaving the isolator? If so, what engineering controls could be used to eliminate this contamination?
a. The use of air locks compatible with vapor phase hydrogen peroxide (VPHP) or other
appropriate decontamination/inactivation should be used, if possible. Otherwise, an RTP or
other closed containment system should be used to transfer the material to a safe area where
the decontamination/ inactivation takes place.
6. What are the methods to manage pharmaceutical-product residues or waste (e.g., liquid waste, solid
waste)?
a. For processes requiring removal of waste during the operation, the waste should be handled
in a contained manner, through a separate pathway from product or product samples, so
that it does not become a source of contamination.
b. If the product exit is not separate from the waste exit, the removal-of-waste operation
should be performed in a contained manner at a dedicated time separate from the product
manufacture.
7. How will the processing area be cleaned to eliminate the risk of cross-contamination?
a. Any inactivation step of residual pharmaceutical product should be performed before
cleaning.
8. Are single use closed systems used? How are cross-contamination risks considered?
a. Contamination can occur at the interfaces of closed single-use systems, such as single-use
bioreactors or single-use bags, both during the process (e.g., during additions or sampling)
and when they are dismantled if the systems and their interfaces are not designed
appropriately.
b. The tightness/integrity should be tested and validated for the single-use system. This
includes both vendor certifications and potentially in-house testing when that testing can be
performed in a manner that is safe for the process, operator, and environment.

=== PAGE 18 ===
9. How will process leaks and spills be handled and addressed?
a. Consider the containment measures to be taken to stop and prevent the spread of the
leak/spill contamination. Ensure that there is adequate protection for air returns that are
near the affected leak/spill area.
b. Establish the necessary and appropriate inactivation/decontamination steps which do not
put product or packaging at risk and create tools that help operators determine what steps
should be performed as soon as the leak/spill occurs and what can wait until after
processing has concluded. (See Item 6.)
c. Consider the necessary handling of any process equipment, containers, or packaging that
may have become contaminated on the outside. (See Item 4.)
d. Establish waste-handling procedures for any materials that were used to contain, inactivate,
or clean up the leak/spill. (See Item 5.)
e. Establish procedures for personnel involved in the remediation of the leak/spill to ensure
gloves or gowning that may have been impacted by the leak/spill or the clean-up are
prevented from any additional spread of contamination (e.g., the requirement to change
affected gowning).
f. Establish deviation and investigation procedures to assess the product/process during which
the leak/spill occurred.
Additionally, to reduce the risk of intrinsic particulate contamination and maintain low mold in support
areas, such as supply and return corridors, kitting of consumables should be performed in, at minimum, an
ISO-8 cleanroom or controlled non-classified area to prevent introduction of particulate matter from the
warehouse. Warehouses should be maintained free of cardboard and paperboard by transferring materials
into totes prior to storage. This lower-particulate warehouse for materials destined for Grade B cell-
processing suites should be separated from the main warehouse where non-product-contact items are stored
separately by rollup doors, ideally with air-wash curtains.
All of these points and the risks they involve should be considered in a CCS document in relation to cross-
contamination.
Rationale
An appropriate closed process, with a cleaning and inactivation strategy, minimizes the chance of
extraneous contamination and cross-contamination of the product and contamination of surrounding areas
and personnel. Furthermore, the risk of possible cross-contamination from the tertiary to the remainder of
the facility will also be minimized. Well-documented procedures and training will also contribute to the
prevention of cross-contamination.

Q 2.2. What should be considered for open and closed systems to achieve the required
product and operator protection for ATMPs?
Recommendation
The use of closed systems offers distinct advantages. They are often used to scale out processes, once the
process has been developed, and can often be performed appropriately within either a BSC or an isolator
(although an isolator offers greater protection in the event of improper handling, spills, or leaks). The
integrity of the closed system ensures a sterile environment. Hybrid or open processes within a closed
isolator offer protection of both the product and the operator, providing the process steps and manipulations
are performed correctly. Open processes within an open BSC require further implementation of mitigation
measures to ensure no extraneous contamination or cross-contamination occurs.
Open System
An open system is one where the material is exposed to the manufacturing environment for even a short
time. Due to this exposure, the environment is required to be of Grade A cleanliness. The manufacturing
environment is a total sum of the events that occur during the process and any particulate or bioburden,
pyrogenic, or viral contamination present in the area has the potential to contaminate the product. Therefore,
each and every action using an open system has the potential to be the vector that carries the contamination.
This is why the environmental controls and monitoring, movement of the operators, effectiveness of
material transfers, and sanitization steps are of key importance.
Open systems are often used for early-phase/proof-of-concept trials when the required manipulations are
still being defined and refined.
Closed System
A closed system is hermetically sealed from the manufacturing environment inside which the product
pathway is considered sterile. Examples include single-use systems that have been gamma-irradiated that
use intrinsic sterile connectors.
Systems are only considered closed when they have been integrity-tested and have been demonstrated to be
hermetically sealed. Fully closed systems can be deployed and used in a Grade C environment if adequate
control measures are implemented to avoid the risk of contamination.
Hybrid System
Hybrid processes, such as those requiring an open addition into a closed system, or that are open during
initial assembly but are closed thereafter, are required to be performed within a Grade A cleanroom with
Grade B background if using a BSC, or within Grade D background when using a closed isolator
(depending on the CCS requirements). The increased cleanliness requirement for the surrounding
environment is due to the fact that hybrid processes may create the opportunity for exposure of the product
pathway to the environment.

Q 2.3. What is common practice related to the pressure concepts for maintaining
classification and containment for ATMPs?
Recommendation
Regulations and Interpretations
The leading regulatory standards for aseptic manufacturing include:
• FDA Guidance for Industry: Sterile Drug Products Produced by Aseptic Processing–Current Good
Manufacturing Practice
• European Union (EU) Annex 1: Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal Products, EudraLex – Volume 4
– EU Guidelines for Good Manufacturing Practice for Medicinal Products for Human and
Veterinary Use
These guidances clearly state that pressure differentials for supporting clean areas must be maintained in
order to keep sterile processing areas clean: that is, free from any contaminant (viable or nonviable).
Background Environment
Aseptic core operations in isolators or in BSCs are conducted in Grade A areas and, therefore, require
pressure cascades to the background environment. In the case of a BSC, the surrounding external
environment is a minimum of Grade B. For an isolator operation, the external environment is a minimum of
Grade C for open isolators when they are connected with small openings for a semi-continuous or
continuous material transfer in/out (Figure 2.3-1), or Grade D for a closed isolator (Figure 2.3-2, Figure
4.2-1, and Figure 4.2-3).
Cleanrooms are designed to maintain positive pressure, preventing "unclean" (contaminated) air from
flowing inside. The idea is to ensure that filtered air always flows from the cleanest to less-clean spaces (i.e.,
a pressure cascade). EU GMP Annex 1 states that a positive pressure differential of a minimum of
10 Pascals should be maintained between each adjacent space (1). In a multichambered cleanroom, for
example, the cleanest room is kept at the highest pressure. Pressure levels are set so that the cleanest air
flows into spaces with less-clean air (e.g., air locks that act as a bubble or as a sink). Thus, multiple pressure

Q 2.4. What should be considered when designing cleanrooms, HVAC, air locks, and
corridors for handling products such as viral vectors in an ATMP facility?
Recommendation
When designing a production site for ATMPs, a variety of factors should be considered, including, but not
limited to the required process, protection of the product, prevention of cross-contamination, and protection
of operators. The potency of the manufactured product and the inherent risk associated with manufacturing
components (e.g., viral vectors) should be considered. Good engineering design practices also consider such
lifecycle factors as initial installation, operation, maintenance/calibration, and eventual replacement and
retirement strategies. In addition to including them in the engineering design features within the facility,
these factors should also be part of a comprehensive CCS for operations, monitoring, and maintenance of
the facility. The majority of these elements are not only requirements for ATMP production, but they are
also applicable to traditional biotechnology or traditional cleanroom operations. Some essential elements
include:
Design
• Fulfilling a minimum of a Grade D background (closed isolator) or Grade B background (BSC)
with appropriate graded air locks for material and personnel.
• Designing all cleanrooms to be easily cleaned and disinfected; however, as some of the ATMPs
require BSL-1 or higher for manufacturing, the rooms design should take into consideration the
specific procedures for cleaning and/or inactivation required by the specific BSL.
• Designing all equipment and room features to be hygienic and engineered to withstand cleaning and
disinfecting/inactivation agents used in the facility. For this reason, cleaning, sanitizing, and
inactivation procedures and chemical compatibility should be considered early in the design of the
manufacturing facility.
• Installing appropriate methods (e.g., autoclaves), based on the required BSL, to
deactivate/decontaminate waste or leftover raw materials/products before leaving the area adjacent
to the filling cleanroom.
• Consider locating emergency response equipment (e.g., emergency shower, eye shower) outside the
cleanroom, according to the required potency or BSL, while ensuring there are no open drains
located within the classified environments.
HVAC
• Designing the HVAC system to be appropriate for the product potency and/or the required BSL.
• Using high-efficiency particulate air (HEPA) filtration to obtain the required cleanroom
classification.
• Designing the air supply for each space to promote appropriate air pressure differentials between
rooms of varying classification or based on required operations (e.g., for maintenance of cleanliness
or containment).
• Designing the installation and replacement of HEPA filters so they can be easily maintained in a
way that minimizes cross-contamination and permits periodic integrity testing.
Air Locks
• Positioning air locks to separate spaces of different classifications and/or to avoid spreading
contaminants in adjacent areas such as corridors. Designing PALs and changing rooms so that the
exiting operators do not contaminate the entering operators (i.e., providing separate routes of entry
and exit).

• Separating MALs from PALs.
• Designing MALs to allow for decontamination procedures within their environment to permit the
movement of materials from a lower grade to the higher grade, or between a process space and a

• Restricting personnel from crossing through a MAL to enter the processing cleanrooms or corridors.

Q 2.5. What environmental risk factors are to be considered outside of the
manufacturing facility (e.g., neighborhood of the manufacturing facility, hospital, clinic,
home setting) when working with highly potent or genetically modified organisms?
Recommendation
Highly potent materials and GMOs are considered major environmental risk factors in facility design since
there are often additional local regulatory requirements to prevent unwanted release of such materials to the
environment. The organization should consult local regulatory requirements, including assessing the
biological materials handled in the facility against such regulations, including GMOs where appropriate.
In addition, the organization should work with the facility that is the point of administration to the patient
(e.g., hospital) to determine any containment control requirements (e.g., administration precautions, patient
waste, hospital waste). A discussion with the local regulatory authorities is recommended since ATMP
technology is developing rapidly, and novel technology may result in novel products with novel risks.
Research laboratories are familiar with the use of novel technology, and biosafety risk is typically managed
through the oversight of an Institutional Biosafety Committee (IBC). The use of ATMPs with biosafety
concerns in healthcare settings has resulted in the extension of the IBC’s role into patient administration
settings, which is not within the traditional purview of conventional IBCs.
With regards to the risk associated with the use of highly potent or gene therapy products in a healthcare
facility, a contact precaution over a 30-day period post-product administration, including waste handling, is
recommended. Similarly, when the patient has returned home, their families are recommended to take
similar precautions for the identical duration (6). Disinfection and cleaning procedures, including the
relevant disinfectant contact time, must be shown to be appropriate for the organisms and/or materials
involved in the ATMP within the post-administration setting (e.g., the home).
One particular risk associated with the use of ATMPs in healthcare settings is related to the ability to ensure
an appropriate level of handling competency. Healthcare settings tend to include the use of floating staff
where the personnel assigned are not always involved in one particular activity. Furthermore, the training of
healthcare personnel (e.g., physician, nurse) is managed differently than for production workers; for
example, the use of a learning management system may not be common in a healthcare setting and,
therefore, tracking specific training competency may be difficult.
Rationale
Safety concerns for highly potent materials or GMOs must be addressed regardless of the setting in order to
protect patients, healthcare workers, home caregivers, and others who may come into contact with the
patient. The evolving nature of ATMPs means that the systems designed to control these extended risks are
not fully mature. Therefore, it is incumbent upon the manufacturer to provide appropriate guidance for the
necessary controls.

Topic 4. Process Equipment
Q 4.1. When choosing a biological safety cabinet for the manufacture of ATMPs, what
factors are important?
Recommendation
A BSC is flexible in its use, although it typically does not have the same levels of integrated control and
monitoring systems as an isolator. Instead, it relies on operators to manually decontaminate the Grade A
space, maintain it through good aseptic practice, and appropriately monitor the environment. This increased
reliance upon the operator means that operations should be carefully planned with an understanding of the
barrier system limitations of the BSC and should incorporate appropriate risk mitigations to safeguard the
aseptic operations. According to EU GMP Annex 1, the BSC is not classified as barrier technology.
The room environment where the BSC is located should support the maintenance of the Grade A
environment within the operational zone for the barrier system in use. This dictates the need for a Grade B
environment to surround the BSC.
Biosafety Cabinets
BSCs are widely used in the manufacturing of ATMPs as they are easy to install and maintain. Further,
BSCs generally occupy a modest footprint that permits highly flexible operations within the enclosure due
to the ease of access to the working area. BSCs allow access to the inner working zone via an open sash.
Note: BSC - Class III are the sole exception to the open-sash design. (Further descriptions of the different
classes are provided in this section.)
Surfaces inside BSCs are typically cleaned and disinfected manually, which requires intensive training of
operators to validate this procedure. Hence, BSCs require comprehensive QRM to ensure maintenance of
aseptic conditions during all activities within the critical zone.
The open-sash operation of most BSC designs provides direct access by the personnel to the ATMP
products being handled. Consequently, due to the exposure of personnel gowning to the products being
worked within the BSC work zone, personnel gowning must support aseptic operations. The increased
gowning requirements, as compared to those required for isolators, are more expensive and translate into a
more complex and costlier EM program. Material transfer into the BSC poses a high risk, as transfers are
done through the open sash, and materials are further decontaminated upon entry. The continuously open
sash, with forearms and hands penetrating the opening to perform the manipulations, and the risk of airflow
disturbance or air mixing during those manual operations contributes to the comparatively higher risk of
BSCs.

The three main classes of BSCs are:
Class I BSC
Class I cabinets provide personnel and environmental protection by filtering air through a HEPA filter
before it is exhausted. These cabinets do not protect the product from potential contamination and are
suitable for general manipulations of low-to-moderate risk agents where product sterility is not a priority.
Class I BSCs should never be used for ATMP-manufacturing activities.
Class II BSC
Class II cabinets are designed to protect the product, personnel, and environment, making them acceptable
for handling ATMPs with a requirement for aseptic conditions. There are multiple subtypes of Class II
BSCs, each with different operating conditions that dictates their suitability for the production of different
classes of ATMP (see Figure 4.1-2 below).
Class II Type A1 BSC
Often used in clinical and research laboratories for standard microbiological work with no chemical
evaporation. Type A1 cabinets maintain a minimum inflow velocity of 75 feet per minute and recirculate
70% of the air while exhausting 30% outdoors after filtration. They are not suitable for handling volatile or
toxic chemicals. Type A1 BSCs are usually only used in development laboratories where the ATMP will not
be administered to a human.
Class II Type A2 BSC
Similar to Type A1 BSCs but with an increased inflow velocity of 100 feet per minute, enhancing
containment. Type A2 cabinets can handle minute amounts of volatile chemicals if vented with an
appropriate thimble (canopy) connection to the facility’s HVAC system.

=== PAGE 32 ===
Class II Type B1 BSC
Designed for work that involves minute quantities of toxic chemicals and radionuclides, providing these
operations occur toward the rear of the work area and are exhausted directly outside. Type B1 maintains a
balance where 70% of the air is exhausted and 30% is recirculated, both through HEPA filters.
Class II Type B2 BSC
Also known as total exhaust cabinets, these are suitable for handling significant quantities of toxic chemicals
and radio nuclides. They maintain a minimum inflow of 100 feet per minute and exhaust 100% of the air.
All inflow and downflow air is removed via the ducts out of the building, allowing no recirculation within
the cabinet.
Class II Type C1 BSC
A versatile cabinet that functions under both Type A and Type B conditions with a switch to change
between modes depending on the chemicals used. This type of BSC provides flexibility in laboratories that
handle both biological samples and chemicals, adjusting the exhaust accordingly.
Class III BSC
Offering the highest level of protection, Class III cabinets are gas-tight enclosures used with high-risk
microbiological agents and hazardous chemicals. Materials are manipulated through attached gloves.

vaporized or nebulized H O ).

• Controlled material transfer in and out of the isolator system through RTPs or MALs with
automated, validated decontamination cycles (see Figure 4.2-1).
• Isolation of the pharmaceutical product within a small, closed environment that avoids spreading
highly potent pharmaceutical products to surrounding areas.
• Higher BSL requirements can be accommodated due to the closed environment, automated
decontamination, and the possibility of inactivation.
• More sustainable due to lower surrounding cleanroom environment (Grade D), which means less
room EM, less gowning, and less related training.
Constraints of Isolators
• Isolators require careful design to ensure that all required aspects of the process can be executed
through the designated glove ports and to verify that all required material transfers can occur
through the designated RTPs or MALs.
• Isolators may, in some circumstances, be less flexible in terms of significant process or equipment
changes.
• Isolators require routine leak-testing of the chamber(s) and integrity-testing of the gloves, sleeves,
and glove ports to ensure that the Grade A environment is maintained.

Operational Comparison of Biosafety Cabinets and Isolators
BSCs and isolators are typically equipped with a filter of at least a HEPA classification. The type of HEPA
filter installed is expected, at a minimum, to have particulate removal of 99.99% when tested, in accordance
with internationally acceptable standards. The filtration capability of HEPA filters in BSCs and isolators
should provide a Grade A operating work zone.
Since a BSC is operated via an open sash compared with the closed operating mode of an isolator, there is a
greater need to manage particulates and contaminants through good aseptic practice. Products in the work
zone of the BSC may be exposed to particulates from gowning worn by personnel since the personnel are
required to manipulate the products directly via gloved hands.
In contrast, an isolator provides a closed environment where personnel indirectly access the work zone
through integrated gloves. The use of gloves attached to the isolator may make product-handling
inconvenient (i.e., due to the loss of some physical dexterity) in an isolator compared to a BSC. However,
indirect access not only protects the product and process but also protects the operator as there is no risk of
transfer of contamination to the operator’s gowning.
Production within isolators also reduces the gowning requirement for operators since they may be gowned
in accordance with the requirements of the background environment. This means operators working with
isolators may be gowned for Grade D (or C) operations, while operators working with BSC must gown for
Grade A operations. The lower gowning requirements can save some expense in training, gowning, time,
and EM, making the isolator more sustainable over the long term.
The surface cleaning and decontamination/disinfection in a BSC is done manually using sterile wipes. In an
isolator, the cleaning is performed manually while the decontamination is performed with an integrated,
automated, and validated technology such as vaporized or nebulized H O or another decontamination
2 2
agent.
Material transfers in a BSC are performed with the open sash, as one operator transfers items to the operator
working in the BSC. During this process, it is important to avoid contamination of the surrounding area with
the material transferred through the open sash by careful decontamination of external surfaces and/or
removal of packaging. Transfer technologies attached to the BSC, such as RTPs, can provide lower-risk
transfers. Isolators use transfer systems like MALs and/or RTPs to provide a safe and rapid transfer after a
vaporized or sprayed decontamination agent cycle. The exposure of the decontamination agent to the
materials being transferred should be investigated for chemical and penetration capability.
According to EU GMP Annex 1, the BSC is not classified as a barrier technology, whereas isolators are.

Q 5.1. What is important to consider in a mock-up for biosafety cabinets and isolators?
Recommendation
A mock-up for the BSC or isolator at the vendor’s site should be built to ensure that all reasonably
practicable steps have been taken to ensure good aseptic practice when performing the necessary motions
for the process, ergonomics for the operators, and the ability to perform the required maintenance. With an
isolator system, the vendor normally performs a mock-up with wood, cardboard, or similar rigid materials
because, after the isolator system is installed, the glove position can only be changed with a significant effort
and cost. With BSCs, the designs are normally standard and vary only with the size of the BSC.
Nevertheless, it is important to ensure that the process can be performed with good aseptic technique within
the size constraints of the selected BSC, and so it might be most appropriate to perform with a mock-up (or
an actual unit) of the intended size.
The mock-up exercise should consider the following:
• Working position, including whether the operator will be seated or standing.
• Positions of all equipment within the Grade A area.
• Positions of all components and materials including hangers, trays, or stands required to support
materials.
• Position of process waste.
• Location for all product-contact tools before and during the process, with special consideration for
ensuring that they are continuously bathed in first air and are not touching any potentially
contaminated surface at any time.
• Process sequence/flow and all required manipulations and interventions.
• Location of critical surfaces relative to first air and required operator movements.
• Locations of continuous viable and nonviable sampling in the Grade A area to accurately capture
any activity within the working position and to ensure that EM materials, where required, can be
introduced and placed within the working zone without breaking first air over the process or process
equipment.
• Operator hand/arm/glove movements throughout when considering the locations of the working
position to ensure the operator’s hand/arm/glove does not move over critical surfaces.
• All required manipulations and variations in setup, including loading and unloading of any
materials from equipment, air locks, or RTP, should be tested by both right- and left-handed
individuals, and the testing should include personnel of varying heights and arm lengths to ensure
that the configuration will be suitable for a wide range of operators performing the process.
• Frequency and duration of operations.
• Weight/force for each action.
• Setup and performance of cleaning and disinfection actions.

=== PAGE 44 ===
For the mock-up, it is highly recommended to involve the operator(s) responsible for handling the
equipment in routine production. The mock-up process should be conducted by a minimum of two trained
operators so the second person can observe and record actions or violations that may not be apparent to the
person conducting the process. The results from the mock-up should be documented.
The mock-up may be performed using simplified representations of tools, materials, and equipment
manufactured from basic materials to aid in rapid, low-cost iteration. This use of a low-cost model is
especially worthwhile during the initial process or initial isolator/BSC design and layout when there may be
multiple iterations of the design. However, for the final mock-up testing, it is important to ensure that the
selected representations are adequate to challenge the weight/force that may be required for the actual
manipulations.
Besides rigid mock-up studies, computerized mock-ups and virtual-reality mock-ups can also support the
physical mock-up (see Figure 5.1-1, Figure 5.1-2, and Figure 5.1-3). Physical, computerized, or virtual
mock-ups are often retained by the operating company in order to be used to test new process movements or
interventions before incorporating them into the process or to provide preliminary training to new operators.
As a consequence, cost spent on making the model realistic can be an investment in the process maintenance
over time.

Q 5.2. Are there any differences in the frequency of chamber leak-integrity testing
procedures between an isolator and a biosafety cabinet?
Recommendation
For isolators, the leak test should be performed frequently with the interval established, based on the
frequency of intended operations in an appropriate risk assessment (1, 7-10).
The leak-testing frequency for isolators should not be limited to periodic evaluation, but rather should
include such situations as:
• After changing gloves and sleeves (documented in the glove change management protocol)
• Following any maintenance or repair that has breached the sealed structure
• Supporting failure investigations, especially those involving viral and/or bioburden contaminations
PDA Points to Consider for the Aseptic Processing of Sterile Pharmaceutical Products in Isolators (2020)
recommends that the leak-testing (integrity) be performed at defined periods, at a minimum, at the
beginning and end of each batch or campaign and following any intervention that would affect the integrity
of the unit (10).
The testing procedures performed for leak integrity in isolators are described in Annex E.3 Quantitative leak
testing of ISO EN 14644-7. Table E.1 in ISO 14644-7 indicates the oxygen method (only for negative
pressure), the pressure-change method, or the Parjo method (very rarely used; mainly in atomic industries)
as being appropriate for very tight ISO containment of Class I devices (11). In addition to a tightness
classification, ISO 10648-2 describes three different methods for leak-testing negative-pressure, rigid-wall
separative devices as the oxygen method, the pressure-changes method, and the constant-pressure method
(12). The oxygen, pressure-change, and constant-pressure methods are listed as appropriate test methods for
isolators with ISO containment Class III. Apart from other methods, PDA Technical Report No. 34: Design
and Validation of Isolator Systems for the Manufacturing and Testing of Health Care Products also
suggests leak-testing using a tracer gas, in which a tracer gas is released inside the isolator, and testing for
the presence of the gas is performed outside the isolator (9).
As BSCs are an open system, a leak test cannot be performed before each batch as is done with an isolator
system. A containment test method, as shown in Figure 5.3-1 and Figure 5.3-2, could be used instead of the
leak test. This testing is typically performed upon first commissioning and validation for use and may be
repeated periodically (e.g., annually or biannually) to check on the continued performance of the system, or
when required for significant investigations into the system’s operation.

Q 5.3. What are the similarities and differences between a biosafety cabinet and an
isolator in the objective, procedure, documentation, and expectations of an airflow
visualization (smoke study)?
Recommendation
The airflow visualization, or smoke study, is a method used to aid in the evaluation of the CGMP-required
airflow generated by a BSC, isolator system, or containment enclosure. Common criteria for these studies
commonly include such elements as unidirectional airflow in Grade A areas and airflow around the
apertures between Grade A and lower-grade areas. Differences in the application of airflow visualization
arise as a result of the intended purpose of the equipment. For example, some systems are intended for the
protection of the operator from the product while other systems are meant for the protection of the product
from external contamination.
For isolator systems, the airflow is designed to ensure that the first air from the HEPA filter flows over the
sterile product and sterile equipment used in its manufacture. This is achieved by unidirectional airflow over
the product and associated critical surfaces. In isolator systems, the objective of the airflow visualization is
to verify that the airflows create a “sweeping action over and away from the product under dynamic
conditions” as defined in the FDA Guidance for Industry: Sterile Drug Products Produced by Aseptic
Processing—Current Good Manufacturing Practice, EU GMP Annex 1, PIC/S Annex 1: Manufacture of
Sterile Medicinal Products, and WHO TRS 1044 - Annex 2: WHO Good Manufacturing Practices for
Sterile Pharmaceutical Products (13-15).
The airflow visualization procedure (smoke study) demonstrates that the airflow meets the design
objectives. The study is focused on aiding in the visualization of the air as it moves down from the filter face
over and away from the critical product and material surfaces. It ensures there are no airflows towards the
product from sources that may generate particulates/contaminants (e.g., manual operations, moving machine
parts). The airflow visualization studies include visualization of flow at the following points:
• Downflow across the chamber/work zone at the working position.
• Airflow with sweeping action over and away from the product.
• Airflow around large objects that may generate turbulence.
• Airflow, for example, during manual operation, interventions, and transfers that demonstrate the
absence of turbulence on critical surfaces and that first air is not disturbed.
• Airflow during the processing of open units demonstrates that no air turbulence enters open units.
• Airflow at apertures between zones of differing pressure that demonstrates flow from the critical
area outward without reflux or entrainment.
Airflow visualization should be performed at rest and in operation, including manufacturing setup in Grade
A zones and during the performance of interventions. The studies should demonstrate that air does not
ingress from the lower-grade cleanroom/zone to a higher-grade cleanroom/zone.
For BSCs, the airflow visualization mandated by the regulatory design standards specifications still applies.
For example, NSF/ANSI 49 – 2019 Biosafety Cabinetry: Design, Construction, Performance, and Field
Certification requires the following airflow visualization be successfully performed at least annually (16):
• Airflow work zone along the centerline of the work surface is downward.
• Airflow along the entire perimeter of the sash at the outside of the cabinet is inward into the grill
before the work zone and does not intrude into the work zone.
• Airflow behind the sash, on top of the access opening, does not escape from the BSC.

=== PAGE 49 ===
• Airflow behind the window, at the sides and top of work area, is not leaking out of the BSC.
At all times, for visualization in either BSC or isolators, the actual setup of the process equipment, materials,
and product should match the intended operations. The airflow visualization testing should comply with the
requirements defined previously in this section and, in addition, should ensure that there are no obstructions
to airflow grills.
For BSCs as containment enclosures, the primary purpose is to ensure that any hazardous product material
liberated in the airflow is transported away from the operator to prevent the risk of harm. This airflow
visualization is used mainly for semi-open Class I or Class II BSCs, according to BS EN 12469
Biotechnology - Performance Criteria for Microbiological Safety Cabinets, and does not quantify the
potential contaminants. The procedure, therefore, demonstrates that the airflow meets the objective and is
focused on aiding visualization to ensure there are no airflows towards the cleanroom, surrounding
environment, and operator (17). This includes visualization of flow at the following points:
• Airflow around large objects that may generate turbulence.
• Airflow around continuous viable and nonviable sampling equipment.
• Airflow around hand movement and equipment while performing required operations.
• Airflow during interventions.
• Airflow remaining undisturbed by external airflow from the surroundings.
The documentation of the airflow visualization should be similar regardless of the objective of the study.
Visual recordings of the process should be made from as many angles as are necessary to capture the flow
(first air) at each critical point, including in dynamic conditions (i.e., when the system is in use). The FDA
and European Medicines Agency recommend that the documentation provide a detailed summary of what
equipment and supplies are present, the number of personnel present, and a list of all activities simulated.
In addition to airflow visualization studies, there are other methods that are commonly employed for BSC
that are nonvisible but use a variety of detectors in order to classify the performance of the system in
quantitative terms. These tests include, but are not limited to, a segregation test, KI-Discus (potassium
iodide) test, or other containment tests.
Unexpected airflows between clean zones can also be detected using the segregation test, according to Chap.
4.2.11 of ISO 14644-3:2019 Cleanrooms and Associated Controlled Environments, Part 3: Test Methods.
The lesser classified area is challenged with a defined concentration of aerosol particles, and the particle
concentration in the protected area is then measured. The difference between the two concentrations results
in the determination of a protection factor (18).
For hazardous applications in semi-open BSCs, the KI-Discus test method, according to EN 12469, is
routinely used as a containment test. This relatively static test uses small salt particles from potassium iodide
with a defined momentum to show the concentration difference between the interior of the BSC and exterior
zone in front of the BSC, expressed as a “protection factor” (see Figure 5.3-1).

Rationale

protected by the designed unidirectional airflow from adverse influences such as particle-generating

the intended process does not contribute additional risk to the environment through the disruption of airflow.

Q 5.4. What methods are used to maintain facility cascade/pressure stability?
Recommendation
Pressure cascades can be measured using two methods. The first method is to directly measure the pressure
difference between two adjacent areas where common areas exist. For ease of validation, it is recommended
that individual tubing be used between each high- and low-pressure sample-point location, although it is
also possible (albeit more complex) that the monitoring points be connected together via a manifold. The
advantage of this first method is a direct response to a pressure cascade change of state. The second method
is to measure the absolute pressure of each space and calculate the differential. The advantage of this
method is typically the use of fewer sensors and a wider view of the comparison of pressures between
multiple spaces across the facility. However, this advantage is offset by a wider error range for
measurement, as two sensors are required for the calculation of each differential. In either case, a loss of
local pressure would be identified (see Figure 5.4-1).

*[Figure 5.4-1 Shows the Pressure Difference Measurement Between the Isolator Chamber and]*

Above the Supply HEPA Filter
A suitable measurement interval for pressure readings should provide a balance between small variances
caused by internal fluctuations within a controlled space and the loss of control parameters, while enabling
the averaging of results accurately over longer periods. The process and use of the cleanrooms (e.g., entries,
exits, frequency of use, movements) should be used to determine the measurement and recording frequency
as this may be different throughout each zone within the facility.
If a pressure loss has been observed, the air-handling unit should react immediately to balance the pressure
loss. Pressure loss should be recorded, and an alarm should signal. The reason for the pressure loss must be
investigated. Alarm signals should be timed to avoid signaling for routine operational events such as entries
and exits.

=== PAGE 53 ===
Manual operation in BSCs and isolators, or the opening of a door at a PAL or MAL, are the most critical
factors of an influence on the air stability in a system. Fast movements should be avoided. A higher degree
of automation could reduce or even eliminate manual operations and the resultant air influence. Delays in
alarms should be minimal (i.e., < 3 min) and should be justified as part of the CCS.
For cleanrooms, air locks with door interlocks on all doors and pressure stabilization are recommended to
avoid pressure fluctuations between the air lock and the processing room. Furthermore, disruption of the
differential pressure between the air locks and the adjacent cleanrooms will disrupt the function of the air
locks as a pressure sink (negative relative to surrounding spaces), air bubble (positive relative to surrounding
spaces), or pressure cascade (positive on one side and negative on the other side). (See Figure 4.3-1.)
Rationale
Stable pressure zones and maintaining the differential pressure between adjacent spaces are important for
the safe manufacture of ATMPs and are essential to keeping aseptic conditions and preventing
contamination and cross-contamination in adjacent areas. A pressure-zone concept, with a risk evaluation
based on the assigned function of every room, as well as the personnel and material flows, should be part of
the overall layout of the rooms within the facility.
