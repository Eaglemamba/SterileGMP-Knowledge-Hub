# VPHP Cycle Development: 50% to 35% H2O2 + BI Format Change

**Model:** Claude Opus 4.6 | **Effort:** High | **Date:** 2026-04-02

---

## VPHP Cycle Development: 50% -> 35% H2O2 + BI Format Change

### Scenario

- Filling isolator currently uses **50% H2O2** for bio-decontamination
- Plan to switch to **35% H2O2** (supply lead time reasons)
- Simultaneously switching BI from **bare stainless steel strip** to **stainless steel disk + Tyvek** packaging
- Re-validation required (3 cycles PQ)
- Goal: establish starting points based on existing parameters to minimize cycle development time

---

### Part 1: Cycle Development Overall Direction

#### 1.1 Two Simultaneous Changes -- Risk Characterization

Both changes must be decomposed through risk assessment during cycle development:

> *"A risk assessment should be used to determine the 'worst case' conditions and configurations to challenge during decontamination cycle development and validation."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-4]`

| Change | Affected Cycle Variables | Risk |
|--------|------------------------|------|
| 50% -> 35% H2O2 | Injection volume, saturation concentration, dew point, aeration time | Medium -- physical relationship allows calculated starting point |
| Bare strip -> Disk + Tyvek | D-value, VHP penetration rate, BI consistency | **High** -- Tyvek has penetration resistance, D-value must be re-confirmed in-system |

> *"There is significant risk associated with using a D-value for a BI lot from a supplier as opposed to confirming it in your own isolator system prior to use."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-6]`

#### 1.2 Recommended Strategy: Decouple -> Baseline -> Develop -> PQ

```
Phase 0  D-value Baseline Establishment
         |-- Use current 50% cycle parameters + new Tyvek BI
         |-- Fractional run (single location, no full load)
         |-- Purpose: confirm new BI D-value in your isolator system

Phase 1  35% Parameter Calculation + Cycle Development
         |-- Based on Phase 0 D-value results + mass equivalence calculation for starting dose
         |-- Flexible protocol (allows parameter adjustment)
         |-- Use EI alongside BI for location-to-location screening
         |-- Single confirmation run acceptable

Phase 2  Formal PQ (3 cycles)
         |-- Parameters fixed
         |-- Full location triplicate BI
         |-- Acceptance criteria: 6-log reduction
```

**Why this phased approach?**

> *"Cycle development establishes the conditions, parameters, and settings for an effective cycle... Cycle development protocols should be flexible, allowing for parameter adjustments as the result of a failure or an unexpected outcome. While triplicate runs are common for validation activities, **a single confirmation run could be acceptable for cycle development.**"*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-5]`

> *"Cycle qualification provides documented evidence of the reliability and effectiveness of the developed cycle... **Triplicate runs are expected for the process qualification.** Failure to meet acceptance criteria would typically involve an investigation and implementation of corrective action along with additional testing."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-5]`

### Part 2: Calculating 35% Starting Parameters from 50% Baseline

#### 2.1 Core Principle -- Equal Mass H2O2

VHP efficacy depends on the **absolute mass of H2O2 deposited on surfaces within the chamber**, not solution volume:

> *"VHP is distributed in the decontaminated chamber until enough VHP has been deposited (i.e., adsorbed/condensed) on the decontaminated surfaces to yield the targeted decontamination effect."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-2]`

Therefore:

$$Volume_{35\%} = Volume_{50\%} \times \frac{50}{35} \approx Volume_{50\%} \times 1.43$$

**For hot plate vaporizer systems** (majority of filling isolators), additional consideration:

> *"Hot plate vaporizers should be operated to ensure flash vaporization of injected VHP. An operating temperature too low or liquid dosing too high may result in VHP boiling that will affect the efficacy and may even lead to explosion!"*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-2]`

35% has higher water content (65% water vs. 50% water), so the energy requirement for flash vaporization differs. If hot plate temperature remains unchanged, verify that increased water volume does not cause liquid residue or boiling.

**For fogging/atomization systems:**

> *"The initial temperature and humidity should be considered for fogging processes as these determine how much VHP vapor can be generated by atomization."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-2]`

35% has different droplet density and surface tension than 50%, atomization efficiency may vary slightly -- but the 1.43x volume conversion remains a reasonable starting point.

#### 2.2 Parameter Starting Points

| Parameter | Current (50%) | Suggested Starting Point (35%) | Rationale & Reference |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Injection volume (mL/m3)** | V50 | **V50 x 1.43** | Equal mass conversion; maintain same H2O2 deposition |
| **Injection rate (g/min)** | R50 | R50 x 1.43 or extend injection time | If generator has max flow rate limit, extend gassing phase time |
| **Gassing time** | T_gas | **T_gas x 1.0 to 1.3** | If injection rate maintained -> x 1.43; if rate increased -> maintain or slightly increase |
| **Dwell time** | T_dwell | **T_dwell x 1.0 to 1.2** | Initial 10-20% buffer recommended; Phase 0 D-value data will refine this |
| **Temperature** | Maintain current | **Maintain current** | Do not change simultaneously -- temperature affects condensation and D-value |
| **Relative humidity** | Maintain current | **Maintain current** | Same as above; if RH changed, Phase 0 D-value baseline becomes invalid |
| **Aeration time** | A50 | **Start with A50, may shorten after testing** | 35% deposition may be lower -> aeration potentially shorter, but must verify |

> Why temperature and humidity should not change:
> *"Since the effectiveness of a gaseous or vapor phase process can be impacted by surface condensation levels, and that temperature plays an important role in determining the degree of condensation, temperature mapping is an important aspect of cycle qualification."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-3]`

#### 2.3 Special Considerations for Aeration

The most commonly overlooked difference between 35% and 50% is in aeration:

> *"Aeration time studies should consider that decontamination agents can be adsorbed by some materials, such as gloves and other plastic materials. Packed materials (e.g., steam sterilized components packed in Tyvek paper) should also be considered, as the decontamination agent may penetrate the packaging material and aeration can be more challenging."*
> `[ISPE Vol.3]`

**Note: You are simultaneously introducing Tyvek BI, and Tyvek itself adsorbs H2O2** -- this means even though 35% total deposition may be lower, the Tyvek BI itself could become an aeration delay factor. However, since BI quantity is limited, impact on overall aeration time is likely low.

> *"Catalytic converters allow aerating of the enclosure in a circulation loop and can provide significant HVAC cost savings. The efficiency of the catalytic converter material can have a significant impact on achievable aeration times."*
> `[ISPE Vol.3]`

Residual acceptance criteria:
> TLV for H2O2 = **0.5 ppm**
> `[ISPE Vol.3]`

### Part 3: BI Format Change -- Specific Impact

#### 3.1 Tyvek Packaging vs. Bare Strip -- Key Differences

| Property | Bare SS Strip | SS Disk + Tyvek |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| VHP contact method | Direct exposure, no barrier | Tyvek semi-permeable membrane, penetration lag time |
| D-value performance | Typically lower (easier to kill) | **Typically higher** (Tyvek resistance delays VHP reaching spores) |
| Position reproducibility | Strip is flexible, orientation difficult to standardize | Disk is fixed, consistent facing, higher reproducibility |
| Batch-to-batch consistency | Depends on inoculation uniformity | Higher standardization |
| Lab handling | Direct contact required, higher contamination risk | Tyvek packaging convenient to handle, lower contamination risk |

Knowledge base requirements for BI packaging:

> *"BI should be packed in a material permeable for H2O2 and easy to handle in the lab."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-6]`

> *"BI should consist of a monolayer of spores to guarantee direct contact of the VHP to the spores on the carrier. A monolayer might not be achieved if the spore suspension used to inoculate the carriers contains too much debris from the cultivation, or if the spores stick together due to other reasons..."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-6]`

#### 3.2 In-System D-value Confirmation (Phase 0 Details)

> *"The D-value should be tested in a given isolator system, not just assigned to a BI lot. Results could depend on the system used to test it and the test method used."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-6]`

> *"**A fractional approach may be used as an initial check at one location without a full load.**"*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-6]`

> *"The D-value depends on the saturation of air with hydrogen peroxide and the consistency of the cycle over time. If hydrogen peroxide levels are not changing and the air is not saturated with VHP, the calculated D-value can vary greatly."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-6]`

**Phase 0 Recommended Approach:**

1. Use current **50% cycle parameters** (validated conditions)
2. Place new Tyvek BI at a routine isolator location (triplicate of 3 at same location)
3. Run fractional exposure time tests: e.g., 0.5T, 0.75T, T, 1.25T (where T = current dwell time)
4. Incubate 7 days, read results, calculate new BI D-value in your system
5. Compare with supplier-provided D-value -- expect new BI D-value to be **higher**

> *"The consistency of BIs within one BI batch should be checked prior to use (e.g., by determining the survival-kill window)."*
> `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-6]`

#### 3.3 Survival-Kill Window Testing

Before entering development, recommended to perform survival-kill window on new Tyvek BI batch:
- **Survival time**: shortest exposure time where all BIs survive
- **Kill time**: shortest exposure time where all BIs are killed
- Narrower window -> more consistent BI quality -> more reliable development results

### Part 4: Leveraging EI to Accelerate Development

> *"Enzymatic indicators (EIs) are increasingly playing a role in cycle development to improve efficiency (time savings) and assessment of location-to-location efficacy. However, accepting the initial BI/EI correlation studies is required, and the final cycle qualification currently requires BI challenge studies."*
> `[PDA PtC-12]`

**Practical approach:**

| Item | BI (Tyvek disk) | EI |
|------|-----------------|-----|
| Phase 0 D-value | Required | Not applicable |
| Phase 1 Development runs | Key locations | **All locations** -- same-day results |
| Phase 2 PQ | **All locations triplicate** | Optional, as supporting evidence |
| Result turnaround | 7 days | **Hours** |

EI value during development:
- Know same-day whether H2O2 dose distribution is uniform across locations
- Rapidly identify shadow zones or insufficient areas
- After parameter adjustment, re-run and verify immediately
- Dramatically shorten development cycle (from 7+ days per iteration -> 1 day)

**Prerequisite: BI/EI correlation study must be completed first** -- confirm EI color change results correspond to BI kill results in your system.

### Risk Assessment for Cycle Development Variables

Complete list of variables to assess per knowledge base `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-5]`:

- Isolator loading configurations, placement, orientation, and loading of materials and equipment within the isolator (e.g., orientation, packaging, number of items)
- Biological indicator selection and placement
- Chemical indication selection and placement
- Process parameters for qualification cycle and for production cycle
- Decontamination agent concentration, dose, and application rate
- Effect of decontamination agent and cycle on isolator interior, material, and equipment surfaces
- Process parameters for aeration and decontamination agent removal
- Effectiveness of decontamination agent
- Interference of the decontamination cycle effectiveness as a result of contact with the isolator interior, equipment, or material surfaces
- Systems and instrumentation in place to control the decontamination cycle
- Automation programs
- Air circulation fan and blower placement, configuration, and speeds
- Distribution of the decontamination agent throughout the isolator
- Temperature and humidity distribution throughout the isolator
- Bioburden within the isolator or on the surfaces within the isolator that require decontamination

### Investigation Checklist for BI Failure During Development

If one or more BIs are not deactivated during development or PQ, investigate systematically `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-7]`:

1. Confirm organism growth (rule out false positive)
2. BI lot or batch variation
3. D-value, population, and resistance characteristics
4. BI preparation and handling errors
5. Spore clumping or occlusion (rogue spores)
6. BI placement configuration and location
7. Cycle parameters (concentration, temperature, humidity, time)
8. Agent application / injection / generation method
9. Instrumentation and sensor calibration
10. Temperature or humidity deviations during cycle
11. Interior bioburden or surface contamination state
12. Residual H2O2 retention effects
13. Insufficient exposure to the decontamination agent

### Vapor Generation Methods Reference

Two main VHP generation approaches `[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-2]`:

**Hot Plate Vaporization:**
> *"Hot plate vaporizers use heat to transform injected liquid aqueous VHP solution into hot vapor, which is then distributed within the chamber to be decontaminated. Hot plate vaporizers should be operated to ensure flash vaporization of injected VHP. An operating temperature too low or liquid dosing too high may result in VHP boiling that will affect the efficacy and may even lead to explosion! Hot vapor entering the chamber should be well distributed to prevent local hot spots or positions with too much VHP condensation."*

**Fogging/Atomization:**
> *"Fogging processes are based on the generation of micron-sized droplets with enormous surface area, which facilitates natural vaporization without the need of additional heat. The vaporization of droplets continues until the maximum vapor saturation is reached and the fog is created."*

- Droplets <15 um = "dry fog" -- minimal sedimentation, vapor adsorption is main mechanism
- Droplets >15-20 um = "wet fog" -- settles and attaches to surfaces, combination of vapor and liquid deposition
- Dry fogging provides faster decontamination and **shorter aeration time** due to less surface deposition

### Temperature and Humidity Mapping Requirements

`[PDA PtC-Isolators Q7-3]`:

> *"Temperature mapping of isolators or decontamination chambers should be consistent with the requirements for decontamination. When using a decontamination agent that is in a gaseous or vapor phase, surface temperatures can influence the amount of localized condensation, which may impact the effectiveness of the chosen decontaminant. Ideally, surface temperatures are uniform, thus promoting homogeneous levels of surface condensate."*

Mapping location considerations:
- Energy sources (lights, fans, servos)
- External room conditions
- Movement of items during decontamination cycle
- Material properties (stainless vs polymer)
- Equipment placement affecting air flow or acting as heat sink
- Attached process equipment (autoclaves, lyophilizers) generating heat
- Loading items at different temperatures (e.g., bottles from freezer)

> *"Infrared cameras can be used during initial assessments to determine the placement of temperature-sensing instruments."*
