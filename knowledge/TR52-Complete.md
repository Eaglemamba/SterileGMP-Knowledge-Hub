# PDA Technical Report No. 52 (2011): Guidance for Good Distribution Practices (GDPs) for the Pharmaceutical Supply Chain

This technical report describes the overall quality system for distribution of pharmaceutical products
and is meant to assist manufacturers in assuring that quality, integrity, and efficacy of the product are
not compromised in the distribution channels.
The document summarizes both regulatory requirements and industry best practices. However
country-specific regulations should be followed, as applicable.

### 1.1 Purpose and Scope

The purpose of this guidance is to provide direction for distribution practices, including handling,
storage, transportation and distribution. This document is intended for use as guidance for the
pharmaceutical industry and their supply-chain partners.
Good Distribution Practices (GDPs) can be divided into seven different categories, or pillars, as
depicted in Table 1.1 below. Each product should be evaluated against the pillars of GDPs to identify
the individual categories that apply within each pillar. This Technical Report provides guidance on the
first four pillars: Stability, Distribution Control Management, Performance Management, and Supply
Chain Partner Management.
More specific guidance on the first pillar, Stability, is provided in PDA Technical Report No. 53: Guidance for
Industry: Stability Testing to Support Distribution of New Drug Products. Separate PDA Technical Reports
will provide guidance on the other GDP pillars: Qualification/Validation, Continuous Improvement,
and Import/Export Compliance.

### 1.2 Responsibility

This guidance applies to all parties that are involved in the handling, storage and/or transportation of
regulated products, including, but not limited to, the manufacturer, the holder of the marketing authorization,
third party Logistics Service Providers (termed 3PLs), wholesale distributors, and transportation carriers.

Good Distribution Practices (GDPs) should include written procedures describing the different
operations that may affect the quality of products and/or distribution activity. Refer to the pillars

Stability
Data from Stability studies is used to determine the temperature conditions required for product

#### 3.1.1 Storage Temperatures

When specific temperature conditions are required, storage or cargo areas should be equipped
to maintain the appropriate temperature. Temperature should be monitored with calibrated
temperature recorders or other devices that will indicate when the specific temperature range has
not been maintained and for how long. Control and monitoring should be adequate to maintain all
parts of the relevant area within the specified temperature range. Monitoring of temperature should
be present in at least one of the following forms and should result in a disposition-based review of
temperature data:
Continuous monitoring
•
Active, qualified monitoring system with automated alarming mechanisms to notify of
•
temperature excursions as they occur.
A contingency plan between the manufacturer or distribution center and approved carrier should be
implemented and documented to provide a consistent, effective response to foreseeable events.
Equipment used for monitoring should be calibrated at defined intervals.
All records should be made at the time each operation is done and in such a way that all significant
activities or events are traceable.

#### 3.1.2 Shipping Temperatures

Pharmaceutical products must be transported in a manner that ensures products will be maintained
within an acceptable temperature range. Temperature should be monitored with calibrated recorders
unless it is demonstrated through temperature mapping or use of qualified packaging containers
that shipping conditions will not expose product to conditions outside of the shipping requirement.
The acceptable temperature range may differ from the conditions specified for long-term storage
and is determined by performing product temperature-excursion studies. These products may be
shipped outside of their respective label storage conditions provided stability data or scientific/
technical justification exists demonstrating that the product quality is not affected and that the data
or justification meets the applicable national and international requirements. Note: some companies
may choose to include shipping conditions on product labeling.
Appropriate stability data must be generated to prove that excursion temperatures have no negative
impact on product quality. This data can be generated by accelerated and long term stability studies,
forced degradation studies, or temperature cycle freeze-thaw studies. Stability data should be
allocated into the various supply chain segments to support cumulative temperature excursions.
If degradation profiles established from stability data meet the profiles predicted by the Arrhenius
equation at established minimum and maximum temperatures (supported by actual stability data),
then Mean Kinetic Temperature (MKT) may be an acceptable method for evaluating product impact.
Note: it is necessary to ensure that the local regulatory agency allows the use of MKT.

the root cause of the temperature excursion
•
any necessary corrective actions (if warranted)
•
any potential product impact (must be documented)

Product impact should be addressed and documented for any temperature excursion and cumulative

system deviations should be properly documented and approved by the quality function.

#### 3.1.3 Stability Testing to Support Distribution

Packaging should ensure protection against mechanical impact (damage) on product during handling
and transport. The transportation modes and destination should be considered in determining the
necessary packaging materials.
When specific temperature conditions are required, packaging used to control temperature
should maintain the appropriate temperature range during handling, storage, transportation and
distribution, and must be qualified. The methods used for qualifying packaging systems should be
based on risk and the product’s sensitivity to temperature change and must be documented (e.g.,
validation master plan). The result of the risk assessment will provide the rationale for the extent of
testing and the need for ongoing temperature monitoring.
Each packaging/pack-out operation throughout distribution should be conducted under monitored
conditions that are supported by stability data. Timelines for situations like Time Out of
Refrigeration (TOR) should be defined, controlled and justified through appropriate stability data
and in accordance with appropriate Service Level or Quality Agreements. Data must be available to
support timeframes outside of labeled temperature and/or humidity.

### 3.2 Distribution Control Management

Distribution control management encompasses the processes and controls necessary to ensure
product integrity and traceability are maintained from the point of receipt through product
distribution.

#### 3.2.1 Qualification and Training of Personnel

Procedures and conditions of employment, including background checks, should be designed and
implemented to minimize the possibility of product diversion. SOPs should be in place for all job
functions. An adequate number of warehouse and shipping personnel should be trained in the
procedures for proper storage and distribution of each product type (hazardous, narcotic, etc.), and
in the safe handling of product spills and other emergency procedures. A training program that
includes both initial and ongoing training should be developed for each job function. The completion
of training should be documented and training records should be maintained for each associate.

Distribution control management encompasses the processes and controls necessary to ensure
product integrity and traceability are maintained from the point of receipt through product

#### 3.2.2 Premises and Equipment

distribution of products. A warehouse should be:
Secure with controlled access and features to protect against invasion, adulteration and theft

Constructed to protect against floods
•
Free of agents that could cause infestation (includes the warehouse surroundings)

In compliance with all local and federal/national regulatory requirements

Environmentally controlled (temperature, humidity), where appropriate

##### 3.2.3.3 Controlled Drugs

Local regulatory requirements should be followed for the activities and documentation involving
controlled drugs (e.g., importing, warehousing, transport, handling, records and official reports, as
applicable).
For importing or exporting activities, both the distribution center and receiving country are respon-
sible to communicate and implement the requirements.

##### 3.2.3.4 Counterfeit Products

Counterfeit products found in the distribution network should be kept apart from other saleable
products in a restricted and controlled area to avoid any confusion. They should be clearly labeled
as not for sale. Competent authorities and the holder of the marketing authorization of the original
product should be informed immediately. Counterfeit products should be dealt with in accordance
with federal/national or local regulations.

##### 3.2.3.5 Records

Records should be generated at the time each operation is completed and in such a way that all
significant activities or events are traceable. Records should be understandable and readily available.
Records retention schedules or equivalent documents should be established and maintained
according to applicable record retention regulations.

#### 3.2.4 Storage and Inventory Control

Computerized systems and/or written procedures should be implemented to record and track
product information that is critical to protect products and customers. This information includes
lot numbers, expiration dates, storage locations, product status and any other information required
to track the product from receipt through distribution and to ensure that customers receive quality
product. Systems/procedures should address the following:

##### 3.2.4.1 Inventory

There should be a documented system for the recording and traceability of lot numbers and expiration
dates. Inventory should be periodically reviewed to assure the control of identity, quantity, location
and status of each inventory item.
Computerized systems should be appropriately qualified and validated.
Inventory accuracy must be maintained and verified. Periodic cycle counts or annual inventory
reviews should be performed and documented in order to detect and investigate discrepancies
and identify corrective actions, and should be used as tools for improvements to inventory
management.

##### 3.2.4.2 Product Status Control

There should be a validated system to control and maintain product status (e.g., on test, approved,
quarantined, restricted). The system should:
Be under appropriate change control
•
Allow changes to product status to be made only by appropriate quality personnel.
•
Computerized systems should be appropriately qualified and validated.

##### 3.2.4.3 In-Transit Storage

In-transit storage facilities (e.g., cross-docking center, airplane, airport, distribution hub and other
facilities which are used to move product from one vehicle to another vehicle) may be used. All in-
transit storage facilities should have adequate security and environmental control, and must be in
compliance with all applicable laws and regulations prior to use.

#### 3.2.5 Transportation

Products should be transported in such a way that product:
Maintains its identification
•
Does not contaminate and is not contaminated by other products or materials
•
Is secure (i.e., protected against spillage, breakage or theft)
•
Is not subjected to unacceptable changes in heat, cold, light, moisture or other adverse
•
influences
Is not subjected to attack by pests.
•
Products requiring controlled temperature storage should be transported with appropriate controls
and monitored. Shipment time should not exceed the timeframe established through validation
studies. Excursions may be justified through continuous temperature monitoring and appropriate
stability studies.
Freezers used for storage of coolants such as chilled or frozen gel packs and the process for control
of those coolants should be qualified. Vehicles containers and/or packaging should be appropriately
matched to avoid negative effects on the integrity and quality of pharmaceutical products.
Products that are temperature controlled that ship internationally should be labeled per IATA
Perishable Cargo Regulations.
Vehicles used to distribute, store or handle pharmaceutical products should be suitable for their use
and appropriately equipped to prevent exposure of the products to environmental conditions that
could affect their stability and packaging integrity, and prevent contamination of any kind. Carrier
studies may be used to support adequacy and qualification of equipment in combination with active
load performance qualification studies. Monitoring should take place in at least the front and back of
vehicle and should support duration of shipment.
Vehicles should be inspected for cleanliness and temperature setting, if applicable, prior to loading
and transporting product. This inspection should be documented.

#### 3.2.6 Product Disposition and Distribution

Product can only be distributed to authorized parties or to persons authorized to supply and/or
receive products. Products due to expire first should be distributed first (FEFO). Adequate controls
should be in place to prevent the distribution of expired products throughout the supply chain.
For pharmaceutical products supplied to a person authorized or entitled to supply products to the
public, a document should be enclosed that makes it possible to ascertain the date, the name and
pharmaceutical form of the product, the quantity supplied, and the name and address of the supplier
(e.g., bill of lading, purchase order or invoice). Documents should also contain quantity shipped and
lot number of material.
It is critical to coordinate shipments throughout the supply chain to ensure proper timing for products
to be transported and received with regard to holidays, weekends or other forms of interruption,
such as weather-related delivery delays or customer receipt schedules. Any potential customs-related
delays, including those by the U.S. FDA, U.S. Department of Agriculture, health ministries in other
countries, or any other governmental agency resulting from international shipments, must also be
considered.

#### 3.2.7 Product Protection

Fundamental to the outcomes of a Quality System surrounding GDP is the need to ensure that
products are handled, stored, transferred and transported in a manner that does not negatively
impact or degrade the product. Thus, GDPs should protect the product from external environmental
conditions that might cause undue deterioration. These include chemicals, light, moisture,
temperature, pest infestation, or other adverse influences that could cause microbial and/or chemical
contamination. If a pesticide program is in place the pesticide should be registered as required by
local regulations.
Paths to degradation of product should be established through stability testing, and required
shipping conditions defined and communicated to the parties of the supply chain so as to enact or
implement appropriate means of environmental control. Data, such as filed stability test results,
forced degradation studies and temperature cycling studies, may be used to support supply chain
temperature excursions.
The storage facilities and vehicles or containers used to transport product should be clean and free
from litter, dust and pests. Adequate precautions should be taken against spillage or breakage that
attracts microorganisms, causes cross-contamination, or could create a health or safety risk to
personnel. There should be adequate space for the orderly placement of materials to prevent mix-
ups between drug products to prevent contamination.
Products with broken seals or damaged packaging or suspected of possible contamination should
be withdrawn from saleable stock, and if not immediately destroyed, they should be kept separate,
secure and quarantined so that they cannot be sold in error or contaminate other goods.
Sufficient security should be provided to prevent theft and product diversion.

#### 3.2.8 Returns Management

Any shipped product presented to a storage facility for return must be evaluated in accordance with
return procedures to determine if the material must be destroyed or if it is eligible for restocking.
Returns should be examined upon receipt, as described in Section 3.7. Returned products should
only be evaluated for restocking eligibility if:
They are in original, unopened containers, in good condition, not damaged, and it is known
•
and documented that they have been stored and handled under proper conditions
They have been evaluated by an authorized person
•
There is documentation that required temperatures were maintained and that security of the
•
materials was never compromised
Documentation indicates the reason for return and that the product was not received into
•
stock by the customer so that chain of custody is maintained. Note: many companies do not
receive cold chain back into stock due in part to inconsistencies in controls for the return
shipment.
Returns for destruction should be processed by an authorized pharmaceutical destruction company
unless the manufacturer has the means to destroy the goods.

#### 3.2.9 Exception Management

Any excursion from the defined process, as identified by the manufacturer, constitutes an exception.
An exception management system should be put in place to track all non-conformances and the
investigation of the non-conformances so that product and quality problems may be routinely
analyzed. The investigation into a quality problem or nonconforming product should be conducted
to a level commensurate with the significance or risk of the problem.
The exception management system should be documented. The documentation should identify the
activities that should be treated as non-conformances and a description of the steps and documentation
that is required to complete the investigation, including product impact. A targeted timeframe for
closure should be defined along with a schedule for review with management.

##### 3.2.9.1 Corrective Action and Preventative Action (CAPA) System

A system with documented procedures should be developed including the following elements:
Description of the investigation that took place
•
Root cause, if one exists
•
Description of how root cause was identified
•
Corrective actions and preventative actions
•
Owners and target due dates for corrective actions and preventative actions.
•
Verification that the corrective and/or preventative action(s) were implemented and found
•
effective.
Evidence of management review
•
The system should define a targeted timeframe for the identification of root cause and the closure of
the associated action plans. Additionally, CAPAs should be included in management reviews.

##### 3.2.9.2 Complaints

Procedures for receiving, reviewing, evaluating and investigating complaints should be established,
documented and maintained. The complaints should be processed in a uniform and timely manner.
History of product complaints should be maintained to track and trend complaints and responses
and any resulting CAPAs.

##### 3.2.9.3 Quality Holds

A system, including documented procedures, should be in place to implement holds on products,
as necessary, to prevent them from being distributed to customers. The responsibility and ability of
affecting the quality release status of a product should be restricted to personnel who are properly
trained and authorized by the quality function.

##### 3.2.9.4 Product Action

A system, including documented procedures, should be in place to respond to all product actions,
including recalls, withdrawals and corrections. The system should include a requirement to segregate
and secure product requiring action to prevent distribution to customers.

### 3.3 Performance Management

Methods and procedures should be established to review the performance of the quality system
on a regularly scheduled basis and to identify opportunities for continuous improvement. These
methods and procedures should include Performance Measurement and Reporting, Self-Inspection
(Internal Audit), and Management Review Meetings.

#### 3.3.1 Performance Measurement and Reporting

Performance of the components of the quality system should be measured against pre-defined goals
to gauge performance and to identify opportunities for improvement. Reports should be generated
and reviewed on a regularly scheduled basis to track performance, monitor trends, and identify
areas for improvement.
3.3.2	 Self-Inspection (Internal Audit)
The quality assurance program should require self-inspections, which should include the quality
systems of internal supply chain partners. These self-inspections should be conducted in order to
monitor the implementation of GDP and compliance with GDP principles, as well as to trigger
necessary CAPA measures and monitor CAPA effectiveness to achieve continuous improvement.
This is termed a ‘closed loop supply chain,’ in which the manufacturer is aware of their supply chain
partners and operations.

#### 3.3.3 Management Review Meetings

Quality management system reviews should take place on a regularly scheduled basis and should
cover:
Audit results
•
Performance metrics (actual vs. goals)
•
Status of non-conformances, corrective actions and preventative actions
•
Customer feedback (returns, complaints, etc.)
•
Product status (recalls, non-availability)
•
Changes that may impact the quality systems.
•
Opportunities and recommendations for improvement
•

#### 3.4.2 Quality Audit

External inspection should verify that written agreements have been executed with the service
providers and that the service providers adhere to the terms and conditions of the agreements (e.g.,
master service agreements, quality agreements, etc). Standards should be established to include
agreement on metrics for regular business reviews where metrics are reviewed and errors and
continuous improvements are discussed. Quality system reviews and audits (via on-site visits or via
questionnaire, depending on the risk) should be performed by the quality organization from the
manufacturer or the marketing authorization holder, and appropriately documented to allow for
reporting, and follow-up.
The attached audit checklist (see 4.0 Appendix) covers all sections of the pharmaceutical supply
chain. All sections may not be applicable to specific services providers, however if a section is not
applicable (N/A) justification should be provided. For example, warehousing of commodities like
expanded polystyrene and corrugate do not need temperature profiles.

#### 3.4.3 Quality Agreements

It is the responsibility of the manufacturer and/or holder of the marketing authorization to ensure
that the different aspects of this guideline are appropriately assigned and implemented to the different
parties of the supply chain. The distribution of responsibilities should be formalized in covenants or
“Quality Agreements” with all third parties. Such agreements should be periodically updated.
