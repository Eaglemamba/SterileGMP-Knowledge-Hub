This document represents the views of the Parenteral Drug Association (PDA). The document is designed to communicate PDA's thoughts and considerations pertaining to the design, operation, and use of Restricted Access Barrier Systems (RABS) for aseptic processing, processes subjected to terminal sterilization and low bioburden, while encouraging further dialog with industry, health authorities, and suppliers of technologies and materials. This document considers the evolution and needs of the modern, global, sterile, healthcare product manufacturing industry. This document does not represent a standard or regulatory guidance.

        

In 2020, PDA issued Points to Consider for the Aseptic Processing of Sterile Pharmaceutical Products in Isolators, which focus on important regulatory and technology updates surrounding isolator design, validation, and operation for aseptic processing. Several other PDA documents equally address many of the principles for working and operating with RABS, including Points to Consider No. 1: Aseptic Processing (Revised 2023) or Technical Report No. 62 Recommended Practices for Manual Aseptic Processes. Additionally regulatory guidance documents as the European Commission's EU Guidelines for Good Manufacturing Practice for Medicinal Products for Human and Veterinary Use Annex 1: Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal Products (2022), U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) Guidance for Industry: Sterile Drug Products Produced by Aseptic Processing-Current Good Manufacturing Practice (2004) and others are addressing the special requirements for RABS. While many of the recommendations and principles from these documents are equally applicable to RABS, until this point, there was no equivalent guideline or Points to Consider (PtC) dedicated to RABS for which technology and operational specific nuances apply. Hence, a taskforce was established to focus on industry best practices, regulatory and technology considerations directly relevant to the application and use of RABS in an aseptic manufacturing setting.

        

A fundamental principle of sterile product manufacturing is to minimize the risks of contamination (microbiological and particulate) through appropriate facility, equipment, and manufacturing process design, and qualification. The use of barrier systems, including RABS, to increase the protection of the product is not only recommended but should be considered in the context of an overall contamination control and risk-reduction strategy. RABS technology provides for increased product protection compared to non-barrier systems used for Grade A and Grade B aseptic manufacturing operations. RABS is a very different technology to isolators and, consequently, key design, regulatory and operational differences, and considerations apply, most notably background environments, cleaning, disinfection and decontamination methods, aseptic setup, and strictly defined conditions and controls governing open-door RABS interventions.

## Q 2.1 Room Classification Surrounding RABS

    

        

        

### Q 2.1. What should be the classification of the room surrounding the restricted access barrier system (RABS)?

        

**Recommendation**

        

The minimum classification of the room immediately surrounding the RABS must be Grade B. However, a manufacturer may also choose to maintain these areas at more stringent levels of control when assessed for risk, especially in the area immediately surrounding the RABS doors, which may require execution of open-door interventions (i.e., during setup or filling) (1, 4).

        

**Rationale**

        

A RABS provides an enclosed and separated environment designed to reduce the risk of contamination to product, components, and product-contact surfaces when compared to conventional "curtained" aseptic processing operations. However, RABS are not fully sealed from the immediate surrounding environment and, in some designs, allow for open-door interventions by aseptic operators. As such, the adjacent background cleanroom environment must be appropriately classified and maintained to protect the critical processing zone(s) within the RABS; therefore, the cleanroom environment should not be lower than Grade B.

        

Companies should provide the maximum assurance possible and practicable so that the controlled RABS environment is not impacted by the surrounding environmental conditions since the RABS is not completely isolated from the surroundings, unlike closed isolators, for example.

        

            "Note: The minimum regulatory expectations (e.g., FDA, EMA) for the classification of manufacturing spaces immediately adjacent (i.e., background) to the Grade A critical processing zone is Grade B."

## Topic 4: Glove Integrity 手套完整性 (p48-p59)

# Topic 4: Glove Integrity-Testing and Maintenance

    

主題四：手套完整性測試與維護

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 12 (2025) — RABS | p48 - p55

    

        

### Recommendation

        

There are various aspects to designing, maintaining, and verifying the integrity of a RABS. The potential loss of integrity can occur at many locations, including the RABS enclosure seams, baseplate openings, doors, glove ports, gloves, transfer ports, UDAF (in the case of an active RABS), and the lack of First Air.

        

A risk analysis should be conducted to assist in identifying design decisions and operational actions that have the potential for a loss of integrity. (See PDA Technical Report No. 54: *Implementation of Quality Risk Management for Pharmaceutical and Biotechnology Manufacturing Operations* for guidance on performing a risk analysis.)

        

As discussed in Questions 1.1-1.6 of this document, quality-by-design techniques can reduce the risk of incursions to RABS by considering process and user requirements, risk analysis, and designs that limit potential points of contamination. However, active monitoring and verification of RABS integrity is key to protecting the product and operator safety, where applicable in cRABS.

        

Considerations on monitoring glove integrity throughout a campaign is important as post campaign integrity test failures can be highly impactful. Also, integrity monitoring after significant interventions needs to be considered. Monitoring environmental conditions in real time within the oRABS or cRABS is essential to maintaining Grade A conditions to support RABS integrity. These parameters include:

        

            
- Temperature

            
- Humidity

            
- Differential pressure

            
- UDAF velocity

            
- EM

        

        

In addition to real-time monitoring of these environmental conditions, glove integrity-testing, both visual assessment and a pressure test are required (1, 17). Firms should establish the procedures for this pressure test based on the specifications of their gloves and testing methods. This process should be incorporated into the CCS. (See Topics 4-6 of this PtC.)

        

A common pathway to RABS contamination is through the use of glove ports and, in particular, the loss of integrity of the glove material (4). While pre- and post-filling EM sampling may detect contamination via the gloves, it is imperative to verify the integrity of the glove itself. Glove integrity-testing should be performed based on an approved risk assessment. An approved risk assessment should determine the frequency of pressure testing, visual inspection, and glove changeout (6, 8). Additional glove visual assessments should be performed prior to each use during both campaign and batch production (3).

        

### Rationale

        

It is critical for RABS to maintain integrity in order to support Grade A conditions. The RABS gloves design, use, and maintenance are key elements to ensuring appropriate control and overall integrity. RABS-related procedures and programs should encompass the continuous review of system and process improvements that support product quality.

### Q 5.1. What should the environmental monitoring (EM) frequency be in a closed or open restricted access barrier system (cRABS or oRABS)?

        

**Recommendation**

        

The frequency of EM for oRABS and cRABS should be the same; continuous viable and total particle monitoring is required for the full duration of critical activities, including setup (1). (See Question 5.5.)

        

Critical activities start at the point when a product-contact part and/or a primary container or closure are transferred and are exposed to the inside of the RABS. Where the RABS is subdivided into discrete zones, the point where critical activities start and end may well be different within the different zones; hence, the continuous EM may start at different times in each zone (4).

        

Continuous viable (i.e., a combination of active and passive air monitoring) and total particle monitoring should begin at the start of the aseptic setup and should continue up until the end of filling. Typically, this is the end of the batch or campaign.

        

The RABS should be well designed to minimize the need for interventions involving opening the doors. By exception and where required, the doors should only be opened under strictly defined conditions, which should be procedurally defined and pre-assessed as part of the QRM. The conditions for the opening of the doors and controls in place to mitigate risks should be considered and documented as part of the overall CCS.

        

Appropriate controls should be in place to minimize the risk posed by the intervention through the opening of the doors and should extend to consideration of further disinfection steps. Additionally, controls should extend to the need to undertake a partial line clearance, for example, to discard exposed primary packaging containers or other direct product-contact parts located close to the intervention (e.g., vials, syringes, stoppers located on the stopper track). While sterility testing cannot be used to rationalize poor practices or otherwise support the undertaking of a high-risk intervention, based on risk, additional sterility samples should be considered as part of an additional control measure for critical interventions (4).

        

The same EM program applies to both product manufacture and APS.

        

**Rationale**

        

There is no difference in expectations around the frequency of EM inside oRABS and cRABS.

        

Once a critical surface is exposed inside the RABS, that surface is potentially at risk of contamination, so continuous EM is required.

        

The requirement for continuous EM ceases once the last unit is closed for the batch or campaign provided any used product-contact parts are reprocessed, and any sterile components are cleared prior to reuse of the line.

        

In the rare event that the RABS door is opened after line setup is completed, the critical zone is put at an elevated risk of contamination from the person(s) intervening and from the surrounding cleanroom. Additional controls should be considered to reduce risks of such interventions and should extend to consideration of additional disinfection steps and partial line clearances.

        

Monitoring should not interfere with production processes in a way that raises the level of risk by introducing an activity in the critical zone, which defeats the purpose of the EM.

## Topic 6: Material Transport 物料傳輸 (p73-p83)

# Topic 6: Material Transport and Loading

    

主題六：物料運輸與裝載

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 12 (2025) — RABS | p73 - p83

    

        

### Recommendation

        

Indirect product-contact parts such as stopper bowls, hoppers, ancillary items (e.g., tweezers, forceps), and guides must be sterilized (1). This is typically done by wrapping the clean items in suitable semi-permeable packaging material and sterilizing them in a moist-heat autoclave, followed by unloading, transportation through the cleanroom environment, and aseptic assembly inside the RABS. Machine items that break First Air (e.g., sensors, holders, brackets at the filling line or rotary table) should be avoided where possible. When machine items do break First Air, they should be sterilized. (See Question 6.5 for more details.)

        

Based on facility layout and whether Grade A continuity can be applied for the sterilized items (e.g., by using UDAF carts), either single-wrapping or double-wrapping can be applied to ensure transport of the autoclaved, sterilized parts from the Grade B autoclave to the entrance of the RABS. To provide for aseptic removal and transfer of the parts into the RABS, good aseptic technique must be used to ensure that the inner layer or part is only exposed within the Grade A environment. The wrapping materials and removal process should minimize particle generation as much as possible.

        

When using double wrapping, the outer wrapping is ideally removed at the barrier door, where the contents are still protected by the inner barrier. For large items, such as the stopper bowl, two people may be needed to properly complete the aseptic transfer. That is, the primary and secondary operators are responsible for different steps: typically, one for the transfer activities outside the RABS, and one for the transfer activities inside the RABS. The inner wrapping is then removed toward the end of the assembly process under Grade A conditions, depending on the design of the manufacturing line just before or after the RABS doors are closed. In case parts of the assembly are done as a closed-door intervention, the wrapping could be removed at an earlier stage to enable removal of the wrap from the RABS just before door closure or as a final open-door intervention. Special considerations must be taken in the sequence of removing the wrapping so as to ensure the First Air of already unwrapped parts is not breached during the removal of other wrappers.

        

Automated surface bio-decontamination processes such as VPHP, are not considered equivalent to sterilization processes, however, they can reduce bioburden risk if applied after the set-up process is complete and the RABS doors are closed.

        

If items can be directly unloaded into the cRABS (e.g., via RTP) or an aseptic transfer from the autoclave to the RABS is otherwise guaranteed (e.g., via Grade A continuity, typically a process requiring rigorous controls), different means of wrapping and/or dry-heat sterilization of the indirect product-contact items may be applied. The wrapping materials and removal process should minimize particle generation as much as possible.

        

In addition to open-door installation, an introduction of indirect product-contact parts via RTP or sterile boxes can be considered for the RABS to minimize the risk of potential contamination during installation by open doors. This especially applies for smaller parts such as guiding and stopper guardrails, for example.

        

### Rationale

        

Indirect product-contact surfaces are critical for the sterility assurance of the product and must be sterilized (1).

**Q 7.1. What are the considerations for planning, designing, and executing a restricted access barrier system (RABS) cleaning and disinfection program?**

        

### Recommendation

        

Before implementing a cleaning and disinfection program, a RABS cleanability assessment should be performed. This assessment should include the suitability of material surfaces to be cleaned as described in Question 1.2. In addition, cleaning accessibility in terms of ergonomics, the extent of reach required into the RABS, and areas that need cleaning tools to reach should be considered. Hard to clean mechanical spaces and surfaces in processing areas should have been eliminated by design as a basic GMP requirement (4). As disinfection programs must include the use of an approved sporicidal, the materials of construction within the RABS should be assessed to be compatible with such disinfectant agents.

        

            *Note: Sensitive equipment (e.g., environmental monitoring equipment) must be considered during cleaning, disinfection and/or decontamination and may need to be covered at a certain cleaning step. This should be documented in the CCS.
        

        

Typically, RABS will include installed process equipment (e.g., filling lines) that are also subjected to cleaning and disinfection together with the RABS barrier. Cleanability assessments will therefore include suitability and access of process equipment surfaces for cleaning and disinfection procedures.

        

Cleaning is a prerequisite to effective bio-decontamination processes. Initially, at start-up and after shutdowns, a cleaning agent with a surfactant will assist with the removal of bonded layers of soiling (e.g., lubricants, grease, fatty acids from human contact). Cleaning agents are likely to leave residues that may require a follow-up wipe removal step with sterile 70% IPA for example, and such actions should be included in SOP control design (1, 6).

        

Agents will be validated to demonstrate the appropriate log reduction, including bacteria, spores, and viruses (as required), with the target of achieving Grade A conditions in the RABS. The process in which agents are manually applied must be qualified to ensure appropriate environmental conditions are rendered (6). RABS Grade A conditions must be established and under control before sterilized parts, including fluid path or filling needles and indirect product-contact parts (e.g., stopper bowls, guides, chutes), are introduced (1).

        

Bio-decontamination processes (e.g., VPHP, dry or wet process technologies) should be automated; an emphasis should be on a validated cleaning process prior to initiating the bio-decontamination process.

        

More automated bio-decontamination processes (e.g., VPHP, dry or wet process technologies) may be applied to oRABS (in combination with cleanroom) and cRABS. If disinfection cannot be implemented manually (i.e., considered in design), and if enhanced sporicidal efficacy and disinfection assurance is required.

        

In such applications, cleaning is not replaced by automated disinfection alone and remains an essential pre-step. In first application or following shutdowns cleaning, where more surface-soiling may be present, a neutral detergent with a surfactant may be appropriate, but inevitability it will leave residues, so a following residue-removal step would be required, typically with isopropanol.

        

A cleanability assessment, selection of cleaning and disinfectant agents, and associated methods of application should be defined in a cleaning risk assessment to cover the risk-based decision-making requirement as part of the QRM. Having a rationale in place, a qualification methodology can be developed ahead of qualification protocols and execution.

        

### Manual Cleaning and Disinfections of RABS

        

RABS require cleaning and disinfection to establish and maintain Grade A conditions. Manual processes for these steps are described below.

        

**Cleaning** is the removal of soiling, residual product, and nonviable contaminants. The process is typically manual, completed with wipes that apply cleaning agents and a physical removal (i.e., collection in a wipe) of contaminants (1, 6).

        

**Disinfection** is a process of applying, via a qualified procedure, a laboratory-validated disinfectant that provides an appropriate log reduction of bioburden to specified levels. All disinfection programs for RABS should include a sporicidal agent (1, 6). Appropriate residue removal is required after cleaning using purified/sterile water (e.g., WFI) or sterile isopropanol.

        

There are two stages of RABS disinfection:

        

            
1. **Establishing control** of the Grade A processing environment on first installation, after shutdown, and between batches and campaigns. This first step of disinfection should include a sporicidal agent, hence, sporicidal disinfection of the complete internal processing area within the RABS.

            
2. **Routine disinfection** in production, which includes after open-barrier interventions and wiping down the barrier door before closing. The selection of the disinfectant should be based on a risk assessment and assurance that Grade A conditions are not compromised.

        

        

It is recommended that primary and secondary operators carry out these disinfection tasks related to interventions. Interventions into Grade A processing zones should be undertaken by a primary operator with the necessary localized internal-zone disinfection followed by personnel monitoring, based on an approved risk assessment, prior to closing the operations. The secondary operator should complete the internal RABS door disinfection before closing the RABS barrier.

        

### Rationale

        

If RABS are cleaned and disinfected by manual application methods, the prerequisite of a cleanability assessment is paramount.

        

Different combinations of cleaning and disinfectant agents may be applied based on process steps and levels of soiling.

### Q 8.1. What are the special considerations for aseptic process simulation (APS) in a restricted access barrier system (RABS)?

        

**Recommendation**

        

A risk-based analysis and approach taken for APS are recommended for all aseptic processing technologies, including RABS systems. By design, the APS should imitate routine manufacturing conditions and should ensure that all critical steps are challenged. A RABS relies on the surrounding cleanroom environment to maintain Grade A conditions for sterile processing. To maintain Grade A, the RABS routinely includes cleaning and disinfection processes, in addition to sterilization processes for processing accessories and equipment. By design, the RABS should only provide for open-door intervention during setup and, by exception, during operations. The cleanroom qualification, operator training, cleaning, and disinfection programs should all be qualified.

        

There are several key considerations that need to be established in support of APS, and they should include, but are not limited to:

        

            
- Cleanroom and clean air equipment, such as UDAF units, must be qualified according to the required characteristics of the environment.

            
- The RABS must be qualified to meet all requirements for aseptic processing including HEPA-filter integrity-testing, pressure differential across the filters, air velocity testing, and AVS. AVS should confirm unidirectional airflow that sweeps over and away from exposed products during processing, without increased risk of contamination through turbulent airflow. Airflow visualization qualification and monitoring should be applied at open-door interventions as a prerequisite to all APS designs to ensure environmental-control conditions are qualified in readiness for an APS.
                  
*Note: APS cannot be used to justify poor design of equipment and processes.*

            
- Operator training and qualification extending to Grade A and Grade B aseptic gowning and aseptic behavior training must be complete. Operators undertaking RABS cleaning and disinfection programs should be qualified and operations should be implemented procedurally.

            
- A list of authorized inherent and corrective interventions should be designed, defined, and appropriately assessed and documented in the CCS to minimize the risk of contamination. Open-door interventions should be a rare and exceptional occurrence. When required, the need to take representative sterility samples should be considered, and the intervention should be effectively demonstrated in an APS.

            
- Qualified methods are in place for the transfer of materials that enter the cleanroom, and subsequently the RABS, that do not compromise the cleanroom-surrounding environment or the RABS Grade A processing zone.

            
- Risk assessments support the establishment of a comprehensive EM program, which should be based on detailed knowledge of the process, facility, and equipment. Sampling locations, sampling frequency and type of EM monitoring (e.g., active air, passive, contact plate) should be defined based on risk assessment output.

        

        

The use of a "piggyback" APS can be considered as part of the overall APS design. A piggyback APS is where an APS run is undertaken at the end of a routine single or campaign filling process and can be used in support of validating an extended run or campaign length/duration. There are specific limitations associated with a piggyback APS which, by design, does not provide for an appropriate simulation of activities and variables that are only performed for the first lot of a campaign, for example, setup activities. Consideration also needs to be given to potential interferences of residual product with the growth-promoting properties of the media used in the APS. Consequently, a piggyback APS alone will not fulfill the validation requirements to demonstrate an effective APS program. Rather, its use can be considered as part of an overall effective APS program, which also must include an APS strategy that covers setup and other potential worst-case scenarios.

        

The scheduling of piggyback APS in conjunction with traditional APS should be supported by a formal risk assessment, for which the limitations of the piggyback approach and its use are considered.

        

**Rationale**

        

The planning of open- and closed-door interventions and simulation of worst-case criteria should be based on risk management principles. The same criteria for the design of an APS as stated in PDA Technical Report No. 22 (Revised 2011): *Process Simulation for Aseptically Filled Products* are also appropriate for all RABS.

        

After establishing Grade A conditions inside a RABS, the barrier technology provides a physical and aerodynamic barrier to contamination ingress to Grade A when the barrier is closed. The barrier is open at setup where presterilized materials are transferred and are installed. At this time, both Grade A conditions and the sterilized parts are at an increased risk for contamination and, thus, can compromise sterility.

        

If rare open-door interventions take place during aseptic manufacturing, there is also a risk escalation that needs mitigation by design, qualifications, and procedural controls, and is subject to additional qualification in an APS. Such in-process significant interventions require consideration for sterility samples to be taken and should be included in the APS.

        

Simulation of the production activities is a necessity for designing the APS studies.
