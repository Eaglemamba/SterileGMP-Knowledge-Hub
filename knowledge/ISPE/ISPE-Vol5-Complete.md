Introduction
1.1
Background
It is a universal regulatory expectation that pharmaceutical manufacturing facilities, systems, utilities, and equipment
are designed, constructed and qualified to be suitable for the intended purpose. Regulatory authorities have indicated
in their guidances and regulations that organizations must build quality into their processes and products.1 This Guide
intends to offer a compliant, integrated commissioning and qualification process that is based on a science and
risk-based approach. This process supports Quality by Design [4] and ensures that risks to product quality from the
systems2 are adequately addressed.
It is recognized that the terminologies for the commissioning and qualification process are varied. The overall
process has traditionally been called commissioning and qualification; ASTM E2500-13 [5] refers to the process
as verification. This second edition of the Baseline® Guide focuses on the approach; the terminology used is not
as important as the approach. For the purposes of this Guide, the term Commissioning and Qualification (C&Q)
is used to describe the process for establishing that facilities, systems, utilities, and equipment are suitable for
the intended purpose. The term verification is used to describe an activity performed during the C&Q process to
establish suitability for the intended purpose.
Commissioning and qualification differ only in that the qualification process has oversight by the Quality Unit, focused
on the system risk to product quality. The Quality Unit ultimately decides whether systems are suitable for the
intended purpose, based on the identification, testing, and documentation of the system features deemed critical to
product quality.
1.2
Purpose and Objectives
This Guide intends to offer a compliant, integrated C&Q approach to making the qualification process efficient and
cost-effective. The approach includes a System Risk Assessment process that identifies the Critical Aspects
(CAs) and/or Critical Design Elements (CDEs). CDEs are system functions and features identified as having the
potential to control risks to product quality and therefore ensure patient safety. Qualification confirms that the CDEs
and design risk controls identified through the System Risk Assessment have all been checked, tested, and found to
be installed and operating as specified. This approach demonstrates that the systems are suitable for the intended
purpose and can be accepted and released for the next stage (performance qualification or manufacturing operations,
as appropriate).
The approach described in this Guide involves utilizing properly performed and documented verification or testing
completed at any project phase to establish that the system is qualified (suitable for the intended purpose). The
utilization of commissioning tests as well as vendor testing can yield an efficient compliant program. The benefits
of this approach are maximized with a strong trusting relationship between vendors, service providers, and
pharmaceutical organizations.
This Guide recognizes industry best practices regarding C&Q and presents concepts that can assist organizations in
meeting current regulatory expectations for system qualification by:
•
Providing guidance on how to apply the Quality Risk Management (QRM) [3] process to C&Q to establish CDEs
•
Defining the focus of an integrated C&Q effort to be the CAs/CDEs and Critical Process Parameters (CPPs)
which provide the ability to deliver the product Critical Quality Attributes (CQAs)

1.3
Scope
This Guide applies to systems used by pharmaceutical manufacturers of human and veterinary drugs, including
biological drug products.
Medical devices have separate regulatory requirements and are outside of the scope of this Guide. For combination
devices, this Guide can be applied to the manufacturing equipment for the combination device, but not to the device
itself.

The process begins with the development of the URS (Chapter 2). This includes defining the product quality related
user requirements that must be met for a system to be suitable for the intended purpose.
The next step in the process is system classification (Chapter 3). The system is categorized as direct impact or not
direct impact, depending on its potential impact to product quality. Either the system has direct product quality impact
or it does not. A system classification tool is provided to facilitate this determination and to document the results.
Direct impact systems are commissioned and qualified while systems that are not direct impact are commissioned.
The next step in the process is System Risk Assessment (Chapter 4), the application of QRM in which a risk
assessment of the direct impact system is used to examine the system product quality risk controls and to identify
CDEs. It is important for the System Risk Assessment to be performed by Subject Matter Experts (SMEs) that
understand the science of the process. CDEs are identified by examining the CPPs that need to be controlled to
ensure product quality, as defined by the CQAs. CPPs are not only those that control delivery of drug substance
or drug product CQAs, but can also be related to CDEs of packaged materials (e.g., ensuring inclusion of product
inserts or variable data such as lot number and expiration date on labeling). The CAs of the process can be identified
from the CPPs, and the CDEs of the system can be identified from the CAs. CDEs can also include features such as
product contact materials of construction. Unacceptable risks are mitigated through the application of risk controls,
which include the CDEs.

C&Q testing and documentation (Chapter 7) is initiated once construction (including fabrication of equipment
at vendor facilities) has reached a point that the testing is meaningful and all prerequisites are met. Testing and
verification do not have to wait until construction is completed. This may include pre-delivery or receipt inspection,
Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT), construction quality document verification, and Site Acceptance Testing (SAT).
C&Q planning should identify aspects of the FAT and SAT that will be utilized to satisfy qualification requirements.
C&Q testing can include clean utilities performance testing and room environmental qualification, also known as
Performance Qualification (PQ), per organization/site policies and procedures.
Completion of testing leads to acceptance and release (Chapter 8), the last phase of C&Q. Completion of this phase
acknowledges that the system is suitable for the intended purpose and can be released for PPQ or for commercial
use, depending on the system type. PPQ is outside of the scope of this Guide.
It is important to ensure that systems remain suitable for the intended purpose after acceptance and release, and
while in use. This is accomplished by various management systems (such as calibration, maintenance, and change
control systems) and a periodic review program (Chapter 9).
The additional programs necessary to support the C&Q process are described in Chapters 10 through 13 and are
used throughout the C&Q process.
The vendor assessment for C&Q documentation (Chapter 10) is used to help identify vendor documentation that can
be used for C&Q purposes. It should be performed prior to finalizing the C&Q Plan.
The overall quality program supporting the C&Q process is the Engineering Quality Process (Chapter 11). This
addresses the quality practices for the C&Q process and is intended to be part of the overall Pharmaceutical Quality
System (PQS) [9] and QRM.
It is important to implement a change management program (Chapter 12) to capture changes as they are identified,
assessed, and implemented.
Good Documentation Practices (Chapter 13) are important in all C&Q process steps and in other support programs to
demonstrate data integrity.
This revision to the Guide is not intended to drive companies to repeat qualification for their existing systems, but to
identify an efficient, science and risk-based approach for future C&Q efforts and provide strategies for implementation
(Chapter 14). Guidance is also provided for modifying existing systems with legacy qualification documentation.
It should be noted that the process, as described in this Guide, is scalable. Depending on the project size, complexity,
and novelty, the documents and deliverables may be combined. As appropriate, this should be documented in the
C&Q Plan.
The appendices provide additional details and examples to the C&Q program. The examples have been included in
the Guide to enhance understanding of the principles. The reader is encouraged to utilize the examples for illustrative
purposes. These examples are not intended to be the only way to execute the process; the intent is to demonstrate
the concepts as applied to example systems. Individuals creating documentation for their systems should involve
those in their organization/site to adapt the examples to meet their needs.

1.6
Key Concepts
1.6.1
Efficient Approach without Redundant Testing
The foundation of this Guide is based on the expectation that engineers and system vendors ensure systems are
installed and operate to the meet the design specifications and user requirements. This is commonly referred to
as commissioning. Best industry practice is to utilize such an approach when installing any new system. However,
the level of testing and documentation for commissioning is variable within the industry. This Guide establishes the
rationale and a methodology for aligning testing and documentation expectations with the intended purpose of the
system.

When a system has direct impact on product quality (direct impact system), the CDEs of the system can be verified
by the approach described in this Guide. This approach ensures that identified CPPs are maintained; CPPs
ensure that CQAs are attained. Testing of system CDEs is utilized to support qualification of the system. Thus, the
documentation of the testing performed in commissioning needs to be acceptable to be presented in a regulatory
audit or inspection. The acceptance of that documentation is based on adherence with Good Documentation
Practices fundamentals (as described in Chapter 10).
1.6.2
Terminology for Product Impacting Attributes
A common source of debate is regarding the terminology for CPPs and CQAs. For systems that are directly used
to manufacture pharmaceutical products, these terminologies are easily identified and supported. However, there is
also a recognition that critical utilities, such as Water for Injection (WFI) systems, may not have product CQAs but
do have compendial requirements and CPPs. There is a recognition that the output of upstream processes (e.g.,
fermentation) and utilities (e.g., WFI system) need to meet specific quality attributes as defined by process definition
or in compendial requirements. These parameters can directly influence the system’s ability to deliver product
meeting those attributes.
These parameters can be termed critical parameters but not in the context of final product quality attributes. The
authors of this Guide continue to use the terminology of CPPs and CQAs for all systems, including WFI and upstream
equipment. It is important for individual organizations/sites to define what is critical for their products, processes, and
systems (e.g., CQAs, CPPs, Critical Parameters, Key Operating Parameters, System Process Parameters, etc.). The
criticality designation should drive the activities related to design, installation, and operation throughout the system
lifecycle.
1.6.3
Quality Unit Oversight
Another common source of debate within the C&Q effort is which group should approve what document. The Quality
Unit is expected to have oversight of the entire validation lifecycle including qualification. Because the Quality Unit is
not expected to have expertise in all aspects of pharmaceutical manufacturing systems, identified SMEs (including
system vendors) should be responsible for determining how best to design and test systems to ensure they are
suitable for the intended purpose. It is important that the SMEs have sufficient knowledge of the processes and
systems to ensure the systems are properly designed, tested, and operate as designed. The Quality Unit needs to be
engaged in the process, be considered part of the C&Q team, approve the appropriate testing plans and documents
for systems that directly affect product quality, and approve the final acceptance of the qualified state and release
to support pharmaceutical manufacturing operations. The authors see this as a Quality Assurance (QA) role, not a
documentation quality control role. An example approval matrix is provided in Chapter 6.

2	 User Requirements Specification
2.1
Introduction
The User Requirements Specification (URS) brings together system requirements from multidisciplinary sources to
support system design, C&Q, operations, and maintenance. Within the C&Q process, it is a foundational document
that identifies the product and process requirements for the system. These product quality related user requirements
are based on product knowledge (CQAs), process knowledge (CPPs), regulatory requirements, and organization/site
quality requirements. The product and process requirements in the URS are inputs to the C&Q process; they provide
the science-based knowledge that forms the basis for applying QRM to C&Q.
It is recommended that a URS is developed for each system that has the potential to impact product quality, or a
direct impact system (as determined through system classification described in Chapter 3). A formal URS document is
not always necessary. A statement of the URS may be contained in various forms, e.g., purchase request, functional
specification, change request, or data sheet. For standard off-the-shelf or simple catalog systems, a statement of
the URS may be a purchase order, vendor cut sheet, or catalog data. The system would be verified against this
document.

2.3
Approval of User Requirements Specification and Changes Post-Approval
The URS should be approved by the system owner, an appropriate SME, and the Quality Unit. However, if the Quality
Unit has already approved the source documents from which the quality requirements within the URS were obtained,
then the Quality Unit does not need to approve the URS. Refer to Section 6.4 for an example document approval
matrix.
The URS should be updated through the system lifecycle (from development through sustaining operations) until
decommissioning of the system. After approval of the URS, changes or additions with the potential to impact product
quality are evaluated against the System Risk Assessment (refer to Chapter 4) to confirm if risks are acceptable or to
determine if additional risk controls are required. Changes to the URS are performed through change management
(refer to Chapter 12).

3	 System Classification
3.1
Introduction
This Guide presents an approach for classifying systems into two categories depending on the system capability to
impact product quality. System classification establishes whether a system is commissioned and qualified or only
commissioned, as follows:
•
Direct impact systems are commissioned and qualified
•
Not direct impact systems are commissioned
Note: In the previous edition of this Baseline® Guide, component classification could be carried out as a subset
of system classification. Component classification is no longer necessary since the System Risk Assessment (as
described in Chapter 4) identifies CDEs for the system in a more efficient manner.
For systems that are not direct impact, the scope of commissioning is determined by SMEs and is based on the
business and HSE criticality of the system. For example, a chilled water system serving an administrative building
(not direct impact) may be balanced only. Meanwhile, a chilled water system (not direct impact) serving direct impact
process equipment may be balanced, with the balancing confirmed at the minimum and maximum design flow rates
to ensure reliable system performance during operation.
3.2
System Boundaries
Projects are commonly divided up into systems to facilitate construction management, document collation, turnover,
and C&Q. System boundaries are markings on design drawings to distinguish systems from one another; this
includes all components and piping to logically define what is included and what is not included within the system.
System boundaries should be defined and documented early on in the project (at the BOD phase) to enable
development of the systems list and subsequent execution of System Risk Assessments. The systems are usually
listed in the C&Q Plan (refer to Chapter 6). System boundaries should be defined such that the systems cover the
entirety of the project scope; this is typically documented in the system Process and Instrumentation Diagram (P&ID)
or Process Flow Diagram (PFD).
The scope of supply from vendors is often used as the basis for defining system boundaries; however, these
boundaries should be carefully reviewed as there may be benefits in defining the boundaries differently. For example,
boundary considerations for a direct impact compressed air system are as follows:
•
A Point of Use (POU) filter is typically outside the vendor scope of supply for a compressed air system but may
or may not be included in the system boundaries.
•
The POU filter could be included within the boundaries for the compressed air distribution system. All
compressed air testing and verification activities would be part of the compressed air system.
•
Alternatively, the POU filter could be included in the process system for which the air is being supplied to. All
compressed air testing and verification activities for the POU filter would then be part of the process system.
During the early stages of a project, system boundaries may also be subdivided to facilitate development of C&Q
documentation by different suppliers. For example, the URS for a compressed air system can include generation and
distribution sections, each of which may have its own set of C&Q documentation.

4	 System Risk Assessment
4.1
Introduction
The System Risk Assessment is the application of QRM to examine the product quality risk controls for direct impact
systems. This assessment identifies the critical design controls (CAs/CDEs) and procedural controls that are required
to mitigate system risks to an acceptable level. It is important for the project team performing the System Risk
Assessment to include technical SMEs that understand the science behind the process and the risks associated with
the CQAs.
System Risk Assessments should be developed for direct impact systems (as determined through system
classification described in Chapter 3). For standard off-the-shelf or simple catalog systems, some organizations may
consider it unnecessary to perform the System Risk Assessment; a simple protocol or report (e.g., Installation and
Operational Verification) could be used to provide the documented evidence that the installation and operation meet
the system requirements.

Acceptance and release of the system (described in more detail in Chapter 8) involves confirming that the controls

•
The design controls (CAs/CDEs) have been shown to operate as specified
•
The procedural controls are in place
4.1.1
Rationale
The traditional approach to qualification did not directly utilize commissioning documentation; instead, it often relied
on an additional set of documents, the Installation Qualification (IQ) and Operational Qualification (OQ) protocols.
These protocols provided the documented evidence that the system was installed and operating according to its
specification. The scope of the IQ/OQ was determined by various elements such as organization/site established
guidelines, vendor specifications, user requirements, and regulatory and guidance documents.
This traditional approach to qualification often resulted in IQ/OQ activities covering more scope than was necessary
and repeating successful tests already performed during engineering start-up and commissioning. Furthermore,
commissioning activities were commonly not robust enough or well documented since engineering teams anticipated
that additional or repeat testing would be performed during qualification activities.
This Guide proposes a more streamlined approach to C&Q. Engineering documentation (i.e., commissioning
inspection and testing documentation) is used to provide the documented evidence that the system has been
installed and operates to meet the specifications. The CQAs and related CPPs are documented for the system, and
a System Risk Assessment is developed to define potential risks to the CQAs and identify design (CAs/CDEs) and
procedural controls used to mitigate these risks. Qualification confirms the system is suitable for the intended purpose
and that the risk controls (design and procedural controls) are in place and functioning as specified.
4.1.2
Purpose and Benefits
The System Risk Assessment has multiple purposes and is a means to:
•
Identify, assess, and record the quality risks to CQAs and associated controls
•
Identify and document the design and procedural controls that are required to control and manage the identified
risks
-
The rationale for these controls can also be documented in the System Risk Assessment
-
Testing of the design controls (CAs/CDEs) and verification of procedural controls provides the
documentation used to support system qualification
•
Provide guidance for the scope of the DQ, i.e., the scope covers the design of the CAs/CDEs
•
Provide the information that is required for the basis of acceptance and release
•
Provide a focus for Quality Unit oversight
The System Risk Assessment establishes (if not available in other documents) and documents the CAs/CDEs of
the system operation and design that can impact product quality. It identifies the design and procedural controls
that would mitigate those risks to product quality to an acceptable level. By verifying these controls, the system is
confirmed to match that risk profile.

System Risk Assessment Process
As a prerequisite, the CQAs and associated CPPs should be identified and documented in the URS.
This Guide presents the following approach to performing the System Risk Assessment:
•
A small team develops an initial baseline draft that includes development of the process description and

•

4.2.3
Using Completed Assessments as a Starting Point for New System Baseline Drafts
The experience and documentation gained from completing System Risk Assessments can be applied to future
systems. In addition, many systems used for pharmaceutical manufacturing and packaging are standard systems that
have been in use for a reasonable period of time, have built-in control strategies, and are developed by experienced
vendors with established engineering quality systems. Therefore, any new systems that are identical to or similar to
existing systems can utilize the completed System Risk Assessments as a starting point.
The small team that develops the baseline draft (as described in Section 4.2.1) should assess the new system
against the completed System Risk Assessment for the existing system that is identical/similar. The SMEs should
determine if the system under review has different functionality and/or components. If the SMEs determine that these
differences may introduce additional or different risks, then the baseline draft needs to be updated to include the risks
and associated risk controls.
The remainder of the System Risk Assessment process would proceed as described in Section 4.2.2. The broader
team reviews the baseline draft and determines the risk profile for the new system.
The use of completed assessments as a starting point for new system baseline drafts can improve the efficiency and
consistency of the process.

4.3
Timing of System Risk Assessments
4.3.1
Development of the System Risk Assessment
The System Risk Assessment can be developed after completion of concept design and prior to the start of detailed
engineering. It can be updated later as required. The System Risk Assessment can be completed when the Issued
for Construction (IFC) design drawings and specifications are available, whether for new or modified systems. These
drawings and specifications represent what will be built including any incorporated changes made through the DR/DQ
process. Therefore, the System Risk Assessment can be confirmed and approved at this stage.
Note: Depending on the level of product and process knowledge, some of the CDEs associated with CAs may
be known while the balance of the CDEs will be identified during the engineering design lifecycle (and can be
documented as part of the DQ). All CDEs should be listed in the System Risk Assessment.
The System Risk Assessment defines the system risk profile, i.e., what it will be after the risk controls have been
verified to be in place and operating as specified. Until the verification is completed, it is considered a provisional risk
assessment.
For novel systems, starting the System Risk Assessment earlier in the design process can be beneficial since this
allows for any identified risks to be controlled by design. Most risk control is achieved through system design; a
procedural control is not considered as robust.
For standard or catalog systems where the potential risks are well understood and design controls are built in
where possible, the System Risk Assessment could be performed at any time before the final DR of the system.
Alternatively, an Installation and Operational Verification document could be used. DQ can also confirm that the
system risk controls are adequate.

5	 Design Review and Design Qualification
5.1
Introduction
This chapter provides guidance for performing and documenting Design Reviews (DRs) and Design Qualification
(DQ). This Guide emphasizes the importance of DRs leading to DQ as a continual process throughout all phases in
the design of new or modified systems.
DRs provide assurance that the design deliverables are consistent with the URS and the mitigation control strategies
developed during the System Risk Assessment. When properly performed, DRs reduce the need for costly design-
related change orders.
DQ is performed only for direct impact systems (as determined through system classification described in Chapter
3). DQ involves more formal documentation, with Quality Unit approval, to show traceability of the CAs and relevant
CDEs in the design to the CPPs and CQAs.
DR and DQ are not intended to be separate activities, but rather separate documentation in which DQ is focused on
CAs/CDEs and involves the Quality Unit as an approver. There should be minimal duplication of work; the final report
from DR is a key input into the DQ process.
5.2
Design Process
The design process can be divided into several phases to ensure the design deliverables are sufficiently developed,
the risks are identified and mitigated, and the project (as defined) meets financial objectives. The phases of the
design process are:
1.
Concept
2.
Basis of Design (BOD)
3.
Detailed Design
The concept and BOD phases define the Scope of Work (SOW) for the project.
In the concept phase, several approaches (scope options) to meeting the URS may be evaluated. These alternatives
should be evaluated for their impact on the business objective, project cost, and risk to quality. The project
stakeholders should select the preferred scope option; the project then progresses to the BOD phase.
In the BOD phase, additional engineering work provides a more developed SOW. The BOD design package
contains Issued for Design (IFD) documents; it is the basis for the detailed design phase which delivers Issued for
Construction (IFC) documents.
5.2.1
Importance of Good Engineering Practice
During the design process, it is important to follow GEP to support the integrity of related data and documentation.
DR activities include evaluating deliverables against engineering standards and business, operations, and quality
requirements as well as identifying gaps and proposing required corrective actions. Documenting these activities
using Good Documentation Practice concepts (as described in Chapter 13) supports the overall effort to ensure the
system is suitable for the intended purpose.

5.2.2
Science and Risk-Based Approach to Design Reviews
A quality risk assessment of the manufacturing process (including the environmental conditions supporting the
process and/or critical systems) is a significant activity outside of the C&Q scope that can influence the early phase
of design. Design stage System Risk Assessment typically uses knowledge from process risk assessments and other
process knowledge to evaluate system design with respect to process control failure modes. These assessments are
an integral part of the science and risk-based C&Q approach.
Design stage quality risk reviews are an iterative process that is linked to the design development of the process
system. The review of the System Risk Assessments and how the results affect the proposed design should be
included in the scope of DRs.
5.3
Design Review
5.3.1
Objectives of Design Review
DRs are defined in ASTM E2500-13 [5] as follows:
“Planned and systematic reviews of specifications, design, and design development and continuous
improvement changes performed as appropriate throughout the life-cycle of the manufacturing system. Design
reviews evaluate deliverables against standards and requirements, identify problems, and propose required
corrective actions.”
DRs include both quality critical and non-critical aspects of the system design.
The documented results of the DR should be the basis for any design modifications/additions performed to correct
any deficiencies in the design under review.
5.3.2
Role of Subject Matter Experts and Quality Unit
Throughout the design process, a cross-functional project team (refer to Section 6.4 for example approval matrix)
reviews the design of the system to ensure that the product and process user requirements, GMP, and regulatory
requirements are included. Thus, it is important that this team includes technical SMEs that have the appropriate
experience and correct knowledge of the process, process equipment, control strategies, and facilities to ensure that
the design under review will meet the requirements of the URS, function efficiently, and consistently produce quality
product.
If the system being reviewed is direct impact, then the team should also include a Quality Unit representative to
ensure that all quality requirements are met. This can help to facilitate the review and approval of the DQ.
5.3.3
Design Review Process
Prior to the DR, design documents should be circulated to the DR project team for their review to ensure a
comprehensive review process. It is the responsibility of the project manager and the design leads to ensure that the
appropriate participants are part of the DR. Senior management from the Quality Unit, operations, and production are
responsible for ensuring the proper representatives with the appropriate experience are present during the DR.
The project team should define the method for conducting DR according to the system type, size, and risk. This
method should be documented and approved in applicable plans (e.g., Project Execution Plan, C&Q Plan).
The effort, formality, and documentation of DRs should follow the ICH Q9 [3] principles of being commensurate with
the level of risk. DR documentation may take the form of engineering meeting minutes or notes. For highly critical
novel systems, DRs may be more focused and detailed.

For off-the-shelf equipment, DR should consider the complexity and/or risk to system or facility operation and

5.4
Design Qualification
5.4.1
Objectives of Design Qualification
DQ provides documented verification that the design of a new or modified direct impact system will result in a system
that is suitable for the intended purpose. The objectives of DQ are to:
•
Verify the design will meet the quality user requirements as listed in the URS
•
Verify the design will adequately control risks to product quality and patient safety, as identified during the
System Risk Assessment
•
Verify the CAs/CDEs necessary to implement these requirements and risk controls
5.4.2
Prerequisites
The prerequisites for DQ include:
•
Approved URS
•
Functional requirements (for automated systems)
•
System Risk Assessments with identification of design (CAs/CDEs) and procedural controls that will be used to
mitigate the unacceptable risks
•
Completed DRs, with verification that all open design issues that affect CAs/CDEs, CPPs, and CQAs are closed
and included in the final design
It is the responsibility of the project manager to ensure that all prerequisites have been completed and are made
available to the participants prior to the DQ for their review. The DQ should be scheduled to allow adequate time for
preliminary review of the material to be covered.
5.4.3
Design Qualification Process
DQ is the documented process for the review and approval of the final system design to verify the following:
•
All process and product requirements in the system URS have been incorporated into the design
•
Quality and regulatory requirements in the system URS have been incorporated into the design
•

6	 C&Q Planning
6.1
Introduction
C&Q is a planned, documented, and managed engineering activity that starts with the establishment of the URS
and continues to start-up and acceptance and release of systems. A well-planned program is essential and leads to
an efficient, “right the first time”, and cost-effective C&Q effort; the result is the delivery of systems that operate in a
safe manner, meet established design requirements and stakeholder expectations, and are suitable for the intended
purpose. Planning provides the ties between process understanding and system design; it focuses qualification
efforts to enhance the likelihood that the system will deliver quality products while maintaining a controlled operating
state. It also acts as a means for communication between members of the multidisciplinary project team.
This chapter focuses on the formal C&Q Plan. There are other plans that can be generated, such as overall project
plans or cleanliness plans, which have great value but are not covered in this chapter. Refer to Chapter 21 (Appendix
7) for a listing of supporting plans and general concepts for each.
Regulatory expectations for C&Q planning are described in Chapter 15 (Appendix 1).
6.1.1
Purpose and Benefits
A well-conceived and executed C&Q Plan can greatly facilitate a timely and cost-effective qualification effort. It is
essential to have excellent communication, planning, and coordination between the Quality Unit, vendors, operations,
automation, engineering, construction, commissioning, and qualification personnel, to name a few, to deliver a project
on time, within budget, and meeting all expectations. A C&Q plan facilitates this communication.
An important secondary goal of the C&Q Plan is to foster an interdisciplinary team approach to C&Q. Well-planned
project logistics are crucial to the success of every project. A well-coordinated effort leads to a high quality, effective,
and efficient C&Q project.
A single, integrated, overall schedule should be developed with input from the entire project team. This schedule
should cover all systems (utilizing defined system boundaries as discussed in Section 3.2) and phases of the projects
lifecycle from construction to Process Validation/PPQ completion. This schedule should be updated and issued
regularly. These updates should not drive a C&Q Plan revision, but the C&Q Plan should identify how the schedule
updates will be captured and communicated.
An integrated schedule, using a critical path method, can increase the awareness of dependent activities and provide
a clear understanding of the critical path. Including all project team members in the schedule development will
engage the team members and foster better planning. All team members should comprehend the schedule format.
Ordering long delivery time items early can help timelines, but consideration should be made to ensure the process
requirements for those items are understood and included in the requisition. Understanding when to engage support
for C&Q helps to support the success of the project.
A well-planned effort can ensure that the user, business, regulatory, and patient expectations are met. All efforts to
ensure the system is suitable for the intended purpose (e.g., start-up, balancing, trial runs, establishing maintenance
programs) should be included in the planning phase since this is where the formality of the documentation can be
established. These expectations include:
•
Systems are delivered on time and within budget
•
Systems meet approved user requirements and are suitable for the intended purpose
•
Stakeholder needs, including speed to market, are met

6.2
C&Q Planning Approach
The amount of planning, documentation, reporting, and level of detail of the C&Q Plan should be based on
expectations of the organization/site, size and complexity of the system or project, and the potential impact on
product quality and patient safety.
A C&Q Plan should not repeat details that are in other documents but needs to capture the organization of those
documents in order to facilitate an efficient project execution.
A separate C&Q Plan is not always necessary. Aspects of C&Q planning can be included with other documents, such
as a validation plan or change control. For example, the simple change of adding a small system would not need
a separate C&Q Plan; the requirements could be incorporated into a change control plan. More complex systems
would benefit from a separate C&Q Plan, for example, the installation of a filling line with terminal sterilization and
secondary packaging or the doubling in capacity of a manufacturing site including additional utilities to support the
expansion.
6.2.1
Timing of C&Q Plan
In an overall scheme, the creation of the C&Q Plan falls in the process map as shown in Figure 1.1 (Chapter 1).
It is important to note that C&Q planning begins prior to the creation of any formal written plans. C&Q planning starts
during the planning and design phase of a project and continues through project implementation. A documented
strategy may be developed as a precursor to a C&Q Plan, to help align team members with the process and
personnel who will be responsible for C&Q.
A C&Q Plan should begin early in a project in the concept or early basic engineering phase.
The C&Q Plan needs to be approved before the respective testing documents (i.e., test instructions) are
approved, and such test instructions need to be approved before test execution starts to support qualification.
This is recommended for commissioning plans and test instructions. The C&Q Plan should address how existing
test documentation and test results from commissioning (including FAT, SAT, etc.) are referenced in qualification
documents, as appropriate.
6.2.2
Lifecycle of C&Q Plan
The C&Q Plan is not finalized until after the risk management process has reached a point to capture the impact of
product quality decisions or of establishing the CDEs.
C&Q Plans are expected to be kept up-to-date as changes to the project are made. Revisions of the C&Q Plan may
be necessary as new information is learned or as modifications to the design are made, e.g., changes in scope
(additions or removals), timing, or identification of additional CAs/CDEs.
Approval is recommended for these early plans and for subsequent changes made by the revision process; this
ensures agreement at various stages of the C&Q approach. Changes to the C&Q Plan should be reviewed and
approved by the original approvers (or their designate) and a revision history should be maintained of significant
changes to the Plan.
It should be noted that revisions may not always be necessary. Minor departures from the C&Q Plan may be
documented in the Qualification Summary Report with appropriate justification and rationale, as deemed appropriate
by the project team.

Elements to be Addressed

##### 6.2.3.1 Scope of Testing

System classification (as described in Chapter 3) identifies which systems require qualification. The System Risk
Assessment (as described in Chapter 4) identifies which elements in those systems require qualification. The
URS (as described in Chapter 2) and System Risk Assessment establish the scope of testing for qualification. The
equipment specifications establish the scope of testing for commissioning.
The testing approach is based on the most efficient method to test the system and should be captured in the C&Q
Plan. If systems are tested separately, the C&Q Plan should include a final test verifying that all systems are able to
work together prior to acceptance and release. If PPQ is included in a separate plan, the linkage to the execution of
that critical phase should be captured.

##### 6.2.3.2 Identification of Document Source

Engineering firms, vendors, contractors, quality, engineering, and owners provide the documentation necessary for
design, construction, commissioning, qualification, operation, and technical support of the systems. It is important to
identify the documentation source early in the process so the impact on the testing of the system is understood and
thus the quality of the documentation understood. This understanding will allow for utilization of the documentation to
support qualification.
For example, the decision should be made during planning if any FAT, construction, SAT, or commissioning testing
results and documentation will be used to satisfy the requirements to declare the system qualified or suitable for the
intended purpose. This decision is based on vendor assessments for C&Q documentation purposes (as described in
Chapter 10) and the criticality of the testing. It is often desirable to minimize the amount of retesting performed.
The C&Q Plan may also address the expectations of the documentation. It should be noted that pristine
documentation, i.e., documentation that has minimal errors or corrections, is not essential but the documentation
needs to be easy to follow and understand. Refer to Chapter 13 for guidance on Good Documentation Practice.

##### 6.2.3.3 Coordination with Overall Project Execution Plan

Another important aspect is working with the project manager to ensure that the planning of C&Q activities,
whether a separate plan or part of another plan, fits seamlessly into the overall Project Execution Plan. C&Q
activities should be properly reflected in the Project Execution Plan at an appropriate level of detail to be able to
identify interdependencies between C&Q as well as to other project activities. Commissioning is normally occurring
throughout construction and needs to occur when it is appropriate; e.g., commissioning the agitation system cannot
be completed until it is energized.
Having these activities in the Project Execution Plan supports:
•
Coordination of test activities among C&Q to prepare for FAT or SAT events
•
Ability to schedule sequence of testing for multiple systems (e.g., electrical supply before energizing a system)

•
Installation: Documentation supporting installation is successfully completed. This may include FAT and SAT
documentation as well as site commissioning for systems built at the site.
•
Operational: Documentation supporting the functionality and performance of the system after the system has
been mechanically completed. This may include testing performed in FAT and SAT.

7	 C&Q Testing and Documentation
7.1
Introduction
This chapter provides guidance on C&Q testing and inspection activities and associated documentation for recording
the results of the testing/inspection. The goal of testing and inspection is to confirm that the system has been installed
and operates to meet the requirements.
Although this chapter provides some general guidance on the safety aspects (e.g., pre-start-up checks), the specific
requirements for safety aspects and any local requirements (e.g., signage, access control, equipment protection,
Material Safety Data Sheets) are outside the scope of this Guide. It is the responsibility of the system designer to
produce a system that is compliant with any related local regulations.
7.1.1
Approach and Scope
C&Q testing and documentation starts as early as design and ends with the acceptance and release of the system
to the end-user. The process includes planning, document review, and static and dynamic testing of the installation,
function, and performance, including testing of any defined worst-case scenarios.
The requirements and stage of the testing/inspection should be predefined in the planning phase to avoid duplication
of testing. Testing should only be repeated if necessary. For example, retesting may be necessary if there are
changes that may impact a test result, e.g., differing utilities or disconnecting equipment and control systems after
FAT to facilitate shipping.
For systems where the automation is an integral part of the system (typical for manufacturing equipment, e.g., WFI
storage and distribution system with a dedicated control panel), the entire system is tested as an integrated system
comprising the mechanical, electrical, and associated automation.
The scope of testing varies depending on the project scope, but for a new process area would include facilities,
utilities, manufacturing equipment and their associated automation or process control systems.
Testing activities and rigor will depend on the type of system (e.g., manufacturing, automated, utilities), potential to
impact product quality, and on the project (e.g., green field, brown field, relocation, stick-built, modular, off-the-shelf
system).
7.1.2
Principles/Concepts
The C&Q testing and documentation approach described in this Guide is based on the following principles:
•
Testing is an engineering driven activity that produces the documented evidence that the installation and
operation meet the requirements
•
Documentation standards need to be predefined (see Chapter 13)
•
Training on the documentation standards need to be completed
•
Test witness and the associated notification requirements need to be predefined
•
Where vendor data will be used to support qualification, a vendor assessment needs to be performed (see
Chapter 10)
•
System changes need to be managed through Engineering Change Management after approval of the design
(see Chapter 12)

##### 7.3.2.2 Factory Acceptance Test: Installation Tests

The FAT is inspection/testing of the system at the vendor (system manufacturer) site. The objective of the FAT is
to provide testing and documentation at the earliest possible time, to verify that the vendor has fulfilled contractual
responsibilities and the system meets the requirements. This allows for quicker and more efficient remedy of any
failings and avoids the delays to schedule that would result from discovering problems later, on-site.
After completing the FAT, the owner representative agrees with the vendor whether the system is ready to be shipped
or on pre-conditions for shipping. Agreements are documented and approved by the vendor and owner.
Where a system needs to be disassembled for shipping, the process should be documented. Details should include
how the system will be disassembled, any marking or labeling of components, and how it will be reassembled and
tested on-site. This information provides the rationale for testing that can be accepted from the FAT as well as testing
that will be repeated as part of the SAT.

##### 7.3.2.3 Receipt Inspection

Receipt Inspection (RI) provides documentation that delivered components, materials, or systems conform to the
requirements, e.g., manufacturer, model, serial number as applicable with the required supporting documentation.
This is typically done immediately upon delivery of the component to the owner site.
RI also provides documentation that all components have been received in good condition and are tagged correctly,
where applicable.
Any items that do not pass RI are separated for issue resolution. Items that pass RI are stored under suitable
conditions for installation.

##### 7.3.2.4 Site Acceptance Test: Installation Tests

The SAT is the testing of the system at the owner organization site. The objective of the SAT is to provide testing and
documentation of the installed system, to verify that the vendor has fulfilled contractual responsibilities and that the
system meets the requirements prior to contractually accepting the system.
The SAT test plan includes a review of the FAT punch list (items carried over from the FAT that did not meet the
acceptance criteria/expected results) and the plan to resolve open items. The SAT also includes reconfirming FAT test
results where required due to system disassembly and reassembly.
After completing SAT testing, the owner representative agrees with the vendor whether the system is ready to be
turned over to the owner or on pre-conditions for system turnover. Agreements are documented and approved by the
vendor and owner. Acceptance of a system with open discrepancies can be made with the approval of the Testing
Coordinator and System Owner.

7.3.3
System Start-Up
System start-up is a GEP approach to ensure that the system is energized and started up in a safe manner. The
system start-up includes all mechanical, electrical, automation, and HSE related system functionalities. This is also
where adjustments to the system, settings to regulate the system, or any balancing needed are made. A prerequisite
to system start-up is that the system is mechanically complete without open critical punch list items for safety. System
start-up may be conducted both at the vendor facility and after installed on-site.
The vendor operation or start-up manual should be reviewed prior to energizing the system. For systems that include
vendor-provided control systems, training should be completed prior to energizing the system. Training of system
operators, engineers, and maintenance personnel should be conducted in association with system vendors and
properly documented.
System start-up is a four-step process, consisting of:
1.
Verification that the system is complete with all valve switches in the correct position.

For stick-built systems and modular systems, ensure that the integrated system (including automation) has been
reviewed before it is energized.
2.
Pre-start-up safety review.
3.
Supervised energizing of the system utilities.
4.
Pre-functional check of the system, with focus on safety functions.
Note that Steps 1 and 2 above need to be completed before Step 3. Step 3 needs to be completed prior to Step 4.

The scope of functional testing includes verifying system functionality. Functional testing may include mechanical,
electrical, automation, and HSE related aspects. Documents, drawings, and operational procedures may be drafted
and redlined, and discrepancy lists may be established. Initial operational tests of mechanical systems and general
operation of the system components is performed.
Typically, the final step of functional testing is a full operational testing. This can be a full load test of the system

conditions. Final tuning of control loops may need to be delayed until system balancing has been performed (e.g., air
or water balancing) or until enough system loads are available to establish various load levels, including full load.
Operational testing should demonstrate the ability of the system to perform its specified functions throughout the
expected operational range (which may be different than the specified range for equipment design/build purposes),
encompassing worst case conditions. Where systems are interconnected (e.g., packaging line), operational
verification should confirm satisfactory operation of the integrated line.
Functional testing includes the following activities, for example:
•
Testing integrated automation controls and equipment for the whole system developed from the knowledge of
processes, systems and equipment for both normal and alarm/failure modes
•
Testing analog control loops at a range of values representing the intended system use – the range of testing
should encompass the anticipated range of normal operation, including upper and lower operating limits (i.e.,
worst case conditions) without inducing process failure
•
Confirming the system can handle predictable simultaneous use scenarios for utility systems
•
Determining and recording manual component settings (e.g., valve positions, limit switch/screw positions, control
switch settings)

8	 Acceptance and Release
8.1
Introduction
This chapter provides guidance on the acceptance and release phase which is where all testing is formally
reviewed and accepted. The conclusion of the acceptance and release phase is that the system can be released for
manufacturing operations, the next stage of qualification (e.g., for a WFI system), or for use in the manufacture of
PPQ or commercial batches.
Acceptance and release represents the initiation of the “qualified state” and the beginning of QA-managed Quality
Change Control (see Chapter 12), continuous performance monitoring, and periodic review. The project team
transitions system ownership to operations/engineering.
The acceptance and release phase should occur as a cooperative review between the appropriate SMEs, including a
representative from the department that will accept care, custody, and control of the system. The SMEs should review
the results from an overall technical perspective.
If the system includes CAs/CDEs (direct impact), then the Quality Unit needs to be part of the acceptance and
release review and approval, focusing on CAs/CDEs. Acceptance of a system with open discrepancies should
be a Quality Unit decision. Typically, all discrepancies have to be addressed; however, non-product impacting
discrepancies may remain open with an appropriate approved action plan.
The acceptance and release phase is not intended to be a catchall for closeout of all system or project
documentation. For a commissioned system, the engineering SME will make a determination as to the requirements
prior to release. For a qualified system, the Quality Unit and an engineering/process SME should determine the
minimum requirements.

The installation, operational, and performance (as required) testing activities that should be completed prior to the
acceptance and release phase include:

Testing results should be reviewed
•
Unexpected results should be investigated
•
Discrepancies to the testing plan should be addressed
•
Engineering changes should be closed
•
Discrepancies should be addressed through Engineering Change Management and/or adjusted and retested

For large projects with interconnected systems, multiple reports may be used to complete the acceptance and
release phase. In this case, the completion of testing for each system may be documented with separate summary
reports. An overall acceptance and release report would reference the completed summary reports for each of the
interconnected systems (rather than listing the individual test descriptions, acceptance criteria, and results); this
report would document completion of all quality system elements and include the formal release statement. The
benefit to this process is that it allows the testing to be closed out prior to finalizing procedures which often include
information from performance testing or testing of multiple linked systems.
With the use of a separate acceptance and release report, it is possible to release a grouping of systems. One
strategy, as shown in Figure 8.4, may be to release per processing area (e.g., cell culture) after all testing is
completed and closed out with separate summary reports. A separate acceptance and release report would be
used to capture the completion of testing documentation and quality system elements implementation for the entire
processing area or grouping of systems.
Note: There are several factors to consider when using this approach. The grouping strategy may complicate future
changes. The grouping would also require presenting information from all systems during regulatory audits even
when only a single system is requested. The advantages are that qualification would more closely reflect the grouping
created by the process risk assessment as well as the batch record structure and eliminate potential gaps with the
integration of systems.

•

10	 Vendor Assessment for C&Q

Documentation Purposes
10.1
Introduction
This chapter describes vendor assessments3 and controls for work where vendors follow their own procedures to
perform testing and produce documentation that may be used to support system qualification. When vendor tests are
used to directly support qualification where appropriate, the result is reduced on-site testing and reduced cost and
time in the schedule without impacting quality. In addition, the SMEs on the project team can contribute to improved
product quality by focusing their attention on other higher risk quality critical activities.
Vendor assessments (for C&Q documentation purposes) do not include the vendor selection and/or commercial
assessment, though the results may impact those assessments. Vendor assessments for C&Q documentation
purposes may be integrated into this broader vendor selection and qualification process or comprise a standalone
activity for C&Q planning and execution purposes.
Regulatory expectations for vendor assessments are provided in Chapter 15 (Appendix 1). For detailed information
on assessing vendors of automated systems, refer to ISPE GAMP® 5: A Risk-Based Approach to Compliant GxP
Computerized Systems [6]. For detailed information on vendor assessments for design services, construction
services, and equipment supply, refer to ISPE Good Practice Guide: Project Management [27].
Note: It is not necessary to perform vendor assessments, as described in this Guide, for vendors that provide
qualification services (sometimes referred to as secondary services) when those vendors are contracted to follow the
regulated owner organization procedures and practices or applicable specialist standards, such as the US National
Environmental Balancing Bureau (NEBB) [28].
10.1.1
Purpose and Benefits
The objective of a vendor assessment is to establish confidence in the vendor and the vendor quality systems to
perform testing, manage changes, and provide documentation that is accurate, complete, and unambiguous, with
results matching what was expected. Vendor verification documentation assessments are highly recommended when
documents may be used to support qualification. Purchaser and vendor cooperation is key to successfully achieving
this goal.
Vendor assessments can minimize business/project risk associated with vendor performance, including project cost
and schedule risk, by performing testing and completing documentation deliverables required to support verification
of CAs (qualification) right the first time.
The use of vendor documentation can prevent repeating work that was appropriately performed and documented
and can help to achieve GMP compliance at a reduced cost and in less time. Vendors are the experts in their
particular systems and the use of vendor testing and documentation should be encouraged. As an example, consider
verification of product contact materials. Accepting a certificate of conformity from a quality vendor can be an
acceptable, low-risk approach for material verification; checking 100% of the related heat certificates would therefore
be unnecessary.

11	 Engineering Quality Process
11.1
Introduction
When the C&Q process is considered as a part of an organization-wide PQS, the result is an achievement of both
GMP and GEP concepts. A separate quality system is not required for C&Q; instead, it can be part of the overall PQS
or a subset of the PQS. For the purposes of this Guide, the term Engineering Quality Process is used to describe
the process within the PQS that covers C&Q. This Guide recognizes that this function is referred to differently across
organizations which have their own structure and assignment of roles and responsibilities. This chapter intends to
focus on the concepts that should be addressed for this function.
The overall organization PQS program provides the quality practices for processes in order to consistently deliver
quality products. The Engineering Quality Process, as a subset process of the PQS, addresses the quality practices
for the C&Q process and is intended to support the overall PQS program.
Both the PQS and Engineering Quality Process need to emphasize quality (i.e., the product or service meeting the
specified requirements) versus compliance (i.e., meeting current procedures or organization requirements). Good
quality practices and methods through the PQS produce robust reliable systems and ensures compliance. The
Engineering Quality Process needs to be established as part of the PQS; the Engineering Quality Process can then
be used for developing sustainable, consistent, cost-effective, and practical engineering processes for project and
operational lifecycle management.
11.1.1
Rationale
Current regulatory authority and ICH [12] guidances emphasize a focus on building quality into the product4 and
not by inspection. ICH Q10 [9] augments the defined GMPs by describing specific quality system elements and
management responsibilities that should be in place to achieve three main objectives; it states:
“Implementation of the Q10 model should result in achievement of three main objectives which complement or
enhance regional GMP requirements:

Achieve Product Realisation [Section 1.5.1]

Establish and Maintain a State of Control [Section 1.5.2]

Facilitate Continual Improvement [Section 1.5.3]”
The FDA 2006 Guidance for Industry: Quality Systems Approach to Pharmaceutical CGMP Regulations [15] states
the following as the goal of the guidance:
“This guidance describes a comprehensive quality systems model, which, if implemented, will allow
manufacturers to support and sustain robust, modern quality systems that are consistent with CGMP [Current
Good Manufacturing Practice] regulations.”
In alignment with ICH Q8 [4], the FDA guidance states:
“The overarching philosophy articulated in both the CGMP regulations and in robust modern quality systems is:
Quality should be built into the product, and testing alone cannot be relied on to ensure product quality.”
4	 The term “building quality into processes” was first coined in June 1960 by Eiji Toyoda.

Another useful resource in understanding Engineering Quality Process as a philosophy is the work of Dr. W.
Edwards Deming [29]. Deming believed in improving the process and in doing so using process measures to guide
improvement efforts. In his book [29], Deming states:
“Conformance to specifications may be achieved in several ways:
1.
By careful inspection, sorting the bad from the good. Dependence on inspection is hazardous and costly.
2.
By work on the production process to shrink variation about the nominal value.”
The approach presented in this Guide embraces regulatory guidance and Deming’s principle that demonstration of
conformance to specifications can be achieved by developing a process, which addresses causes and concerns for
variability and reduces reliance on inspection. Thus, the focus of the Quality Unit should be to ensure adherence to a
process that identifies CAs/ CDEs and confirms that they have been satisfactorily tested.

12	 Change Management
12.1
Introduction
Change management processes should be established and applied throughout the lifecycle of manufacturing
systems. The risk-based application of SME-led change management is one of the biggest challenges in the
implementation of QRM-based C&Q as it applies the principles of ICH Q9 [3]. This Guide presents an approach which
involves two modes of change management:
•
Engineering Change Management (ECM)
•
Quality Change Control (QCC)
Project change management that covers the elements of project scope, cost, and schedule is outside the scope of
this Guide.
A system is considered to be changed when it is modified, altered, added to, removed, or improved in a way that
makes its functions, physical features, or performance different from what they were prior to the change.
Replacing an existing part of a system with an identical one (like-for-like) may not be considered as a change.
Organizations should define and follow a robust assessment process for classifying changes as like-for-like.
12.1.1
Purpose and Benefits
Change management is a documented program that:
•
Defines what is subject to change
•
Determines the risk for product impact caused by the change\
•
Establishes a process for initiation, review, and approval of the request for change
•
Defines when the Quality Unit should be involved
•
Establishes a process for evaluation of the actual impact of the change
Change management is a well-managed process for instituting changes and is needed for the following reasons:
•
Ensures control of the impact on the current state of the system and the process it belongs to
•
Provides a basis for peer (SME) review and approval
•
Is a regulatory requirement (e.g., 21 CFR 211.100 [30] and EU GMP Volume 4 (§5.23) [31])
•
Is a good business practice
Change management processes should not be (or perceived to be) an obstruction to fostering innovation and
continual improvement. All stakeholders in the change management system should understand the purposes and
benefits of effective change management. Efficient use of change management adds value by delivering technically
sound solutions in an efficient manner and makes innovation and continual improvement less cost or time prohibitive.

#### 12.2.1 Scope and Sequence of ECM

International GMPs require that changes that have the potential to impact product quality and patient safety be
handled within the PQS (refer to Chapter 15 (Appendix 1) for the regulatory basis). This drives the need to establish
a defined endpoint for application of ECM to GMP and non-GMP changes within the project lifecycle; from this point
forward, full application of QCC to CAs/CDEs within systems commences.
Figure 12.1 (in Section 12.1) highlights the potential benefits to be gained when applying an SME-led science and
risk-based approach, by not commencing QA-managed QCC until the acceptance and release phase at the earliest.
From a regulatory perspective, it is only after the acceptance and release of a system (for execution of PQ/Process
Validation, if applicable, or for manufacturing for commercial use) that changes to systems with CAs/CDEs should go
through QA-managed QCC.
During the operational phase of a system, ECM should continue to be applied to non-critical aspects of the system.
Each change to the system should therefore be evaluated on whether CAs/CDEs are affected by the change.

13.2
C&Q Document Development Basics
Testing and verification documentation are usually written in the local language. Summary documents are generally in
English to facilitate regulatory review.
The use of forms/templates is encouraged to promote consistency. Forms/templates can be approved once when
created and then utilized multiple times without pre-approval if the relevant content is not changed. Where this
approach is used, there should be a procedure to ensure that the form/template used for data entry is current at the
time of use.
When forms/templates are used, the SME should review the form to ensure that it includes all of the required
testing for the system being verified; if additional tests are required, the form/template should be supplemented with
additional testing.
Test scripts may be approved individually and assembled into a test plan/report. If test data sheets are used to
supplement test scripts, they should be approved in accordance with the requirements defined in the test plan.
The test procedures should be clear and unambiguous such that the results are repeatable and reproducible, as
explained below:
•
Repeatable: The same result would be achieved if the same procedure is repeated by the same trained and
qualified personnel.
•
Reproducible: The test procedures should be performed the same way each time and the same result would be
achieved by different trained and qualified personnel.
The test document should indicate the order that the cases are to be executed. Acceptance criteria needs to be
established and it should be clear that the tests demonstrate that the acceptance criteria have been met.

14.1
Introduction
This chapter describes strategies how organizations can effectively transition to the science and risk-based C&Q
process described in this Guide. Implementing change successfully often requires high-level sponsorship due to the
numerous stakeholders impacted.
The key to a successful transition is to recognize that there are multiple ways that the regulatory expectations can
be met; adopting the science and risk-based approach does not invalidate current work or existing documentation. It
should be noted that any process used for C&Q is expected to be science and risk-based.
Qualification documents represent a picture of the system at a point in time; this information changes with time
and there are quality systems in place to manage this change (including associated revision of the qualification
and engineering record documents), such as change management, maintenance, calibration, and periodic review.
Therefore, existing documentation can remain acceptable to provide the documented evidence that the system is
suitable for the intended purpose.

Chapter 9: Periodic Review
Part of the product lifecycle is ensuring that the process remains under control and that facilities, equipment, systems,
and utilities remain in a qualified state for the full duration of manufacturing operations, as described below:
•
“Equipment, facilities, utilities and systems should be evaluated at an appropriate frequency to confirm that they

calibration procedures and schedules (21 CFR part 211, subparts C and D). The equipment and facility
qualification data should be assessed periodically to determine whether re-qualification should be performed and
the extent of that re-qualification. Maintenance and calibration frequency should be adjusted based on feedback
from these activities.” [2]

Detailed Meeting Agenda Items for GMP Design Review Meetings
The concept GMP design review is anticipated to be a high-level review of the concept package against the user
requirements and GMP expectations in the regulatory design basis, quality guidelines, and engineering design
standards. It is important to obtain consensus on major approaches and decisions to avoid later rework. The meeting
agenda list is not meant to be all inclusive and items may need to be added or deleted based on the project scope
and type. Items that should be covered include:
Concept GMP Design Review
A. General Project GMP Requirements
•
Overall project scope and business case requirements
•
Project type
•
Regulatory design basis review
B. Facility Layout and Material Handling
•
Overall facility layout
•
Room classification/hygienic zoning schemes
•
Product, people, material, and waste flow
•
Transitions requirements
•
Gowning levels
•
Cold rooms, freezer rooms, and incubator rooms (locations and requirements)
C. Process and Operations
•
Overall process summary
•
Identification of sterile process steps
•
Overall equipment cleaning philosophy
•
Review of product quality risk assessment including risk of contamination from (1) particulates internal and
external to the equipment, (2) microorganisms (for sterile products), and (3) other products or residues (i.e.,
cross-contamination)
D. HVAC
•
Room pressurization
•
Air handling unit zoning and segregation
•
Temperature and relative humidity requirements
•
Filtration
E. Support Functions
•
Identification of approach for required support functions including Quality Operations labs, in-process test
labs, warehouse space, sampling and dispensing areas, and offices impacting manufacturing areas
F.
Automation and Documentation Systems
•
Automation strategy
•
Critical parameter monitoring approach
•
Building Automation System
