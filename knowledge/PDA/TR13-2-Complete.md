# PDA Technical Report No. 13-2 (2020): Fundamentals of an Environmental Monitoring Program — Annex 1: Environmental Monitoring of Facilities Manufacturing Low Bioburden Products

## 1.0 Introduction

Environmental monitoring (EM) is used to evaluate the effectiveness of contamination controls used
in the manufacture of pharmaceutical products. EM programs need to be designed and implemented
based on the needs of the specific facility and processes involved. PDA Technical Report No. 13 (Re-
vised 2014): Fundamentals of an Environmental Monitoring Program (TR 13) focuses on the environ-
mental requirements for sterile product manufacturing (1). This addendum will provide elements
to consider when designing a risk-based EM program to support the manufacture of low bioburden
products using low bioburden processes.
Regulations and guidance are readily available for EM requirements supporting sterile, nonsterile,
and advanced therapy medicinal products (ATMP) manufacturing processes (2–6). Guidance for
EM supporting the manufacture of low bioburden products is very limited. Where it is, the docu-
ments often cite resources intended for aseptic processes and then recommend establishing a risk-
based approach to designing an EM program to support low bioburden processes (7, 8). This is
largely due to the fact that most manufacturing operations where low bioburden control is required
combine elements of both sterile and nonsterile processing. However, no specific guidance identifies
the elements to which these sterile and nonsterile guidelines should be applied.
This document provides a means of determining the critical aspects, assessing the associated risks,
and designing the appropriate EM program for areas where bioburden control of manufacturing pro-
cesses is required. It can be applied across many types of manufacturing operations used to produce
pharmaceutical products including biologics, small molecules, ATMPs, and others. This document
should be viewed as technical guidance and is not intended to establish any voluntary or mandatory
standards.

### 1.1 Purpose

This addendum complements the existing PDA TR-13 and is not meant to change or modify its
content. The addendum provides recommendations for the development of an environmental
control and monitoring program for classified areas and the associated GMP processes used for
manufacturing operations requiring bioburden control in the biotechnology and pharmaceutical
industries. The content includes a review of regulatory requirements and the development of risk
assessments based on the criticality and complexity of processes, as well as industry examples of these
risk-based approaches.
Risk assessment tools such as Failure Mode Effects Analysis (FMEA), Hazard Analysis and Critical
Control Points (HACCP), or hybrid approaches can be used for these evaluations. This addendum
presents a hybrid approach that is intended to help analyze operational risk in order to develop ap-
propriate levels of EM required to monitor operations. When implementing these tools in the design
of any EM program, the resulting program should fit the intended purpose. It should not unneces-
sarily impede the operation or introduce additional risk to the process.

### 1.2 Scope

TR-13-2 focuses on environmental controls and monitoring programs for classified areas where
manufacturing processes are required to ensure a low bioburden level of the material being produced.
Although a comprehensive EM program addresses both viable and nonviable aspects of the facil-
ity, this addendum concentrates primarily on the viable aspects as these parameters have a greater
potential to impact product and process quality related to bioburden. This document is not intended
to address EM programs for traditional aseptic or sterile manufacturing, which are covered in TR-13,
nor does it address traditional nonsterile dosage forms.

## 3.0 Low Bioburden Products and Processes versus Traditional

Sterile and Nonsterile Products and Processes
Sterile drug manufacturing is designed to produce a final sterile product through sterilizing process
steps and subsequent manufacturing processes and controls. In contrast, manufacturing processes that
require bioburden control are designed to limit the introduction of microorganisms into the process.
Based on the physical properties of the materials being manufactured or, in some cases, regulatory
requirements, mitigative steps designed as bioburden control mechanisms, such as the use of anti-
biotics, preservatives, solvents, low or high pH, sterile filtration and heat, may not be possible. In
many cases, the process streams for these processes support the proliferation of any microorganisms
present, often requiring control mechanisms during manufacturing operations to prevent or reduce
microbial growth. Specific process steps taken if microorganisms are present, such as purification and
ultrafiltration, often reduce bioburden levels although, in general, they are not specifically designed
to do so. Due to the inherent susceptibility of the manufacturing process in these cases, an added
focus must be placed on minimizing the ingress and proliferation of microorganisms and their me-
tabolites that can affect product quality, safety, and stability.
Control of the process and processing environment is a major component of reducing the potential
for product stream bioburden. Elements of control include the design of the facilities, utilities, and
processing equipment (closed/open), as well as personnel and procedural measures. EM is used as a
tool to measure the efficacy of these controls in a manufacturing facility.
The use of a local unidirectional airflow hood (UDF) for specific open manufacturing steps is a com-
mon practice where bioburden control is desired. These units are designed to produce a localized EU
Grade A/ISO 5 environment. In this application, the units are not being used to provide protection
for a sterile manufacturing-related operation but rather a nonsterile manufacturing operation as part
of a bioburden control program. In such cases, based on their use and a thorough risk assessment,
the placement of the UDF in Grade C environments is often acceptable. The level of gowning as-
sociated with their use should also be evaluated and requirements set based on the operation being
performed and the level of control required.
The implementation and utilization of a quality risk management program provides scientific ratio-
nale for the manufacturing controls and EM program developed for these types of manufacturing
processes (10, 12). Quality risk management is predicated on understanding the specific risk factors
of the environment and the process used to manufacture the product.
Once the manufacturing process and the potential contributors to the level of bioburden (e.g.,
environmental factors, facility and/or equipment design) have been identified and characterized, a
thorough and well-documented risk assessment should be conducted to identify the points of po-
tential microbial ingress. The manufacturer may incorporate specific components of a regulation or
guidance document into the risk assessment as it applies to a particular process or step; this addition
may vary depending on the intent of the risk assessment. Upon completion of the risk assessment,
supporting programs can be developed to mitigate the risks identified. While all risks may need to be
monitored for a period of time, any risk that cannot be mitigated should continue to be monitored.
When a manufacturing area has been classified in line with a regulatory or industry classification
scheme, the EM program is often expected to meet the applicable requirements regardless of how the
area is used. For example, if an area is classified as EU Grade A, the EM program and other support-
ing programs (e.g., gowning, cleaning, and sanitization) must reflect the requirements contained
in the corresponding regulatory document. In many cases, these guidance documents are designed
specifically to address the manufacture of sterile medicinal products, not nonsterile manufacturing
operations designed to control but not eliminate the bioburden load present.
Consider the scenario of an aseptic transfer of materials in the UDF to minimize the introduction
of additional bioburden. This is a nonsterile manufacturing step. A risk assessment may demonstrate

that only certain aspects of the EU Grade A classification requirements are pertinent to the process.
Monitoring performed during the manufacturing process should be aligned with the risk assess-
ment and reflect the monitoring needed to support the process. For example, in a situation where
the concern is focused on the addition of bioburden (microorganisms) to the manufacturing opera-
tion the risk assessment may conclude that the need to perform total particulate monitoring during
the manufacturing operation should be reduced or eliminated. In that case, the total particulate
monitoring would still be performed at a set frequency to ensure the UDF is continuing to oper-
ate appropriately but not during the actual manufacturing step. Similarly, the surrounding room
environment should be determined based on the assessment of the manufacturing process and the
control needed. For traditional nonsterile operations, for example, the EU Grade C requirements
may be more appropriate based on the operation being performed. An alternative could be to classify
the area according to ISO 14644 (e.g., ISO 5, 7, 8), as appropriate for the specific process and area
(13). This approach must be documented in the site risk assessment. The supporting EM and other
control programs should be designed to monitor the room or facility to the levels needed for the
specific processes being performed.
An additional example where the use of a risk assessment may be of value is in the use of a UDF
where preparation of the inoculum used for seeding of a bioreactor is being performed. In this exam-
ple, during mammalian cell culture preparation, a designated room is used to prepare the inoculum
for seeding the bioreactor. In the scale-up process, aseptic techniques are used to transfer the contents
of a thawed cryovial to a sterile T-flask (or similar container). This activity is performed in a UDF to
protect the process from the introduction of microorganisms, as microbial contamination would be
the largest concern. The UDF is classified as EU Grade A because an aseptic technique must be per-
formed in order to exclude the introduction of extraneous contaminants. This process takes less than
two minutes to execute. Based on the air quality criteria described in EudraLex, Volume 4, Annex 1:
Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal Products, both continuous nonviable particulate counts and active
airborne viable monitoring would be required during the manufacturing process based on the UDF
classification as Grade A (2). This would present some challenges if applied. Annex 1 has gener-
ally been interpreted to expect a sample size of one cubic meter of air for both types of monitoring,
which takes between 5 and 35 minutes to collect depending on the type of equipment used. Con-
sidering that the scale-up process takes only two minutes to execute, the requirements for the sample
volumes would be difficult to satisfy; however, if the process only takes two minutes, the monitoring
would encompass the entire process and meet the intent of the regulation. In this case, a risk as-
sessment should be completed to evaluate the process and monitoring requirements. If the primary
concern is the introduction of viable organisms, the risk assessment may conclude that performing
in-process total particulate counts not only provides little value but increases the risk to the operation
based on the distraction it creates for the operator (assuming the use of manually operated particu-
late counters) and should not be conducted during the process while viable monitoring is of greater
importance. As presented in the aseptic transfer example above, total particulate monitoring would
be expected at an established frequency to ensure the biological safety cabinet is performing properly.
A number of well-known risk assessment tools are available, such as flow charts, check sheets,
FMEA, and HACCP. In order to perform a successful risk assessment, the following must be in-
cluded to ensure that all aspects of the process are appropriately addressed:
• Knowledgeable representation from the functional areas involved in the process (e.g., validation,
manufacturing, quality, facilities, microbiology)
• Defined risk questions
• Detailed process map
• Facility map, including personnel and material movements
• Production batch records and standard operating procedures

## 4.0 Environmental Risk Assessment

Several risk assessments need to be performed as part of an overall facility risk assessment; these can
include a facility (also called an environmental) risk assessment, a process risk assessment, and an EM
risk assessment. The environment (or facility) risk assessment should determine the suitability of the
facility or environment in which the process is conducted. A process risk assessment should include
identification of potential sources of contamination and associated inherent risks to the process from
an operational perspective, including points of potential contamination. The EM risk assessment de-
termines how the contamination risks should be addressed within an effective EM control program.
All are part of the overall contamination control strategy for a facility.
This technical report focuses on the EM risk assessment that may be performed to:
• Develop or expand programs used to identify, control, and/or monitor contamination risks to
determine:
–	 Appropriate level of facility control for the process being performed
–	 EM locations, sampling frequency, and sampling methodology
–	 Appropriate environmental controls to be employed in manufacturing areas based on the type
of processing performed
–	 Develop or revise area and material transfer or cleaning and/or disinfection practices
• Determine gowning strategy and controls required to prevent contamination by personnel
• Determine appropriate personnel, material, and waste flows
• Develop or expand a bioburden and endotoxin-burden monitoring program
EM risk assessments can also be used to investigate a contamination event or trend, such as:
• Microbiological contamination in a process train or critical utility system
• Adverse data trend in a microbial measurement system (i.e., bioburden/endotoxin, EM, or critical
utility system monitoring)
• Identification and evaluation of a potential source of contamination or atypical recovery

### 4.1 General Guidelines

Risk assessments highlight manufacturing process steps or room locations that exhibit the greatest
opportunity for introduction of potential contamination to the product or process stream, a criti-
cal utility system, or the environment. A process flow map based on production records, applicable
standard operating procedures (SOPs), personnel/equipment/material flow diagrams, and piping and
instrumentation diagrams is used to identify individual process steps. Using a process flow map helps
ensure that each risk assessment is performed systematically and that all key aspects of the operations
or systems are considered. As a best practice, the system-owner maps out the process or systems prior
to development of the risk assessment by the multifunctional team to ensure that the scope of the
risk assessment is clearly identified.
Tours of the facility and manufacturing operations using the process flow map are also recommended
to collect information and determine the criticality factors relevant to the risk assessment being per-
formed (see Table 4.3.1-1 and Table 4.3.1-2). Only the criticality factors that apply to the scope of
the risk assessment should be selected.

### 4.2 Criticality Factors

Criticality factors assist in identifying contamination hazards by determining how each factor might
impact the potential for product, process, or environmental contamination. The criticality factors are
based on the various sources of contamination.

Eight major potential sources of adventitious microbial contamination sources and their relevance to
the risk assessment are discussed in Sections 4.2.1–4.2.3.5, topics that should be considered appli-
cable to any facility and addressed through the risk assessment.

#### 4.2.1 Personnel

The human body is the source of greatest risk of contamination in the manufacturing environment.
The design of the EM program should consider the risk to the various environments based on the
engineering and procedural controls designed to minimize the impact of personnel on the environ-
ment. Some potential controls are:
• Establishing maximum personnel load for the room or area
• Facility designs that eliminate the need for entrance into manufacturing areas (e.g., viewing galleries)
• Use of facility-dedicated shoes and clothing
• Gowning and/or personal protective equipment requirements and glove/sleeve sanitization proce-
dures, as dictated by the specific process or process step
• Unidirectional vs. bidirectional personnel flows
• Use of good aseptic working practices
• Personnel training and qualification
4.2.2	 Materials/Waste
The second major source of potential contamination is the transfer of materials into and waste out of
the manufacturing and support areas and, potentially, into exposed product. All ingredients, process-
ing aids, filters, components, labels, packaging materials, etc., must be disinfected (or sterilized if de-
termined appropriate) and managed in a manner that reduces the risk of transferring contamination
to the manufacturing and support areas. Waste should be managed in a manner that ensures a low
risk of microbial and/or product cross-contamination. A comprehensive EM program must include
sampling locations and frequencies to provide timely indications that materials and waste are being
transferred and handled appropriately.

#### 4.2.3 Facility Design and Construction

The design of the manufacturing and support areas and the implementation of the following mini-
mum requirements are needed to facilitate cleaning and disinfection activities, as well as minimize
the potential for contamination of those areas and the product (this list is not exhaustive):
• All surfaces should be smooth, impervious, nonabsorbent, unbroken, and compatible with ap-
propriate agents
• The amount of exposed surface area should be minimized to the extent possible (e.g., pipes
should be contained in interstitial spaces wherever possible)
• Construction materials should resist chipping, flaking, oxidizing, or other deterioration
• Wall and floor junctions should be curved, rather than meeting at sharp angles
• All flooring materials should be seamless
• Any recesses should be reachable and cleanable
• Ledges, shelves, cupboards, and equipment where particles or microbes can settle and accumulate
should be minimized
The facility design and construction should be appropriate for the proposed manufacturing process
and product. If during the execution of the risk assessment, the facility is found to be inappropriate
in any way, the deficiency should be addressed through appropriate mitigation and the efficacy veri-
fied through the EM program.

##### 4.2.3.1 Facility Cleaning and Disinfection

The types of cleaning and disinfection agents (i.e., sanitizers, disinfectants, and sporicides), method-
ology, and frequency should be considered when designing an EM program, and the frequency of
cleaning and disinfection activities should be justified based on historical EM data for existing facili-
ties. For new facilities, this frequency should be determined through appropriate in-situ qualification
as defined in PDA Technical Report No. 70: Fundamentals of Cleaning and Disinfection Programs for
Aseptic Manufacturing Facilities (4).

##### 4.2.3.2 Process Design

The design of the process should be considered when establishing the EM program. The degree of
manual manipulations, number and complexity of required steps, and the process duration all affect
the potential for microbial contamination to enter the system. Therefore, each step of the process
should be considered individually in the risk assessment. For example, open operations may provide
more opportunity for product contamination, whereas closed or hard-piped systems and isolators
can significantly reduce the risk of contamination as they prevent exposure of the process stream to
the environment and personnel. From an operational standpoint, additional bioburden reduction
steps, such as filtration, should be considered.

##### 4.2.3.3 Nature of the Product

The nature of in-process intermediates and final product should also be considered, such as the
potential for growth-promoting characteristics. For example, upstream cell culture media would be
highly sensitive to microbial proliferation whereas potential downstream purification materials with
high salt buffers may prohibit microbial proliferation.

##### 4.2.3.4 Environmental Air

Controlling air as a source of contamination is an important factor to consider when designing an
EM program. Points to consider include, but are not limited to:
• Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning design
• Number of air changes per hour
• Use of localized filtered air (e.g., UDFs)
• Use of pressurized airflow cascades
• Use of transitional spaces or rooms (e.g., airlocks and gowning rooms)
• Results of airflow visualization studies (e.g., smoke studies)

##### 4.2.3.5 Utilities

The main types of utilities used in controlled environments are water, compressed air or gases, vacu-
ums, and drains. Risk of potential contamination from exposure to these utilities should be consid-
ered when determining sampling locations and frequencies for an EM program. How these utilities
impact the process and product should also be considered, as they impact the quality and design of
the resulting EM program.

### 4.3 Risk Assessment Design

Many methods are available for designing and implementing a risk assessment. The selection of the
method should be based on the process in question and the relevant criticality factors selected. For
the purposes of illustration, Sections 4.4 and 4.5 present examples of hypothetical risk assessment
designs for a typical mammalian cell culture process (upstream).
Each risk-ranking is performed in accordance with the risk tool selected. The risk control activities
are performed according to the criteria specified in the applicable risk tool methodology. If a location

or process step is not well controlled and/or monitored and considered a high risk, then that location
or process step automatically becomes a potential source of microbial ingress, and tightening con-
tamination controls should be considered.

#### 4.3.1 Criticality Factors

Criticality factors are criteria that are divided into three main groups: process step, potential con-
tributors to contamination, and unusual/unexpected sources of contamination. Tables 4.3.1-1 and
4.3.1-2 provide examples of elements that aid in determining the level of risk—High, Medium, or
Low—and the criticality factors of each. These elements may change based on the manufacturing
operations, equipment, facility design, or other components. The criteria may be numbers or values
but need to be able to allow the user to evaluate the risk on a scaled basis. The criticality and weight-
ing for each criterion may vary depending on the area and process or other factors.

##### 4.3.1.1 Group 1 – Process Step

Each stage of the manufacturing process is considered a process step. The process steps shown in
Tables 4.3.1-1 and 4.3.1-2 are examples of those that would be used by the risk assessment team.
These may differ based on the products being manufactured, manufacturing process, stage of the
process, equipment set-up, facility age, and controls, among other elements.

##### 4.3.1.2 Group 2 – Potential Contributors to Contamination

These criticality factors include the manufacturing process, environmental factors, facility or equip-
ment design, and contamination control programs implemented, any of which may contribute to
the potential for contamination. For the factors in this group, enhanced contamination controls may
be identified during risk mitigation. In this mammalian cell culture example, the potential sources of
contamination are shown in Tables 4.3.1-1 and 4.3.1-2.

##### 4.3.1.3 Group 3 – Unusual/Unexpected Sources of Contamination

Group 3 evaluates sources of contamination that are extraneous to the design intent of the environ-
ment, equipment, or process. Any unusual or unexpected source of contamination is considered
a high risk that must be mitigated. This may include faulty or leaking equipment or utility con-
nections or breaches in the integrity of the manufacturing environment that are detected during a
facility walk-through. Since the source of this hazard is unexpected and must be mitigated, controls
should be implemented, and it should be monitored until the unexpected source of contamination
has been eliminated. Examples include, but are not limited to, a reverse in pressure differentials due
to a power failure, presence of contractors and their equipment, damage to the facility surfaces, or
condensation on piping.

#### 4.3.4 Conclusion

EM is an important element in bioburden control strategies for environments used for manufactur-

The output of the EM risk assessment is the level of risk at the EM sampling location expressed as a
high, medium, or low EM sampling priority. The EM sampling priority provides information used
to assess the EM sampling plan for each room by EM sample location. This ensures the identifica-
tion of EM sample locations within the rooms that provide meaningful EM data and those sample

contamination risk or low detection capability.

mented to increase their environmental control. Medium EM sampling priority locations presented
an acceptable level of risk and provided meaningful EM data. Low EM sampling priority locations
were assigned in areas that either posed a very low risk of product contamination or sites that may
have had higher risk classes, but the risk was mitigated by high-detection capabilities. The priority
may have equated to a frequency or need to sample based on the potential to contribute to overall
environmental contamination risk.

### 4.5 Risk Assessment Design: Example 2

This report summarizes results of the risk analysis conducted to evaluate the EM sample plan for a
media supplementation (via aliquoting) process for a cell therapy manufacturing process.
Knowledgeable personnel from the microbiology, manufacturing, quality assurance, compliance, and
manufacturing science and technology departments participated in the risk analysis.
In cell therapy manufacturing, media supplementation is performed by adding sterile supplements
into sterile media using a sterile pipette or other sterile transfer method. The process occurs inside
an ISO 5-classified UDF hood. Routine EM is performed based on the results of a risk assessment.
Personnel are appropriately trained on the process and on the proper aseptic techniques. Personnel
also wear sterile gloves and sterile sleeves while performing the process.
The media, supplements, and containers used during the process are sterile. In addition, an absence-
of-contamination test is performed post-process using a rapid microbiology method.

#### 4.5.1 Executive Summary

The EM program is designed to monitor manufacturing areas and ensure that the levels of environ-
mental controls are maintained as specified in site SOPs and in the laboratory information manage-
ment system. Using FMEA and following the risk assessment guidance methodology, an EM risk
analysis was conducted to identify and quantify risks. The effort was intended to optimize the EM
sample plans for media supplementation as well as to determine the appropriate ISO/Grade level
needed. (See Table 4.3.1-1 for recommended classifications and types of activities.) Other risk tool
methodology may be used depending on the specific process.

#### 4.5.2 Discussion

A risk tool was executed using appropriate methodology to evaluate contamination risks during
media supplementation. EM sample site locations, including personnel monitoring, and the types
of monitoring were determined based on the nature of the process. The nature of the materials (i.e.,
sterile), duration of the process, personnel, material flow, and post-processing testing were considered
during the analysis. The risk analysis evaluated each EM sample location for hazards including the
various physical conditions, operational activities, and EM-specific indicators that could contribute
