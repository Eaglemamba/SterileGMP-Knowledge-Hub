The success factors defined in the charter will serve as the boundaries within which the TT Teams will
work (see Section 5.1.2 for more details). These success factors should be developed by the TT PM
with agreement and input from the steering committee. Often based on business and regulatory strate-
gic objectives, some typical success factors that would be documented in the TT charter might include:
•
Right-first-time execution of process validation batches
•
Demonstration of comparability
•
Regulatory filing submitted
•
Preapproval inspection (PAI) authorized
•
Budget goals achieved
•
Time-to-market requirements met
•
Handover to the RU post-transfer operations team
•
Process improvements impacting production efficiency or cost
Any changes to the requirements for TT success or the business assumptions defined in the charter
should prompt the steering committee to revisit the Initiation and Planning stages to assess the poten-
tial impact of the changes.
Any changes to the charter after the Planning stage should be approved by the steering committee and
documented.

will have defined deliverables for each of the functional areas. While the Reference Guide recommends
a specific functional area to lead each activity and deliverable, all functional areas must intersect and
work in parallel to ensure success of the TT.

While a TT PM can use the Reference Guide to develop a robust TT program, the guide does not
address each unique requirement for an individual product transfer. The TT Team will need to de-
termine the specific requirements necessary to ensure that the RU obtains and implements all the
knowledge needed to manufacture safe, effective, and consistent product in compliance with CMC
requirements.

### 5.1 Initiation Stage

The Initiation stage covers the process of determining whether the TT of CMC activities for a phar-
maceutical or biopharmaceutical product should be performed. In this stage, the team evaluating the
need for the TT often has limited information on the product characteristics, process requirements,
and analytical testing from the SU, particularly if the product is still under development. While the
Initiation stage is primarily driven by the Business function of the MAH, technical experts also should
conduct high-level risk assessments.
A PM should be assigned to facilitate RU site selection and to develop the initial charter. This stage
results in a decision on whether to perform the TT, the choice of an RU site, the formation of a steer-
ing committee, and the development of an initial TT charter as well as an overall product strategy,
including business and regulatory considerations. Table 5.1-1, excerpted from the Reference Guide
(Figure 5.0-1), shows the activities and deliverables for the Initiation stage.

#### 5.1.1 Business

The Business function at the MAH takes the lead role in the Initiation stage, developing the busi-
ness case for initiating a TT activity. It facilitates obtaining key information from the SU, such as the
quality target product profile (QTPP) and/or specifications to identify the product characteristics and
high-level process requirements. Using a high-level risk assessment, the Business function develops
the product strategy to guide the TT decision. Legal counsel also is engaged for intellectual property
review to ensure the freedom to operate, if needed.
At this point, the RU site selection process can begin. If the product will be sourced externally, the
Business function secures nondisclosure agreements or confidentiality agreements with potential RUs.
The criteria laid out in the QTPP or product specification, the product strategy, the intellectual prop-
erty review, and the information required for product lifecycle phase (see Section 3.1) are used in
the site selection process. Typically, potential RUs are assessed for capability, age of facility, location,
capacity, resourcing, costs, tax implications, compliance history, regulatory strategy, and other logis-
tical considerations. Once a RU site is selected, the initial TT charter is approved, and the steering
committee is formed. Section 8.1 presents a case study of best practices and benefits of establishing a
steering committee for a project.
The stage gate for Initiation results in a decision to proceed with the TT; selection of the RU, often
with a signed contract; and approval of the initial charter. Two case studies, Section 8.4 on using a
regulatory strategy for decision-making and Section 8.8 on TT of an aseptic drug product, illustrate
factors to consider when selecting a RU or supplier.

#### 5.1.2 Initiation Stage Project Manager

Involvement of a PM during the Initiation stage of a TT is critical to provide overall leadership and
accountability for the project. The PM needs to be knowledgeable across all relevant functional areas
and able to communicate across organizational levels. Additionally, this role requires such soft skills
as facilitation, negotiation, decision-making, and motivation to effectively lead a cross-functional
team to achieve business goals. Once the RU has been selected and the TT moves into the Planning
stage, a TT PM will be assigned. In many TTs, the RU PM will serve as TT PM for the remainder
of the project.

#### 5.1.3 Process

The Process function first determines the subject matter expertise required on the TT team for the
product under evaluation by modality or stage of manufacture. For instance, a small-molecule drug
substance would need a process expert who has worked with organic chemistry processing, while a
filled-vial drug product would need an expert in sterile manufacturing. Second, the MAH Process func-
tion develops a preliminary process flow and works with the SU to develop or obtain the QTPP and/or
specification for the product. Third, using the assumptions provided by the Business function, Process
develops batch size options and assesses scale-up or scale-down requirements against the current process
at the SU. Finally, working with the Engineering, Manufacturing, and Analytical functions, Process
assesses the process fit and evaluates the facilities, equipment, and operations group of the potential
RU site to determine its capability to manufacture the product, noting gaps and investments required.

#### 5.1.4 Analytical

The Analytical function uses the SU’s QTPP and/or specification to determine product testing require-
ments and to evaluate potential testing of raw materials, single-use and other components, and pack-
aging. Test methods are categorized either as compendial or non-compendial for the target markets to
define the complexity of method transfer from the SU. With this information, the Analytical function
assesses the potential RU’s laboratory capabilities and capacity and provides a high-level estimate of
what would be required for the TT. This evaluation covers instrumentation needs, glassware, reference
standards, personnel, external laboratory services, and timing. Evaluation of site analytical capabilities
of potential RUs is an essential element of due diligence. At this stage, the Analytical function docu-
ments the laboratory and method evaluations.

#### 5.1.5 Engineering

Evaluation of site manufacturing capabilities is another essential element of due diligence in selecting
the most appropriate RU and deciding to proceed with the TT. This evaluation, also referred to as a
facility fit assessment, includes such items as site utilities and infrastructure, processing equipment,
and automation capabilities. The Engineering function, working closely with Process, Analytical, and
Manufacturing functions, performs the facility fit assessment to form an initial rough estimate of capital
that would be required. The case study in Section 8.6 provides more detail on the facility fit assessment.

#### 5.1.6 Manufacturing

To perform site selection, the Business function needs to understand the potential RU’s ability to
implement the process. Capability extends beyond capital requirements. The Manufacturing function
assesses the potential RU’s resource flexibility, operational training program, previous and current
manufacturing activities in the facility, organizational structure, current operating shifts, and process
data management systems.
Environment, health, and safety (EHS) issues at the potential RU should be evaluated as well. An
employee health and safety viability report can be used in evaluating potential issues. The Manufactur-
ing function should assess the environmental and occupational permits or certifications that will be
needed, along with the RU’s ability to obtain them and their potential impact on the TT timeline.

#### 5.1.7 Regulatory

Involving the MAH Regulatory function as soon as possible is critical to a TT project. A regulatory strate-
gy should be developed to align the product strategy with the Business function and to establish boundar-
ies and assumptions for the TT charter. Key elements of the initial regulatory strategy are filing category,
regulatory assumptions, likely data requirements and source documents required, potential need for a
health authority meeting, projected timing of regulatory submission to each health authority, and regula-
tions applicable to the product in the region(s) in which it is to be manufactured and marketed.
The regulatory strategy is a living document that will evolve throughout the project. It should be
reviewed at each stage for required updates and further refinement based on the knowledge gained at
that stage. Therefore, the ultimate regulatory strategy may look significantly different than this initial
draft as knowledge is transferred and more information becomes available.
The regulatory strategy typically would address the following:
•
List of markets and the planned timing of submission for each market
•
Proposed filing category for the TT and the justification for this determination
•
Specific regulations applicable to the product and region(s) where it will be marketed
•
Specific data requirements and source documents to support the change related to the TT that the
governing regulatory agency would expect (e.g., sterility and aseptic processing, stability, qualification
and validation considerations, clinical/bioequivalence requirements, and potential required permits)
•
Key regulatory assumptions and risks that could impact the overall TT success and possible
mitigation strategies
•
Documents the RU will need to prepare for authoring, review, approval, and submission of the
regulatory filing
•
Product approval timeline
•
GMP inspection history (lack of)
•
Current GMP certificates
The Regulatory function also will perform due diligence of potential RUs for outstanding actions
taken by regulatory authorities that would make the RU unsuitable for use.

#### 5.1.8 Quality

The Quality function at the MAH performs the quality assessment as part of due diligence on the
potential RUs. In preparing the quality assessment for each RU, which includes the RU site assess-
ment, the Quality function typically asks the potential RU to complete a questionnaire and conducts a
search for compliance history records. For external RUs, the compliance history records may be public,
while the assessment of an internal site can involve records internal to the company. The purpose of the
quality due diligence is to determine the maturity of the RU quality system, the RU’s organizational
mindset regarding GMPs, and its adherence to applicable regulatory requirements. Quality will pro-
vide a compliance risk evaluation of the potential RUs.

5.1.9	 Sourcing/Supply Chain
The Sourcing/Supply Chain function develops initial capacity-planning models using estimated yield
and cycle times. These models are used to evaluate potential RUs for batch size, manufacturing cycle
time, and on-site storage needs as part of the facility fit assessment. The capacity model should in-
corporate product volumes for development and validation activities as well as commercial scale. The
Sourcing/Supply Chain function evaluates sourcing of the required materials, consumables, and com-
ponents to determine any potential long-lead items and specially regulated materials (e.g., materials
subject to national quotas), and it works with the Regulatory or appropriate function to identify spe-
cial import/export requirements where permits would be needed. The Sourcing/Supply Chain func-
tion also prepares a preliminary supply chain strategy to assess the impact of each potential RU on the
product value chain.

### 5.2 Planning Stage

The Planning stage begins after the site selection decision has been made and the initial charter has been
approved; it is the start of the knowledge transfer from the SU. At this point, the TT PM is assigned,
and the core TT Team at the RU is resourced. The members of the TT Team at the SU or MAH may or
may not be the same personnel who performed the initial assessments in the Initiation stage.
Building on the risk assessments developed in the Initiation stage, the TT Team collaborates with the
SU and/or MAH to perform detailed risk assessments to define all CMC and operational readiness
requirements for the TT. This risk assessment becomes a living document that results in a revised and
more detailed version of the project plan, which covers project timing, capital required, resource needs,
and critical path items. Other key outputs of the Planning stage are the final TT charter and a TT
agreement between the SU, RU, and MAH (see Section 5.2.2). Once the Planning stage activities
and deliverables have been completed, the TT team should hold a stage gate meeting with the steering
committee to obtain agreement for moving to the Knowledge Transfer stage. At this stage gate, the
steering committee will approve the final charter and the project plan, which signals acceptance of the
risks defined for the TT. Table 5.2-1, excerpted from the Reference Guide (Figure 5.0-1), shows the
activities and deliverables for the Planning stage.

#### 5.2.1 Business

The MAH Business function coordinates with the Sourcing/Supply Chain function to execute supply
agreements (e.g., Master Service Agreement) with the RU and external laboratories. Critical to the
outcome of the TT, supply agreements should be reviewed by the TT PM to ensure they contain the
same boundaries and assumptions as the TT charter, align with the RU roles and responsibilities in the
TT agreement, and support the requirements of the quality agreement. Many RUs ask that the supply
agreements guarantee a fixed number of batches for both the initial qualification and the annual com-
mercial production, as this allows for better resource planning.
Jointly, the Business functions and steering committee provide oversight of the project by approving
resource and capital needs. A resource plan should be developed to understand staffing requirements
both for the TT and for the long-term commercial needs, based on the following key inputs:
•
Personnel required and organization of shifts to be used
•
New equipment to be qualified and degree of automated vs. manual operational requirements
•
Type of expertise needed
•
Complexity of the technical requirements, including risk mitigation requirements

#### 5.2.2 Technology Transfer Project Manager

Once the Business function determines that a TT will take place and has reached agreement with
the RU, the TT PM is assigned. The TT PM, provides overall leadership and accountability for the
remainder of the project. Therefore, the same qualities are important: knowledge across all relevant
functional areas, ability to communicate across organizational levels, and skill in facilitation, negotia-
tion, decision-making, and motivation. Section 4.1 discusses the selection and role of the TT PM.
The TT PM should put in place a team representing the relevant cross-functional areas to begin plan-
ning and assessing the requirements for knowledge transfer. This team is established in collaboration
with each site’s functional managers. The case study in Section 8.2 discusses the importance of estab-
lishing a cross-functional project team.
The TT PM works with the SU and RU to define the roles and responsibilities for all knowledge trans-
fer requirements. If both the SU and RU are external, then the MAH should be included as a third
party. A responsibility assignment matrix denoting those to be responsible, accountable, consulted,
and informed (RACI) can prove valuable in documenting key responsibilities to ensure alignment and
clarity throughout the project. Once negotiated, these roles and responsibilities should be documented
in a TT agreement, or in a shared procedure for internal-internal TTs, and personnel can be assigned.
The TT agreement can aid significantly in defining resource needs and in facilitating communication.
The TT PM plays the lead role during the Planning stage by coordinating the final risk assessments
and developing the project plan. Using the detailed risk assessments, the TT PM will work with the
TT Team to ensure that the project plan includes the schedule, critical path activities, and resource
requirements. The TT PM also will ensure that the project requirements and timeline meet the needs
of the business and stakeholders, taking into account the capabilities of the functions and systems sup-
porting the project. The project plan typically covers:
•
Capital procurement, installation, and qualification timing
•
Procurement and release of all materials and consumables
•
Process development and/or demonstration
•
Analytical method validations, transfers, or qualifications
•
Operational readiness requirements
•
Plant-scale process validation
•
Key milestones and critical path activities
Once the project plan has been developed, the TT charter can be expanded to include the communica-
tion plan. A communication plan and a system for sharing documents between the parties is necessary
to facilitate knowledge transfer. The communication plan should define modes of escalation to ensure
the TT Team can resolve any roadblocks that may occur.
At this time, the TT PM should hold a kick-off meeting with the final cross-functional TT team mem-
bers (including both the SU and RU) and project sponsor.

#### 5.2.3 Process

During the Planning stage, the Process function begins to specify the key elements of the commercial
manufacturing process for the TT, including batch scale, unit operations needed, cycle time, and material
handling requirements, which will result in an initial bill of materials. These knowledge elements are used

by the Sourcing/Supply Chain function for production planning and plant logistics and by the Engineer-
ing function to design and order the required capital. The Process function aligns QTPP and/or specifica-
tions with the RU. Often, Process will develop a comparability plan to identify the information necessary
to show that the product made at the RU is comparable to that of the SU. The Process function also
performs a more detailed risk assessment to define the changes that will be required at the RU based on its
equipment, scale, and capabilities. The risk assessment should include evaluation of the properties and re-
quirements of the product being transferred. This risk assessment not only defines the knowledge transfer
requirements from the SU, but also is used by Engineering to estimate the capital requirements. If the TT
involves a new product or technology, the process needs to be designed such that it will run reproducibly
at the RU to consistently meet the requirements of the QTPP and/or specifications.
The Process function, working with Quality and Regulatory, will define strategies for both process and
cleaning validation at the Planning stage and ensure that the SU and RU align their change control
processes. The Process function will issue the change request for the TT in the change management
system as well.

#### 5.2.4 Analytical

The Analytical function will perform a more detailed risk assessment to determine final analytical equip-
ment requirements that will help determine analytical testing sites. Testing sites may be located at the
SU, RU, MAH, or an external laboratory. The analytical risk assessment needs to include raw materials,
packaging, in-process controls, and product, stability, and cleaning test requirements, specifically noting
which methods are compendial. Adequate quantities of source materials, reference standards, and criti-
cal analytical reagents must be estimated and made available for analytical method validation, transfer,
and future manufacturing. As the amount of sample source material can impact yield from validation
batches, Analytical will work with the Process and Regulatory functions to develop an initial sampling
plan that covers the number of samples taken, amount of sample required, tests performed, sample loca-
tion, and whether the sample should be assessed only during process validation. This initial assessment
of the sampling plan allows for planning of adequate analytical resources during qualification.

#### 5.2.5 Engineering

Working closely with the Process function, Engineering will develop the capital requirements for the
chosen RU, which are then documented in a capital appropriation request (CAR). Even before the TT
user requirements specifications (URS) or CAR are complete, it may be necessary to decide to order
long-lead equipment to meet TT timing requirements. Engineering also should be involved in change
control for facility retrofits as well as laboratory modifications.
If the RU is a CMO or CDMO, the responsibilities of the Engineering function may reside at the
contract site with strong oversight from the TT Team. The TT PM works closely with Engineering to
coordinate the timing for equipment and facility installation, qualification, and training to align with
the overall project plan.

#### 5.2.6 Manufacturing

In the Planning stage, Manufacturing coordinates with the TT PM to ensure that the project timeline consid-
ers all necessary steps for full operational readiness of the RU site. Operational requirements of the technology
being transferred should be considered relative to the staff capabilities at the RU, permit requirements, and
operational training that will be needed for the new process and modified facility. Manufacturing also will
work with the Engineering and Process units on plant design to define URS and CAR requirements.
EHS considerations must be assessed against the RU’s procedures and experience so necessary permits
can be prepared. The EHS assessment includes protective equipment, product biosafety, aseptic practices,
hazardous materials, and potential vent and waste streams. A report on the operational requirements and
an EHS assessment report will focus attention on the areas that need to be addressed in the project plan.

#### 5.2.7 Regulatory

The Regulatory function will further define the regulatory strategy in the Planning stage, identifying any
items that could impact the Business function’s product strategy (see Section 5.1.1). The regulatory strat-
egy should consider the documents that will be needed and include a projected timeline for key milestones
such as drafting, review, and approval. These will later be expanded into a detailed submission tracker.
The regulatory strategy should include the regulatory risks, with mitigation options, and the regula-
tory assumptions identified by both the MAH and the RU. Collaboration between the MAH and RU
Regulatory and Quality functions at this stage is important in understanding the risk tolerance and
concerns of each, both in general and specific to the product. Establishing clear understandings at this
stage can help avoid delays and frustration later in the process.
The Regulatory function communicates the submission requirements to the Process, Analytical, En-
gineering, and Manufacturing functions, which will perform detailed risk assessments. Based on the
level of risk the MAH is comfortable with, Regulatory may be able to define opportunities to reduce
complexity and accelerate regulatory submission and approval. Sources of risk that often are consid-
ered in the regulatory strategy include:
•
Stability strategy
•
Analytical method transfers
•
Number and timing of validation batches
•
Process changes or improvements
•
Amount of data and activities the RU can leverage from the SU
The MAH Regulatory function may meet with regulatory authorities to review the proposed regula-
tory strategy to ensure success.
The RU Regulatory function works with the MAH Regulatory unit to obtain current CMC informa-
tion from the registered dossiers and to evaluate any compendial or compliance standards that have
changed. It also is useful to obtain information about any changes implemented across the product’s
lifecycle to better understand challenges in manufacturing or testing of the product. Additionally, the
outputs in the submission (e.g., CQAs and critical process parameters (CPPs)) should be provided to
the RU so the RU can understand any critical challenges with the product and determine the inputs
necessary to meet those requirements. If the TT is for a new product, the MAH Regulatory function
will define the requirements based on the target markets and manufacturing locations.

#### 5.2.8 Quality

The MAH Quality function will work with the RU Process function to document in the QTPP and
product specification the requirements that will ensure the quality, safety, and efficacy of the product. In
this stage, quality agreements must be developed concurrently and executed, as required, between the
MAH and RU, and between the RU or MAH and additional GMP providers and external laboratories.
Based on the target market(s), current stability requirements, and commitments made to regulatory
authorities, Quality will work with the Process, Analytical, and Regulatory functions to prepare the
stability requirements. Regulatory uses the stability plan to further develop the overall regulatory time-
line to ensure that all conditions of the current commitments can be met.
Using the regulatory strategy, Quality also begins GMP audit-readiness activities at the RU to ensure
full inspection readiness when needed in the Execution stage. First, the MAH Quality function per-
forms an audit of the chosen RU so that all compliance gaps impacting the TT can be addressed prior
to physical implementation of the cross-functional requirements. When significant quality changes are
required, a quality improvement plan will be developed; however, the quality assessment performed
during the Initiation stage should have identified if a site has a significant compliance gap.

Quality also performs a gap assessment of the SU, RU, and MAH quality systems and negotiates an
aligned QMS for the TT. This alignment should be performed even when the SU and RU are both
internal sites, as interpretation of the global QMS may differ across regions.
5.2.9	 Sourcing/Supply Chain
Once the decision to perform the TT is made, production planning begins. The Sourcing/Supply
Chain function works closely with the TT PM to evaluate the time that will be needed for the TT,
based on the time needed for Engineering installation and qualification, the number of engineering
batches to be performed, the number of qualification batches needed, and the time needed for stability
studies and regulatory approvals. Planned downtime in production, such as for maintenance (reactive
or preventive) and cleaning, also should be considered.
During the Planning stage, the target market volume also is used to determine future RU capacity and
resource needs for commercial supply. These requirements typically are part of the supply agreement
negotiated with the RU during the Planning stage.
During this stage, the sourcing of raw materials, consumables, and packaging components should begin
with identification of sources for the items with the longest lead times and sole-sourced items. Any changes
to the supply of critical materials identified as part of the TT risk assessment also should be addressed.
The Sourcing/Supply Chain function also evaluates overall product logistics to further develop the product
supply chain strategy. At this stage, the product supply chain strategy should show import/export consid-
erations, distribution, and interim points involved with both the product and materials. A detailed supply
chain map showing distributors and manufacturers for key components is a key output to identify supply
chain risks, consider second sources of critical materials, and facilitate future troubleshooting.

## 1.0 Introduction

Technology transfers play an increasingly important role in the regulated pharmaceutical and biophar-
maceutical industries. The expanded use of contract development and manufacturing organizations,
advances in manufacturing flexibility, increase in corporate mergers and acquisitions, and enhanced
regulatory complexity all contribute to the need for technology transfers to maintain the continuity of
drug supply. In order to replicate systems safely, maintain high-quality standards, and meet regulatory
requirements, manufacturers have established approaches to successfully transfer manufacturing and
analytical technology and knowledge from one site to another.
Pharmaceutical technology transfer (TT) uses a systematic approach to ensure the knowledge required
to execute the manufacturing process and product testing performed at one site can be incorporated at a
new site, while maintaining understanding and product characteristics. This results in the same safe, ef-
fective, and consistent pharmaceutical product being manufactured at both locations. TTs can occur at
all stages of the product lifecycle, from drug development through clinical trials to commercial release.
The information in this technical report can be used to guide TT of both small- and large-molecule
drug substances (biologics) as well as sterile and nonsterile drug and biological products. This docu-
ment describes the steps for a single TT event. For large, complex TTs, these tools can be implemented
in separate parallel processes, with a TT team established and a procedure executed for each discrete
aspect being transferred. For instance, separate TT teams could be established for the transfer of drug
substance manufacturing, drug product manufacturing, and packaging, with a management oversight
committee ensuring the interconnectivity of the overall TT program across the supply chain.
The product realization requirements outlined in this technical report follow the guidelines of the
International Council for Harmonization of Technical Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human
Use (ICH) and should be documented in the chemistry, manufacturing, and controls (CMC) section
of the regulatory filing.

### 1.1 Purpose

This technical report aims to provide a standardized approach to the TT process by supplying a ma-
trixed “Reference Guide to Technology Transfer Activities and Deliverables,” which can be used to
coordinate cross-functional TT activities that help achieve operational readiness and culminate in
regulatory approval. As specific details around each TT differ greatly, based on the type and complexity
of the manufacturing process or analytical method being transferred, only a certain level of detail could
be covered in a single technical report. This technical report is intended to provide a level of detail and
approaches that can be applied across TT types.

### 1.2 Scope

This technical report covers the full range of requirements needed for TTs, such as new product in-
troductions and the transfer of products that are being manufactured for phase III clinical trials or
commercial release, which are the most comprehensive in nature. In transfers of products at an earlier
stage of development, when process and product knowledge is less understood and therefore CMC
requirements are less detailed, certain steps described in this technical report will not be necessary. This
report does not detail those differences.
While this information was not developed specifically for medical devices, combination products, and
advanced therapy medicinal products due to their unique requirements, many of the fundamentals
expressed here can be applied to those products.
For this technical report, it is assumed that all involved parties have established systems for commercial
production. Thus, standard requirements such as equipment and facility maintenance, annual product

--- PAGE 7 ---
reports, lifecycle validation, complaint handling, and continued process verification (CPV) programs
are not addressed. For purposes of this technical report, the TT ends with the handover to operations
upon regulatory approval.

## 3.0 Types of Technology Transfers

ICH Quality Guideline Q10: Pharmaceutical Quality Systems describes TTs as new product transfers
during development through manufacturing and transfers within or between manufacturing and test-
ing sites for marketed products (3). This broadly encompasses all transfers, including analytical meth-
od transfers to an alternate laboratory, transfers to scale up a development product, and transfers from
the laboratory to a current good manufacturing practices (GMP) production environment. When
establishing a TT program, it is important to understand the impact of the product lifecycle phase, the
type of sites involved, and the associated CMC requirements.
Although this section describes all types of TTs, this technical report focuses on the fundamental
activities necessary for introduction of a new product to a manufacturing site when the product is
moving from clinical phase III to commercial manufacturing or from one commercial manufacturing
site to another.

### 3.1 Product Lifecycle

During its lifecycle, a product typically progresses through discovery and development, culminating
in commercial readiness and regulatory approval as depicted in Figure 3.1-1. TTs are a key part of

--- PAGE 9 ---
the lifecycle as a new drug product and its corresponding drug substance move from clinical phase I
through to commercial manufacturing, often at different production scales and different manufactur-
ing sites. While TTs may occur at any point throughout the product lifecycle, each TT should have its
own purpose (see Section 4.3) and will have a discrete beginning and end.
With the aim of driving reproducibility, elements of TT are performed at each phase of the product
lifecycle as knowledge of the process, product, and testing increases. TTs can be described based on
product lifecycle phase: transfers within the clinical phases (clinical-clinical); transfers from the devel-
opment phase, which may be clinical phase III, to the commercial phase (development-commercial);
and transfers within the commercial phase (commercial-commercial). This section further describes
each of the lifecycle phase groups and their influence on TT requirements.

#### 3.1.1 Clinical-Clinical Technology Transfers

While this technical report does not describe the requirements for clinical-clinical TTs, TTs at clinical
stages should be developed, reviewed, and executed with thoughtful consideration and should be phase-
appropriate from a CMC perspective. TTs during these phases should address clinical patient safety and
begin building the foundation for future commercial production including the development of the TPP.
During early clinical phases, the involved sites should constantly consider future needs related to scaling
up and validating the manufacturing process, determining the type of facility required, and defining
the product, process, and analytical characteristics necessary to ensure the quality, safety, and efficacy
of the product being transferred. Clinical phase TTs often are complicated by small material demands
and by extended pauses for site inspections and regulatory approvals as clinical data is gathered, making
knowledge management throughout the early clinical phases especially important.

#### 3.1.2 Development-Commercial Technology Transfers

Development-commercial TTs, which often begin when the product is in clinical phase III, involve
the transfer of the full scope of CMC requirements. By clinical phase III, the product, process, and
analytical development should progress in parallel with the TT program to define the final CMC
requirements for commercial readiness and process and analytical validation. For instance, the final
dosage form and dosage should be known; the drug substance manufacturing process should be es-
tablished such that there are minimal changes in the impurity profile from clinical phase III to pro-
cess validation; process parameters and critical quality attributes (CQA) should be defined, including
characterization of critical components and materials; and initial stability for both drug substance and
drug product should have been evaluated. Risk assessments should be performed throughout final
development to determine the impact areas for commercial manufacture. PDA Technical Report No.

#### 3.1.3 Commercial-Commercial Technology Transfers

In commercial-commercial TTs, the product is already being marketed and produced at an existing facility,
yet business may dictate the need to move or expand production of the drug substance or drug product to
another facility. This may require an operational scale-up or scale-down. The addition of a new market may
require the business to use alternate packaging facilities, which would require TT of the packaging operations.
Alternate laboratories also may be used, requiring transfer of the analytical methods to the new location.
In TTs involving a marketed product, the original or current manufacturing location should provide the
full CMC requirements, with historical product and process knowledge, that demonstrate manufacturing
robustness. These types of TTs may be an opportunity to implement process improvements at the receiving
unit, provided that those improvements align with the business and regulatory strategies for the TT.

### 3.2 Site Types

Typically, two types of sites are involved in a TT: the sending unit (SU) and the receiving unit (RU).
The SU is the site with the current knowledge of the product, process, and analytical methods, while
the RU is the location where the product will be manufactured or tested in the future. The use of
contract manufacturing organizations (CMOs) and contract development and manufacturing organi-
zations (CDMOs), the advent of the virtual company business model, and the rise of global manufac-
turing supply chains have expanded the variations of SU and RU sites involved in TTs.
Both the SU and RU sites can be classified as internal or external. Internal sites include any facility for
which the existing or future marketing authorization holder (MAH) has ownership or complete con-
trol, while external sites are separate from the MAH and include CMOs, CDMOs, business partners,
and external laboratories. The type of site and its relationship to the MAH impacts the effectiveness of
knowledge transfer as well as the overall TT team and governance framework described in Section 4.0.

#### 3.2.1 Internal-Internal Technology Transfers

A TT performed between locations of the same company or facility is considered the most manageable
in terms of knowledge transfer between the SU and RU sites for several reasons. Most likely, these sites
are governed by the same quality management system (QMS), have integrated document management
systems, and employ a standardized TT process. If the company is transferring technology for an exist-
ing commercial product, historical data on process or test performance also should be readily available.
Even with use of the same site, however, a complex process or an analytical method that draws on
unique knowledge held by the SU can still present challenges.
If the internal SU and RU sites are in different geographical locations, an initial evaluation will be
needed to determine the potential impact of language, local implementation of the global QMS, im-
port and export requirements, and regulatory requirements. These TTs typically will require additional
budget for site-to-site interactions that allow the SU to share knowledge with the RU and participate
in initial manufacturing at the new location.

#### 3.2.2 Internal-External or External-Internal Technology Transfers

The MAH may choose to work with an external company in many different scenarios, such as:
•
Product developed internally will be manufactured at commercial scale at an external company,
such as a CMO or CDMO, for cost or time-to-market purposes or due to limited capability in
internal facilities.

--- PAGE 11 ---
•
Product developed at a CDMO will be manufactured at an internal site.
•
Commercialization was performed internally and transferred to an external company after receiv-
ing regulatory approval, or vice versa.
•
Use of multiple manufacturing facilities will better meet demand and/or provide risk mitigation
for supply.
•
Expansion of a product to a new market requires alternate global manufacturing capability.
•
A corporate acquisition or merger results in manufacturing consolidation.
In these types of scenarios, the TT project manager (PM) has the additional challenge of managing the
TT activities across multiple sites, organizations, and cultures.
Unlike internal-internal transfers, any transfer to or from an external site may involve differences in
organization, quality systems, and specific procedures. In some cases, the external organization may
have competing priorities, which can be difficult to discern. Defining the differences in organizational
structure, quality systems, and business priorities early can facilitate alignment on the TT boundaries
and success factors. Agreements on the presence of SU subject matter experts (SMEs) at the external
site during the TT process to ensure a successful knowledge transfer is an important point that cannot
be overlooked and should be documented in the appropriate agreements.

#### 3.2.3 External-External Technology Transfers

Often, the MAH must manage a transfer between two external sites, which may also involve two dif-
ferent companies. This type of TT can present the greatest challenge, particularly if the two external
parties are not known to each other (e.g., the result of a business strategy). If the external parties cannot
interact, the MAH will need to act as the SU for the TT and facilitate the knowledge transfer to the
RU. Agreements on the presence of MAH and SU SMEs at the external site during the TT process to
ensure a successful knowledge transfer is an important point that cannot be overlooked and should be
documented in the appropriate agreements.

In the Initiation stage, potential RUs are evaluated to identify a site with a risk profile that meets the
business objectives. Once a suitable RU is selected, a TT Team is formed at the RU site. In the Plan-
ning stage, the TT Team assesses the TT risks and identifies elements critical to the knowledge transfer

operational readiness. In the Execution stage, the RU demonstrates and qualifies the process at scale,
culminating in a regulatory submission. The final Handover stage closes the TT with regulatory ap-

Each TT involves unique technical, business, and regulatory requirements that should be considered
in structuring the project. Each company may choose to add or consolidate stage gates and TT process

risk tolerance.
In every TT, assessing and managing risks is essential. A mature quality risk management program, like
that described in PDA TR 54, should be in place prior to starting a TT project.

requirements. The person serving as team lead may or may not have a “project manager” job title
but serves as a primary point of contact and facilitates communication, knowledge transfer, and task

product is being transferred from one production line to another), a single team lead may be adequate.

coordinator as the TT PM. Often, the PM at the RU will serve as the TT PM. In an external-external
TT, however, an individual from the MAH may act as the TT PM to ensure a smooth and continuous
flow of knowledge transfer. In large, complex TTs, the TT PM may be someone different from the site
PMs. Wherever the TT PM is located, this role is critical in facilitating constant communication and
should be the primary contact point for changes, decisions, and team alignment.

### 4.2 TT Management Oversight

The organizational structure supporting a TT requires clarity and definition to ensure the TT Team
can maintain focus, manage resourcing, and resolve issues. A defined structure for the TT allows for
improved communication and clear definition of priorities among team members and management.
A TT is usually considered a special project with a fixed scope and defined goals. As such, members
of the TT Team may not be permanently assigned to the TT and may continue to report functionally
outside of the team. TT Team members typically are assigned to the team under the direction of the

### 5.1 Initiation Stage

The Initiation stage covers the process of determining whether the TT of CMC activities for a phar-
maceutical or biopharmaceutical product should be performed. In this stage, the team evaluating the
need for the TT often has limited information on the product characteristics, process requirements,
and analytical testing from the SU, particularly if the product is still under development. While the
Initiation stage is primarily driven by the Business function of the MAH, technical experts also should
conduct high-level risk assessments.
A PM should be assigned to facilitate RU site selection and to develop the initial charter. This stage
results in a decision on whether to perform the TT, the choice of an RU site, the formation of a steer-
ing committee, and the development of an initial TT charter as well as an overall product strategy,
including business and regulatory considerations. Table 5.1-1, excerpted from the Reference Guide
(Figure 5.0-1), shows the activities and deliverables for the Initiation stage.

#### 5.1.1 Business

The Business function at the MAH takes the lead role in the Initiation stage, developing the busi-
ness case for initiating a TT activity. It facilitates obtaining key information from the SU, such as the
quality target product profile (QTPP) and/or specifications to identify the product characteristics and
high-level process requirements. Using a high-level risk assessment, the Business function develops
the product strategy to guide the TT decision. Legal counsel also is engaged for intellectual property
review to ensure the freedom to operate, if needed.
At this point, the RU site selection process can begin. If the product will be sourced externally, the
Business function secures nondisclosure agreements or confidentiality agreements with potential RUs.
The criteria laid out in the QTPP or product specification, the product strategy, the intellectual prop-
erty review, and the information required for product lifecycle phase (see Section 3.1) are used in
the site selection process. Typically, potential RUs are assessed for capability, age of facility, location,
capacity, resourcing, costs, tax implications, compliance history, regulatory strategy, and other logis-
tical considerations. Once a RU site is selected, the initial TT charter is approved, and the steering
committee is formed. Section 8.1 presents a case study of best practices and benefits of establishing a
steering committee for a project.
The stage gate for Initiation results in a decision to proceed with the TT; selection of the RU, often
with a signed contract; and approval of the initial charter. Two case studies, Section 8.4 on using a
regulatory strategy for decision-making and Section 8.8 on TT of an aseptic drug product, illustrate
factors to consider when selecting a RU or supplier.

#### 5.1.2 Initiation Stage Project Manager

Involvement of a PM during the Initiation stage of a TT is critical to provide overall leadership and
accountability for the project. The PM needs to be knowledgeable across all relevant functional areas
and able to communicate across organizational levels. Additionally, this role requires such soft skills
as facilitation, negotiation, decision-making, and motivation to effectively lead a cross-functional
team to achieve business goals. Once the RU has been selected and the TT moves into the Planning
stage, a TT PM will be assigned. In many TTs, the RU PM will serve as TT PM for the remainder
of the project.

#### 5.1.3 Process

The Process function first determines the subject matter expertise required on the TT team for the
product under evaluation by modality or stage of manufacture. For instance, a small-molecule drug
substance would need a process expert who has worked with organic chemistry processing, while a
filled-vial drug product would need an expert in sterile manufacturing. Second, the MAH Process func-
tion develops a preliminary process flow and works with the SU to develop or obtain the QTPP and/or
specification for the product. Third, using the assumptions provided by the Business function, Process
develops batch size options and assesses scale-up or scale-down requirements against the current process
at the SU. Finally, working with the Engineering, Manufacturing, and Analytical functions, Process
assesses the process fit and evaluates the facilities, equipment, and operations group of the potential
RU site to determine its capability to manufacture the product, noting gaps and investments required.

#### 5.1.4 Analytical

The Analytical function uses the SU’s QTPP and/or specification to determine product testing require-
ments and to evaluate potential testing of raw materials, single-use and other components, and pack-
aging. Test methods are categorized either as compendial or non-compendial for the target markets to
define the complexity of method transfer from the SU. With this information, the Analytical function
assesses the potential RU’s laboratory capabilities and capacity and provides a high-level estimate of
what would be required for the TT. This evaluation covers instrumentation needs, glassware, reference
standards, personnel, external laboratory services, and timing. Evaluation of site analytical capabilities
of potential RUs is an essential element of due diligence. At this stage, the Analytical function docu-
ments the laboratory and method evaluations.

#### 5.1.5 Engineering

Evaluation of site manufacturing capabilities is another essential element of due diligence in selecting
the most appropriate RU and deciding to proceed with the TT. This evaluation, also referred to as a
facility fit assessment, includes such items as site utilities and infrastructure, processing equipment,
and automation capabilities. The Engineering function, working closely with Process, Analytical, and
Manufacturing functions, performs the facility fit assessment to form an initial rough estimate of capital
that would be required. The case study in Section 8.6 provides more detail on the facility fit assessment.

#### 5.1.6 Manufacturing

To perform site selection, the Business function needs to understand the potential RU’s ability to
implement the process. Capability extends beyond capital requirements. The Manufacturing function
assesses the potential RU’s resource flexibility, operational training program, previous and current
manufacturing activities in the facility, organizational structure, current operating shifts, and process
data management systems.
Environment, health, and safety (EHS) issues at the potential RU should be evaluated as well. An
employee health and safety viability report can be used in evaluating potential issues. The Manufactur-
ing function should assess the environmental and occupational permits or certifications that will be
needed, along with the RU’s ability to obtain them and their potential impact on the TT timeline.

#### 5.1.7 Regulatory

Involving the MAH Regulatory function as soon as possible is critical to a TT project. A regulatory strate-
gy should be developed to align the product strategy with the Business function and to establish boundar-
ies and assumptions for the TT charter. Key elements of the initial regulatory strategy are filing category,
regulatory assumptions, likely data requirements and source documents required, potential need for a
health authority meeting, projected timing of regulatory submission to each health authority, and regula-
tions applicable to the product in the region(s) in which it is to be manufactured and marketed.
The regulatory strategy is a living document that will evolve throughout the project. It should be
reviewed at each stage for required updates and further refinement based on the knowledge gained at
that stage. Therefore, the ultimate regulatory strategy may look significantly different than this initial
draft as knowledge is transferred and more information becomes available.
The regulatory strategy typically would address the following:
•
List of markets and the planned timing of submission for each market
•
Proposed filing category for the TT and the justification for this determination
•
Specific regulations applicable to the product and region(s) where it will be marketed
•
Specific data requirements and source documents to support the change related to the TT that the
governing regulatory agency would expect (e.g., sterility and aseptic processing, stability, qualification
and validation considerations, clinical/bioequivalence requirements, and potential required permits)
•
Key regulatory assumptions and risks that could impact the overall TT success and possible
mitigation strategies
•
Documents the RU will need to prepare for authoring, review, approval, and submission of the
regulatory filing
•
Product approval timeline
•
GMP inspection history (lack of)
•
Current GMP certificates
The Regulatory function also will perform due diligence of potential RUs for outstanding actions
taken by regulatory authorities that would make the RU unsuitable for use.

#### 5.1.8 Quality

The Quality function at the MAH performs the quality assessment as part of due diligence on the
potential RUs. In preparing the quality assessment for each RU, which includes the RU site assess-
ment, the Quality function typically asks the potential RU to complete a questionnaire and conducts a
search for compliance history records. For external RUs, the compliance history records may be public,
while the assessment of an internal site can involve records internal to the company. The purpose of the
quality due diligence is to determine the maturity of the RU quality system, the RU’s organizational
mindset regarding GMPs, and its adherence to applicable regulatory requirements. Quality will pro-
vide a compliance risk evaluation of the potential RUs.

5.1.9	 Sourcing/Supply Chain
The Sourcing/Supply Chain function develops initial capacity-planning models using estimated yield
and cycle times. These models are used to evaluate potential RUs for batch size, manufacturing cycle
time, and on-site storage needs as part of the facility fit assessment. The capacity model should in-
corporate product volumes for development and validation activities as well as commercial scale. The
Sourcing/Supply Chain function evaluates sourcing of the required materials, consumables, and com-
ponents to determine any potential long-lead items and specially regulated materials (e.g., materials
subject to national quotas), and it works with the Regulatory or appropriate function to identify spe-
cial import/export requirements where permits would be needed. The Sourcing/Supply Chain func-
tion also prepares a preliminary supply chain strategy to assess the impact of each potential RU on the
product value chain.

### 5.2 Planning Stage

The Planning stage begins after the site selection decision has been made and the initial charter has been
approved; it is the start of the knowledge transfer from the SU. At this point, the TT PM is assigned,
and the core TT Team at the RU is resourced. The members of the TT Team at the SU or MAH may or
may not be the same personnel who performed the initial assessments in the Initiation stage.
Building on the risk assessments developed in the Initiation stage, the TT Team collaborates with the
SU and/or MAH to perform detailed risk assessments to define all CMC and operational readiness
requirements for the TT. This risk assessment becomes a living document that results in a revised and
more detailed version of the project plan, which covers project timing, capital required, resource needs,
and critical path items. Other key outputs of the Planning stage are the final TT charter and a TT
agreement between the SU, RU, and MAH (see Section 5.2.2). Once the Planning stage activities
and deliverables have been completed, the TT team should hold a stage gate meeting with the steering
committee to obtain agreement for moving to the Knowledge Transfer stage. At this stage gate, the
steering committee will approve the final charter and the project plan, which signals acceptance of the
risks defined for the TT. Table 5.2-1, excerpted from the Reference Guide (Figure 5.0-1), shows the
activities and deliverables for the Planning stage.

#### 5.2.1 Business

The MAH Business function coordinates with the Sourcing/Supply Chain function to execute supply
agreements (e.g., Master Service Agreement) with the RU and external laboratories. Critical to the
outcome of the TT, supply agreements should be reviewed by the TT PM to ensure they contain the
same boundaries and assumptions as the TT charter, align with the RU roles and responsibilities in the
TT agreement, and support the requirements of the quality agreement. Many RUs ask that the supply
agreements guarantee a fixed number of batches for both the initial qualification and the annual com-
mercial production, as this allows for better resource planning.
Jointly, the Business functions and steering committee provide oversight of the project by approving
resource and capital needs. A resource plan should be developed to understand staffing requirements
both for the TT and for the long-term commercial needs, based on the following key inputs:
•
Personnel required and organization of shifts to be used
•
New equipment to be qualified and degree of automated vs. manual operational requirements
•
Type of expertise needed
•
Complexity of the technical requirements, including risk mitigation requirements

#### 5.2.2 Technology Transfer Project Manager

Once the Business function determines that a TT will take place and has reached agreement with
the RU, the TT PM is assigned. The TT PM, provides overall leadership and accountability for the
remainder of the project. Therefore, the same qualities are important: knowledge across all relevant
functional areas, ability to communicate across organizational levels, and skill in facilitation, negotia-
tion, decision-making, and motivation. Section 4.1 discusses the selection and role of the TT PM.
The TT PM should put in place a team representing the relevant cross-functional areas to begin plan-
ning and assessing the requirements for knowledge transfer. This team is established in collaboration
with each site’s functional managers. The case study in Section 8.2 discusses the importance of estab-
lishing a cross-functional project team.
The TT PM works with the SU and RU to define the roles and responsibilities for all knowledge trans-
fer requirements. If both the SU and RU are external, then the MAH should be included as a third
party. A responsibility assignment matrix denoting those to be responsible, accountable, consulted,
and informed (RACI) can prove valuable in documenting key responsibilities to ensure alignment and
clarity throughout the project. Once negotiated, these roles and responsibilities should be documented
in a TT agreement, or in a shared procedure for internal-internal TTs, and personnel can be assigned.
The TT agreement can aid significantly in defining resource needs and in facilitating communication.
The TT PM plays the lead role during the Planning stage by coordinating the final risk assessments
and developing the project plan. Using the detailed risk assessments, the TT PM will work with the
TT Team to ensure that the project plan includes the schedule, critical path activities, and resource
requirements. The TT PM also will ensure that the project requirements and timeline meet the needs
of the business and stakeholders, taking into account the capabilities of the functions and systems sup-
porting the project. The project plan typically covers:
•
Capital procurement, installation, and qualification timing
•
Procurement and release of all materials and consumables
•
Process development and/or demonstration
•
Analytical method validations, transfers, or qualifications
•
Operational readiness requirements
•
Plant-scale process validation
•
Key milestones and critical path activities
Once the project plan has been developed, the TT charter can be expanded to include the communica-
tion plan. A communication plan and a system for sharing documents between the parties is necessary
to facilitate knowledge transfer. The communication plan should define modes of escalation to ensure
the TT Team can resolve any roadblocks that may occur.
At this time, the TT PM should hold a kick-off meeting with the final cross-functional TT team mem-
bers (including both the SU and RU) and project sponsor.

#### 5.2.3 Process

During the Planning stage, the Process function begins to specify the key elements of the commercial
manufacturing process for the TT, including batch scale, unit operations needed, cycle time, and material
handling requirements, which will result in an initial bill of materials. These knowledge elements are used

by the Sourcing/Supply Chain function for production planning and plant logistics and by the Engineer-
ing function to design and order the required capital. The Process function aligns QTPP and/or specifica-
tions with the RU. Often, Process will develop a comparability plan to identify the information necessary
to show that the product made at the RU is comparable to that of the SU. The Process function also
performs a more detailed risk assessment to define the changes that will be required at the RU based on its
equipment, scale, and capabilities. The risk assessment should include evaluation of the properties and re-
quirements of the product being transferred. This risk assessment not only defines the knowledge transfer
requirements from the SU, but also is used by Engineering to estimate the capital requirements. If the TT
involves a new product or technology, the process needs to be designed such that it will run reproducibly
at the RU to consistently meet the requirements of the QTPP and/or specifications.
The Process function, working with Quality and Regulatory, will define strategies for both process and
cleaning validation at the Planning stage and ensure that the SU and RU align their change control
processes. The Process function will issue the change request for the TT in the change management
system as well.

#### 5.2.4 Analytical

The Analytical function will perform a more detailed risk assessment to determine final analytical equip-
ment requirements that will help determine analytical testing sites. Testing sites may be located at the
SU, RU, MAH, or an external laboratory. The analytical risk assessment needs to include raw materials,
packaging, in-process controls, and product, stability, and cleaning test requirements, specifically noting
which methods are compendial. Adequate quantities of source materials, reference standards, and criti-
cal analytical reagents must be estimated and made available for analytical method validation, transfer,
and future manufacturing. As the amount of sample source material can impact yield from validation
batches, Analytical will work with the Process and Regulatory functions to develop an initial sampling
plan that covers the number of samples taken, amount of sample required, tests performed, sample loca-
tion, and whether the sample should be assessed only during process validation. This initial assessment
of the sampling plan allows for planning of adequate analytical resources during qualification.

#### 5.2.5 Engineering

Working closely with the Process function, Engineering will develop the capital requirements for the
chosen RU, which are then documented in a capital appropriation request (CAR). Even before the TT
user requirements specifications (URS) or CAR are complete, it may be necessary to decide to order
long-lead equipment to meet TT timing requirements. Engineering also should be involved in change
control for facility retrofits as well as laboratory modifications.
If the RU is a CMO or CDMO, the responsibilities of the Engineering function may reside at the
contract site with strong oversight from the TT Team. The TT PM works closely with Engineering to
coordinate the timing for equipment and facility installation, qualification, and training to align with
the overall project plan.

#### 5.2.6 Manufacturing

In the Planning stage, Manufacturing coordinates with the TT PM to ensure that the project timeline consid-
ers all necessary steps for full operational readiness of the RU site. Operational requirements of the technology
being transferred should be considered relative to the staff capabilities at the RU, permit requirements, and
operational training that will be needed for the new process and modified facility. Manufacturing also will
work with the Engineering and Process units on plant design to define URS and CAR requirements.
EHS considerations must be assessed against the RU’s procedures and experience so necessary permits
can be prepared. The EHS assessment includes protective equipment, product biosafety, aseptic practices,
hazardous materials, and potential vent and waste streams. A report on the operational requirements and
an EHS assessment report will focus attention on the areas that need to be addressed in the project plan.

### 5.3 Knowledge Transfer Stage

The Knowledge Transfer (KT) stage significantly overlaps with the prior Planning stage and the fol-
lowing Readiness stage. During Planning, key pieces of the KT are needed to finalize the TT charter
and perform risk assessments. KT often is still in progress while key activities in the Readiness stage
are being developed. Throughout the TT, and especially in the KT stage, any events or changes that
occur at the SU that impact the product being transferred require immediate communication to the
TT PM. KT can be a highly iterative and challenging process. At the KT stage, all cross-functional
roles involved in performing the TT are fully engaged.
The TT agreement, quality agreement, TT charter, and project plan that were completed in the prior
stage allow the SU and RU to execute the full transfer of information, systems, data, technical require-
ments, and practical experience necessary to deliver the CMC and operational requirements to the
RU. The TT Team members will work very closely during the KT stage, employing the communica-
tion plan and document-sharing platform developed during the Planning stage.
KT extends beyond the direct TT team members. All supporting personnel across the SU and RU
need to understand the TT requirements, product and regulatory strategies, and success factors as
defined in the TT charter so workloads can be prioritized across the organization and timelines will be
met. In addition, supporting personnel across the SU and RU may hold key information and expertise
based on experience with the intended equipment, a similar process, or a previous project. This ad-
ditional expertise can assist in KT.
During the KT stage, a proactive knowledge management (KM) approach can improve KT, create ef-
ficiencies between the SU and RU, and facilitate more effective transfer of both explicit (documented)
and tacit information held by personnel. The transfer of tacit information may offer the greatest op-
portunities for improvement, as this type of information often may not be uncovered unless a failure
result leads to its discovery. A robust KM approach that includes review of TT performance also can
provide more detail that can be used to refine the control strategy after the TT is complete and to
improve future transfers. Every opportunity should be taken to leverage KM to preserve, transfer, and
increase knowledge about the product and process (2).
The TT package developed in the KT stage will include all the details needed to reproducibly manu-
facture, test, and release the product being transferred. During the stage gate at the end of the KT
stage, the steering committee ensures that the TT package is complete and assesses the risk factors,
with mitigation strategies, that remain after the KT.
Table 5.3-1, excerpted from the Reference Guide (Figure 5.0-1), shows the activities and deliverables
for the KT stage.

#### 5.3.1 Business

With the required contracts and nondisclosure agreements in place, the Business function focuses
on refining and executing the resource plan developed in the Planning stage. The Business function
monitors progress of the interactions between the SU and RU and removes any contractual roadblocks
that may limit KT activities. The case study in Section 8.10 discusses best practices and the benefits
of proactively driving KT between the SU and RU to help ensure a right-first-time project execution
mindset.

#### 5.3.2 Technology Transfer Project Manager

Using the TT agreement developed during the Planning stage, the TT PM, with the TT Team, facili-
tates KT across key functions in the SU and RU (and third parties, as applicable) to ensure timely and
complete knowledge-sharing. The KT is typically documented by the SU in a TT package containing
all the information needed to manufacture the product robustly.

#### 5.3.3 Process

If the product is already established commercially at the SU, the SU shares process performance qualifi-
cation (PPQ) information for the process with the RU Process function. This information will include
CPPs and their impact on CQAs, in-process tests, and proven acceptable ranges for the manufacturing
process. The SU should share learnings from the implementation of commercial or pilot-scale manufac-
turing processes, along with process improvements that have been made. To ensure full understanding
of the process history, lessons learned, challenges, and early development of the process, the SU and/or
MAH also should share with the RU historical performance and development data wherever possible.
For new products, the RU will work with the SU to define the PPQ requirements and ensure their
feasibility at the RU facility. These PPQ requirements will include a list of CPPs that are derived from
quality risk assessments and laboratory and or pilot-scale data. For more information on parameter
classification and the quality risk assessment process, please see PDA Technical Report No. 60: Process
Validation, A Lifecycle Approach (5). Using the PPQ requirements, the Process function will determine
the data to be collected and will define how this information will be captured to ensure data integrity.
In this stage, the Process function writes the process and cleaning validation master plans (VMP) as
key deliverables, using the strategies developed during the Planning stage and the RU’s standard oper-
ating procedures (SOPs). The SU shares with the RU all information required for cleaning validation,
including solubility, toxicology category (with data, if possible), and safety data sheets, so that the
cleaning VMP can document the cleaning limits.
The process VMP outlines the PPQ requirements covering:
•
Process description, including control strategy and CPPs
•
Batch size and expected yield
•
Raw material qualification plan
•
Number of PPQ production runs, detailing any matrix or bracketing assumptions
•
Hold-time strategy
•
Rework or reprocess steps allowed
•
Stability strategy
•
Success criteria
Using the risk assessment from the Planning stage, the Process function performs activities needed to
define the control strategy, including CPPs that impact CQAs and their allowed ranges, in-process
testing, and control systems. For drug products, these activities may include, for example, mixing,
hold time, filter integrity, container closure, and transport or shipping studies. When possible, the RU
should demonstrate the process in the laboratory to ensure full understanding of the knowledge being
transferred from the SU.

#### 5.3.4 Analytical

In the KT stage, the Analytical function at the RU develops the full method-implementation strat-
egy, known as a validation plan, using the sampling plan, actual non-compendial methods used at
the SU, and compendial methods for the markets included in the business and regulatory strategies.
The analytical validation plan must align with the QTPP and/or specification requirements provided
by the SU or documented in the marketing authorization file. When a marketed product is being
transferred to a different site, validation may be performed through the analytical method transfer
plan. The Analytical function determines the requirements for reference standards to allow for the
ordering of reference standards for method qualification and testing. During the KT stage, potential
opportunities for harmonization of analytical methods are identified and an implementation plan for
use of these methods is discussed with the Regulatory function. The Analytical function documents
the agreed-upon approach to implementation of all analytical TT requirements, including the use of
any contract testing sites.

#### 5.3.5 Engineering

The Engineering function performs a detailed facility-fit assessment in the KT stage. In doing so, the
Engineering function uses the knowledge being transferred from the SU to compare the existing (for
approved product) or proposed manufacturing process with the RU’s capabilities. Process steps can be
modified as part of the final facility fit to facilitate execution of the manufacturing process at the RU.
However, any process modification should be proven to work prior to the Readiness stage. Engineering
also should design and implement an appropriate waste management system covering all steps from
waste identification to disposal. The Engineering function writes the change controls for the facility,
often as part of the overall TT change control.
Working closely with the Process, Analytical, Manufacturing, and Quality functions, Engineering
will define all facility, equipment, automation, and laboratory requirements and document these in
the URS. Single-use components (sometimes known as disposables) also should be part of the URS
development. A layout should be used to confirm single-use component design and to ensure all quali-
fication requirements (i.e., extractables/leachables) are identified. The key aspects of the equipment
involved in the TT, defined in the URS, are used in designing and ordering resources. In the KT stage,
vendors are identified, engineering drawings are completed, and all required capital equipment is or-
dered. The timeline for Engineering function activities and deliverables can be highly influenced by
how the URS is defined, which vendors are selected, whether on-site vendor support is used, and how
well standard vendor activities can be leveraged for the capital qualification plan. For instance, some
vendors offer validation packages or will participate in equipment qualification to ensure a smooth
process. Use of the factory acceptance test (FAT) or site acceptance test (SAT) as part of the equipment
qualification may also reduce effort and documentation requirements during the Readiness phase.
Engineering works with the Quality and Regulatory functions to develop a capital VMP that describes
the strategy for qualification or requalification of the facility. The capital VMP also may include the
minimum requirements for developmental purposes in order to execute an engineering batch.

#### 5.3.6 Manufacturing

The RU Manufacturing function works with the Engineering and Process functions to ensure that
the RU plant has adequate capability to complete the TT. Manufacturing will review the SU’s master
batch record, if applicable, and define the operational documentation requirements for the TT (e.g.,
batch records, SOPs, work instructions, training). Throughout the KT stage, all cross-functional areas
should be interacting with Manufacturing to ensure plant design and training result in a process that
can be operated in a reproducible manner to make a product of consistent quality. SU personnel or
experts should meet with RU personnel to convey practical experience in making and/or developing
the product, including lessons learned from process deviations, if applicable.

In the KT stage, Manufacturing should define personal protective equipment requirements to protect
both operator and product so that the Sourcing/Supply Chain function can order the needed equipment.

#### 5.3.7 Regulatory

During the KT stage, the Regulatory function supports the RU’s ability to receive the process, re-
fines the regulatory strategy, aligns with validation approaches, continues planning activities, and
focuses on building key relationships. KT from the SU or MAH Regulatory unit should include
relevant health authority interactions relating to the regulatory process, historical audits, and licen-
sure. Regulatory should communicate project-specific risks to the TT Team to ensure alignment and
understanding.
The regulatory strategy developed during the Initiation and Planning stages should be further devel-
oped during the KT stage, considering the finalized stability plan. Regulatory also should consider the
need for any changes to the primary and secondary labeling.
The revised regulatory strategy also should address the timing and content of planned interactions
with regulatory authorities, including meetings, scientific advice, protocols, and/or correspondence.
Important considerations for the regulatory strategy include definition of the matrixing approaches
for qualification and validation as well as any approach to be taken to prove comparability. The
MAH may consider submitting correspondence or requesting a meeting with the regulatory author-
ity if the acceptability of the comparability strategy is uncertain. The MAH also should consider
submitting a post-approval change management protocol to gain concurrence on the approach be-
ing used for this TT or to potentially reduce the reporting category for future submissions addressed
in the protocol.

#### 5.3.8 Quality

At the KT stage, the RU Quality function performs a quality risk assessment to evaluate the ability of
the RU’s quality systems to make a safe and efficacious product. In conducting this risk assessment,
the Quality function will use the knowledge and information that has been shared by the SU. The

### 5.4 Readiness Stage

In the Readiness stage, the TT Team has all documentation, equipment, and analytical methods in place,
and the RU site is prepared to safely perform process qualification in a compliant manner. Preliminary
KT has occurred, so the RU can finalize the control strategy, qualify the analytical methods, and complete
the facility modifications. Activities in this stage often are performed in parallel with the KT stage, which
requires an iterative process in document development and approval. Because the risk assessment is a liv-
ing document, risk identification and risk mitigation strategies should be constantly reviewed throughout
the TT project. A practical plant capability assessment should be conducted prior to PPQ. During this
assessment, preliminary water or engineering runs may be performed as part of the overall project plan.
As the name implies, Readiness sets the stage for a successful PPQ at the RU. In the stage gate for
Readiness, the steering committee determines whether all requirements are in place for batch execu-
tion and gives approval to proceed with the engineering batch. The case study in Section 8.11 high-
lights the importance of executing a stage gate meeting to ensure that all Readiness requirements have
been met prior to proceeding to the Execution stage.

budget, launch date, and projected volume.

#### 5.4.3 Process

The Process function in the Readiness stage focuses on having all data necessary to finalize the product
control strategy. The control strategy defines the criteria to ensure a robust and repeatable process for
making a product that meets the QTPP and includes hold points, in-process sampling, CPPs, process
ranges, and critical unit operations. Close collaboration with Process, Engineering, Analytical, and
Manufacturing is needed to ensure deployment of an effective control strategy.
In accordance with ICH Q10, the classification of process parameters and their ranges typically is de-
termined by the results of laboratory batches and the increasing knowledge gained during the clinical
phases of development. For commercial products, the RU can leverage a control strategy already in
use by the SU by using its historical data and practical experience. For new products, process charac-
terization studies at laboratory- or pilot-manufacturing-scale will provide understanding of the design
space and the edges of failure for product quality and process efficiency. The results of such studies will
be proven acceptable ranges and normal operating ranges for all unit operations. These lab runs also
may include evaluation of product compatibility with single-use systems or assessment of equipment
materials of construction proposed for the manufacturing process.
Final engineering batches that demonstrate the control strategy should be performed prior to PPQ. To
complete the Readiness stage, the entire process should be tested to demonstrate operational readiness
before moving into PPQ. Such readiness includes any final modifications, document or procedural
readiness, and third-party resources. The TT Team should avoid introducing any new conditions or
variables to the process once the Readiness stage has closed.
In the Readiness stage, Process writes the process validation protocols capturing all process parameters,
the sampling plan, and the analytical testing strategy. The strategy for testing during PPQ typically in-
cludes additional sampling locations and requirements compared to routine commercial production.
Shipping and cleaning validation protocols also are written and approved during this stage.

#### 5.4.4 Analytical

The RU Analytical function executes the validation plan developed during the Planning stage. In Readi-
ness, all method validation and/or transfer protocols covering raw materials, product, packaging, clean-
ing, microbiological testing, and in-process tests are written, approved, and executed. Compendial
methods should be performed at the RU to verify suitability. The Analytical function also writes the sta-
bility protocols prior to the Qualification stage. New analytical equipment will need to be installed and
qualified before the method validation activities begin, and analytical personnel will need to be trained.
In the Readiness stage, the Analytical function also supports the Process function by analyzing re-
quired samples from laboratory- or pilot-scale batches.

#### 5.4.5 Engineering

In the Readiness stage, the Engineering function installs and qualifies all facility, utility, process, and
analytical equipment, including control systems. Engineering works with vendors to facilitate FATs
at the vendor sites to confirm compliance with specifications derived from the URS, and with subse-
quent SATs upon delivery to the RU. The capital VMP often leverages the FATs and/or SATs to reduce
the number of qualification protocol deviations or to use FAT/SAT data to meet initial qualification
requirements. While some developmental studies and testing may begin with equipment or at facilities
that have not completed qualification, qualification of all new equipment and facility modifications
must be complete and must pass all requirements prior to proceeding to the Execution stage.

#### 5.4.6 Manufacturing

The Manufacturing function writes all SOPs, work instructions, and batch records required for the RU
to execute the process. These documents are used to train all personnel involved in the TT at the RU
and are used as part of the EHS risk assessment, which is completed during this stage.
Manufacturing cleans the plant facilities and equipment following the Engineering qualification ac-
tivities, supports the TT Team during execution of various Readiness activities, and conducts testing
to determine whether the plant is ready for use. Manufacturing also should finalize documentation
and training requirements prior to proceeding to the next stage to ensure the Manufacturing function
is fully ready to support execution of the PPQ.

### 5.5 Execution Stage

PPQ is performed in the Execution stage, during which all operational readiness and compliance require-
ments are completed to allow for manufacture at the RU. Materials are tested and released to perform
the engineering batch and PPQ batches, as defined in the project plan and process VMP. With multiple
execution activities taking place in this stage, the team may decide to perform several stage gates, rather
than just one, to continually assess the results from Execution stage activities (see Section 5.5.1).
Table 5.5-1 shows the activities and deliverables for the Execution stage, as excerpted from the Ref-
erence Guide (Figure 5.0-1). One major deliverable of the Execution stage is the submission of the
regulatory filing. After the PPQ batches are complete, the TT Team usually will provide support for
both the regulatory filing and the expected regulatory inspections.

#### 5.5.1 Business

The MAH Business function is actively engaged in defining the launch strategy, anticipating comple-
tion of PPQ batches, regulatory submission, and PAI. Business will work closely with the Sourcing/
Supply Chain function to define the use of the PPQ batches and estimated market demand.
For the Execution stage, multiple stage gates may be used. A stage gate may be performed prior to the
engineering run to verify readiness. Another may be conducted before PPQ to verify results of all en-
gineering and process readiness activities. Stage gates also may be executed after the engineering batch
and PPQ if any changes to the process occur, risks are identified that must be mitigated, or additional
studies are required. After the steering committee approves the regulatory filing, the Business function
can proceed to the Handover stage.

#### 5.5.2 Technology Transfer Project Manager

During the Execution stage, the TT PM supports the team to remove any remaining obstacles for the
engineering and PPQ batches. The TT PM ensures that all documentation is approved and complies
with all operational readiness requirements. All relevant change controls and deviations should be
closed, and training should be completed.
As part of Execution, the TT PM ensures that all compliance and operational requirements have been
met to perform PPQ with the highest probability of success. Continual communication across all
organizational levels is essential. The TT PM also guides the SU and RU teams to define and execute
a person-in-plant program in which SU personnel are present to support RU execution activities and
associated training.
A key deliverable for the TT PM in the Execution stage is approval of the TT report documenting
completion of the TT requirements defined in the TT protocol.

#### 5.5.3 Process

The RU Process function oversees qualification exercises including shipping validation and PPQ runs.
Their responsibilities include investigation of process-related deviations, execution of supporting stud-
ies as defined in the process VMP, and troubleshooting. During this stage, the Process function will
provide process expertise and technical support for equipment to ensure the process is performing
according to planned expectations, evaluating the effectiveness of the control strategy. Throughout the
PPQ, having at least one Process staff person present for each shift of operation is recommended. Prior
to starting the PPQ, the Process function ensures that the knowledge gained during the Readiness
stage is incorporated clearly into the production batch records, procedures, and personnel training.
During this stage, the SU and RU Process functions should analyze data obtained during PPQ to
confirm that the outcomes meet expectations. Any significant differences, as defined in the acceptance
criteria and plan, should be investigated.

testing may be required as well. Typically, PPQ requires more testing than is necessary during routine

#### 5.5.5 Engineering

Having completed all qualification requirements for the facility and equipment, the Engineering func-
tion acts in a supporting role in addressing any issues that occur during the engineering and PPQ runs.
Engineering will investigate deviations and issue change controls related to the equipment or facili-
ties. Engineering may support validation-related sampling, data analysis, and reporting to satisfy all
requirements of the PPQ and VMP and to ensure all requirements of the PPQ have been met.

#### 5.5.6 Manufacturing

A major deliverable of the Execution stage for the Manufacturing function is execution of the engi-
neering and PPQ batches. At a minimum, the SU team should participate with the RU team during
the engineering batch if possible. Knowledge gained from the engineering batch commonly leads to
process adjustments and improvements, prompting updates to SOPs and the master batch record. The
engineering run should use materials from the suppliers that will be used for PPQ.
The engineering batch may be produced under GMP or non-GMP conditions. If conducted under
GMP requirements, the engineering batch may be used to produce product for continued clinical sup-
ply. If allowed by both the process VMP and the PPQ protocol, both of which have been approved in
advance, an engineering batch run under GMP conditions also may serve as a PPQ batch, particularly
when the raw material supply is limited.
The Manufacturing function is a major contributor of feedback on operations, training effectiveness,
and batch records following completion of the qualification activities. This feedback should be incor-
porated into lessons learned prior to handover to the RU post-transfer operations team.

#### 5.5.7 Regulatory

During the Execution stage, the Regulatory function is actively drafting the regulatory submission
and, based on the submission tracker, actively managing the authoring, reviewing, and finalizing of
documents. The submission timing and content should be aligned with the regulatory strategy that
was finalized in the Readiness stage. The RU should provide frequent updates to the MAH Regulatory
function to ensure that new and evolving information discovered during Execution can be incorpo-
rated into the submission. The RU should inform the MAH Regulatory function of the assumptions
being made and of their evaluation of regulatory risks.
At the end of the Execution stage, after ensuring that the CMC content has been extensively reviewed
cross-functionally, the steering committee will authorize filing of the submission. The steering com-
mittee should be apprised of risks and assumptions before authorizing filing.
The Regulatory function also works with Quality on PAI preparation. This may include suggesting high-
risk items to include in storyboard development as well as ensuring alignment of the key claims made
in the dossier, plant-level documentation, and storyboards. High-risk items and topics likely to arise
during PAI include activities associated with deviations during PPQ and subjects that the inspectorate
has historically identified as focus areas for the site, manufacturing process, product class, or company.

#### 5.5.8 Quality

The Quality function is integral throughout the Execution stage as it releases materials for use. Quality
is responsible for product release, including batch record review and overall batch disposition, in accor-
dance with site quality procedures. Quality also is responsible for supporting the review and approval
of all documentation for the PPQ, including protocols, reports, batch records, forms, results, change
controls, and deviation and investigation close-outs. The Quality function would be expected to close
out the quality risk assessment associated with the TT at the end of the PPQ.
At this point in the TT, Quality performs the final inspection readiness activities in anticipation of the
regulatory filing which, depending on TT type, may trigger a PAI. A mock inspection (in-depth audit)
of the RU performed in anticipation of a PAI may be used to identify issues that should be addressed
prior to filing a submission.
Completion of the PAI may be an endpoint to the Execution stage depending on the overall business
and regulatory strategies.
5.5.9	 Sourcing/Supply Chain
During the Execution stage, the Sourcing/Supply Chain function will work closely with the Business
function to finalize shipping requirements. The Sourcing/Supply Chain function also will work with
the Manufacturing and Engineering functions on PPQ requirements, including scheduling, material,
and storage needs as well as logistics. As outputs of the Execution stage, Sourcing/Supply Chain will
have established suppliers, a clear supply chain, and defined standards for production planning in
order to move forward to commercial manufacture.

### 5.6 Handover Stage

The Handover stage comprises the transition of the product from the TT Team to the RU post-transfer
operations team, covering routine production of marketed products. Because the risk assessment is
a living document, risk identification and risk mitigation strategies should be constantly reviewed
throughout the TT project. The Handover stage includes multiple activities across several functions at
the RU site to ensure it is ready to work independently for commercial production or for the next stage
of product development. Requirements for a smooth transition will vary depending on the type of TT
and the endpoints established in the TT charter. For instance, the extent of Sourcing/Supply Chain
requirements will vary based on how quickly the Business function is able to establish the customer
base and volume requirements.
Ensuring that all lessons learned from the TT are incorporated into the existing documentation for
improved commercial production is a key element of the Handover stage. (This technical report does
not address such standard post-transfer requirements as equipment/facility maintenance, APR, life-
cycle validation, complaint handling, or CPV programs.)
Table 5.6-1 shows the activities and deliverables described in the Reference Guide for the Handover
stage. A stage gate is held with the steering committee and RU site management to approve formal
handover of the process to the RU for routine operations.

#### 5.6.1 Business

Once the PPQ is completed, the Business function typically performs a review of the business contract
with the RU. The Business function ensures estimated commercial volumes can be produced and develops
a resource plan for any changes needed to accommodate the product and its associated requirements.

#### 5.6.2 Technology Transfer Project Manager

The TT PM ensures that the TT charter elements have been addressed in executing the TT. As part of
the Handover stage, the TT PM documents all lessons learned and may help the RU incorporate key
improvements prior to the final transfer. The TT PM also will ensure that the project is formally closed
out and that all project requirements have been met. Section 8.7 presents a case study of the benefits
of planning ahead for commercial readiness and conducting the handover.

#### 5.6.3 Process

The Process function of the TT Team may continue to support the product following qualification
during routine post-transfer production, as Process personnel are critical to KT during the Handover
stage. The Process function also may take the lead in executing process improvements identified from
the PPQ, including associated change controls.

#### 5.6.5 Engineering

During the Handover stage, the Engineering function should consider any lessons learned relating
to equipment operation during qualification. Engineering may identify process improvements, up-
grades, or replacements of equipment associated with the new process. These changes will be evalu-
ated against the site change control and filing requirements for impact prior to incorporating ahead
of commercial start.

#### 5.6.6 Manufacturing

The Manufacturing function is key to providing feedback for lessons learned and potential improve-
ment areas following qualification. This feedback typically focuses on training, equipment operation,
and the manufacturing batch record. Manufacturing will ensure all personnel supporting the ongoing
commercial needs are trained adequately and are ready to support commercial demand.
Manufacturing may support and/or take ownership of CAPAs resulting from qualification that need
to be addressed ahead of commercial production, such as deviations resulting from unclear instruc-
tions. The RU Manufacturing function should verify that it is ready to take on all responsibilities for
commercial start, including cleaning, product changeovers, sample pulling and submission, media
fills, change control, maintenance activities, and batch operations.
The case study in Section 8.12 describes what could go wrong during commercial production if the
Handover process with the Manufacturing and Sourcing/Supply Chain functions is inadequate to
ensure readiness for post-transfer production.

#### 5.6.7 Regulatory

The Regulatory function communicates expected timing for regulatory approval. Any potential delays
should be communicated to the TT Team in a timely manner. Regulatory also contributes to the
launch of products, reviewing promotional materials and medical information for consistency with
product labeling.
The MAH Regulatory function will need to identify the RU personnel who will be responsible for
ongoing communications on regulatory matters.
The TT Team may review opportunities for efficiencies or cost reductions that may result in post-
approval submissions.

#### 5.6.8 Quality

As part of the Handover stage, the MAH Quality function establishes the quality management review
process, which should involve both the RU and the SU. The quality agreement may need to be up-
dated to address everything associated with commercial manufacture.
Quality will ensure the closure of all documentation from the TT, including change controls, valida-
tion reports, deviations, and CAPAs. It also may play a role in identifying lessons learned from the
Execution stage that should be incorporated in future training for the post-transfer operations team to
improve process quality.
5.6.9	 Sourcing/Supply Chain
The Sourcing/Supply Chain function continues to perform production planning and material sourc-
ing activities similar to those conducted during the TT process, incorporating any improvements
identified from the Execution stage.

As the key stakeholder accountable for the product with regulatory authorities, the MAH determines
the goals for the TT and is the contract giver.
Once the decision has been made to proceed with the TT, a steering committee is formed. Each site
involved in the TT should assign steering committee members who will be accountable for achieving
the TT goals. Typically, steering committee members are one management level above the TT team for
each of the functional areas working on the TT and have decision-making authority. The steering com-
mittee is empowered to remove roadblocks, assume risk, provide resources, and determine whether the
requirements of each stage have been fulfilled.
Upper management may identify a Project Sponsor to oversee the TT. The person in this role typically
is the single point of contact between the TT and upper management and has overall accountability to
ensure the TT is delivered as required by the business. The Project Sponsor may be part of the steering
committee, but the steering committee is accountable to the Project Sponsor.
Stage gates at each of the six stages allow the steering committee to determine whether a TT is ready
to proceed to the next stage. Often TT activities will be performed across stages in parallel rather than
sequentially due to business needs. Each stage gate, however, allows the steering committee to ensure
that all necessary activities and deliverables for that stage have been satisfactorily completed and that
the TT charter, as described in Section 4.3, can be accomplished. When a TT Team finds information
during a stage that may change the TT boundaries and/or assumptions, it should be communicated
immediately to the steering committee rather than waiting for the stage gate review.

### 4.3 TT Charter and Success Factors

The overall framework of a TT should be documented in a charter. The charter should be developed by
the TT Team and must be approved by the steering committee and, if applicable, the Project Sponsor.
Approval by the steering committee establishes that the sites have reached the alignment necessary to
proceed.

### 8.1 Technology Transfer Charter, Technology Transfer Governance,

and Communication Plan
Product Lifecycle Phase: Commercial-Commercial
TT Process Stage: Planning
Site Types: Internal-External, External-External
To reduce costs, Company A made a business decision to consolidate outsourcing of drug product
packaging, expecting to achieve savings quickly. Company A awarded a multiproduct bundle to a con-
tract manufacturing organization (CMO), Company B, transferring packaging for 15 drug products
from other contract providers. These products were new to the facility of Company B, the receiving
unit (RU). This external-external TT was performed over a two-year period. The TT goal was to
complete the transfer of the first product in seven months, with a new product in place each month
thereafter, requiring all TTs to occur in parallel.
As Company B worked to achieve the TT goals, Company A continuously changed knowledge re-
quirements, including labels, component design, preferred vendors and suppliers, and the qualifica-
tion of backup sources. In addition, Company A asked the TT team to change the planned order of
products being transferred. These changes prevented Company B’s TT Team from meeting project
timelines, a situation Company A escalated to senior management in both companies.
With senior leadership from both companies involved, a formal process for the TT was implemented.
This process included steering committee and governance oversight, a communication plan, additional
staffing, and improved TT project manager (PM) tools to allow greater visibility of the project’s prog-
ress and risks. The changes specifically included the following:
•
Additional resources were provided to enable key functions to focus on their specific workstream
for a cluster of products based on process similarities and timeline.
•
Following an all-day workshop, workstream meetings were held weekly to allow each function from
both Company A and Company B to maintain alignment of their activities across the entire portfolio.
•
The TT PM implemented a weekly progress meeting across workstreams during which the
project timeline, needs, and risks were discussed and reported to Company A. The use of
improved visual tools for these updates allowed for better alignment with project goals.
•
A steering committee of one-level-higher management from both companies was formed. A
weekly steering committee meeting allowed timely feedback, risk management, and escalation
support from both Company A and Company B.
•
Using input from the steering committee, the senior management of both companies met monthly and
held face-to-face meetings on a quarterly basis throughout the project to provide overall governance.
Company B learned the value of establishing a TT charter and of ensuring that Planning stage activi-
ties were completed before moving to the next stage. After implementing the governance structure and
a communication plan, Company B encountered significantly fewer change requests from Company
A, allowing the TT Team to complete knowledge transfer (KT).

Two events jeopardized the TT timeline, but the new timeline risks were quickly communicated to
the steering committee for risk mitigation and the information relayed to the governance team. First, a
vendor delay was resolved by negotiating a payment to the vendor to expedite the material. Second, a
competing priority arose with another commercial activity using shared equipment. With the involve-

### 8.2 Selecting the Right Team to Ensure Success of the Technology Transfer

Project
Product Lifecycle Phase: Development-Commercial
TT Process Stages: Initiation, Planning
Site Types: Internal-External
Company A decided to transfer a new sterile drug product (Product X) to a CMO (Company B).
Company A’s limited TT experience and limited internal expertise to address the cross-functional re-
quirements, comprising only two executive company leaders from the Quality and Supply functions,
had caused problems with a previous TT.
Company B assigned dedicated personnel from cross-functional disciplines including Project Man-

Company A realized it was understaffed for this new project and could not support these functions

manage the project and make all decisions.
After experiencing challenges due to its understaffing in the early stages of the TT of Product X, Com-

empower its TT Team to fully manage the project efficiently.

### 8.3 Importance of the Initiation and Planning Stages to Technology Transfer

Product Lifecycle Phase: Commercial-Commercial
TT Process Stages: Initiation, Planning
Site Types: Internal-Internal (Regional)
Company A needed to increase capacity for a drug substance (Product X). Since its sending unit (SU)
site was unable to meet the full volume forecast of Product X, Company A made a business decision
to transfer the process to another facility within its company network to serve as a second source of
supply. Implementing a cloned process at the RU facility with minimal changes was critical to its regu-
latory strategy. To do this, the RU was instructed to purchase identical equipment (where possible)
and replicate the existing process from the SU site. The SU shared full details of the equipment model
and specifications, process documentation, and learnings with the RU to facilitate setup of the process.

--- PAGE 59 ---
Because of the process equivalency required and the ability to easily share knowledge between the sites,
Company A assessed this TT to be of low risk and rapid execution, with minimal development and
technical requirements. As a result, Company A underestimated both the budget and the amount of
time needed for completion. Additionally, the project was staffed with only part-time internal support,
primarily personnel from the RU site, that did not include the cross-functional expertise needed for
the TT.
Despite having almost identical equipment and process-operating history, Company A experienced
multiple technical challenges. For one, Company A could not purchase the exact models from the
same vendors and had to select newer models in their place, yet no risk assessment was performed
to identify the impact of what seemed to be minor differences in the new equipment. As the process
would be identical, a full gap assessment for the process impact at the RU was not performed. Also, a
lack of resourcing for the TT at the SU site prevented a thorough KT from the SU to the RU. Subse-
quently, the RU could not leverage shared learnings, historical data, or expertise the SU site had gained
over the years or from the original development and qualification. As a result, the process validation
and operational robustness depended on dedicated SU resources to continue assistance with their
historical knowledge of the process.
Internal TT teams often fail to perform adequate due diligence of a TT in the Initiation and Planning
stages of the process. This case study demonstrates the importance of following a TT process, resourc-
ing appropriately, and maintaining effective KT even for internal TTs.

### 8.4 Use of Regulatory Strategy for Initiation Stage Decisions

Product Lifecycle Phase: Commercial-Commercial
TT Process Stage: Initiation
Site Types: External-External
Company A used CMO C for manufacture of a veterinary drug product (Product Y) marketed only
in the United States. When CMO C notified Company A that it no longer wanted to be a CMO,
but would be manufacturing only for itself, Company A had to find a new CMO within a short time
frame. Product Y is a sterile, lyophilized product with an approved indication only for the veterinary
market. The TT was to include complete product and process transfer as well as analytical testing (re-
lease and stability) and stability storage.
Finding a company that can manufacture a lyophilized product can be a challenge, but this case pre-
sented additional barriers. Many companies with lyophilization capabilities manufacture lyophilized
products for the Brazilian market. At the time, the Brazilian agency, ANVISA, specified that if a drug
substance had no approved human indication, the veterinary product must be manufactured on a
separate production line in a separate building from products for human use. Therefore, Company A
needed to find a CMO that could manufacture Product Y on a separate production line from human
products. As many CMOs had only one or two lyophilization production lines, the available pool of
CMOs for transfer was limited.
Company A formed an initial TT Team comprising a PM and representatives from the Quality, Regu-
latory, and Supply Chain functions. The team reviewed the capabilities of multiple CMOs and found
three companies (CMOs D, E, and F) that were able to manufacture Product Y in the U.S. in a
manner that would be compliant with the current ANVISA requirements. The TT Team evaluated
the CMOs based on their quality system and documentation, capability and expertise in lyophilized
product manufacturing, equipment equivalence, analytical capabilities, costs, and projected timelines
to commercial launch. Site evaluations revealed that CMO D was not interested due to the small

--- PAGE 60 ---
volume that is common for veterinary products. The remaining two sites estimated similar costs, but
the projected timelines to launch differed by six months, with CMO F having the longer timeline.
The TT Team further investigated the drastic difference and determined that the longer timeline was
more realistic. CMO F had more experience and better understood the complexities of transferring a
lyophilized product. The TT Team chose CMO F, despite the longer timeline, because it had consid-
ered in more detail what needed to be done and it employed a strong PM to help guide the process.

### 8.5 Knowledge Transfer to Ensure Process Qualification Success

Product Lifecycle Phase: Development-Commercial
TT Process Stage: Knowledge Transfer
Site Types: Internal-External
Company A decided to outsource production of a new vaccine drug product. The selected CMO,
Company B, had significant experience performing TTs of new processes, working with dozens of
customers each year at their facility. Company A was relatively new and had limited experience in
the pharmaceutical industry; in fact, this would be its first experience taking a product in its pipeline
through to commercialization.
Company B had implemented lessons learned from a previous project with a different company, Com-
pany C. Company B determined that multiple delays during the project with Company C had re-
sulted from added equipment requirements, lack of clearly established goals and boundaries, and lack
of transparency during KT. Additionally, complex differences in operations had required alignment
between Company B and Company C to ship and receive materials from each other, resolve unique
labeling requirements, and enhance material inspection requirements.
With these learnings in mind, Company B implemented significant improvements in its TT process
to ensure a successful and proactive KT with Company A. Company B developed several tools and
checklists to help with the KT upon project award, including:
•
Use of a handover form and checklist to establish all product requirements, TT boundaries, and goals.
•
KT checklist and a prepared playbook to be shared with Company A immediately upon award,
ahead of the kick-off, detailing the customer’s expectations, key next steps, and key data the RU
would request. The materials included a questionnaire and checklist to help both companies ask
the right questions and share key documents early in the process.
•
Break-out meetings for each function, held virtually prior to the TT kick-off, to ensure the kick-

•
A face-to-face kick-off to formally start the project, align on requirements for each phase of the
project, and align on any differences already discovered through the playbook, checklist, and
questionnaire.
In addition:
•
The team performed an in-depth technical risk assessment of the TT to aid in effective KT for
implementation at Company B, using a tracking tool and risk management document during the
Execution stage to capture all knowledge and risk mitigation.
•
The team leveraged on-the-floor support from key experts at Company A to capture tacit knowl-
edge transfer between functions as the TT progressed to development personnel and again to
commercial production personnel.
Use of a detailed knowledge transfer process with transparent communications allowed the TT project
to be completed on time with no delays.

### 8.6 Performing Process Fit Assessment to Facilitate Technology Transfer

Product Lifecycle Phase: Development-Commercial
TT Process Stage: Initiation, Planning
Site Types: Internal-Internal
Company X developed a multivalent vaccine (Product Alpha) and produced phase I and II clinical
drug substance materials at pilot scale. Company X determined that it required a commercial facility
to produce drug substance for phase III clinical trials and commercial supply. A review of the market
requirements pointed to the need for a change in scale, and company policy encouraged an increase in
the level of automation to improve digitization of process data, reduce reliance on paper records, and
reduce potential for human error.
In order to guide equipment specifications, process engineers assigned to the TT Team at Company X
(the SU) developed scaling factors for the pilot-to-commercial volumes covering each unit operation.
The fact that Product Alpha was multivalent presented complications, as some serotypes had differ-
ent volume requirements for the same unit operation. To avoid purchasing a piece of equipment for
each serotype, process engineers grouped the serotypes according to maximum and minimum volume
requirements and determined the minimum amount of process equipment required to cover all cases.
An initial process fit assessment was conducted in the Initiation stage with a general idea of requirements,
followed by a detailed assessment during the Knowledge Transfer stage. With yield information from the
SU, and the previously mentioned scaling factor, the detailed process fit assessment was more accurate
and complete. In it, ingredient additions, flowrates, maximum and minimum vessel volumes, and similar
mass balance parameters were assessed to check the fit of the process in both new and existing equipment.
It was also necessary to determine what process development work, if any, would be required to support
the process transfer to the new facility, especially since a change in scale was necessary. Residence and mix-
ing times were altered in equipment and piping in the larger facility. Such changes were considered as part
of the TT risk assessment. In this case, the mixing for a key reaction step was studied using computational
flow dynamics modeling and a model chemical reaction to ensure adequate mixing at the larger scale.
The process fit assessment begun during the Initiation stage and revised during the Knowledge Trans-
fer stage was vital to ensuring that the right equipment was specified and procured and that the right
process development work was conducted to ensure a successful TT of Product Alpha.

### 8.7 Planning Ahead for On Time Commercial Launch

Product Lifecycle Phase: Commercial-Commercial
TT Process Stages: Initiation, Planning, Execution, Handover
Site Types: Internal-External
Business considerations are usually the driving factor for performing a TT, with identification of the
RU being the first key deliverable. In this case study, Company A (the SU) wanted to consider transfer-
ring a sterile parenteral drug product to another facility.
A small team of experts from Company A’s project management, business, quality, and manufactur-
ing functional areas performed due diligence in the choice of the RU (Company B). Once the RU
was identified and a nondisclosure agreement was executed, the TT PM at the SU expanded the SU
TT Team to include the Analytical, Regulatory, and Engineering functions. The RU also formed a
TT Team of corresponding functional areas. These two TT teams established regular meetings to fa-

--- PAGE 62 ---
cilitate KT and communication. Working together, the teams developed the project plan, defined the
regulatory strategy for approval of the drug product, and executed a risk assessment of the knowledge
elements needed to adapt the manufacturing process to the equipment at the RU in order to produce
product to the quality target product profile and specifications required.
While completing the regulatory filing submission and health authority inspections, preparations be-
gan to hand over the product to manufacturing. The RU TT Team held meetings of increasing fre-
quency closer to the start date for commercial production. During these meetings, the RU TT Team
shared all lessons learned from product performance, equipment and process knowledge, and supplier
management, including component and material suppliers with lead times. Formal introduction to
key contacts were made, and key commercial systems such as material and batch management, supply
management, and equipment were set up and ready for the new product. The RU TT Team recom-

incorporated into the batch record. The RU TT Team also recommended changes to the production

facility was on a critical timeline for manufacturing of regulatory batches, regulatory approval, and
launch of commercial product from the vendor site. The TT was closed by the handover from the TT
Team to a post-transfer operations team with complementary expertise and responsibilities for control
of commercial production.
As a result of this successful planning and management of Handover activities, the site was ready to
begin commercial production at the scheduled start without any delays.

### 8.8 Navigating Process, Capital, & Ownership Changes During a Drug

Product Transfer
Product Lifecycle Phase: Development-Commercial
TT Process Stage: All: Initiation, Planning, Knowledge Transfer, Readiness, Execution, Handover
Site Types: External-External
In today’s manufacturing, it is common when transferring technology from one manufacturing site
to another manufacturing site for both sites to be external to the MAH. So-called “virtual companies”
may start with one external supplier, then later add suppliers or switch suppliers for quality or pricing
reasons. This case study, which involved the decision to switch manufacturers upon completion of
clinical phase III, discusses the basics that should be considered for a seamless transfer.
Initiation: Following a capacity analysis aligned to the business strategy, the MAH decided to contin-
ue with external manufacturing. With regulatory considerations the driving factor, the MAH decided
to transfer production from the external party manufacturing the clinical phase III supplies (Company
A) to a different external party for commercial production. The MAH’s existing cross-functional TT
Team remained with the product to facilitate this new TT, and a TT proposal was drafted.
Led by the TT PM, the TT Team visited the prospective commercial site (Company B) to evaluate
the potential fit and identify the process, equipment, and analytical method gaps that would need
to be addressed for a successful transfer. A material supply agreement and related quality agreement
were reached. Because the MAH did not want Company A to know about the pending transfer to
Company B, the material supply agreement remained confidential. In this case, the MAH acted as the

--- PAGE 63 ---
responsible SU and no communication was allowed between Company A and Company B. With the
approval of the TT proposal, the steering committee made the decision to launch with Company B.
Planning: A detailed project plan that spelled out clear deliverables by phase and stage gate and de-
tailed the project governance that was created and approved by the SU and the RU (Company B).
The same raw material vendors would be used, though all vendors needed to be requalified. For qualifi-
cation, each vendor was required to supply three lots of raw materials used in manufacturing, followed
by reissuing of all specifications, methods, and other supporting chemistry documents. The batch record
was transferred, and the equipment was purchased to match as closely as possible the current process.
In this case, the scale was to be increased, which resulted in a new registration batch followed by three
validation batches. Based on process knowledge and guided by the process failure mode effects analysis,
a process validation master plan was approved that defined the necessary protocols to be executed.
The MAH discussed scalable processes, engineering batches, and process sampling and controls. The decision
was made to separate the batch record into two—one batch record for mixing and one for filling—as there
was a significant hold time after mixing and a check was needed on the product before filling. The steering
committee approved the stage gate, authorizing the TT Team to proceed to full-scale manufacturing.
Execution: Once new equipment was installed and validated, dynamic smoke studies were conducted,
and Company B decided to perform media fills using their specific vial and equipment to demonstrate
that aseptic conditions were maintained under the slightly changed conditions. Microbiological test-
ing of all personnel and the environment was performed throughout the process using touch plates,
and a swab study was performed after a risk assessment to determine the appropriate environmental
monitoring program.
According to the stability plan, stability studies were performed in both the upright and inverted
positions for all batches. All the RU’s cleaning procedures, cleaning swab-testing, protocols, reports,
training, and risk assessments were determined to be adequate as is and were used for the transfer. Se-
rialization, packaging, and labeling were agreed upon. Company B wanted the equipment to be able
to label for unit-dosing before unit-dose labeling became mandatory.
Methods were successfully transferred and were qualified or validated per requirements.
Project Closure/Lessons Learned: The TT was successful but, in hindsight, the process could have
been smoother had the MAH and Company B identified early on which party would be responsible
for the purchase of the additional capital. In the end, the MAH and the RU agreed to share these costs,
with the exception that the Company B agreed to purchase a couple of the more expensive items. An
additional difficulty was that the MAH had not negotiated or retained the rights to the methods, batch
records, and all documents necessary for the transfer that Company A was performing for them. Had
the MAH preemptively ensured it owned those rights, the TT would have been easier to execute.

launched the product by the middle of the following year, almost six months late. Company A was
forced to consider extending business relations with Company B to supply their product in the interim
or otherwise impact market supply.

### 8.10 Effective Use of Knowledge Transfer

Product Lifecycle Phase: Commercial-Commercial
TT Process Stage: Initiation, Planning, Execution
Site Types: External-Internal
In this case study, the technology of a commercialized sterile drug product (mAb-X) approved for
commercial drug product manufacturing at an external site (Site A) was transferred to Site B, the RU.
Site B was an internal site of the MAH for mAb-X (Company Y) and was new to commercial drug
manufacturing. Site A and Site B did not communicate directly during the TT. The Manufacturing
Science and Technology group of Company Y served as the SU and was responsible for the KT from
Site A to the Site B.
Company Y had experience with TT for product mAb-X having previously transferred the product
to Site A in a development-to-commercial phase TT. Therefore, Company Y already possessed infor-
mation important to the TT, such as the commercial manufacturing process, required equipment,

--- PAGE 65 ---
process parameters classification, risk assessment for the commercial process, and analytical methods
for product quality testing. This information was used as a reference for the TT of the same product
to Site B.
The goal of the TT was to minimize the number of changes to the existing manufacturing process used
by Site A while designing a process for commercial manufacture that fit within the constraints of Site
B. To achieve this objective, a facility-fit and gap assessment was performed for Site B, which became
the key to the success of the TT. The existing risk assessment information for mAb-X at Site A was
used as the starting point for the risk assessment for site B. Also, since the RU was a new commercial
drug product manufacturing site, the TT team worked with the on-site Validation group at Site B to
map out the equipment validation activities required to support this TT (installation qualification,
operational qualification, and performance qualification). An integrated project plan was drafted dur-
ing the Planning stage that included an overview of all TT activities needed for the successful TT and
their interdependencies.
The SU shared the information on analytical methods from the prior mAb-X TT with the RU. An
evaluation for implementation of existing analytical methods for Site B was completed, and resources
were allocated for the method transfer, training, and execution. Change controls and documentation
procedures for manufacturing mAb-X were established before the TT activities to Site B were execut-
ed. All changes related to process, raw material specifications, equipment, and process parameters for
the TT were captured under the central change control management system.
As part of the Readiness stage, the sourcing of raw materials, components, and equipment was re-
viewed, and relevant information was included in the validated electronic system (in this case, SAP).
The information for product, packaging, and component specifications was reviewed and approved by
both the RU and the SU. In addition, upon request, vendors/suppliers supplied drawings and other
required information. Suppliers also provided certificate of analysis information for all raw materials
and components, which was reviewed. Inspection and acceptance criteria for receiving raw materials
and components in a GMP facility were agreed upon by the RU and SU teams with input from the
Quality function.
As part of process performance qualification (PPQ) readiness activities, the SU Engineering group,
in collaboration with their RU counterparts, prepared process description documents capturing the
commercial manufacturing process for mAb-X at Site B. Information from the process description
document for mAb-X at Site A was used as a template, and appropriate changes were made to capture
the differences in the process at Site B. Process characterization studies were carried out to support
the changes in the commercial process for Site B based on the facility-fit and gap assessment exercise,
which was critical for the success of this TT. Also, prior knowledge from the TT of mAb-X to Site A
was leveraged for process characterization studies.
Engineering runs were performed using surrogate and using mAb-X to understand the design space
for the operation of process parameters and to determine the normal operating range and proven ac-
ceptable range for various unit operations. The required number of PPQ batches were manufactured
using a matrix approach to bracket the batch size (minimum and maximum), and the rationale for
the PPQ strategy was documented prior to execution. Post-PPQ, all deviations were closed and docu-
mented, and required CAPAs were identified and implemented. At this point, PPQ summary reports
were written that captured the process parameter data for all PPQ batches, hold-time data for various
process steps, product quality attribute results (in-process and lot-release testing results), and devia-
tions for the PPQ batches. In addition, at the end of the PPQ campaign, completed batch records and
cleaning batch records were reviewed.

### 8.11 Use of Stage Gates to Address Process Readiness Challenges

Product Lifecycle Phase: Development-Commercial
TT Process Stage: Readiness
Site Types: Internal-Internal (Global)
Company A transferred a very complex process involving a new drug product from development
(Product X) in this case study. The new molecular entity required new technologies for which limited
industry expertise existed. The TT Team encountered significant delays, and they spent more than
two years troubleshooting various problems with the new equipment and repeating developmental
studies on the new equipment to finalize the process parameters and develop a robust process. The TT
Team did not feel it was possible to meet the company leadership’s goals of both advancing Product X
to PPQ to meet launch requirements and maintaining the project within the adjusted budget, which
was well over the original budget. This misalignment prompted the TT Team to implement a steering
committee and stage gates.
The team designed the formal TT process to allow both functional management and senior leadership
to review key project data, risks, accomplishments, and proposed options for each phase of the project
for risk mitigation. By engaging senior leaders from elsewhere within the company, senior manage-
ment was able to provide additional expertise on the new technology and similar processes. These
leaders also offered different perspectives on the suboptimal data. Following review of the data, and
with support from management to improve the process, the TT team agreed on the next steps, tried
the recommended strategies, and leveraged on-site support from proposed SMEs within the company.
The improvements to the processes resulted in a product that met requirements.
Before proceeding, the TT Team facilitated another stage gate meeting to review recent data and
ensure alignment on next steps. The process was much improved, but given the unique process and
requirements, new technologies, and limited expertise, the steering committee decided the TT Team
should execute one more study to facilitate the learnings and gain additional experience. That study
would be followed by a mock run (water batch) to improve the training and experience of the produc-
tion personnel prior to proceeding to PPQ. Ultimately, the team successfully executed their qualifica-
tion batches right first time. Although the added developmental studies and mock run took additional
time, the TT Team agreed that by managing the previous misalignment, they had saved time by pre-
venting a failed qualification or Handover.

### 8.12 Importance of Handover Stage for a Successful Commercial Start

Product Lifecycle Phase: Development-Commercial
TT Process Stage: Handover
Site Types: Internal-Internal
Company A had little experience with TT; however, with a new building and equipment, it had
gained the technological capability to support a new sterile drug product transfer being planned
(Product X). In addition, Company A hired personnel, bringing new functions into the facility in-
cluding TT PMs, scientists, regulatory affairs staff, and process engineers. Company A also brought
in several experienced consultants to support the new equipment and its validation at the facility,
who stayed on through the Product X launch. The TT Team was well supported with project staffing,
regular stage gates, a steering committee, and engaged leadership. Due to their limited experience in
TT and the risks associated with the new process, equipment, and technology, the company man-
agement also ensured that SMEs would be closely involved to monitor and support each step of the
project.

During development and process qualification, the TT Team used an “all hands on deck” approach,
expecting the entire team to participate on the floor and oversee the process during execution of the
studies and commercial batches. The team managed the checklists, oversaw the batch records, and led
each step to ensure the production operators and area personnel were correctly executing the process
requirements. Production personnel also incorporated interventions, adjustments, and recommen-
dations based on learnings from the TT Team’s experience. The team wanted to make sure that the
expertise and learnings from their history with the project and equipment were leveraged to ensure
the commercial batches would be completed right first time. As a result, the project was successful: the
commercial batches were executed flawlessly and met all requirements for release. Following project
closure, the TT Team personnel were repurposed to other positions within the company and the con-
sultant contracts were terminated.
As the facility entered routine commercial operations for this product, however, the production per-
sonnel found the new process and product requirements very challenging. Without the expertise on
the floor to oversee and guide them every step of the way, the batch records, SOPs, and training proved
insufficient. Due to the production personnel’s lack of understanding on how to set up and operate
the new equipment, the facility experienced significant downtime and eventually had to obtain on-
site vendor support to retrain and create training guides for the team. Because of the lost time, failed
batches due to human error, investigations, and CAPAs, the manufacturing team decided to update
the SOPs and batch records associated with the new process and retrain the personnel. After several
months—and significant product losses and costs—the production personnel had obtained the nec-
essary knowledge on the process and had received adequate training through the updated SOPs and
batch records to successfully and repeatably execute the processes without additional support.
In hindsight, if the TT Reference Guide approach had been used, critical knowledge elements could
have been identified in the KT stage, and risk mitigation strategies could have been developed to en-
sure the successful deployment of commercial operations.

About PDA Technical Reports
PDA Technical Reports are global consensus documents, prepared by member-driven Task Forces (listed on inside
front cover) comprised of content experts, including scientists and engineers working in the pharmaceutical/bio-
pharmaceutical industry, regulatory authorities and academia. While in development, PDA Technical Reports are
subjected to a global review of PDA members and other topic-specific experts, often including regulatory officials.
Comments from the global review are then considered by the authoring Task Force during the preparation of the
final working draft. The level of expertise of the Task Force and those participating in the global review ensure a
broad perspective reflecting best thinking and practices currently available.
The final working draft is next reviewed by the PDA Advisory Board or Boards that sanctioned the project. PDA’s
Advisory Boards are: Science Advisory Board, Biotechnology Advisory Board, and Regulatory Affairs and Quality
Advisory Board. Following this stage of review, the PDA Board of Directors conducts the final review and deter-
mines whether to publish or not publish the document as an official PDA Technical Report.
While PDA goes to great lengths to ensure each Technical Report is of the highest quality, all readers are encouraged
to contact PDA about any scientific, technical, or regulatory inaccuracies, discrepancies, or mistakes that might be
found in any of the documents. Readers can email PDA at: TRfeedback@pda.org.
