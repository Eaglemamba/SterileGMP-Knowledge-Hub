# PDA Technical Report No. 68 (Revised 2024): Risk-Based Approach for Prevention and Management of Drug Shortages

## 1.0 Introduction

PDA Technical Report No. 68: Risk-Based Approach for Prevention and Management of Drug Shortages
was originally developed in 2014 as part of the interassociation collaborative response to the European
Medicines Agency’s (EMA) initiative on medicinal product shortages caused by manufacturing,
quality, and/or good manufacturing practice (GMP) issues. This revision updates the 2014 version
to recognize emerging regulatory expectations and current terminology. The risk-based approach and
concepts in the original technical report are broadly recognized and remain unchanged.
This revision recognizes the advances made in pharmaceutical manufacturing and aligns the text of
TR-68 with the current regulatory environment, for example, the International Council for
Harmonisation (ICH) Quality Guideline Q9(R1): Quality Risk Management, ICH Quality Guideline
Q12: Technical and Regulatory Considerations for Pharmaceutical Product Lifecycle Management, regional
regulations and guidelines, and terminologies used for drug shortages that have since been published.
Examples for preventive measures to avoid drug shortages consider lessons learned since TR-68 was
first published in 2014. Elements in the templates and forms included in this revision have been
updated to reflect current regulatory expectations and terminology used in the European Union and
its Member States and in the United States, while it also supports compliance with global expectations
on managing and preventing drug shortages.
Sustainable access to safe, efficacious, and high-quality products every time a patient needs them is
just as important as the therapeutic advances that have transformed the lives and survival of patients
worldwide. The ICH Q9(R1) defines harm as “damage to health, including damage that can occur
from loss of product quality or availability” (1). Ensuring uninterrupted availability of and access to
products, especially medically necessary, lifesaving or life sustaining critical medicines that do not have
effective alternatives, is essential.
1.1
Background
Drug shortages have become more frequent, more severe, and longer-lasting globally since the early
2000s for a variety of reasons. As globalization increases, the complexity of supply chains grows, global
and regional regulatory expectations evolve, and economic and business motivators change. The driv-
ers behind drug shortages in different geographic areas and countries vary, and those causes are usually
multifactorial. The range of causes includes financial, economic, and business concerns, increased or
unanticipated market demand (e.g., pandemic), evolving regulatory expectations, manufacturing and
quality issues, capacity constraints, lack of redundancy, and supply chain issues (2-5). Recognition has
evolved that the multifactorial root causes for shortages extend beyond manufacturing and quality is-
sues, as has the need for multistakeholder interaction and collaboration to address the local issue of a
shortage in a multiregional environment.
The emerging global challenge of supply disruptions is drawing greater focus from the pharmaceuti-
cal industry, regulators, legislators, healthcare providers, patient organizations, and patients, and drug
shortages are now covered in the media to a much larger extent. ICH Q9(R1) places greater emphasis
on product availability risks—a noteworthy change from the original guideline—in the context of
quality and manufacturing issues, including GMP noncompliance, that can be a “significant cause
of product availability issues (e.g., product shortages).” Furthermore, while “manufacturing and sup-
ply chain diversity can be enablers of product availability, increasingly complex supply chains lead to
interdependencies that can introduce systemic quality/manufacturing risks impacting supply chain
robustness” (1). Thus, the interests of patients are served by risk-based drug shortage prevention and
mitigation activities that help to proactively manage supply chain complexities and ensure timely avail-
ability of needed medicinal products.
The legislative and regulatory processes for shortage-related issues continue to evolve. In 2012, EMA
published a reflection paper and implementation plan for drug shortages that affect patients. EMA followed

The focus on drug shortages has also increased in the United States. In 2013, the U.S. Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) established a Drug Shortage Task Force, which published a strategic plan for
preventing and reducing drug shortages. The task force continues to identify the root causes of drug
shortages and advance long-term solutions, reporting annually to Congress. As in many countries,
notification of a potential drug shortage is a legal requirement in the United States, and early and timely
notifications has reduced the number of shortages by enabling manufacturers and health authorities to
jointly take steps to minimize supply disruption (11). The annual reports to Congress for calendar years
2021 and 2022 included the impact of the increased risk of shortages during the COVID-19 pandemic
and the actions taken to address them; both reports highlighted the crucial role early notifications
played to reduce the impact and threat of shortages (12, 13). In 2021, the White House published
a report, Building Resilient Supply Chains, Revitalizing American Manufacturing, And Fostering Broad-
Based Growth, that emphasized the importance of increasing the resilience and transparency of global
supply chains, incentivizing quality management maturity, and increasing economic sustainability of
U.S. manufacturing (14). A 2023 Majority Staff Report of the U.S. Senate Committee on Homeland
Security and Governmental Affairs, Short Supply: The Health and National Security Risks of Drug
Shortages, concluded that active drug shortages had risen by 30% between 2021 and 2022, lasted one-
and-a-half years longer on average, and impacted patient care. According to the report, overreliance
on foreign resources was deemed a national security risk, FDA lacked critical information to mitigate
shortages, and both industry and regulators lacked end-to-end supply chain visibility (5).
Consequently, health authorities and the pharmaceutical industry have undertaken more proactive
efforts and increased multistakeholder engagement to recognize the importance of managing
stakeholder interdependencies. There is greater awareness of how the unintended consequences of
actions taken by any one stakeholder group can negatively impact other stakeholder processes. The
European Union, United States, and other countries acknowledge the broad range of multifactorial
root causes, recognize the need for multistakeholder interactions and collaborative solutions, appreciate
the importance of end-to-end supply chain visibility, and share a common understanding and political
will to significantly reduce shortages. The awareness and acknowledgment of this complex ecosystem
is gradually growing, as depicted in Figure 1.0-1.
It is essential for the senior management of a firm that is the Marketing Authorization Holder (MAH)
to have control of and be accountable for ensuring the uninterrupted supply of their products as long
as it is within their control (15). Senior Management decisions impact not only a firm’s ability to cause
shortages but also to prevent and recover from shortages in an expedient manner. As stated in ICH Q9
(R1), the principles of quality risk management (QRM) that enable risk-based decision-making by
both companies and health authorities are fundamental for the proactive prevention of drug shortages.
This concept of prevention and the use of risk-based principles cannot be overstated in terms of
importance. The November 2020 ICH Concept Paper that formed the basis for the revision of ICH
Q9 stated that “addressing lifecycle risks to manufacturing reliability and quality assurance is the
foundation for supply predictability” (16).

3.0
Recognizing Drug Shortages as a Global Problem
The primary objective in the management of drug shortages is preventing harm to patients. In any situation,
it is important to maintain the continuity of supply such that all patients receive the medicines they need
every time they need them to manage their medical condition. The risk of potential drug shortages should
be considered when making decisions related to the supply and/or manufacture of drug products and their
corresponding active substances. This technical report addresses how drug shortages caused by manufacturing
and quality issues can be anticipated and avoided using a risk-based approach at a product level.
A good example of a shortage that had global impact and needed to be managed by multiple health
authorities was “The Great Acetonitrile Shortage” in 2008-2009. Acetonitrile is used as a solvent in many
analytical procedures that are included in submissions for the approval of drug products and in chemical
reactions for the manufacture of active pharmaceutical ingredients (API). The two main manufacturers
of this solvent, both located in the same area in China, were required to stop manufacturing acetonitrile
to reduce air pollution for the 2008 Summer Olympics. A few months later, in September of 2008,
acetonitrile production in Texas was interrupted due to hurricanes in the Gulf of Mexico. The combination
of these two events caused shortages of several APIs and drug products. Only a few companies were aware
that the product would not be manufactured for a year and, subsequently, had built up their inventories of
the product in advance. Most regulatory agencies accepted alternative analytical methods on a short-term
basis. The FDA also allowed changes to existing validated test methods within an approved application,
either a new drug application (NDA) or an abbreviated new drug application (ANDA), to accommodate
using less acetonitrile or an alternative solvent to acetonitrile. The Agency allowed companies to process
this as a minor change in the Annual Report for the given drug application as long as the change met
the criteria stated in the FDA Guidance for Industry: Changes to an Approved NDA or ANDA and Code of
Federal Regulations [(21CFR314.70(d)(2)(vii)] (25, 26).
This example illustrates that, in some cases, drug shortages can occur very suddenly and for reasons
that are difficult to predict. Common antibiotics such as amoxicillin have also experienced shortages

3.1
Evolution of Terminologies
T﻿hhough single, internationally harmonized definitions do not currently exist for “drug shortage,”
“meaningful disruption,” and “medically necessary product,” various definitions of these terms have been
published. The FDA defines drug shortage as “a period of time when the demand or projected demand for
the drug in the United States exceeds the drug’s supply ” (28). In general, the FDA Drug Shortage Staff
focuses on shortages of medically necessary products that have a significant effect on public health (18, 23,
29). The EMA earlier described drug shortages as “disruptions to the manufacturing supply chain” (6, 30)
and updated the definition of shortages in 2019 to be more specific in terms of the gap between supply
and demand (21). In addition, individual EU member states have local definitions of drug shortages (31).
The EU’s current definition of “critical medicines” also includes the criteria of therapeutic use,
availability of alternative(s), and supply-chain vulnerabilities. The World Health Organization
(WHO) refers to a “shortage” as a situation where the supply of medicines, health products or vaccines
identified as essential by the health system is considered to be insufficient to meet public health and
patient needs. WHO indicates that:
On the demand side: a “shortage” will occur when demand exceeds supply at any point in
the supply chain and may ultimately create a “stockout” at the point of appropriate service
delivery to the patient if the cause of the shortage cannot be resolved in a timely manner
relative to the clinical needs of the patient (32).
The understanding of these terms may also vary in some respects by the different stakeholders in the
supply chain (MAHs, regulatory authorities, distributors, wholesalers, providers, and patients). How-
ever, there is alignment on the concept that these definitions are based on the fundamental importance
of ensuring an uninterrupted supply of medicines to prevent harm to patients. They are also aligned
with public-service obligations held by MAHs and wholesale distributors in relation to ensuring the
supply of medicinal products in the markets in which they operate.

In the European Union, Article 13 of EU GMP Directive 2017/1572 defines the legal requirement

(e.g., individual EU member states, Health Canada, Swissmedic, Therapeutic Goods Administration)
are also implementing regulatory requirements in this regard. ICH Q9(R1) further highlights the need
for appropriate and timely management of health authority notification.
While reporting requirements and mechanisms for drug shortages vary across different health
authorities and are subject to varying legal requirements, the expectation of visibility to shortage
information has increased over the last several years. More information can be found at the websites
of the individual health authorities on local drug shortage situations.1 Other associations, such as the
American Society of Health System Pharmacists, also may provide information on drug shortages and
their management through their websites. Health Canada has provided a multistakeholder toolkit
that provides strategies and tools for key players in the supply chain. This list demonstrates the variety
of stakeholders (e.g., manufacturers, group purchasing organizations, regional health authorities,
pharmacists, health care professionals, federal government among others) that should be responsible
for addressing drug shortages at specific stages in the supply chain (36, 37). The differences in
reporting requirements and approaches in different countries continue to make it challenging to
draw meaningful comparisons of data on drug shortages or to quantify the problem at a global or
regional level. The European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations (EFPIA),
European Generic and Biosimilar Medicines Association (EGA), Association of the European Self-
Care Industry (AESGP), and Plasma Protein Therapeutics Association (PPTA) jointly developed a
harmonized communication framework for potential drug shortages, which includes harmonized
trigger points for notifying competent authorities of drug shortages that are due to quality and
manufacturing problems. The paper also provides a detailed listing of information systems from
EMA and some EU member states that provide information on drug shortages (38). In 2019, EMA
also published a good practice guidance for EU national competent authorities to communicate
medicines availability issues to the public (39).
In the United States, Congress legislates the FDA’s authority to act in response to drug shortages.
In October 2013, FDA published its Strategic Plan for Preventing and Reducing Drug Shortages. It
addresses mitigation strategies for manufacturers, such as notifying the FDA of potential shortages
promptly so that manufacturers and the FDA can jointly take steps to reduce supply disruption.
Another element of the FDA’s drug shortage strategic plan focuses on preventive strategies, such as
providing positive incentives for manufacturing and quality improvements and using proactive risk-
based approaches for early identification and prevention of manufacturing and quality problems (11).

this reason, FDA, EMA, and many other health authorities have taken the crisis around drug short-
ages seriously and regulations continue to evolve to heighten the attention, sensitivity, and response
to shortages.

### 3.3 Framework for Managing Drug Shortages due to Manufacturing and

Quality Problems
As illustrated in Figure 3.0-1, the potential causes of drug shortages are often multifactorial and
complex. Business, environmental, and economic policies are also important in controlling the risks
to drug availability across the end-to-end product value chain, manufacturing being a key step. There-
fore, efforts must be concentrated on maintaining uninterrupted manufacturing and distribution and
minimizing the risks that packaged drugs will be unavailable.
In the context of product value or supply chain, a drug shortage starts whenever an API, excipient,
API starting material, reagent, solvent, component, or even packaging material cannot be made avail-
able for the manufacturing process. This can occur due to several reasons, including manufacturing,
quality, and GMP compliance issues, inadequate manufacturing capacity to meet increased product
demand, lack of redundancy, complexity of the supply chain, evolving global regulatory requirements
and the complexity associated with implementing them, and dynamic market conditions. Country- or
region-specific import testing requirements can add complexity to supply chains and restrict compa-
nies from recovering quickly, potentially turning “near misses” into actual drug shortages.
Manufacturing and quality issues are often listed as a cause of drug shortages in many regions. In this
context, shortages might occur as a result of issues related to such factors as the supply of materials,
maintenance and technical operation of facilities and equipment, unavailable equipment, or variable
manufacturing processes. Any of these factors may result in the generation of nonconforming batches or
in GMP- or regulatory-compliance issues being identified by the company, health authorities, or others.
As part of the EMA interassociation drug shortages project, PDA and the International Society
for Pharmaceutical Engineering (ISPE) coordinated efforts at both the product and systems levels
to address the prevention of drug shortages caused by manufacturing and quality problems. They
advised that companies need to focus on operating effectively in an environment of increasing global
complexity while continuing to supply products.

4.0
Management Responsibilities
A drug manufacturing company’s interest is in developing, producing, and making pharmaceutical or
biopharmaceutical medicines available to patients in a financially sustainable manner. Once approved by
relevant health authorities, a drug should be available with no interruptions to supply; the manufacturer is
accountable for producing and making the drug available while the wholesale distributors have a general
service obligation to bring the drug to the patients. Senior management of the MAH is accountable for
decisions that may impact the company’s ability to cause, prevent, and/or recover from drug shortages. It
is also accountable for ensuring the availability of their medicinal products in the amount needed and of
the right level of quality. To ensure continued supply, it is important for management to ensure regulatory
compliance, as well as compliance to GMP and good distribution practices (GDP), and a robust quality

5.0
Risk-Based Approaches for Prevention of Drug Shortages
A holistic approach to the prevention and management of drug shortages focused on patient safety is
important and complex. There are multiple elements, both at the system and product levels, that work
individually or in combination to prevent a shortage:
•
At the product level, an end-to-end understanding of the value chain for manufacturing and
distribution provides the ability to understand and implement end-to-end controls.
•
At the system level, appropriate oversight and a robust pharmaceutical quality system (PQS)
ensure compliance with applicable laws and regulations (22).
Both approaches should also establish mechanisms that provide early warning signals of potential
shortages, including near-misses, assess potential root causes for the shortage considering multiple aspects
(Figure 3.0-1), drive targeted manufacturing and product quality improvements (e.g., introduction of
new technologies, continual improvement, innovation), and enable timely communications with key
stakeholders and health authorities. Other foundational elements should:
•
Ensure strong management accountability
•
Foster a sustainable quality culture that enables employees to speak up
•
Reward behaviors that drive joint ownership between operations and quality units for timely
issue resolution
•
Build people skills and capabilities that drive change leadership and excellence in pharmaceutical
operations
•
Establish a governance model that tracks operational and quality performance with appropriate
metrics (40).
As part of a robust quality system, companies should implement QRM principles and knowledge
management capabilities to accomplish the following (1, 22):
•
Proactively identify what controls are, and are not, in place for shortage prevention (for critical
products at least)
•
Proactively identify and control risks of interruption in manufacturing and product supply
•
Link to clinical and safety risk assessments for their products
•
Facilitate risk-based decision-making by companies and health authorities in response to
shortages in order to control and reduce harm to patients
Opportunities for informed and timely decision-making based on QRM principles, knowledge
management, and a good understanding of patient impact in the event of a shortage allow companies

to proactively manage and prevent drug shortages. When drugs become unexpectedly unavailable in
the manufacturing supply chain, risk-based decision-making enables companies to provide key risk-
indicating information to health authorities in a timely manner before the shortage becomes severe

In addition to internal risk and knowledge management, it is helpful to have systems that generate
reliable and up-to-date information about the marketplace for a company’s products (e.g., trends in
demand, usage volumes, market-share data). This combined body of information can also be very use-

5.1
Concepts of Risk and Knowledge Management
According to ICH Quality Guideline Q10: Pharmaceutical Quality System, QRM and knowledge man-
agement are enablers of the PQS. As such, QRM and knowledge management are intended to facili-
tate the practical integration of all elements of the PQS “to enhance the quality and availability of
medicines around the world in the interest of public health” (22).
A good understanding of the manufacturing process and end-to-end supply chain, up to the transfer of
product from the MAH to the first economic customer (i.e., to wholesale distributors), is necessary for ap-
propriate QRM to identify, assess, control, and review the risks of potential drug shortages. Once the hazards
are identified and the risks are assessed, appropriate risk control measures can be implemented to reduce (con-
trol, mitigate, or eliminate) the risks of potential drug shortages before they lead to actual drug shortages. Of
course, not all hazards can be anticipated; in such instances, as soon as new information on a hazard becomes
available, risk-review activities can ensure timely assessment and implementation of risk control actions.
Knowledge management supports the PQS by facilitating easier access to product and process knowl-
edge throughout the product’s lifecycle. This availability of knowledge regarding process-performance
and product-quality monitoring systems can enhance process understanding. In addition, ICH Q10
calls for process and product monitoring throughout product development and commercial manu-
facturing, so the information can be used to establish and actively manage a timely control strategy
for manufacturing. Finally, management review of process performance and product quality should
identify appropriate actions for continual improvement, such as the capture and dissemination of
knowledge and the identification of post-approval changes (PAC).
Most companies have mechanisms to respond to drug shortages when they occur; however, these
mechanisms might not be comprehensive or fully integrated into company procedures. A comprehen-
sive approach for managing potential drug shortages should involve:
•
Ability to respond quickly to minimize the impact of a shortage
•
Framework for anticipating drug shortage triggers and risks and for establishing mechanisms to
control risks and prevent shortages
•
Mechanisms and processes for timely communications
The EFPIA Good Practice Guide: Reducing Risk for Drug Product Shortages describes a “good practice”
model that a company can adopt to prevent and/or manage potential drug shortage situations (41).
A company’s QRM policy and related procedures should address risks to patient safety in the context
of product quality and availability. Typically, risk ranking levels in a company’s QRM policy include
ranking for GMP and regulatory compliance risks and patient safety risks based on product quality.
Table 5.1-1 provides an example of a risk ranking scale that incorporates considerations for both prod-
uct quality and product availability in the ranking of patient safety risks.
While manufacturing and quality issues could impact product quality or patient safety, there may
be instances when product quality or patient safety are not impacted. Still, there may be issues with
regard to compliance with GMP or GDP or regulatory commitments provided in the product dossier.
When handling GMP and regulatory compliance issues or deviations that may negatively impact the
release of a product, the company must quickly identify the potential issue and work on a solution(s)
to mitigate the risk of a drug shortage. Hence, it is important that the education and qualification of
employees who manage deviations include aspects associated with the assessment of potential supply
impact in case an issue evolves further. It is also important to establish mechanisms for escalating these
GMP, GDP, and regulatory compliance issues to the appropriate level of management, as solutions
might often require the allocation of resources and identification of alternatives. Companies often
handle such issues internally, but they should involve the relevant health authorities early on in case
there is a potential risk of a drug shortage, and a solution is unachievable in a short timeframe.

5.2
The Importance of Interacting with Health Authorities
There is a legal requirement for MAHs to notify relevant health authorities of any potential drug
shortages in the European Union and in the United States (17, 18). Therefore, early interactions should
take place between a pharmaceutical company and relevant health authorities when a meaningful
disruption in supply is expected (38, 42). Laws and regulations related to drug shortages are continuing
to evolve and are defining or clarifying responsibilities for stakeholders, such as industry, patient, and
healthcare professional organizations, with respect to ensuring uninterrupted drug supplies to patients
(43, 44).
Health authorities generally prefer to be involved in several aspects of drug shortage management and
response. For example, they might want to:
•
Oversee how the company or MAH is managing the shortage
•
Engage other manufacturers of the same product or alternatives to increase their production
•
Coordinate actions, communications, and responses to the shortage among various stakeholders,
including other MAHs and manufacturers, as well as patients, healthcare professionals, and govern-
ment agencies that purchase medicines
•
Ensure that adequate corrective actions are taken to prevent recurrences
At a higher level, health authorities may wish to contribute to an environment in which supply chains
become more robust and shortages can generally be prevented. This can support the primary goal of health
authorities, which is generally the protection of public health and the availability of medicinal products.

--- Page 20 ---
Health authorities usually take a risk-based approach to shortages. For example, they participate in
the management of and response to shortages that may have an impact on patients now or in the near
future. Health authorities, however, generally do not have the resources to become closely involved
in all aspects of shortages. Risk assessments can help identify shortages that require their attention.
Health authorities and industry have a mutual interest in understanding the causes of shortages, even
where there is little to no impact on the patient population, to prevent them from recurring.
Knowing which shortages may or may not impact patients requires careful analysis as well as effective
multistakeholder and media communications management, and the situation may vary from country
to country. Robust risk assessment based on robust knowledge management of the manufacturing and
supply processes and active, timely dialog with health authorities and other relevant stakeholders can
facilitate this determination.
6.0

6.1
Description of the Risk Triage Model
The risk triage method uses a preliminary hazards analysis (PHA) approach, which simplifies the
process after information is collected on the potential or actual drug shortage. A four-step PHA triage
approach is used to evaluate the relationship of three risk elements—therapeutic use of a product,
availability of alternatives, and likelihood of occurrence—to determine the risk of a drug shortage. The
steps in the triage process depicted in Figure 6.1-1 are as follows:
1.	 Identify impact level (impact on a patient) based on therapeutic use and availability of alternatives.
2.	 Determine the likelihood of a drug shortage for the product, taking into account any drug
shortage prevention controls that are in place and their likely effectiveness.
3.	 Determine the risk priority level based on a combination of impact on the patient and the
likelihood of a drug shortage for the product.
4.	 Plan and implement the suggested risk controls for the assessed product based on the risk
priority level.

6.1.1-6.1.4.

#### 6.1.2 Step 2: Assess the Likelihood of Shortage

In the second step of the triage, the likelihood of shortage for a product is assessed. Hazards that could
increase the likelihood of a shortage should be assessed and current controls should be assessed to identify
the hazards. Where good, robust controls exist for a hazard, the likelihood of occurrence would be low; if
there are limited to no controls for a hazard, the likelihood of occurrence increases. Historical data may
be available for certain hazards, and this historical data should be leveraged to assess the relative likelihood
of risks. For example, product sourcing may be one of the controls assessed to determine the likelihood of
a shortage. Single-sourcing of a product or key material supplier increases the likelihood of occurrence of
a shortage in the event of an issue at that sole-sourced facility or supplier. Other controls may be related
to infrastructure maintenance and improvements, technology updates, business continuity planning and
capacity assessment, process capability, inventory levels, or supplier diversification.
This assessment of likelihood of occurrence may not be quantitative, which is considered acceptable
because it is more important to assess the controls and their likely effectiveness and to understand the
relative likelihood of the occurrence of a shortage for identified hazards. In this risk triage model, a simple
three-level (high, medium, low) scale for assessing the likelihood of occurrence has been proposed.

Supply Chain and Value Stream Mapping
The MAH is accountable for ensuring that risks for patient safety, product quality, and product
availability are adequately managed in the end-to-end product supply chain. In addition to assessing
the risks with various suppliers and external service providers (e.g., CDMOs, CLOs) and addressing
them through their supplier management controls, the MAH should also review the QRM processes
used, especially by their key suppliers and service providers. This can help ensure that these suppliers
and service providers have an adequate framework in place, in accordance with the principles of ICH
Q9(R1), to assess and manage their quality and compliance risks.
Process mapping, in general, can play a key role in managing the risk of drug shortages particularly as
supply chains continue to become increasingly complex. Mapping of the end-to-end product supply
processes is one of the most important tools that can be leveraged for managing the risk of drug
shortages due to manufacturing and supply problems. VSM (also known as material and information
flow mapping), a particularly powerful tool, graphically displays the flow of the manufacturing and
transportation processes for a product and highlights potential manufacturing, inventory, and supply
stress points. Although VSM is often used to reduce amounts of inventory and inefficiencies across the
supply chain, it is also used to maintain proper levels of in-process inventory to ensure predefined final
product inventories. Maintaining agreed-upon inventory levels of products required for pandemics or
flu seasons is an example of how VSM can be used. Figure 6.2-1 displays an example of how VSM
serves as a useful tool to help manage drug shortages proactively and reactively.
While VSM is a great tool for mapping products and information flow, it is not the only approach
available. Other tools that could be used are DRIVE (Define, Review, Identify, Verify, Execute), ICOR
(Inputs, Outputs, Controls, and Resources), Cause-and-Effect Diagrams, and Process Flowcharting.
The following elements constitute an effective mapping tool:
•
Team Makeup: The mapping team should be cross-functional and include individuals who are
intimately familiar with manufacturing and supply processes and equipment. The most suc-
cessful mapping exercises involve laboratory technicians and operators. Mapping requires good
facilitation, preferably by an objective, neutral individual.
•
Planning and Preparation: The scope of the mapping (local to a site or region or broader in
the supply chain) should be identified in advance. The team should establish timelines and goals
for the mapping (in this case, specific to drug shortages). The exercise will likely become part of
a larger initiative for developing a Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan.
•
Evaluation: The team should perform all steps of the mapping process and capture data and
information on how the process works. The team should also have access to existing procedures
as well as quality events to capture all stress points. Depending on the mapping technique used,
specific symbols are used to indicate times, inventory, issues, and areas for improvement.
•
Map: After a map of the current state is drawn, listing all the steps and handoffs in the process,
a future-state map can be drawn using the same steps to identify risk reducing actions that will
meet the needs of the product.
•
Action List: To develop and maintain an effective Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan, an
action list needs to be developed to address the gaps and risks based on the future-state map actions.

### 6.3 Managing Drug Shortage Risks Due to Aging Manufacturing Facilities

and Technologies
Manufacturing and quality risks introduced by aging or legacy facilities and technologies (e.g., out-of-
date manufacturing facilities, equipment, analytical technologies) that are not fully up to current stan-
dards or expectations can contribute to an increased likelihood of issues that could potentially result in
drug shortages. These risks should therefore be proactively identified and managed during the lifecycle
of the manufacturing facility, process, equipment, or analytical methods. In order to accomplish the
objectives of ICH Q10, continual improvement is an important element of product realization and of
maintaining a state of control. The EU GMP guidelines on pharmaceutical quality systems expect for
medicinal products that:
Product realization is achieved by designing, planning, implementing, maintaining, and con-
tinuously improving a system that allows the consistent delivery of products with appropriate
quality attributes. Continual improvement is facilitated through the implementation of qual-
ity improvements appropriate to the current level of process and product knowledge (46).
ICH Q9(R1) states that a robust facility infrastructure can facilitate reliable supply; it includes suitable
equipment and well-designed facilities for manufacturing (including packaging and testing). Multiple
factors can affect robustness, such as an aging facility, insufficient maintenance, or an operational
design that is vulnerable to human error. Risks to supply can be reduced by addressing these factors,
as well as through the use of innovative technology, such as digitalization, automation, and isolation
technology, among others.
Updating legacy facilities and technologies or implementing new innovative technologies can present
multiple challenges, not always cost-related, that prevent companies from pursuing these strategies
as part of lifecycle management. However, where aging facilities or technologies increase the risks to
product quality, patient safety, and/or supply interruption, it becomes essential for manufacturers to
address these risks.

#### 6.3.1 Causes and Effects of Aging Facilities, Processes, and Analytical Technologies

Manufacturing facilities, processes, and analytical technologies age and, over time, may no longer be
capable of meeting performance requirements or delivering the expected product quality attributes.
Some of the main reasons why facilities, processes, or analytical technologies become obsolete are:
•
Lack of long-term planning and poor understanding of the importance of reinvestment and
recapitalization of facilities and processes
•
Concern about the unanticipated negative impact of improvements due to a lack of robust pro-
cess knowledge or of knowledge and experience with the new technology
•
Short-term focus on maintaining or reducing costs, either cost of goods sold or capital expenses
•
Negative financial return on investment due to low drug prices (e.g., generics)
•
Challenges, long lead times, and a lack of harmonized processes for global regulatory approvals
across multiple health authorities
•
Absence of positive mindset, attitude, and culture for change and continuous improvement
These factors can produce the decline that occurs over time, for example:
•
As a cost-saving effort, a company may decide to move to a limited maintenance schedule
on equipment or reduce the availability of critical spare parts and stock of intermediates. The
lowered or partial maintenance is believed to save costs; however, breakdowns due to the lack of
maintenance or a negative trend in the manufacturing environment may stop production or lead
to product rejections in the future, at a far higher cost.

--- Page 33 ---
•
A conscious decision to drastically reduce or eliminate facility, process, and/or equipment opti-
mization and improvement activities, over the long run, can result in antiquated and noncom-
pliant facilities and processes with high levels of variability and risk. Additionally, aging facilities
may limit the ability to increase capacity to meet the product demand.
•
Maintaining a false assumption that equipment will not become obsolete in the future and will
continue to be supported by the equipment manufacturer is imprudent. In almost all cases, equip-
ment is designed with an intended life span. As equipment manufacturers move to newer models,
they often identify a point in time after which they will no longer support older models. The sub-
contractor should be responsible for informing the equipment user as early as possible about any
changes in the technology status, including obsolescence of equipment, availability of next-gener-
ation equipment, and spare parts status, so the user can change or update the relevant equipment
before shortages arise. When the manufacturer fails to plan for equipment obsolescence, it often
results in a quick upgrade done at a high cost and with some loss of production time.
•
In some cases, a process or analytical technique was designed for one product or output, and a
change in a product or output might be incompatible with the old process or product.
Quality and GMP compliance gaps associated with such aging facilities, processes, and technologies
should be addressed even though the existing equipment or methods may be functional and work
smoothly without problems. Well-trained personnel with a process ownership mentality may make
such facilities, processes, and analytical methods run, but when such personnel retire or move to
other tasks, the facility, processes, or analytical methods see their first excursions. These events occur
especially when the succession personnel are not trained appropriately or rely on the existing process
without knowing its weaknesses. It is very important to invest in training qualified personnel to run
these often manual, processes, equipment, and methods.
In addition to not meeting current regulatory expectations, the effects of aging facilities, processes,
and analytical technologies can be detrimental and may result in a total shutdown of the production
of a particular drug product, potentially resulting in drug shortages. The continual maintenance and
improvement of facilities, processes, and analytical methods is essential to maintain specified product
quality and to assure supplies of the product.

#### 6.3.2 Continual Improvement of Aging Facilities and Processes

Processing technology and facility designs are forever evolving. The motivation of suppliers to develop
and distribute new technologies is mainly driven by industry seeking new solutions for current pro-
cessing problems. However, implementation of these solutions becomes challenging in spite of the
value they can bring.
Given this, implementation of new technology has resulted not just in financial viability, but an overall
improvement in quality and process efficiency. For example, single-use process technology has many
advantages over traditional stainless steel equipment, such as faster equipment turnaround, which en-
hances production capacities. Also, single-use components or unit operations can be connected via
aseptic connector or tube welders, which creates a closed system that enhances the safety of both the
product and the operator. The technological improvements of single-use technologies have been widely
embraced, not only for new, but existing processes, as the advantages of using this technology outweigh
any challenges change commonly bring. [More information about single-use systems can be found in
PDA Technical Report No. 66: Application of Single-Use Systems in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing.]
Facility and process improvements due to technology innovation are not always as dramatic as single-use
process technology or new modular cleanroom designs; they can be gradual improvements of existing
equipment to the next generation. Subtle, but continual improvements demonstrate the right quality

--- Page 34 ---
attitude and ownership. Such continual improvement activities inevitably lower the risk of process or fa-
cility failure, leading to operations that run more reliably and efficiently and resulting in cost reduction.

#### 6.3.3 Lifecycle Management and Continual Improvement of Analytical Technologies

During the commercial lifecycle of a product, analytical technologies continue to improve. However,
commercial product analytical control systems—in-process, release, and stability test methods, specifi-
cations and acceptance criteria, and reference-standard controls—tend to improve minimally, if at all.
This slow pace of change can be attributed to such factors as high cost, resource requirements, and the
complexity of the global regulatory environment for making PACs. Revisions to control systems can
take two to five years, depending on their extent and the number of countries that need to approve
the PAC. Revisions can take even longer for complex molecules for which physicochemical character-
ization data are limited, development of new methods is challenging, and new methods require new
equipment in laboratories. Even if a new method is developed, collecting data to establish acceptance
criteria and obtaining regulatory approvals from multiple health authorities for PACs, can take time.
A proactive approach to control system lifecycle management is important to ensure alignment with
contemporary product knowledge and industry standards. In addition, companies manufacturing
follow-on biologics, for example, are publishing detailed physicochemical characterizations of innova-
tor products using new or improved technologies. Lifecycle management of analytical control systems
should be reviewed periodically (e.g., every five years), not only in response to control system issues
identified (e.g., through investigations), during routine commercial use.
Ensuring that the analytical control system addresses relevant product quality attributes and that these
product quality attributes are well controlled throughout the product’s commercial lifespan is im-
portant. Periodic assessments of an analytical control system should determine whether the system
continues to control drug substance and product quality at the product’s release and at the end of
its shelf life. Such determinations should be based on comprehensive reviews of current information
about the molecule, including adequate monitoring of relevant product quality attributes, current
manufacturing process capability, alignment of acceptance criteria with process capability, compliance
with evolving regulatory requirements and expectations, the ability to fulfill its intended purpose, and
current analytical technologies.
Periodic assessments of the control system should include evaluations of gaps or improvement oppor-
tunities, for example, when a new critical quality attribute is identified because of new information
on a molecule, such as antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity, when superior technology is
available, or when new regulatory requirements are implemented. Such gaps and improvement op-
portunities should be used to determine whether new methods are needed or if current methods need
to be revised or eliminated. Some examples of when an update to the control system may be needed
are when:
•
The current method has a high failure rate, low throughput rate, or analyst ergonomic or safety
limitations
•
The method is no longer suitable for the control system
•
The current method validation is no longer applicable
•
Superior technology is available
•
New or revised specifications or acceptance criteria need to be implemented
Acceptance criteria for current control system test methods should be evaluated against manufactur-
ing and process capability (via analysis of lot-release data) and stability data (via annual stability and
qualification-lot data). The process capability index measures the extent to which the process oper-
ates within its specification limits, given process variability (see PDA Technical Report No. 59 (Revised
2012): Utilization of Statistical Methods for Production Monitoring). A larger index indicates an in-
creased likelihood that a result will be within the specified limits.

--- Page 35 ---
Release acceptance criteria can be evaluated by comparing 95 or 99 tolerance intervals of historical
batches to current acceptance criteria. If the acceptance criteria do not match manufacturing capabil-
ity, those criteria may require revision. Drug substance and drug product shelf-life acceptance criteria
can be evaluated by analyzing historical trend plots.
Revisions to current analytical control systems (test methods, acceptance criteria, or shelf-life specifica-
tions) require submission of an application to the relevant health authorities. Therefore, they should
be coordinated as much as possible with manufacturing process improvements, transfers, and other
activities requiring regulatory approval. Early discussions with relevant health authorities regarding
reporting level and documentation requirements for anticipated PACs well before these changes occur
will allow both the health authorities and the company to plan for the changes in a manner that will
help reduce the risk of drug shortages.
6.4
Reducing Drug Shortages Through Expedited Post-Approval Changes
When companies face a potential drug shortage, they can mitigate the risk by expediting a change that
may have already been submitted to a national health authority for approval. For example, a company
is experiencing an unexpected increase in demand in Country 1 that cannot be met with the current
capacity of Facility A. If Facility B has been approved for manufacture of the product by multiple coun-
tries and it meets the GMP expectations of Country 1, though Country 1 has not yet approved Facility
B, Facility B can produce additional products using the same manufacturing process. In this example,
requesting an expedited review and approval of the PAC application for Facility B in Country 1 can be
a viable alternative to mitigate the risk of the drug shortage. Section 6.4 describes how PACs can be
expedited while demonstrating that patients are not exposed to any added risk because of the change.
Current regulatory PAC processes vary among countries in the levels and terminology of change cat-
egories, requirements for documentation of changes, the need for preapproval facility inspections, and
timelines for approval. For these reasons, companies usually need to run multiple versions of a manu-
facturing process in parallel for the same product, depending on when each national health authority
is likely to approve a change. Doing so is logistically challenging from the standpoint of production,
inventory management, and supply. In some countries, when the anticipated PAC approval timelines
do not match the forecasted demand, it can lead to the lack of availability of the product. This situ-
ation is especially challenging for biological products due to the highly complicated manufacturing
processes involved; it can become even more complex when multiple changes pertaining to a product
are under review at the same time by different countries.
In such cases, the company should communicate with the health authorities responsible for trade
where a potential drug shortage might occur and request an expedited review of pending PACs or of-
fer a previous version of the product if available in the inventory. Health authorities assess PACs using
science and risk-based approaches while ensuring that they meet national legislative and regulatory re-
quirements. The outcome of these assessments, ideally, should be the same regardless of the geographic
location of the assessor; it almost always is. However, it would be beneficial if the regulatory approval
processes for PACs could be harmonized. Harmonization would also facilitate continual improvement
and product realization, two objectives of ICH Q10.
An example of an effort towards harmonization of regulatory expectations is The International Coali-
tion of Medicines Regulatory Authorities’ (ICMRA) Pharmaceutical Quality Knowledge Management
System Initiative (PQKMS) (47). According to ICH Q10, Annex 1, companies that demonstrate an
effective PQS and process and product understanding, including the use of QRM principles, may be
eligible for enhanced regulatory approaches. This is sometimes referred to as “regulatory flexibility,” where
there may be an opportunity to “optimize science and risk-based post-approval change processes to maxi-
mize benefits from innovation and continual improvement” (22). Additionally, ICH Quality Guideline

--- Page 36 ---
Q12: Technical and Regulatory Considerations for Pharmaceutical Product Lifecycle Management provides
a framework via PQS to facilitate the management of PACs in a more predictable and efficient manner
throughout the product lifecycle. Such opportunities may include managing more PACs in the PQS
only, rather than as a prior-approval PAC. The intent of the guideline is to promote continual improve-
ment and innovation, while strengthening quality assurance and reliable product supply (48). The In-
dustry One-Voice-of-Quality for PAC (1VQ for PAC) initiative has developed solutions and guidance
on how a company can demonstrate an effective PQS (49, 50). The PIC/S paper, Recommendation: How
to Evaluate and Demonstrate the Effectiveness of a Pharmaceutical Quality System in relation to Risk-Based
Change Management, may facilitate a health authority’s assessment of a company’s change management
system and has the potential to provide transparency to how effectively PACs are assessed within that
change management system (51).The concept has been proposed that health authorities provide incen-
tives to companies for innovation and implementation of new technologies that reduce risk and lead to
measurable shortage prevention. Some examples of innovation that health authorities might encourage
with incentives are the replacement of conventional open processes with isolator technology, the use of
closed rather than open manufacturing processes, enhancements in microbial and adventitious agent
controls, introduction of rapid microbiology methods, and the implementation of technologies that
reduce manufacturing cycle times, improve process capability, make a process more efficient, or enhance
a product’s analytical capabilities.
Companies and health authorities could work together to enable, where appropriate, streamlined re-
view and approval of the following changes, provided that agreed-upon acceptance criteria from health
authorities are met and that these changes do not introduce increased risks to patient safety or product
quality or efficacy:
•
Enhanced redundancy
—	 Additional manufacturing site or line already approved in at least one significant market or
country that can serve as a reference
—	 Additional raw material or and API suppliers
—	 Additional suppliers of container closure or other processing components
—	 Additional dosage forms already approved in at least one reference country
•
Improved process and product controls
—	 New or improved equipment and technology
—	 Manufacturing changes that lead to improved process capability
—	 New analytical technologies that enhance product controls, including adventitious agent
controls
•
Other types of changes (provided that validated and preset criteria are met)
—	 Change in reference standards
—	 Use of new working cell banks
—	 Addition of a product to a multiproduct facility
—	 Scale-up of a manufacturing process
—	 Extension of shelf life with supporting documentation
—	 Change in utilities (e.g., HVAC, clean steam) or their operation with no impact on product quality

—	 Change in steam-in-place or clean-in-place process
—	 Change in environmental monitoring program (e.g., sample location and frequency)
—	 Change in cleaning methods
In all cases, the company concerned should be able to demonstrate that it has an effective PQS in
place. In this regard, companies should expand their PQS to address PAC planning and assessment,
especially how to conduct risk assessments and how to demonstrate risk control. The industry should
work with health authorities to develop predefined global change protocols (gCP) that can be available

--- Page 37 ---
and used as a standard (non-product-specific) template for the categorization, predefined data require-
ments, and implementation of the different types of changes. Health authorities have provided guide-
lines on procedures and data requirements for PACs, such as Health Canada’s Guidance Document:
Post-Notice of Compliance (NOC) Changes: Quality Document and WHO’s Annex 3: Guidelines on pro-
cedures and data requirements for changes to approved biotherapeutic products (TRS No. 1011). Common
gCPs that are developed in partnership with health authorities for the different types of changes can
help standardize activities and requirements across the industry for a particular change type such that
expectations are clearly known prior to considering the change. This would help expedite the review
and approval of the PAC. Such gCPs could potentially result in a lower regulatory reporting category
and expedited review from multiple health authorities yet address individual health authority require-
ments. The gCP should include validation activities and even acceptance criteria (where possible) that
should be expected for the different types of changes.
Each company could then use the gCP for the relevant type of change as a starting point in develop-
ing the submission for each of the changes in the PAC Management Protocol for their product. The
company should update the predefined gCP with the specific aspects of the change for their particular
product. The change(s) should not have a negative impact on product quality, safety, or efficacy. The
company should describe and demonstrate through the gCP that the change does not present an in-
creased risk to patients. Some of the changes listed in this section can be associated with potential high
risks. Therefore, it is important that the risks associated with the change(s) are adequately identified,
assessed, and controlled. The risk assessment for the change(s) should be included in the submission
for the change to demonstrate that appropriate risk control measures are in place and provide assur-
ance that patient safety, product quality, and product efficacy are not impacted.
7.0
Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan
The risk assessment triage results in an assignment of a drug shortage risk priority level to each product
based on the product’s indication, availability of alternative therapies, and probability of a shortage.
Section 7.0 describes how a Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan should be developed based
on the outcomes of the risk triage. While agencies are working on new guidances, such a plan might
also be known as a Shortage Prevention Plan or Mitigation Plan in the future.
A Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan can provide a holistic framework at a product level
to address both the proactive and reactive management of a drug shortage. This plan should be pro-
actively developed for medically necessary, life supporting, or life sustaining products and shared with
health authorities during inspections. Medium risk products used for short-term acute conditions or
long-term chronic conditions can also benefit from a Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan,
especially if the risk triage has identified potential drug shortage risks at Risk Priority Level 1.
A Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan can be an effective mechanism for managing a prod-
uct’s continuously changing drug shortage risk profile. Drug shortages may present potential oppor-
tunities for product diversion and counterfeiting. Therefore, a company’s surveillance program can
leverage the Drug Shortage Risk Register as useful input into its process for managing product diver-

8.0
 Stakeholder Management and Communications related to
Drug Shortages
Multiple stakeholders are affected by, and need to be involved in, addressing a drug shortage—manu-
facturers, health authorities, distributors, pharmacies, healthcare providers and, sometimes, patients,
depending on the cause(s) and level of the shortage. The legal obligations for MAHs to notify the rel-
evant health authorities of a potential drug shortage are described in Article 13 of Commission Directive
2003/94/EC for the European Union and in Section 506C of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act
for the United States. In addition, Part 1, Section 5.71 of the EU GMP Guidelines has been updated to
provide guidance on notification of restrictions in supply. A collaborative dialog between the company
and the relevant health authorities is key to facilitating the control and/or prevention of a shortage and,
ultimately, ensuring a continued supply of medicinal products for patients.
Companies need to consider many things when reacting to a current or impending shortage of one of
their medicines in a given country or region. Developing a harmonized risk communication mecha-
nism to alert health authorities of a potential or existing shortage is important to creating a shared
understanding of the shortage. The guidance paper Quality and Manufacturing Driven Supply Dis-
ruptions: Industry Communication Principles to Authorities provides communication principles and a
framework to harmonize reporting and communications between MAHs and health authorities re-
garding drug shortages arising from quality and/or manufacturing disruptions. The paper proposes
a harmonized trigger point, time point, and recipient(s) for communicating potential drug shortage
events for nationally and centrally approved products in Europe (38).
Relevant health authorities, those where the product is marketed and where the shortage is expected to
occur, must be notified when a meaningful disruption could occur in the supply of medicines due to
manufacturing and quality issues, that is, when normal volumes of medicines cannot be delivered for
further distribution. The risk matrix in Table 6.1.1-1 categorizes risks to patients in a tiered mecha-
nism for determining communication for the various risk levels based on the product’s therapeutic use
and the availability of alternative treatments. The level of effort and formality in the risk assessment
for determining patient impact, resulting corrective actions, and level of communication with health
authorities should be commensurate with the level of risk to patients.
A safety or clinical risk assessment should inform the risk communication strategy so that appropri-
ate risk mitigation actions can be taken at the healthcare provider and/or patient level. For defective
products (e.g., those with a potential lack of sterility assurance), a comparison might be needed of the
clinical benefits and risks for patients of continued product use, especially when no alternative prod-
uct or treatment is available. Partnership and expedited dialogue with health authorities in making
appropriate risk mitigation decisions and returning to normal operations in such cases are important.
8.1
Key Risk-Indicating Information to Compile for Health Authorities
When anticipating a shortage may occur, companies should compile key pieces of information for
health authorities when their involvement is needed. Knowing the reasons for the stock shortage or de-
pletion not only facilitates the implementation of corrective and preventive actions (CAPA), but it can
also help develop practical marketplace solutions for the shortage. Based on product ownership, it is
important to openly clarify communication accountabilities between the health authority and the (1)
manufacturer or first distributor; (2) wholesaler, agent, trader, distributor, repackager, or relabeler; and
(3) suppliers of the product to the patient (e.g., retail pharmacies, hospital pharmacies, physicians).
The notification content and format described in the AESGP, EFPIA, EGA, and PPTA collaborative
paper describe what data should be provided to health authorities for effective communications in
the event of potential drug shortages. This includes details on the MAH, the product, the causes and
details of the potential drug shortage, the risk level (A, B, or C) determined in the first step in the risk

--- Page 41 ---
triage (Figure 6.1-1), the expected duration of the shortage, the steps taken or planned to address the
shortage, the countries impacted, and the notification to other parties. Additional secondary informa-
tion that can be helpful for the MAH to collect in order to reach a shared understanding of a shortage
with the relevant health authorities and how best to address includes:
•
Patient-Related Information
—	 Types of patients (e.g., by disease, demographics, age, genetics, origin) using the medicine.
A good level of clinical understanding is important to assess whether any patient groups may
be particularly affected by the shortage (e.g., neonates, patients with cancer or diabetes).
—	 Scale of the problem, for example, the number of patients likely to be affected, the duration
of the shortage, and where the problem may be most acute. The following information can
be useful to compile and assess:
▪	 Likely number of patients treated with the product each month and the percentage of
patients that may be affected by the shortage or stock depletion. The current market
share, usage profile of the product, and monthly volume of prescriptions for the prod-
uct may also be useful to review.
▪	 Monthly sales volume of the product and the proportion of this volume that may be
affected by the shortage or stock depletion
▪	 Possible local, regional, or global impact on product supplies
•
Distribution Information in the Country or Region
—	 Name and address of the primary distributor of the product, if any
—	 If other distributors (i.e., secondary wholesalers) supply the product
—	 If the product is supplied only to hospitals or to both hospital and retail pharmacies
—	 If the product is supplied through other channels, such as clinics and nursing homes
—	 Opportunities for parallel import, repackaging, and relabeling, depending on regulations
•
Current Stock in Each Country or Region
—	 Whether the product is currently out of stock in the country or region, or if another version
of the product or process is available during the stock-out period
—	 Whether the product stock is expected to be depleted in the near future (e.g., within the
next few weeks or months) because of the supply issue
—	 Whether the product is in stock but available quantities are insufficient to meet patient needs
—	 Whether stockpiling is requested by the government, legislation, or guidance or as result of
the Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan
This information can help determine the likely impact of the shortage on patients, if any, as well as the
level of risk presented by the issue and whether the health authorities should be informed.
8.2
Risk-Based Decision-Making by the Company and Health Authorities
In the event of a shortage, having a product risk assessment developed proactively using the triage
method and the risk matrices in Section 6.1, and the key risk-indicating information compiled in
Section 8.1 becomes very useful in determining the appropriate actions to take. In addition to the
manufacturer or MAHs, CAPAs to address the risks may need to be owned by other stakeholders in
the supply chain. Therefore, communication between the stakeholders, including, for example, affili-
ates of the MAH in a country or region, distribution centers, or pharmacies as well as the press, is most
important to detect unintended consequences of a corrective or preventive measure early in the system.
Several decisions may need to be made by health authorities in response to reports of actual or pending
product shortages:

--- Page 42 ---
•
Whether to become formally involved in managing the shortage or in coordinating a response to
the shortage. The risk triage method described in Section 6.1 will be useful in determining:
—	 Whether health authorities need to become directly involved (e.g., by taking direct mea-
sures, coordinating responses, developing work plans and action items)
—	 Whether becoming directly involved is not necessary for health authorities at that time
—	 Whether health authorities need to monitor the situation closely without becoming directly
involved
•
Whether to facilitate the sourcing of alternative supplies of the product, for which the decision
usually involves determining:
—	 Whether the MAH should be allowed to import and supply a version of the product intend-
ed for a different market that may not be authorized in the country.
—	 Whether this product could be batch packaged for the other market and supplied in that
country’s packaging and delivery. The packaging and product information may be in a
different language than that of the country.
—	 Whether the imported product needs to be repackaged and, if so, the level of regulatory ap-
proval and oversight required for the repackaging.
—	 Whether the healthcare professionals should be advised that an unlicensed version of the
product is available or that an unlicensed version of a different but clinically equivalent
product is available.
•
Whether to expedite approval of a request to use an alternate unlicensed facility or a post-approval
change that has been submitted by the MAH. A related decision is whether the approval should
be conditional, temporary, or permanent.
•
What communication strategies will be required by the shortage or stock depletion, and how
those strategies will ensure that companies, health authorities, healthcare professionals, and/or pa-
tients can take appropriate risk mitigation actions. After the clinical risk assessment is completed,
the health authority may decide to advise healthcare professionals to take specific steps, such as:
—	 Giving priority to certain groups of patients (e.g., patients already treated with the medi-
cine) over others for continued treatment with the medicine
—	 Using a different medicine or treatment for new (or all) patients
—	 If a product is defective (e.g., loss of sterility assurance), weighing the clinical benefits and risks for
patients of continued use of the product when no alternative product or treatment is available
Any time a shortage situation is encountered it should be viewed as an opportunity for the organi-
zation to review their risk register and, where relevant, assess suitable updates to their risk control
program to improve prevention practices. This might involve examining the real root cause of why
it occurred, modifying CAPAs to prevent recurrence of a shortage for that and similar products, and
mitigating any gaps that the shortage event might have uncovered.
9.0
Conclusions
The objective of ensuring an uninterrupted supply of safe, efficacious products to patients is shared by
regulators, legislators, healthcare providers, and industry bringing them together to communicate for
jointly developing solutions that serve the best interests of patients. As the risk to the patient popula-
tion from a drug shortage rises, the level of rigor, effort, and cross-functional collaboration within an

--- Page 43 ---
organization to address the risk should also increase. This effort should be coupled with effective
communication among the organization, health authorities, and other relevant stakeholders to
proactively manage drug shortage risks.
Although challenging, the industry should address the concerns associated with drug shortages, given
the objective of protecting patients from harm. Proactively identifying relevant drug shortage hazards
and robustly managing drug shortage risks in the end-to-end product value chain, as well as effectively
and promptly communicating potential shortages to all stakeholders, is essential to reduce the risk of
drug shortages.
TR-68 provides a practical framework that companies can use, a triage model to assess drug shortage
risks, and templates for developing a Drug Shortage Risk Register and a Drug Shortage Prevention and
Response Plan at a product level. It also describes ways that industry can engage more proactively with
health authorities to request expedited review and approval of certain PACs by multiple health authorities
to reduce the risks of drug shortages. This could be achieved by using agreed-upon PAC management
plans and harmonized gCPs. Such protocols would be helpful for companies to address the risk of drug
shortages due to aging facilities, processes, and analytical technologies by modernizing their facilities and
implementing new technologies. This technical report also leverages the guidance developed by the AESPG,
EFPIA, EGA, and PPTA collaboration for communications between MAHs and health authorities.

About PDA Technical Reports
PDA Technical Reports are global consensus documents, prepared by member-driven Task Forces (listed on inside
front cover) comprised of content experts, including scientists and engineers working in the pharmaceutical/bio-
pharmaceutical industry, regulatory authorities and academia. While in development, PDA Technical Reports are
subjected to a global review of PDA members and other topic-specific experts, often including regulatory officials.
Comments from the global review are then considered by the authoring Task Force during the preparation of the
final working draft. The level of expertise of the Task Force and those participating in the global review ensure a
broad perspective reflecting best thinking and practices currently available.
The final working draft is next reviewed by the PDA Advisory Board or Boards that sanctioned the project. PDA’s
Advisory Boards are: Science Advisory Board, Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products Advisory Board, Biopharma-
ceutical Advisory Board, and Regulatory Affairs and Quality Advisory Board. Following this stage of review, the
PDA Board of Directors conducts the final review and determines whether or not to publish the document as an
official PDA Technical Report.
While PDA goes to great lengths to ensure each Technical Report is of the highest quality, all readers are encouraged
to contact PDA about any scientific, technical, or regulatory inaccuracies, discrepancies, or mistakes that might be
found in any of the documents. Readers can email PDA at: TRfeedback@pda.org.
