Introduction
This document represents the views of the Parenteral Drug Association (PDA). The document is designed to
communicate PDA’s thoughts and considerations pertaining to the design, operation, and use of Restricted
Access Barrier Systems (RABS) for aseptic processing, processes subjected to terminal sterilization and low
bioburden, while encouraging further dialog with industry, health authorities, and suppliers of technologies
and materials.  This document considers the evolution and needs of the modern, global, sterile, healthcare
product manufacturing industry. This document does not represent a standard or regulatory guidance.
In 2020, PDA issued Points to Consider for the Aseptic Processing of Sterile Pharmaceutical Products in
Isolators, which focus on important regulatory and technology updates surrounding isolator design,
validation, and operation for aseptic processing. Several other PDA documents equally address many of the
principles for working and operating with RABS, including Points to Consider No. 1: Aseptic Processing
(Revised 2023) or Technical Report No. 62 Recommended Practices for Manual Aseptic Processes.
Additionally regulatory guidance documents as the European Commission’s EU Guidelines for Good
Manufacturing Practice for Medicinal Products for Human and Veterinary Use Annex 1: Manufacture of
Sterile Medicinal Products (2022)1, U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) Guidance for Industry:
Sterile Durg Products Produced by Aseptic Processing-Current Good Manufacturing Practice (2004) and
others are addressing the special requirements for RABS. While many of the recommendations and
principles from these documents are equally applicable to RABS, until this point, there was no equivalent
guideline or Points to Consider (PtC) dedicated to RABS for which technology and operational specific
nuances apply. Hence, a taskforce was established to focus on industry best practices, regulatory and
technology considerations directly relevant to the application and use of RABS in an aseptic manufacturing
setting.
A fundamental principle of sterile product manufacturing is to minimize the risks of contamination
(microbiological and particulate) through appropriate facility, equipment, and manufacturing process design,
and qualification.  The use of barrier systems, including RABS, to increase the protection of the product is
not only recommended but should be considered in the context of an overall contamination control and risk-
reduction strategy. RABS technology provides for increased product protection compared to non-barrier
systems used for Grade A and Grade B aseptic manufacturing operations. RABS is a very different
technology to isolators and, consequently, key design, regulatory and operational differences, and
considerations apply, most notably background environments, cleaning, disinfection and decontamination
methods, aseptic setup, and strictly defined conditions and controls governing open-door RABS
interventions.
1 The Pharmaceutical Inspection Co-operation Scheme (PIC/S) Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) Annex 1 is
identical to the European Union (EU) Annex 1 and hereafter EU Annex 1 is also understood to mean EU PIC/S
Annex 1.

As such, this PtC is organized by Topics, followed by relevant points for reader consideration. Each point
begins with a problem statement in the form of a question, representing issues or points needing clarification
on that specific topic. Recommendations from the authors, who are subject matter experts, are presented in
response to each question. The rationale for the recommendation follows, as well as references to support
these recommendations, where available. The questions and answers reflect topics suggested by the
pharmaceutical industry and recommendations of the members of the task force on what to consider when
designing and managing RABS systems with consideration to current regulatory guidance.
Scope
This PtC is designed to address key questions and considerations raised from industry relating to the use,
application, and operations of RABS in an aseptic manufacturing setting. While the scope of the document
relates to considerations of RABS employed for aseptic processing, the principles contained within are
equally applicable to RABS for other applications, including products subjected to terminal sterilization and
low bioburden processes. In addressing these considerations, the document communicates best practices
across the lifecycle of RABS systems including their design and application, qualification, and operation,
and extending to barrier system interfaces for both existing and new systems.

Glossary
Definitions have been provided to help clarify
the concepts discussed in this document. While
some definitions used vary among companies,
geographic location, etc., the definitions
described below are consistently used within this
Points to Consider document. Where a definition
is based on another published source, the source
is cited.
Aseptic Process Simulation (APS)
A simulation of the entire aseptic manufacturing
process in order to verify the capability of the
process to assure product sterility. Includes all
aseptic operations associated with routine
manufacturing, e.g. equipment assembly,
formulation, filling, lyophilization and sealing
processes as necessary (1).
Bio-decontamination
A process that eliminates bioburden via use of a
sporicidal chemical agent (1)
Note: In the context of open RABS (oRABS),
this is a manual treatment process with
sporicidal agents.
Biological Indicator (BI)
Test systems that contain viable microorganisms
with a defined concentration and resistance to a
specific decontamination process. They help
monitor whether the necessary conditions were
met to kill a specified number of
microorganisms for a given process.
Campaign
Processing of multiple lots or batches of the
same product serially in the same manufacturing
line.
Contact Time
The minimum amount of time that a sanitizer,
disinfectant, or sporicide must be left in
complete (wet) contact with the surface to be
treated in order to be effective (2).
Note: This is typically referred to as “wet contact
time”.
Critical Zone
A location within the aseptic processing area in
which product and critical surfaces are exposed
to the environment (1).
Critical Surfaces
Surfaces that may come directly into contact
with, or directly affect, a sterile product or its
containers or closures. Critical surfaces are
rendered sterile prior to the start of the
manufacturing operation, and sterility is
maintained throughout processing (1).
Decontamination
The overall process of removal or reduction of
any contaminants (chemical, waste, residue, or
microorganisms) from an area, object, or person.
The method of decontamination used (e.g.,
cleaning, disinfection, sterilization) should be
chosen and validated to achieve a level of
cleanliness appropriate to the intended use of the
item decontaminated (1). See also Bio-
decontamination.

=== PDF PAGE 12 ===
Disinfection
The process by which the reduction of the
number of microorganisms is achieved by the
irreversible action of a product on their structure
or metabolism to a level deemed to be
appropriate for a defined purpose (1).
Note: In this document, it is understood that
sanitizers and disinfectants are used in the same
context.
First Air
Refers to filtered air that has not been
interrupted prior to contacting exposed product
and product contact surfaces with the potential
to add contamination to the air prior to reaching
the critical zone (1).
Format Parts
Container specific parts (e.g., based on different
container sizes) that allow the proper
guidance/transfer of product containers through
a manufacturing line.
Grade A Air Supply
Air which is passed through a filter qualified as
capable of producing grade A total particle
quality air, but where there is no requirement to
perform continuous total particle monitoring or
meet grade A viable monitoring limits.
Specifically used for the protection of fully
stoppered vials where the cap has not yet been
crimped (1).
Note: For the purposes of this document, the
term Grade A is understood to be
interchangeable with International Organization
for Standardization (ISO) 5.
Hold Time
Set durations between qualified events or
process steps.
Aseptic Hold Time
A defined and validated maximum time for
‘holding’ conditions for aseptic preparation
and processing.
Clean Hold Time
Duration between when cleaning is
implemented to meet a target requirement
before the use of parts or areas. This type of
hold time would typically apply before
sterilization or bio-decontamination
processes.
Sterile Hold Time
Duration sterilized items can be held after
sterilization and before use.
Intervention
Activities, manipulations, and tasks performed
in Grade A, or other critical processing areas,
during aseptic operations.
Corrective Intervention
An intervention that is performed to correct
or adjust an aseptic process during its
execution. These may not occur at a set
frequency in the routine aseptic process.
Examples include such activities as clearing
component jams, stopping leaks, adjusting
sensors, and replacing equipment
components.
Inherent Intervention
An intervention that is an integral part of the
aseptic process and is required for either set-
up, routine operation and/or monitoring
(e.g., aseptic assembly, container
replenishment, environmental sampling).
Inherent interventions are required by
procedure or work instruction for the
execution of the aseptic process.
Critical Intervention
An intervention (corrective or inherent) into
the critical zone (1).
Note: An example of this type of
intervention would be an open-door
intervention when critical surfaces are
exposed within the Grade A RABS. This
could be a corrective or inherent
intervention.

=== PDF PAGE 13 ===
Isolator
A contained, decontaminated environment
meeting Grade A/ISO 5 conditions used for
aseptic process manufacturing that provides an
uncompromised, continuous isolation of its
interior from the external environment. Once
decontaminated by a validated cycle, an isolator
prevents the microbiological contamination of
sterile products and product contact surfaces of
the interior by enclosures and the supply of
continuous, controlled overpressure of HEPA-
filtered air (3).
Closed Isolator Systems
Exclude external contamination from the
isolator’s interior by accomplishing material
transfer via aseptic connection to auxiliary
equipment, rather than using openings to the
surrounding environment. Closed systems
remain sealed throughout operations (1).
Open Isolator
Systems are designed to allow for the
continuous or semi-continuous ingress and/
or egress of materials during operations
through one or more openings. Openings are
engineered (e.g., using continuous
overpressure) to exclude the entry of
external contamination into the isolator (1).
Product Contact Parts
Direct Product Contact Parts
Are those that the product passes through,
such as filling needles or pumps (1).
Indirect Product Contact Parts
Are equipment parts that do not contact the
product, but may come into contact with
other sterilized surfaces, the sterility of
which is critical to the overall product
sterility (e.g., sterilized items such as
stopper bowls and guides, and sterilized
components) (1).
Rapid Transfer Port (RTP)
A System used for the transfer of items into
RABS or isolators that minimizes the risk to the
critical zone. An example would be a rapid
transfer container with an alpha/beta port (1).
Restricted Access Barrier System (RABS)
System that provides an enclosed, but not fully
sealed, environment meeting defined air quality
conditions (for aseptic processing grade A), and
using a rigid-wall enclosure and integrated
gloves to separate its interior from the
surrounding cleanroom environment. The inner
surfaces of the RABS are disinfected and
decontaminated with a sporicidal agent.
Operators use gloves, half suits, RTPs and other
integrated transfer ports to perform
manipulations or convey materials to the interior
of the RABS. Depending on the design, doors
are rarely opened, and only under strictly pre-
defined conditions (1).
Closed RABS (cRABS)
A RABS for which the barrier prevents air
exchange with the surrounding environment.
Closed RABS uses a dedicated
unidirectional air flow unit (UDAF) system
for air supply. Unlike a closed isolator, a
closed RABS typically has mouseholes, and
commonly has no automated
biodecontamination system.
Open RABS (oRABS)
A RABS for which the barrier permits air
exchange with the surrounding environment.
Passive Open RABS uses the room HVAC
for supply. Active Open RABS uses a
dedicated unidirectional air flow unit
(UDAF) system for air supply but exchanges
the exhaust with the room.
Note: Although Open RABS cannot be
automatically biodecontaminated, there are
some installations where the cleanroom, and
all the equipment it contains including the
open RABS, is biodecontaminated, but this is
a special case.

6-Log Reduction
The reduction of viable microorganisms with the
chance of 1:1,000,000 to have a surviving
organism.
Sporicidal Agent
An agent that destroys bacterial and fungal
spores when used in sufficient concentration for
specified contact time. It is expected to kill all
vegetative microorganisms (1).
Unidirectional Airflow (UDAF)
An airflow moving in a single direction, in a
robust and uniform manner, and at sufficient
speed, to reproducibly sweep particles away
from the critical processing or testing area (1).
Unidirectional Airflow (UDAF) Unit
A cabinet supplied with filtered unidirectional
airflow (previously referred to as a Laminar
Airflow Unit or LAF) (1).
Vapor Phase Hydrogen Peroxide (VPHP)
Agent for the decontamination of the interior of
the isolator, including all exposed surfaces, fixed
isolator glove exteriors (portion extending into
the barrier), transfer systems, equipment, and
certain supplies, materials, and parts, using a
defined and validated gaseous mixture or micro-
atomization of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) over a
defined and validated period of time, under
defined and validated conditions.
Working Position
Position where high-risk operations occur and
where product and/or components are exposed
(1).
Note: This is sometimes referred to as “working
height”.

Topic 1. Restricted Access Barrier System Design
Q 1.1. What are the different types of restricted access barrier systems (RABS)?
Recommendation
A Restricted Access Barrier System (RABS) is a series of components and processes that enclose and
provide a Grade A environment for aseptic processing (e.g., dispensing, filling and closing, lyophilization
loading). Due to the protection offered by the barrier system, a RABS can provide a higher level of product
and personnel protection than conventional Grade A or Grade B filling operations. See Question 2.3.
All RABS have rigid barriers, glove ports for operator access, a unidirectional airflow (UDAF) unit source
providing H14 high efficiency particulate air filter (HEPA) filtered air (i.e., First Air), and a method to
transfer materials and supplies in and out of it. Having these features in common, RABS differentiate in
three different types of air handling: passive open RABS (oRABS), active oRABS, and closed RABS
(cRABS), each offering an increasing level of protection for product and personnel. See Topic 3.
Note: This document does not cover isolator design. (See PDA’s Point to Consider for the Aseptic
Processing of Sterile Pharmaceuticals in Isolators for additional information.)
Figures 1.1-1, 1.1-2, and 1.1-3 depict the general layout of each RABS type highlighting the UDAF source,
rigid barrier coverage, and typical environmental classifications (4, 5).
Note: Diagrams are provided to illustrate the ways that air is supplied to and exits from the different types
of RABS systems. Diagrams are not intended to inform readers how operators should interact with the
equipment enclosed; that detail follows in later sections.

Barrier Considerations
Non-barrier technology is obsolete and is not compliant with regulatory expectations. Non-barrier systems have only individual barriers in place, such
as polycarbonate, or curtains and aseptic operations are open to the surrounding space of the room.

=== PDF PAGE 22 ===
All types of RABS have a barrier system in place (e.g., translucent rigid barriers) that surrounds the aseptic
operations. In oRABS (i.e., passive and active), the barrier system is open to the room via openings below
the product level that allow the unidirectional airflow to vent to the surrounding space. This type of oRABS
is not sufficiently sealed during operations, therefore the RABS cannot be subjected to a Leak Rate Test to
verify enclosure integrity while in operation.

=== PDF PAGE 23 ===
In cRABS, the barrier system is closed to the room such that the unidirectional airflow is recirculated inside
the cRABS. The barrier system is sealed, and the cRABS may be subjected to a Leak Rate Test to verify
closure integrity even during operations. However, the lack of inflatable gaskets can lead to leaking into the
room, and the Leak Rate Test should not be conducted without additional precautionary measures.
Unidirectional Airflow Source
Non-barrier systems and passive oRABS have unidirectional airflow supplied by the surrounding
space/room via a ceiling-mounted HVAC system with HEPA-grade filters. Active oRABS and cRABS have
unidirectional airflow supplied by a dedicated RABS HVAC system with HEPA-grade filters.
Glove Ports
Non-barrier systems do not have glove ports because the process is open to the surrounding space/room. All
types of RABS have glove port(s) installed on the rigid barrier; number and placement will vary. Glove
ports allow for aseptic interventions to occur without the need for the operator to enter the RABS (i.e.,
aseptic processing area).
Rapid Transfer Ports (RTPs)
Non-barrier systems have no material transfer ports because supplies and samples that are transferred into
and out of the processing area are done so directly. (See Topic 6 for additional information.) All types of
RABS have material transfer port(s) that are integrated into RABS. These ports allow for the aseptic transfer
of materials and samples in and out of the RABS.
Note: Although RABS doors can be opened, in the absence of appropriate controls this would potentially
allow the introduction of lower-grade air into the Grade A space, potentially contaminating the Grade A
processing area environment with lower-grade air. Open-door interventions should be extremely rare
occurrences, and proper risk mitigation precautions should be implemented (1).
Environmental Control
Factors such as temperature, humidity, and differential pressure are controlled by the HVAC unit mounted
in the surrounding room/space for non-barrier systems and passive oRABS. These factors are controlled by
the dedicated RABS HVAC unit for active oRABS and cRABS.
Typically, no positive pressure is present over the processing area compared to the surrounding space.
Minimal to no positive pressure is present in passive oRABS over the processing area compared to the
surrounding space. In active oRABS and cRABS, positive pressure is established in the processing area
compared to the surrounding space. However, in some designs there is the capability to selectively control
the air volume in specific areas to enable a positive air flow.
Pressure Cascade
No pressure cascade is possible in non-barrier systems, or oRABS (i.e., active or passive), but this is
possible in cRABS.
Bio-decontamination
Bio-decontamination (by gassing or fogging) is possible for non-barrier systems or oRABS only if the room
is also bio-decontaminated. In the case of cRABS, it is possible to perform bio-decontamination without the
room, however, if the cRABS does not utilize inflatable gaskets, there could be leakage around the gaskets
leading to health safety concerns and adequate precautions are needed to ensure personnel safety.

=== PDF PAGE 24 ===
Product
Only nontoxic products are able to be manufactured in non-barrier systems and in oRABS (i.e., active or
passive). In cRABS, it is possible to manufacture nontoxic or toxic drug products.
Additional design features should be considered including, but not limited to the following:
•
Design must ensure that all processes (i.e., with the exception of setup) will be designed to avoid
open-door interventions.
•
The barrier walls should be rigid, resistant to chemical cleaning and disinfecting agents, and also
resistant to mechanical stresses (e.g., scratches/cracking) from cleaning tools. Typical barrier
materials are polycarbonate or tempered/safety glass.
•
The positioning of equipment, components, and sensors within the critical zone (e.g., filling
needles, open vials) should be considered to ensure that First Air is not disrupted. For fixed machine
components (i.e., transport mechanisms), having an effective disinfection program in place is
required.
•
The glove positions inside the RABS barrier wall should be appropriate to allow operator arm entry
into RABS glove(s) without disruption of First Air above critical surfaces, for example, open vials
at the accumulation turntable, above fill needles, above stopper bowl, or above environmental
monitoring (EM) equipment. It is key that these locations be determined early in the design process
and tested during the mock-up phase of the project to verify operator ergonomics for all intended
operations. Having support from experienced operators during this design process is beneficial and
recommended.
Note: Any tools that are stored inside the RABS for operator use must be sterilized.
•
To protect the product from particulate contamination, it is recommended that RABS parts and
components in the aseptic processing area be constructed of non-shedding, non-leaching, non-
corrosive, chemical-resistant materials. It is recommended that all metallic components be
constructed of low-carbon 304 stainless steel or better. In the case of metallic product contact and
indirect product-contact components, low-carbon 316 stainless steel or better should be used (5). All
nonmetallic materials, including RABS gloves, gasketing, seals, and barrier walls and doors need to
withstand exposure to various cleaning and disinfecting agents and be suitable for the process.
The following are some points to consider in design and operations of RABS that impact achieving
specified environmental control conditions within the RABS:
•
Appropriate lighting to see the equipment, product, and components without degradation of light
sensitive product.
•
Rounded corners and smooth materials to minimize the risk of compromising the glove integrity
during changes or line interventions.
•
Safety curtain (i.e., light curtain) to detect operator glove entry into the RABS.
•
Alarms for environmental parameters (Temperature/Humidity/UDAF velocity) and door status
(door open or closed).
•
EM system viable and total particles in continuous operation during setup and filling operations.
•
EM system (settle plates and active air-monitoring locations) readily accessible via glove ports.
Rationale
The RABS design attributes as described in Table 1.2-1 are beneficial in assuring that environmental
conditions are maintained and minimizing microbial contamination associated with direct human
interventions in the critical zones.

Q 1.3. What are the design criteria for closed restricted access barrier systems
(cRABS)?
Recommendation
A cRABS, while not providing the same design constructs as an isolator, does provide some advantages
over an oRABS design. The benefits of a cRABS can only be experienced when the RABS remains closed
following setup. All qualified interventions should be able to be performed through the glove ports. The
need to open the doors should be rare and heavily scrutinized on a case-by-case basis; these types of rare
open-door interventions may require a deviation (1, 3, 6).
Differences between cRABS and active oRABS:
•
The rigid barrier completely encloses the Grade A space within the cRABS.
•
Air is recirculated within a cRABs, hence there is no spillover to adjacent space as in an oRABS.
(See Figure 1.1-3.)
•
Pressure can be maintained within a cRABS, allowing for better control of a pressure cascade
between the RABS and upstream and downstream equipment.
Differences between cRABS and an isolator:
•
If inflatable door seals on a cRABS are not present, a vapor-phase hydrogen peroxide (VPHP) cycle
is not recommended without adequate safety precautions.
•
Due to the lack of a positive door seal, the background environment for a cRABS is Grade B to
minimize the risk of contamination.
•
In the case of rare, open-door interventions and the potential for not being able to conduct
automated biodecontamination for a cRABS, a Grade B background is required to minimize the risk
of contamination.
Rationale
The cRABS design attributes are beneficial in providing further assurance of environmental conditions and
minimizing microbial contamination associated with direct human interventions in the critical zones.

Q 1.4. What are the design considerations for the placement of the restricted access
barrier system (RABS) glove ports?
Recommendation
The correct positioning of the RABS glove ports is one of the key elements that should be considered during
the design of a RABS. The position of the glove ports plays a vital role in the operation of the RABS and its
ability to remain closed during setup and operation. Efforts to minimize the need to use RABS gloves
should be taken to reduce the impact or risk to process performance and product sterility. Examples include
reducing, eliminating, or repositioning activities, manipulations, and interventions. Figure 1.4-1
distinguishes which part of the RABS are the glove ports and which are the RABS gloves (1).

When determining where to place/position the RABS glove ports (typically done during mock-up studies),
several factors that may pose a risk to the performance of the aseptic process and sterility of the product
should be considered such as:
•
Use of a simulation method to review glove port positioning
•
Ergonomics to address personnel fatigue and safety
•
Diversity of personnel height and reach, from which a determined range may inform optimum port
positioning
•
Quantity of glove ports
•
Orientation and location of planned and anticipated activities, manipulations, and
interventions (i.e., inherent and corrective interventions)
•
Glove port distance to open containers (e.g., vials, syringes, cartridges, EM components) and
equipment *
•
RAB gloves range of motion
•
Weight and orientation of materials and items to be handled via the glove port

=== PDF PAGE 27 ===
•
Ability to install RABS gloves and donning RABS gloves in an aseptic manner
•
Orientation of RABS gloves during decontamination/disinfection, aeration, if applicable, and
nonuse
•
Proximity to sharp objects or surfaces that can pierce RABS gloves
•
Position of EM apparatus, instruments, and sampling sites
*Note: Interventions cannot be performed directly using the RABS gloves, but rather by using a sterilized
tool and/or sterile material for an additional layer of protection where the First Air concept needs to be
maintained.
Rationale
With the use of RABS gloves, it is possible to perform interventions inside the RABS without the need to
open it. The correct positioning of glove ports enables the operator to reach machine parts without the
introduction of the operator gloves and gown, which are potential particle and microbial contamination
sources. For this reason, the design and position of glove ports is an important decision point and should be
part of the risk assessment and contamination control strategy (CCS).
Generally, a review of the positioning should be performed using a simulation method. Modeling, rendering,
3-D model, virtual reality, mock-up, or any method that allows for demonstration and testing of glove-
related procedures with different operators of diverse heights and arm length could be used.
The glove port needs to be positioned to ensure that the RABS gloves have sufficient distance to the open
containers (e.g., vials, syringes, cartridges, EM components, etc.) and to the equipment that comes into
direct and indirect contact with the product. This allows for a RABS glove entry by the operator without
disruption for First Air above critical surfaces.
The design of glove ports should be sufficiently cleanable (i.e., hygienic design) and resistant to the
appropriate cleaning/disinfection agents. The design should also allow for the range of motion necessary
with the RABS gloves to avoid excessive operator reaching or stretching of RABS gloves.
Restriction of airflow interrupting or interfering with adequate distribution of decontamination chemicals,
vapors, or gases (i.e., typically only seen with cRABS) should be avoided.
If applicable, select position of glove stretchers to allow for automatic bio-decontamination of the whole
surface (cRABS only).

Q 1.5. What are the design considerations for restricted access barrier system (RABS)
gloves?
Recommendation
Quality risk management (QRM) principles should be used to identify, assess, and address special
considerations for the safe and effective use of RABS gloves to mitigate the risks posed by the installation
and use of RABS gloves during aseptic operation (1).
Once the glove-port positioning is determined, a number of factors that may pose a risk to the performance
of the aseptic process and the sterility of the product should be considered. The following considerations
should be given to the composition of RABS gloves (3, 4):
•
Quality and design of RABS gloves should be sufficiently cleanable (i.e., hygienic design) and
resistant to the appropriate cleaning/disinfection agents and steam sterilization.
•
Glove size, though usually supplied as standard size ranges, should be considered based on intended
user population and demographic to ensure that gloves are sufficiently sized and practical for use.
•
Method of physical and visual inspection for integrity (See Topic 4).
•
Ability to confirm or test the integrity of RABS gloves (See Topic 4).
•
Ability to decontaminate, disinfect and, if applicable, aerate RABS gloves
•
Selection of glove material (e.g., ethylene propylene diene monomer (EPDM), chlorosulfonated
polyethylene synthetic rubber (CSM)) to allow for resistance during sanitization/decontamination
and use; also, to ensure sufficient dexterity to allow for the accurate execution of interventions
•
Thickness of glove material to address dexterity needed to perform task in RABS gloves
•
Thickness of glove material to resist puncture, cracking, or excessive wear
•
Ability to adequately install RABS gloves in glove ports to maintain aseptic status of the line
Additional operational considerations include, but are not limited to:
•
A sufficient number of spare RABS gloves should be available for each run.
•
Expiration date of RABS gloves
•
Exposure to direct sunlight that can degrade RABS gloves or glove seal material
•
Safe position for intermediate storage of RABS gloves in the RABS glove port after or between
operations to avoid both the obstruction to First Air during operation, as well as ensuring that the
gloves cannot be damaged by moving parts within the RABS
Rationale
Performing the above recommendations will reduce the possibility of leakages due to the tearing of RABS
gloves, which impacts the integrity of the RABS. These recommendations are to promote compliance of
good aseptic practices/techniques to reduce contamination risks and promote the prevention of open-door
interventions.
With the use of RABS gloves, it is possible to perform interventions inside the RABS and to reach machine
parts without the introduction of the operator gloves and gown (a potential particle and microbial
contamination source). For this reason, the manner of usage, construction materials, and installation
procedures is an important decision point and should, at a minimum, be recorded in the CCS.

Q 1.6. How should the restricted access barrier system (RABS) be designed to minimize
the risk posed by interventions?
Recommendation
Human interventions (i.e., open-door and/or inherent interventions) within the RABS are required during the
setup and assembly of the aseptic filling machine, including the installation of RABS gloves, and during
routine manufacturing operations (e.g., aseptic filling with open- and closed-door inherent and corrective
interventions).
Risk-based assessments should be used to evaluate the need for and design of human interventions. Where
possible, interventions should be eliminated, reduced, or modified to reduce their relative risk. A process
should be created to monitor and control the frequency of interventions. Changes to the process should be
made to improve the overall robustness of the manufacturing process and should eliminate the need for
interventions, when possible.
It is not recommended to approach the need for manufacturing changes with the goal of reducing
intervention. Corrective, open-door interventions should be eliminated or occur only as very rare events.
Therefore, the prevention of open-door interventions should be a key factor in the consideration for
designing a RABS (1, 6).
The positioning of RABS gloves in proper locations should eliminate the need for open-door interventions
during filling operations where good aseptic technique applies. Interventions within the RABS (i.e., inherent
and corrective) are to be designed to minimize microbiological ingress, including retention, within the
RABS. Such factors are proximity to exposed sterile product, components, indirect product contact surfaces,
intervention duration, complexity, and frequency.
The following points for consideration are applicable for an aseptic filling line and also for other aseptic
working steps (e.g., dispensing, lyophilizer loading/unloading), where a RABS is used:
•
The RABS and the filling line should be designed to allow for interventions (i.e., open and closed
door) in the Grade A RABS with due consideration of adherence to proper aseptic technique, for
example, to follow First Air working principles for the operator during open-door interventions and
also RABS gloves during closed-door interventions. See Question 1.1 for more information.
•
In the case of an open-door intervention that requires direct entry of part of the operator’s body
within the Grade A area, design considerations should extend to operator posture and body
positioning and ensure that the door openings and location of the RABS glove ports do not prevent
risk of operator contact. Tools should be used in place of an operator entering the RABS when
possible. Airflow visualization studies should be performed to confirm adequacy under dynamic
conditions. Further precautions and controls are required for open-door interventions (1, 6-8). (See
Topic 9.)
•
The number and location of glove ports should be designed based on the need for and ease of
performance of interventions, in particular, in locations where First Air principles can be followed.
•
Materials and equipment within the RABS should be positioned to avoid crowding and the need to
reach over exposed sterile products, materials, and surfaces.
•
The design and positioning of a RABS filling line should prevent the use of swing conveyors to
pass from the front to the back of the filling line.
•
The RABS design should consider, under risk assessment, a zone of protection using a UDAF unit
to cover, at a minimum, the open-door radius. The positions must be determined by a risk analysis

=== PDF PAGE 30 ===
and should consider typical open-door activities, such as the setup of stopper bowls or feeding
lanes.
•
The dynamic use of the RABS, where there are inherent and/or corrective interventions being
performed and equipment is operating, needs to be confirmed by airflow visualization studies to
demonstrate that unidirectional flow of air is occurring, and First Air principles are maintained.
•
The use of automated process steps (e.g., sterilization in place (SIP) after installation of the filling
line), and intervention-reducing technology (e.g., rapid transfer port (RTP) filling path or
components loading as closures, to install EM samplers for preventing mobile air sampler transfer)
should be considered to minimize interventions during setup, operations, and sampling.
•
The use of robotics and advanced automation should be considered during the design of a new
RABS line to increase the level of sterility assurance by removing operator interventions for critical
processes (e.g., the setup of the filling path). When using robotics in the aseptic environment for
aseptic filling, the design and operating principles must be carefully evaluated for operation
suitability within a cleanroom environment. Robotic systems, regardless of type, bring a certain risk
to the unidirectional airflow due to a tendency to generate turbulence. Therefore, robots should not
be used for the sake of using robotics, but rather should bring a benefit for the aseptic filling
process. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) and airflow visualization studies (static and dynamic)
should verify the impact of the robot to the unidirectional airflow and its impact on First Air.
Besides the robot itself the requirements to meet first air also applies for any tools the robot is
carrying and connections made on the robot (e.g., media hoses etc.). In addition to considerations
regarding impact to First Air, cleanability, disinfection and accessibility to the robot also require
consideration.
Rationale
All interventions into a RABS (i.e., open-door intervention) and within the RABS (i.e., closed-door
intervention) in the vicinity of exposed sterile product and critical surfaces can add the risk of
microbiological contamination to sterile product. Where possible, the elimination or reduction of these
activities through process design, equipment layout, technology, and automation can reduce risk. Where
activities cannot be eliminated or reduced, process and equipment design that allows for proper aseptic
technique and practices can reduce risk. Where interventions cannot be eliminated or reduced in number,
modifying these activities to reduce risk factors can reduce the overall relative risk.

Q 1.7. How can open-door interventions be prevented during filling?
Recommendation
RABS are designed with the goal of operating predominately as a closed system. Where open-door
interventions may be required, appropriate measures must be taken to mitigate the potential for increased
risk to the aseptic process.
Detailed design reviews of the production process undertaken at the onset of design can help avoid or
minimize the need for open-door activities during the filling operation (1). These reviews should consider,
but not be limited to, the following:
•
Since the open-door barrier manual set-up process presents the highest risk of contamination of the
Grade A zone when entering sterilized parts, the design of the set-up operation should be assessed
using QRM principles to reduce any unnecessary risk.
•
Following QRM principles, RABS design and operation should avoid the need for open-door
interventions so after the last bio-decontamination at set up the barrier doors remain closed until
batch end. Primarily inherent and corrective interventions should be via barrier closed and any
open-door intervention fully justified and rare.
•
RTPs used for introduction of pre-sterilized process components, consumables, product containers
and closures plus EM plates if required to be changed or recovered for incubation during aseptic
manufacturing operations.
•
Use of robotic systems and automation that drives intervention free processing including
introduction of EM systems that do not rely on growth media plate change (e.g., real time
continuous viable monitoring).
•
Design considerations equally extend to consideration of the use and position of glove ports and
should be considered in conjunction with the intended operations to define access requirements
within the enclosed system and the type of operations for which interventions may be required.
•
Design considerations extend to the use of clean-in-place (CIP)/SIP and single-use systems for fluid
or powder pathways.
Periodic reviews of the type, frequency, and duration of interventions for the filling process should be
reviewed to inform the next periodic aseptic process simulation (APS) protocol and to otherwise inform a
potential engineering or other control solution that may be introduced to eliminate the need for future similar
open-door interventions (1, 4, 9).
Notwithstanding these considerations, not all possible events that may be encountered during manufacturing
operations can be considered during the design or early stages of RABS introduction for a new product or
process. Rather, the intent of a well-designed aseptic program involving the use of barrier technology is to
ensure that the RABS protection concept is the primary focus and continuously assessed, with the aim of
eliminating or reducing the need to open the barrier during filling activities. When a closed-door
intervention is possible to address an issue, an open-door intervention must be prohibited.
Procedural controls should be established to ensure that adequate provisions are included in governing
procedures and processes for such unavoidable scenarios and should extend to a detailed review and
documentation including, but not limited to:
•
Preassessment of airflow visualization studies (AVS) and positive-pressure differentials to ensure
that positive airflow from the enclosure to the external environment can be maintained prior to
opening doors. A pre-intervention assessment should also extend to a documented review of what

=== PDF PAGE 32 ===
needs to be performed and whether the activity can be considered comparable to a preexisting
intervention (1, 4, 9).
•
Consideration of whether the unqualified intervention represents a departure from critical controls
established and whether a deviation or formal risk assessment is required in support of finalizing
and undertaking the planned intervention. The need for additional EM data, personnel monitoring
requirements, sterility sample requirements, particulate-monitoring data and alarm review should be
considered. An assessment of whether a media qualification and AVS may be required prior to
release of the batch should also be understood and documented.
•
A robust disinfection of the nonproduct-contact surfaces, implicated by the open-door activity using
appropriately qualified disinfectants. If necessary, to ensure there is no disinfectant residue, the use
of 70% IPA after disinfection should be considered
•
Consideration of need for batch segregation and/or discarding of any filled product or components
that may have been compromised or placed at unnecessary risk during the intervention.
•
Line clearance to eliminate open containers and/or components as applicable that may be in the
vicinity of the intervention.
•
Confirmation that Grade A conditions have been reestablished prior to recommencing filling.
Note: Although not recommended, in rare cases, an unanticipated corrective intervention may be required
that necessitates open-door operator intervention (e.g., to mitigate equipment challenges). In such cases, the
inherent increased risk to the product requires careful consideration in line with formal QRM principles and
should inform future engineering controls or other provisions that may be introduced with the aim of
eliminating recurrences or the requirement for the open-door intervention. Before the intervention is
executed, it needs to be risk assessed. If the risk-assessment does not support the execution of the
intervention, it should not occur.
Rationale
By design, the intent of a RABS or other barrier system is to separate the operator from the critical process
zone to reduce the overall risk of contamination. Since interventions within a barrier system pose the highest
risk to the aseptic core, the aim of any barrier technology, combined with a system of (engineering) controls,
should be to reduce or eliminate any unnecessary interventions. Hence, the use of the barrier technology in
conjunction with a system of controls comprising suitable design, robust quality systems, and aseptic
oversight is required.

Q 1.8. How can contamination risks of Grade A environments and compromise of
sterilized component sterility be minimized during setup?
Recommendation
Setup, if done through closed doors via the glove ports, requires fewer precautions; however, setup most
often requires activities through open doors. If that is the case, there are several ways to minimize the risk:
•
Operate with only one door open at a time.
•
As much as possible, equipment should be assembled prior to sterilization to avoid the need for
aseptic connections. Sterilized items should be introduced with protective covers or sterile boxes
and those covers or boxes should only be removed after the doors have been closed through the
glove ports. For legacy systems, where removal of protective covers after the doors are closed is not
possible, alternative approaches must be considered and documented as part of the CCS. (See Topic
6.)
•
Utilize a “top down” approach to line build, when possible, so installation is not occurring over an
exposed critical surface (e.g., stopper hopper, stopper slide, stopper bowl).
•
Don additional gowning, such as sterile sleeves or longer gloves (1).
•
Perform operator activities with a primary and secondary operator model: In this model, the
secondary operator opens the door and hands material to the primary operator who only operates
within the Grade A space. (See Topic 9.)
•
The RABS design should consider, under a risk assessment, a zone of protection using an UDAF
unit to at least cover the open-door radius. The positions have to be determined by a risk analysis
and should consider frequent open-door activities such as the setup of stopper bowls or feeding
lanes. Figure 1.8-1 shows an example for UDAF extension (1, 9).
•
Evaluate the need to take additional (continuous) air samples (viable and total particulates) in the
Grade B area with UDAF immediately surrounding the open-door area for the duration of the open-
door setup activities (1, 9). (See PDA Technical Report No. 62: Recommended Practices for
Manual Aseptic Processes for additional guidance for personnel behavior.)
•
AVS qualification should be done to ensure that there is a UDAF flush away and out of the RABS
during setup.
•
Evaluate the possibility of performing VPHP decontamination after setup (cRABS or oRABS with
cleanroom).
•
After setup, the use of automatic decontamination reduces the risk of ingress of the operator into
Grade A.

Rationale
Open-door activities typically necessary for equipment setup are of a higher risk as personnel must ingress
into the Grade A space. All activities that minimize the risk of introducing bioburden into the Grade A
environment will reduce the risk to the environment and, subsequently, the product. Such activities include,
but are not limited to, the following:
•
Minimizing the number of open doors to reduce the potential disruption of the UDAF within the
RABS.
•
Maintaining protective covers on equipment, such as bonnets on a stopper bowl, keeps the sterilized
surfaces from being exposed until the RABS doors have been closed and ensuring greater
protection. It also allows for the door-open time to be minimized, completing the setup activities
through the glove ports in a second step (4).
•
The utilization of additional protective measures (sterile sleeves, longer gloves, etc.) needs to be
evaluated based upon an approved risk assessment.
•
Similarly, the primary/secondary operator model safeguards the integrity of the gowning of the
primary operator, further reducing the risk of introducing contamination. (See PDA TR-62 for
additional information.)
•
Having UDAF immediately adjacent to the doors, so that once the door is open, the area remains
covered by Grade B surrounding the open door, will reduce the risk of introducing bioburden into
the RABS enclosure.

Q 1.9. What construction design considerations are required for materials and
maintenance?
Recommendation
RABS surfaces should be manufactured in materials compatible with the environmental classification
conditions required for the proposed use.
Construction Materials Considerations:
•
Interior metallic surfaces in direct or indirect contact with the primary packing should be
constructed in 316 stainless steel or better and, for the rest of the line, metallic components could be
constructed of low-carbon 304 stainless steel or better (1, 6).
•
Interior transparent surfaces made of polycarbonate or glass.
•
Seals joining the RABS with upstream and downstream equipment should take temperature and
differential pressures into consideration (e.g., depyrogenation tunnel to filler RABS or freeze
dryer).
•
Cleanable with detergents commonly used in the cleanroom with no absorbing or desorbing effect
when in contact with them (10).
All RABS surfaces should be resistant to the following conditions and agents to avoid the release of
particles:
•
Scratches and abrasions.
•
Detergents and sporicidals used for CIP and cleaning-out-of-place, or disinfection (e.g., turntables,
guides) (11).
•
Sterilization process as SIP for fixed-system components (e.g., filling path).
•
External sterilization processes with moist heat or radiation, or chemical sterilization processes for
complementary components removable from RABS (e.g., stopper bowl, feeding lanes), or ready to
use.
Maintenance Considerations
The internal RABS working areas should be designed to minimize the risk of contamination in critical zones
for troubleshooting and repair activities.
The access to maintenance panels and/or mechanical spaces should be outside the RABS and below the
operating/processing level (product height) as much as possible. Even if outside of the machine, these areas
should not be opened without additional controls being evaluated and implemented (e.g., requires to
exchange gowning after those activities).
All maintenance activities for a RABS and related equipment should be scheduled in a plan to occur at the
same time, when it is possible, to avoid multiple preventive-maintenance work-order days.
Rationale
The RABS and equipment construction materials should be designed to meet such production requirements
as cleanability, sanitization, and practicality of use, with the scope to reduce the contamination risk by
particles that could be released from routine use or from eventual damages and abrasion occurred during
routine production or maintenance activities.

Q 1.10. What unique design provisions are required for highly potent compounds
exposed within closed restricted access barrier systems (cRABS)?
Recommendation
Environmental Health Safety vs. Good Manufacturing Practices
When designing a RABS for aseptic highly potent compounds, both the good manufacturing practice
(GMP) and environmental health and safety (EHS) aspects are very important. While overpressure of the
containment is necessary from a GMP perspective, negative pressure is required from the EHS standpoint to
ensure operator safety (6).
Therefore, the pressure differential between the inside of the RABS and the outside environment must be
positive. To avoid contamination from the most highly potent exposure area (e.g., filling zone), this zone is
operated in negative pressure towards adjacent RABS sections. To make this physically and technically
possible, a cRABS is needed. To allow for the creation and maintenance of the pressure differences during
operation, it needs at least a cRABS, if recirculating air is used, a dedicated air handling unit (AHU) must be
present.
Additional Considerations:
•
Ability to safely change out terminal HEPAs by a safe-change filter system or one-pass aeration
systems (with bag-in/bag-out exhaust HEPAs) (See Figure 1.10-1.)
•
Interlocked door sensors and other mechanical devices to ensure operator safety
•
Vacuum systems should be avoided where possible; otherwise, the integration of filters that prevent
contamination at the base of the machine should be considered
•
Transfer ports should be present to manage the removal of contained material used for spillage
clean-up without having to open the RABS.*
•
Pressure differential-monitoring systems to alarm in case of loss of pressure differential or RABS
leakage.
•
Double glass doors are to be avoided (See Figure 1.10-1.)
•
Important for the commissioning of such products is the correct protective equipment of the
employees and their behavior in case of an accidental breach.
*Note: This consideration does not apply for monitoring equipment using vacuum, such as total-particle
counters or active viable monitoring. The monitoring equipment must be located in the most critical
positions according to the EM risk analysis. A strategy to prevent contamination of the vacuum lines and the
monitoring equipment must be evaluated (e.g., the particulate filter above the machine plate in the aseptic
area) immediately after the sampling head for active viable (6). The sampling head must be treated as any
other exposed equipment surface within the RABS and needs to be appropriately cleaned before the
sterilization cycle. For particulate measurement systems, a different strategy must be considered. Total
particle counters should be dedicated to certain areas and changed in situ to reduce the movement of the
equipment and to limit exposure. Particulate filter can be placed after the device to ensure contamination
prevention of the vacuum lines and pumps.

Cleaning
A highly preferred method for cleaning a cRABS contaminated with toxic substances is the automatic wash-
in-place (WIP) process. The WIP process can either be executed with spray guns mounted inside the RABS
for manual wash-down or with a spray ball to achieve an automatic wash down. This means that even hard-
to-reach areas can be cleaned more easily while the doors remain closed and, therefore, the risk of operator
exposure is minimized. The handling of the wastewater must be considered with a special focus on the
draining systems (i.e., EU Annex 1: Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal Products excludes drains in Grade A
or Grade B environments) and toxic water separation. Depending on toxicity, measuring residuals in
wastewater is recommended to ensure the RABS environment is free from potential potent residuals. A risk
assessment should be performed to determine the proper neutralization of the wastewater from the toxic
process and proper design. Nevertheless, the machine and the containment must, of course, be designed in
such a way that all parts can be easily cleaned and equipped in a hygienic design.
Rationale
Potent compounds are hazardous to operators and, while product sterility must be maintained, it is equally
important to ensure that operators and the environment they operate in can be protected from exposure.
Utilization of cRABS, with negative pressure and WIP systems, allows for the operation and cleaning of the
system, without ever exposing the operator or the environment outside of the RABS to the compound.

Topic 2. Physical Environment
Q 2.1. What should be the classification of the room surrounding the restricted access
barrier system (RABS)?
Recommendation
The minimum classification of the room immediately surrounding the RABS must be Grade B. However, a
manufacturer may also choose to maintain these areas at more stringent levels of control when assessed for
risk, especially in the area immediately surrounding the RABS doors, which may require execution of open-
door interventions (i.e., during setup or filling) (1, 4).
Rationale
A RABS provides an enclosed and separated environment designed to reduce the risk of contamination to
product, components, and product-contact surfaces when compared to conventional "curtained" aseptic
processing operations. However, RABS are not fully sealed from the immediate surrounding environment
and, in some designs, allow for open-door interventions by aseptic operators. As such, the adjacent
background cleanroom environment must be appropriately classified and maintained to protect the critical
processing zone(s) within the RABS; therefore, the cleanroom environment should not be lower than Grade
B.
Companies should provide the maximum assurance possible and practicable so that the controlled RABS
environment is not impacted by the surrounding environmental conditions since the RABS is not completely
isolated from the surroundings, unlike closed isolators, for example.
Note: The minimum regulatory expectations (e.g., FDA, EMA) for the classification of manufacturing
spaces immediately adjacent (i.e., background) to the Grade A critical processing zone is Grade B.

Q 2.2. Which environmental monitoring criteria are required in extended unidirectional
airflow surrounding restricted access barrier system (RABS)?
Recommendation
Due to the presence of the UDAF around the open-door portion of the RABS, total particulates and viables
should always reflect Grade A limits at the working position, at rest. Locations that are found to be critical

The remainder of the room should be subjected to sampling frequencies and limits (e.g., total particulates,

adhering to Grade B limits.
Rationale
In the extended UDF surrounding the RABS, EM should be conducted to demonstrate the maintenance of a
protective environment during the times when the doors are opened. The function of the extended UDF is to
provide enhanced protection around critical zones where there is a break in the integrity of the Grade A
RABS zone (i.e., during open-door interventions). During times when the doors are closed, this environment
can be monitored as a more traditional Grade B environment, whereas during open-door interventions, it
should be treated as an enhanced Grade B environment with limits (alert and action) defined based on a risk
assessment.

Q 2.3. How should airflow pattern visualization/demonstration be performed in a
restricted access barrier system (RABS) for airflow visualization studies (AVS)?
Recommendation
The purpose of airflow visualization studies (AVS) (also known as “smoke testing”) is the verification of
unidirectional airflow maintained during all required equipment functions and manual interventions. This is
aligned to the principle of assuring First Air in contact with product and direct and indirect product-contact
equipment. The intention is to demonstrate that all permitted interventions are appropriately designed and
can be executed according to the approved methodology or procedure.
AVS should verify protective airflow is maintained at RABS entry ‘mouseholes’ without compromise to the
process environment that protective airflow is applied to.
An additional reason to perform an AVS is to have a visual record of process operations conducted per
requirements to allow the following:
•
Used as training material to demonstrate correct process execution. An important aspect about AVS
is that they should be exactly representatives of operational conditions, so people should have the
right gowning, validated loads and packages should be used.
•
Presenting to internal or external inspectors who may require visualization of a process that may not
be (fully) running at the point of inspection
•
Assess EM sampling site locations and design of interventions or intervention discard strategies.
Note: It is important to know what the air velocity is at the working position during the airflow
visualization testing so that a correlation can be established in routine requalification studies to demonstrate
the continued applicability of the AVS.
Airflow Pattern Visualization Process Activity Considerations
It must be recognized that the act of performing airflow visualization disturbs the environment that it is
designed to visualize due to the purposeful injection of a smoke or fogging spray into the critical zone that
would self-evidently not occur during routine operations.
To minimize the impact of this disturbance in the air supplied to the RABS, the following should be
considered:
1.
Develop a Qualification Protocol
̶
Work with affected departments (Manufacturing, Quality Assurance, Engineering,
Microbiology) to determine and document the setup, operation, and intervention steps
necessary for the process. The test objective is to show unidirectional airflow sweeping
over critical surfaces.
2.
Select Smoke-Generation Equipment Type (e.g., Fogger, Cartridge, Dry Ice)
̶
There are multiple acceptable equipment options for the generation of "smoke" for airflow
pattern visualization testing, including foggers that use dry ice, vaporization of water for
injection (WFI) or deionized water, or liquid nitrogen.
̶
The use of foggers that generate glycerin-based vapor is not recommended because they
can leave an oily residue on the test area, personnel, and/or floor areas. Such residues can
potentially impact equipment performance, equipment cleanliness, and personnel safety and
should be avoided.

=== PDF PAGE 45 ===
3. Select Water Vapor-Generating Equipment
̶
Water vapor-generating equipment has a variety of generation mechanisms and design
styles, including cartridge-based (handheld) and continuous supply via external devices.
̶
Depending on the specifics of the application and the RABS design, one of these styles
may be the better choice as there would be no residue formation.
4. Amount of Smoke
̶
The amount of smoke generated should be sufficient to visualize the airflow (and possible
turbulence), but not too much smoke to obscure the operators’ actions or machine
operations.
5. Smoke Speed
̶
The smoke should be generated at slow speed in the opposite direction to the airflow, at
approximately 45° against the direction of a vertical airflow and at a speed that minimizes
disturbance. The speed of smoke injection can be checked and adjusted before the test, with
the objective of not creating turbulence by the act of injection.
̶
It should be noted that if the smoke flow was in the same direction as the normal airflow,
that can reinforce the airflow directionality. Any perturbation to airflow (e.g., injection of
smoke) changes the system compared to the routine operation.
6. Smoke Generator and Smoke Diffuser (i.e., Manifold)
̶
Movement of the smoke generator and smoke diffuser should be reduced to be as slow as
possible, as recommended by good aseptic practices.
̶
To limit movements, the use of a smoke diffuser of sufficient size is suggested such that it
becomes unnecessary to move it during the AVS.
7. Smoke Generation Location
̶
The smoke is to be generated as high as possible above the working position, according to
the design of the equipment, but not too close to the HEPA filter to avoid filter-clogging
due to residue accumulation.
8. Air Movement at Interfaces
̶
To check air movement at interfaces to the Grade B surrounding environment (e.g., when
doors are open), the smoke is injected inside and outside the RABS. This is done at
different locations to demonstrate the flush of the air from the Grade A RABS and its
demonstrated unidirectionality (when injected inside) and the absence of air ingress (when
injected from outside) into the RABS zone.
9. Wall and Vertical Panel Considerations
̶
Injecting smoke near walls or vertical panels should be avoided, as this may create
additional disturbances in airflow that would not occur in routine operations.
Note: Air will flow slower along the RABS walls, and there will be minor eddies along the
walls. This is to be expected by the application of physics. Therefore, it should never be
necessary to perform critical operations immediately adjacent to any RABS walls.

=== PDF PAGE 46 ===
10. Airflow Visualization Study Frequency
̶
The frequency of re-executing an AVS is not timebound in the first instance; it should be
based primarily on events (equipment or process change control).
̶
As periodic validation review is regarded as a qualification study, the study may need to be
repeated for other reasons (e.g., improvements in film-capture quality over time, or
perception that the filmed operations and manipulations are not sufficiently “current”.
11. Open-door Intervention Considerations
̶
In the case of open-door interventions, airflow visualization qualification should be done to
ensure that there is a unidirectional airflow flush away and out of the RABS during the
intervention.
Selection of Video Recording Equipment and Cinematography
The record that exists after the execution of airflow visualization testing is a GMP record that assures the
data integrity, a video record that may need to exist for many years. As such, careful thought must go into
both the selection of the recording equipment and the modalities concerning film cinematography to
maximize value of what is shown to the reviewer in the video footage. The following points and
recommendations should be followed:
1.
Equipment and Physical Setup
̶
The equipment used and the physical setup to video-capture the performance of the airflow
visualization testing must be considered to ensure that the record contains sufficient detail
to demonstrate that unidirectional flow is maintained in all required areas. A good strategy
to ensure this is to set up multiple cameras at different angles with significantly overlapping
fields of view.
̶
Where applicable, built-in cameras and recording devices can be used to capture video of
smoke studies; however, complete and detailed coverage of the study area must be
accomplished.
2.
Visibility of Vapor on the Video Recording
̶
The rear glass doors of the RABS may need to be covered by an opaque backing or a
contrasting color in order to facilitate visibility in the final video footage.
3.
Cinematography Considerations
̶
Contextualizing the cinematography by filming a timepiece that indicates the time and date
before panning the camera to visualize the smoke directionality is recommended. Filming a
schematic drawing of the RABS system and then the equipment section can also assist the
viewer in this regard.
̶
Ensure that the differential pressure across the filter and velocity were within standard
operating limits during the AVS.
̶
Each camera shot should be carefully prepared to ensure that no unnecessary activity is
performed in the background (e.g., maintenance and/or cleaning).
̶
 The video should be taken using two to four (2-4) different camera angles to facilitate, as
much as possible, a three-dimensional view of the unidirectionality of airflow over the
critical zones.

̶
Best practice is to synchronize the timing of all filmed scenes, so that direct comparison of
the airflow visualization can be made from all filmed angles while viewing in a single
pane.
̶
When footages are edited to be combined into one stream, the original video file, if

Practical Considerations
Similarly, the logistics of the study execution require careful planning so that the captured film footage is
integral and does not allow for any misinterpretation or gaps to the record. The following should be
considered for implementation:
•
Simulate a walk-through of interventions (dress rehearsal) to assist with seamless professional
execution that minimizes the need for extensive editing. Consider the floor communication method
(i.e., radios, hand signals) during execution if participating individuals are on opposite sides of a
RABS.
•
Simulate all manipulations strictly following good aseptic behavior, including glove
decontamination, and limit the personnel in the space to the smoke-equipment operator, aseptic

Rationale
Focus of the video reviewer may be diverted from the smoke study evaluation if simulated operations are

•
Cumulative effect of process and/or facility changes
•
Quality of the video footage being improved over time
•
Improvements in the methodology of smoke generation and insertion techniques

Q 2.4. What are the considerations for facility layout design regarding acceptable
personnel passage through the Grade A restricted access barrier system (RABS)?
Recommendation
Using a conveyor swing arm to lift the process line and doors that close into both sides to protect the line

Topic 3. Personnel
Q 3.1. Should operator gloves be disinfected prior to entering the restricted access
barrier system (RABS) gloves?
Recommendation
Disinfection of operator gloves prior to entry into the RABS gloves is an important component of the CCS
and should be undertaken prior to each entry into the RABS gloves.
Gloved hands should be disinfected with an approved disinfectant (e.g., sterile isopropanol) prior to donning
the RABS gloves (1, 4). The disinfectant can be applied by spraying. It is considered a basic and vital
element of hygiene and cleanroom practices to minimize contamination risk or the transfer of contaminants
within the clean room in general. It is also important that the risk of transfer of contamination from one
operator to another is also considered and mitigated as part of the overall CCS.
Since RABS gloves are typically shared by more than one operator, the risk of contamination transfer from
one operator to another through the shared use of the RABS gloves should be minimized by sanitizing the
operator’s own gloves before they enter into the glove ports or after exiting from the glove ports. The risk
identified above (multiple operators using same gloves) is with reference to mitigating risk of contamination
transfer between operators. Of course, the risk of product contamination requires equal consideration.
Rationale
RABS gloves are an integral design element and form part of the physical barrier that enables product
protection by separating operators from the process.
Disinfection of gloves worn underneath RABS gloves will reduce the microbial load within the RABS
gloves, in contrast to nondisinfected gloves. In the case of a pinhole in a RABS glove, a reduced microbial
load on the inside of the RAB glove will reduce the chance of microbial ingress into the RABS Grade A
environment (14).
RABS gloves are not infallible and, with continued use, may become worn, torn, or perforated and, hence,
may potentially compromise the integrity of the system. While controls, including visual inspection (pre-
and post-interventions of use) of gloves, provide for some degree of detection of holes or leaks, the
identification of a leak through glove integrity-testing is usually undertaken post-batch or post-campaign
manufacturing. This requires a retrospective assessment to determine the impact on the batch in the event of
a confirmed glove-integrity breach.
For closed-door interventions that require the use of the RABS gloves, the risk profile associated with a
potential breach of the Grade A RABS environment owing to a RABS glove integrity failure is high. This
can be further compounded in the absence of operator glove disinfection, since the physical barrier between

=== PDF PAGE 50 ===
the operator hand (normally afforded through integral RABS gloves) and the Grade A environment is
breached. A vector for microbiological contamination is thereby created, with risk profile reduced by
ensuring adequate glove disinfection.

Level 1 – Training
A well-rounded personnel qualification training program should include, but not be limited to, the

•
Basic elements of microbiology and hygiene with specific focus on cleanroom practices (slow,

Q 3.3. Is there a clear distinction between the operator qualification for closed- and
open-door operator interventions, and are operators qualified for closed-door
interventions allowed to perform open-door interventions (For operators qualified for
specific tasks, e.g., primary operator, secondary operator, based on criticality)?
Recommendation
After setup, open-door interventions should be rare events. The personnel authorized to conduct open-door
line setup and permitted open-door interventions should be trained and qualified specifically for open-door
interventions after they have been permitted to undertake closed-door interventions.
Requirements for qualification of open- and closed-door interventions should be clearly distinguished.
An operator qualified for closed-door intervention is not allowed to perform open-door intervention due to
the risk factors associated with aseptic techniques and intervention type. But a person qualified for open-
door interventions can be allowed to perform closed-door interventions due to the low risk and considering
the similarities in the intervention process. However, this should be clearly documented in the site’s quality
risk assessment and CCS documents (1).
A qualified-operator list should clearly specify the personnel authorized to perform interventions into the
Grade A environment, with open-, closed-, or both interventions and, within that category grouping, can be
done based on the similarities (e.g., loading of stoppers and/or seals if the latter is performed in the RABS).
Rationale
Interventions should be designed and performed using proper aseptic techniques to minimize the risk of
product contamination. Operators should be first performing closed-door interventions and gaining a skillset
at a later stage to perform open-door interventions. (See Question 3.2 for additional information.)

Q 3.4. What are the recommendations for qualified and unqualified interventions?
Recommendation
Best Practices for Qualified Interventions
Interventions should be conducted by trained and qualified personnel. (See Question 3.2 on how to
qualify.)
An approved standard operating procedure (SOP) containing all defined interventions with step-by-step
instructions, mitigation actions (if any), and images (where applicable) should exist (1, 4, 7, 15, 16). A
dynamic AVS should be performed simulating all the listed interventions, and these studies should be
repeated with a defined frequency as described in Question 2.3.
Simulation of all qualified interventions in routine APS should be performed on a rotational basis; its
maximum frequency of occurrence in routine batch-manufacturing should be covered in an initial
qualification run and semiannual qualification runs. A rotation policy can be defined in an internal-site risk
management process but should occur at least annually.
Management of Unqualified Interventions
When an unqualified intervention occurs, a real-time risk-based impact assessment shall be performed (See
Question 1.7 on how to risk assess). If the assessment determines the unqualified intervention can be
performed, only authorized personnel (See Question 3.2 on authorized personnel) may execute the
unqualified intervention. If the risk assessment does not support the intervention as safe, then the
intervention should not proceed and the appropriate steps to end the batch should be taken. As part of batch
release, the impact of the unqualified intervention must be assessed for quality and compliance on the
batch.
Unqualified interventions identified to become qualified interventions shall have AVS to demonstrate
unidirectional airflow and preservation of First Air are not impacted. The unqualified intervention will then
be included in the ongoing APS program and upon successful completion the intervention can be added to
the local procedure. Unqualified interventions that are similar to qualified interventions may not require
additional AVS execution or inclusion in the APS program, to become a qualified intervention, if proven
equivalent with supporting technical and scientific rationale. In certain situations, unqualified interventions
may not be recommended to become a qualified intervention, based upon special events, and will not
require simulation in AVS and APS programs.
Rationale
Interventions should be designed and performed using proper aseptic techniques to minimize impact to the
production environment and the risk of product contamination. An unqualified intervention does not have
sufficient data to demonstrate the capability of control, hence, simulating the unqualified intervention in
APS testing to demonstrate the process capability is recommended.

Topic 4. Glove Integrity-Testing and Maintenance
Q 4.1. How do you ensure the integrity of restricted access barrier system (RABS)
gloves?
Recommendation
There are various aspects to designing, maintaining, and verifying the integrity of a RABS. The potential
loss of integrity can occur at many locations, including the RABS enclosure seams, baseplate openings,
doors, glove ports, gloves, transfer ports, UDAF (in the case of an active RABS), and the lack of First Air.
A risk analysis should be conducted to assist in identifying design decisions and operational actions that
have the potential for a loss of integrity. (See PDA Technical Report No. 54: Implementation of Quality Risk
Management for Pharmaceutical and Biotechnology Manufacturing Operations for guidance on performing
a risk analysis.)
As discussed in Questions 1.1-1.6 of this document, quality-by-design techniques can reduce the risk of
incursions to RABS by considering process and user requirements, risk analysis, and designs that limit
potential points of contamination. However, active monitoring and verification of RABS integrity is key to
protecting the product and operator safety, where applicable in cRABS.
Considerations on monitoring glove integrity throughout a campaign is important as post campaign integrity
test failures can be highly impactful. Also, integrity monitoring after significant interventions needs to be
considered. Monitoring environmental conditions in real time within the oRABS or cRABS is essential to
maintaining Grade A conditions to support RABS integrity. These parameters include:
•
Temperature
•
Humidity
•
Differential pressure
•
UDAF velocity
•
EM
In addition to real-time monitoring of these environmental conditions, glove integrity-testing, both visual
assessment and a pressure test are required (1, 17). Firms should establish the procedures for this pressure
test based on the specifications of their gloves and testing methods. This process should be incorporated into
the CCS. (See Topics 4-6 of this PtC.).
A common pathway to RABS contamination is through the use of glove ports and, in particular, the loss of
integrity of the glove material (4). While pre- and post-filling EM sampling may detect contamination via
the gloves, it is imperative to verify the integrity of the glove itself. Glove integrity-testing should be
performed based on an approved risk assessment. An approved risk assessment should determine the
frequency of pressure testing, visual inspection, and glove changeout (6, 8). Additional glove visual
assessments should be performed prior to each use during both campaign and batch production (3).

Rationale
It is critical for RABS to maintain integrity in order to support Grade A conditions. The RABS gloves
design, use, and maintenance are key elements to ensuring appropriate control and overall integrity. RABS-
related procedures and programs should encompass the continuous review of system and process
improvements that support product quality.

Q 4.2. What are the considerations for a restricted access barrier system (RABS) glove-
management strategy (e.g., selection, integrity, testing, and maintenance program)?
Recommendation
A RABS glove-management strategy is essential to maintaining the RABS environment (i.e., product
quality) and providing operator safety. This strategy includes glove selection, integrity testing, and an
established glove changeout frequency (6). Interventions should be designed to in a way to minimize the
risk of glove damage and loss of integrity. Within a Glove Management Strategy (part of CCS) the regime
for glove integrity testing should be specified based on risk and impact of glove integrity breaches. Visual
inspection, and where appropriate physical methods of glove integrity testing, should be applied as
combined assurance that glove integrity has been maintained through critical process steps of set-up and
during routine operations where inherent and corrective interventions apply.
There are multiple considerations with the selection of glove material. (See Question 1.5 for details on
glove materials of construction).
Depending on the glove selected, a risk assessment should establish the appropriate and validated integrity
test method and frequency (1). The risk associated with the process and product would have the greatest
impact on establishing the appropriate integrity-test requirements. The integrity-test method should include
both visual and physical testing components as detailed in Question 4.6.
Based on the glove selected and the established integrity-test requirements, the risk assessment should
evaluate a preventive maintenance (PM) program to establish the duration of glove changeout and
replacement frequency (6). Once in operation, the PM frequency of the gloves should be reviewed on a
periodic basis and adjusted based on data acquired from the RABS. For example, if certain gloves are
experiencing higher wear due to machine setup and adjustment, operator interventions, and/or cleaning and
disinfecting agents, the PM frequency may be increased.
Glove rotation can be considered to minimize the damage or failure rate for reusable-type gloves.
Consideration should be given to the location(s) where the glove is subjected for low/high utilization during
processing for rotation from batch or campaign operations (e.g., from a high-interventions zone to a low-
interventions zone. The rotation policy and its recording procedure is to be defined by QRM for its
traceability.
Rationale
The selection of the proper gloves, glove testing, and management of gloves is necessary to ensure operator
safety and to maintain the RABS environmental conditions. The firm should evaluate their process and
perform a risk assessment to determine an appropriate integrity test that reliably detects glove defects.

Q 4.5. How do you prevent restricted access barrier system (RABS) glove damages
after installation?
Recommendation
To keep the gloves in good condition during the processing of a batch or maintenance intervention, any
operation that could compromise glove integrity should be avoided. For example, tools with sharp edges
(e.g., scissors), should be those with rounded or covered tips designed specifically for cleanrooms.
Any operation that may overstretch gloves should be avoided, using instead activities within the specific
glove range of action or using another glove in closer proximity of the intervention. When not in use, RABS
gloves should be retracted to avoid possible interference and damage due to moving parts as well as
disruption to First Air. When the gloves are retracted, best practice is to keep the fingers of the gloves
extended for ease of the next use.
See Topic 1 of this PtC for RABS design considerations related to gloves.

Gloves should be replaced whenever the relative conditions are different from the technical characteristics
defined in manufacturer data sheets or from the internal risk assessment that could compromise the RABS
aseptic internal conditions and product quality (1, 3).
Glove replacement frequency is based on the following recommendations, which include, but are not limited
to:
•
Physical attributes
•
Manufacturer expiry date
•
Color variation from specification
•
Loss of elasticity
•
Viable and total particle counts trending higher
Prior to removal of gloves from the RABS, a visual inspection and/or pressure test should be conducted
based on an approved risk assessment of any damage. Gloves should be removed gently from the RABS
station and carefully placed on a sterile or sanitized cart or in sterile pouches. Any other hard or sharp object
should not be placed over the gloves after removal from RABS station as that may damage the glove during
handling for washing and sterilization processes.
Rationale
Integrity of the gloves is key in order to maintain a Grade A environment within the RABS enclosure.
Maintaining glove integrity includes procedurally controlled glove use and replacement, as well as both
periodic and routine glove inspection or testing based on an internal risk assessment.

Q 4.6. What methods should be used for verifying the integrity of restricted access
barrier system (RABS) gloves?
Recommendation
Integrity-testing of the RABS gloves should be performed by the operator using visual inspection and
physical (integrity test) methods that are defined in the CCS or by quality risk assessment.
There are three recommended processes to verify glove integrity; each method provides a different level of
inspection:
•
A visual assessment should be performed by the operator before and after each glove use (18). This
is a relatively quick visual verification for holes, tears, abrasions, and discolorations. This visual
assessment is done from the outside the RABS.
•
A thorough visual inspection should be performed by trained and qualified personnel either before
or after each system usage, batch, or campaign, including after the installation of gloves on the
RABS (1, 6, 17). It is recognized that visual inspection methods can be very effective at identifying
glove defects.
•
A physical test (pressure hold test) should be performed by using a commercially available
automated system that uses either the pressure-decay method that measures a pressure drop inside
the glove/sleeve, or the constant-pressure method that measures the leak rate as a flow. Physical
tests should be conducted for integrity-testing periodically based on a quality risk assessment or
CCS, with the trending of real-time data as an input (1, 3, 6, 17). Performing the physical test, at a
minimum, not longer than 30 days or at the end of the campaign is recommended. However, the
detection of failures, without the identification of a root cause, should result in more frequent
integrity-testing. Alternative physical tests, as long as validated, can be used.
There are two ways to perform the physical integrity-testing with a commercially available, automated
system:
1.
RABS gloves remain installed and are tested in place.
2.
RABS gloves are disassembled and are tested remotely.

Q 4.7. What are the acceptance criteria for pinhole detection in restricted access
barrier system (RABS) gloves?
Recommendation
While there is no industry standard or governing health authority acceptance for pinhole-size breaches, any
breach in integrity should be appropriately investigated, including holes in gloves and seams, to determine
the impact to the RABS environment (14). While the glove material of construction and the manufacturing
process within the RABS enclosure impact the potential glove defect size, it is recommended that, at a
minimum, a pinhole detection system should detect a 200 µm pinhole.
Physical glove integrity testing can be completed by using automated glove integrity-test systems that can
detect pinhole sizes lower than what is detectible through visual inspection. Many factors affect the
determination of the pinhole size to be qualified (e.g., glove material, glove length, glove thickness).
Key consideration should be given to the different risks presented by pin holes. Primarily airborne
contamination transfer that can result in a momentary pressurization of gloves or finger pockets at glove
entry, reversing the pressure differential. In addition, surface contamination transfer from cleanroom gloves
fingers can transfer contamination by touch if the pinhole is at or greater than the glove thickness. For each
glove type intended for use, a testing cycle should be developed and qualified with the glove integrity-test
system to determine the appropriate pinhole detection limit based on the risks of contamination transfer that
glove pinholes present.
Additionally, any proposed acceptance criteria for pinholes should be evaluated through a comprehensive
risk assessment. This risk assessment should consider the manufacturing process, personnel training and
qualification, specifications of the RABS enclosure, gloves, and use of an automated glove integrity-tester.
The glove management strategy should include an integrity testing procedure (which combines both visual
and physical testing means) and the rationale for detection size.  All of these must be documented in a CCS.
There are many other areas of control that should be evaluated prior to justifying pinhole acceptance
criterion including, but not limited to (4):
•
Quality of glove
•
Tools
•
Training (ensuring gloves stay away from critical surfaces)
•
Detection (visual and physical)
•
Preventive maintenance
•
Gowning
•
Materials
For example, if a company selected a thinner commercially available glove (e.g., 0.2 mm) when there are
other commercially available gloves (e.g., 0.4 mm), the increase in thickness can assist in the reduction and
overall control of the glove. Furthermore, an evaluation of the use of tools to support aseptic operations may
be appropriate, for example, using a needle recapping device as opposed to an operator manually recapping,
in order to reduce pinholes.

be related to other areas of the manufacturing process, which should be evaluated. Areas to consider may
be:
•
Manufacturer quality assessment (e.g., new or different glove manufacturer)
•
Manufacturing steps (e.g., increase use of needles)
•
Gowning (e.g., reduction of inner/outer glove or sleeves)
•
Operators (e.g., training)
•
Equipment (e.g., detection manufacturer)
Rationale
A pinhole detection system should detect a 200 µm pinhole, at a minimum, and be supported with a risk

system (RABS) gloves sterilization, bio-decontamination, sporicidal disinfection, and/or

Recommendation
For RABS, gloves used in the Grade A area should be sterilized before installation and sterilized or
effectively biodecontaminated again by a validated method prior to each manufacturing campaign (1, 9). If
exposed to the background environment during operation, disinfection using an approved methodology
following each exposure should be completed (1).
The frequency of sterilization, bio-decontamination-sporicidal disinfection, and disinfection is based on an
approved risk assessment considering the following:
•
Sterilization or bio-decontamination
•
Prior to batch or campaign operation. In between batches of a campaign, no bio-decontamination or

not been breached (e.g., doors remained closed, HVAC system functioning).
•
Sporicidal Disinfection or Disinfection
•
Either between batches of a campaign or between batches
•
Open-door intervention when exposed to external background during batch operation such as an
open-door intervention. Each intervention should be individually assessed for its impact and
determine the full scope of disinfection needed after the intervention. At a minimum, the

of inversion).
•
The application of either sporicidal disinfection or disinfection must be defined in the CCS.

environment), they are required to be sterilized before installation. Additionally, RABS gloves are to be
sterilized or effectively biodecontaminated by a validated method prior to each manufacturing campaign
(1).
Rationale
To ensure product quality, gloves should be effectively sterilized before installation and biodecontaminated
during usage (e.g., prior to each manufacturing campaign). The effectiveness of this sterilization, bio-
decontamination, sporicidal disinfection, and disinfection regime should be verified through a qualified EM
program.

Topic 5. Environmental Monitoring
Q 5.1. What should the environmental monitoring (EM) frequency be in a closed or
open restricted access barrier system (cRABS or oRABS)?
Recommendation
The frequency of EM for oRABS and cRABS should be the same; continuous viable and total particle
monitoring is required for the full duration of critical activities, including setup (1). (See Question 5.5.)
Critical activities start at the point when a product-contact part and/or a primary container or closure are
transferred and are exposed to the inside of the RABS. Where the RABS is subdivided into discrete zones,
the point where critical activities start and end may well be different within the different zones; hence, the
continuous EM may start at different times in each zone (4).
Continuous viable (i.e., a combination of active and passive air monitoring) and total particle monitoring
should begin at the start of the aseptic setup and should continue up until the end of filling. Typically, this is
the end of the batch or campaign.
The RABS should be well designed to minimize the need for interventions involving opening the doors. By
exception and where required, the doors should only be opened under strictly defined conditions, which
should be procedurally defined and pre-assessed as part of the QRM. The conditions for the opening of the
doors and controls in place to mitigate risks should be considered and documented as part of the overall
CCS.
Appropriate controls should be in place to minimize the risk posed by the intervention through the opening
of the doors and should extend to consideration of further disinfection steps. Additionally, controls should
extend to the need to undertake a partial line clearance, for example, to discard exposed primary packaging
containers or other direct product-contact parts located close to the intervention (e.g., vials, syringes,
stoppers located on the stopper track). While sterility testing cannot be used to rationalize poor practices or
otherwise support the undertaking of a high-risk intervention, based on risk, additional sterility samples
should be considered as part of an additional control measure for critical interventions (4).
The same EM program applies to both product manufacture and APS.
Rationale
There is no difference in expectations around the frequency of EM inside oRABS and cRABS.
Once a critical surface is exposed inside the RABS, that surface is potentially at risk of contamination, so
continuous EM is required.

=== PDF PAGE 69 ===
The requirement for continuous EM ceases once the last unit is closed for the batch or campaign provided
any used product-contact parts are reprocessed, and any sterile components are cleared prior to reuse of the
line.
In the rare event that the RABS door is opened after line setup is completed, the critical zone is put at an
elevated risk of contamination from the person(s) intervening and from the surrounding cleanroom.
Additional controls should be considered to reduce risks of such interventions and should extend to
consideration of additional disinfection steps and partial line clearances.
Monitoring should not interfere with production processes in a way that raises the level of risk by
introducing an activity in the critical zone, which defeats the purpose of the EM.

Q 5.2. Which surfaces should be monitored in a restricted access barrier system (RABS)
(open or closed)?
Recommendation
Firms should conduct a risk assessment to define which surfaces of a RABS to monitor after use. Swabs
should be used for complex geometric surfaces; contact plates should be used for flat surfaces. Sampling is
only representative; not all surfaces can be sampled (e.g., not all filling needles and not all other surfaces
above the critical surfaces).
Typically, based on risk, firms will sample:
•
Direct- and indirect-contact parts
•
Sterile tools that contact or breach First Air over contact parts or sterile primary packaging during
the batch/campaign
•
RABS intervention gloves used during the batch/campaign for critical interventions*
•
RTP doors, if used, where the door breaches First Air over a critical surface
•
Parts above the critical surfaces that cannot be sterilized
*Note: When RABS intervention gloves are used ambidextrously, considerations should be given to EM
sampling on multiple sides of gloves. This should be assessed using QRM principles.
Other locations linked to the risk assessment may also be sampled (e.g., occluded/hard to reach surfaces).
Surface samples are taken at the end of use (i.e., the batch or campaign). The surfaces sampled should be
returned to a state suitable for aseptic manufacturing before the parts are reused. Typically, sterilized
surfaces will be removed, subjected to a qualified cleaning process, and then resterilized. Other surfaces,
including barrier gloves, should be cleaned and returned to a state suitable for aseptic manufacture.
Rationale
Surface monitoring is typically aimed at surfaces that have the greatest potential impact on product sterility
because they directly or indirectly contact the product, or they are permitted to breach First Air (e.g.,
forceps).
A risk assessment is required to select and scientifically justify both the surfaces to be sampled and locations
on those surfaces that are most representative of the risk. Sampling the entire product-contact surface is not
necessary; instead, risk-based sampling should occur on the parts most likely to have been put at risk during
installation or use.
RABS gloves are used for interventions; therefore, they are required to be sterile before installation and bio-
decontaminated in situ thereafter. During use, RABS gloves are likely to contact surfaces inside the RABS
that have been disinfected, not sterilized. Where open-door interventions are permitted, a barrier glove may
well reside in a lower grade for the duration of the intervention and then be disinfected as the door is shut.
proximity with or breach First Air over sterile primary packaging, open product, or product-contact parts
potentially puts the product at risk and should be sampled; such events should be avoided whenever
possible.
Tools used for critical interventions should be sampled. Consideration should be given to the use of
dedicated sterile tools that are used exclusively for conducting certain types of interventions (e.g., involving

=== PDF PAGE 71 ===
direct or indirect product-contact parts). Sterile tools should be kept in Grade A condition and maintained in
a manner that prevents contamination (i.e., proper aseptic technique).
RTP doors are not sterile; they are bio-decontaminated inside and out in situ.
Surface samples are taken at the end of use because the surfaces being sampled need to be maintained both
sterile and clean until the end of use; the samples allow this to be assessed as part of batch release. Care
must be taken to ensure that the act of sampling does not put the surfaces being sampled at risk of
contamination due to either improper sampling techniques, or the use of wrong or incorrectly prepared
sampling equipment or consumables.

Recommendation

RABS.

•
Plates sterilized in beta bags or containers that are transferred via alpha RTP ports in the RABS.
•
Plates sterilized in impermeable bags that enter and exit the RABS via an integral bio-

•

•

Q 5.5. When and how should environmental and personnel monitoring be performed
during setup and open-door interventions?
Recommendation
All open-door interventions need to be monitored from the point where the first critical surface enters the
RABS. The minimum level of monitoring inside a RABS during critical activities is continuous viable and
total particle monitoring; continuous viable monitoring is achieved by passive-air and active-air
monitoring.
For open-door activities, including line set-up, monitoring within the intervention zone is recommended.
This monitoring needs to be based on QRM principles and may need to be changed to ensure monitoring
covers the performed activities. Sampling locations, as defined in the EM risk assessment, should be linked
to qualified and unqualified interventions.
Additional samples might be taken, based on QRM principles, in the UDAF immediately outside the
intervention zone, close to the operator and open-door. This can be achieved with either passive or active
monitoring; samples should be taken at the working position level.
Each primary operator involved in open-door interventions, including line setup, should be monitored
immediately as they exit the RABS Grade A interior, after having completed all the intervention steps in the
critical zone, including any disinfection that may be required. The RABS door disinfection and closure
should be done by the secondary operator.
Representative samples should be taken as defined in the risk assessment of the EM program. Sample
locations must be selected as per the risk assessment. The risk assessment should consider any body part
entering the Grade A environment and any location selected through the risk assessment, and these must
meet Grade A limits (e.g., taking samples from the operator’s RABS gloves, sleeves, head, and chest, as
appropriate to the specific intervention).
It is recommended that a second, fully trained operator should support the monitoring to prevent false
positive growth. This sampling should be done in an environment that prevents contamination of the sample
(e.g., the UDAF zone of protection or another UDAF zone close to the RABS).
To be representative, samples should be taken as close as possible (i.e., physical and temporal) to the end of
the operators’ activities inside the RABS. Gowns should be removed and replaced following sampling. Care
must be taken to ensure that agar is not transferred to the RABS or surrounding cleanroom where the
sampling takes place, during the degowning and gown disposal step.
The individual whose clothing was sampled will then need to leave the cleanroom and replace some or all of
their aseptic clothing (i.e., RABS gloves and gown). The second person should also open any doors to allow
the person sampled to pass without transferring agar onto cleanroom surfaces.
Rationale
Entry of personnel into the RABS reduces the effectiveness of the barrier and potentially puts the product at
risk of contamination. Performing personnel monitoring after, and active air sampling during, the open-door
activities provides valuable additional data to support batch disposition.
An approved EM risk assessment should be used to determine the representative locations that require
sampling meeting Grade A limits (e.g., body parts entering the Grade A environment). Relying only on

=== PDF PAGE 77 ===
glove-sampling data may not be adequate, especially where RABS gloves are disinfected or changed
immediately prior to the intervention.
When sampling occurs close to the point where the person exits the RABS, the data collected will most
closely reflect the risk posed by the operator to the RABS and will be less influenced by the surrounding
lower-grade cleanroom and surfaces outside the RABS that are touched up to the point where the samples
are taken. Ideally, a second person would sample the person exiting the RABS at the point they exit, this
minimizes any external influences on the data (e.g. handling of plates, pens, documentation in grade B) and
ensures that the samples fully represent the risk posed by the first operator to the critical zone and have not
been unnecessarily compromised.
Note: Operators should leave the RABS and change gloves to prevent the spread of media residues after
finger dabs.

Q 5.6. Should environmental monitoring (EM) for line setup and filling be kept
separate?
Recommendation
Yes. While not mandated, there are benefits to keeping EM for line setup and filling separate. Keeping the
data from open-door line setup separate aids the investigation and process improvement in the event of EM
recoveries (1, 7).
Rationale
Line setup activities usually pose a higher risk to the critical zone because they typically include frequent
open-door interventions, material movements into the RABS, and the presence of personnel in the critical
zone, whereas filling activities are typically conducted via closed-door interventions. Segregating the two
activities for EM allows the data from line setup and filling activities to be visualized and trended
independently. Where EM recoveries occur, identifying the assignable root cause of the microbiological
data deviation is aided when the data for the two activities are separate.

Q 5.7. Where should the fixed environmental monitoring (EM) air-monitoring sampling
points be located within the restricted access barrier system (RABS)?
Recommendation
Firms should conduct a risk assessment in the design phase to determine the number and locations of EM
samples that should be taken. (See PDA Technical Report No. 13 (Revised 2014): Fundamentals of an
Environmental Monitoring Program.)
The sampling locations should be positioned as closely as possible to the most critical operations, such as
the point of fill, sorting bowl, or rotary-table. They should be positioned vertically at or close to the height
of the critical surface they are monitoring and ideally in the same plane.
Note: FDA’s 2004 Guidance for Industry: Sterile Drug Products Produced by Aseptic Processing—Current
Good Manufacturing Practice states:
Air in the immediate proximity of exposed sterilized containers/closures and filling/closing
operations would be of appropriate particle quality when it has a per-cubic-meter particle
count of no more than 3520 in a size range of 0.5 μm and larger when counted at
representative locations normally not more than 1 foot away from the work site, within the
airflow, and during filling/closing operations. This level of air cleanliness is also known as
Class 100 (ISO 5).
Sampling locations should not breach First Air over critical surfaces. They should be positioned so that they
capture the risks from any permitted intervention, for example, between the RABS door or glove and the
critical surface being monitored, and between the door and critical surfaces where open-door interventions
are permitted.
Viable- and total-particle sampling locations should be co-located wherever possible (if supported by risk-
assessment) both to allow a point of comparison and for ergonomic reasons. Sampling locations should not
cause an interference on the airflow between the sampling points (i.e., ideally isokinetic sampling for viable
monitoring). AVS should demonstrate the noninterference during the sampling (e.g., passages of RABS
gloves during the plate changing).
Total-particle sampling positions need to be set to allow a tube no longer than one meter (1 m) to connect
the isokinetic sampling-head to the particle counter. Where lengths greater than 1 m must be used,* studies
are required to assess the particle loss, in particular, the 5.0 µm particle loss, and determine if improvements
are required. Ideally, the connecting tube would run straight. Any necessary bends should have smooth bend
radii, where a 90° bend is achieved over no less than 15 cm of tube.
*Note: RABS should be designed/installed to avoid use exceeding 1 m.
Rationale
It is not possible to monitor the entire RABS. A risk assessment is required to position EM samples in
locations where they are most likely to detect the risks posed from the main contamination risks, that is,
conducting both open- and closed-door interventions.
Each unique particle size will have a different deposition profile in tubing based on, for example, airflow
velocity and sample volume, tubing diameter, sampling port arrangement, temperature, bend radius, tube
length, and tubing material. Because particulate monitoring attempts to assess a range of sizes (e.g., 0.5 µm

=== PDF PAGE 80 ===
and greater in optical diameter), no single set of radii or length conditions can describe best practices for
each of these particle sizes. Minimizing the number of tubing bends, maximizing radii, and minimizing
overall tubing length should be the goal, and it should be done in accordance with the particle-counter
equipment manufacturer’s recommended best practices.
The particle-counter equipment manufacturer should provide technical justification for its recommendations.
Instrument calibration should address sampling configuration.
Note:
•
EU Annex 1 now states that tube length should typically be no longer than 1 m and any bend radii
should be avoided.
•
International Organization for Standardization (ISO) 14644-1:2015 Cleanrooms and Associated
Controlled Environments — Part 1: Classification of Air Cleanliness by Particle Concentration also
states that tube length should be no longer than 1 m.

Topic 6. Material Transport and Loading
Q 6.1. How should indirect product contact surfaces be sterilized or decontaminated?
Recommendation
Indirect product-contact parts such as stopper bowls, hoppers, ancillary items (e.g., tweezers, forceps), and
guides must be sterilized (1). This is typically done by wrapping the clean items in suitable semi-permeable
packaging material and sterilizing them in a moist-heat autoclave, followed by unloading, transportation
through the cleanroom environment, and aseptic assembly inside the RABS. Machine items that break First
Air (e.g., sensors, holders, brackets at the filling line or rotary table) should be avoided where possible.
When machine items do break First Air, they should be sterilized. (See Question 6.5 for more details.)
Based on facility layout and whether Grade A continuity can be applied for the sterilized items (e.g., by
using UDAF carts), either single-wrapping or double-wrapping can be applied to ensure transport of the
autoclaved, sterilized parts from the Grade B autoclave to the entrance of the RABS. To provide for aseptic
removal and transfer of the parts into the RABS, good aseptic technique must be used to ensure that the
inner layer or part is only exposed within the Grade A environment. The wrapping materials and removal
process should minimize particle generation as much as possible.
When using double wrapping, the outer wrapping is ideally removed at the barrier door, where the contents
are still protected by the inner barrier. For large items, such as the stopper bowl, two people may be needed
to properly complete the aseptic transfer. That is, the primary and secondary operators are responsible for
different steps: typically, one for the transfer activities outside the RABS, and one for the transfer activities
inside the RABS. The inner wrapping is then removed toward the end of the assembly process under Grade
A conditions, depending on the design of the manufacturing line just before or after the RABS doors are
closed. In case parts of the assembly are done as a closed-door intervention, the wrapping could be removed
at an earlier stage to enable removal of the wrap from the RABS just before door closure or as a final open-
door intervention. Special considerations must be taken in the sequence of removing the wrapping so as to
ensure the First Air of already unwrapped parts is not breached during the removal of other wrappers.
Automated surface bio-decontamination processes such as VPHP, are not considered equivalent to
sterilization processes, however, they can reduce bioburden risk if applied after the set-up process is
complete and the RABS doors are closed.
If items can be directly unloaded into the cRABS (e.g., via RTP) or an aseptic transfer from the autoclave to
the RABS is otherwise guaranteed (e.g., via Grade A continuity, typically a process requiring rigorous
controls), different means of wrapping and/or dry-heat sterilization of the indirect product-contact items may
be applied. The wrapping materials and removal process should minimize particle generation as much as
possible.

In addition to open-door installation, an introduction of indirect product-contact parts via RTP or sterile
boxes can be considered for the RABS to minimize the risk of potential contamination during installation by
open doors. This especially applies for smaller parts such as guiding and stopper guardrails, for example.
Rationale
Indirect product-contact surfaces are critical for the sterility assurance of the product and must be sterilized
(1).

Q 6.5. How should equipment, accessories, ancillary items and sterile tools (e.g.,
forceps) be transferred into a restricted access barrier system (RABS)?
Recommendation
As a general concept, the transfer of all materials and equipment into the Grade B and, finally, the Grade A
RABS should be a unidirectional process, separated from the path for removal of those items. Material and
equipment introduced, whenever possible, should be wrapped and sterilized, when necessary.
The principles discussed below are based on the criticality of the item regarding product contact.
Direct Product Contact Parts
Direct product-contact parts (e.g., vials, stoppers, piping, single-use systems (SUSs), connectors or
components), which have come into direct contact with the product, must be sterilized (1, 4). This is
founded on two basic principles:
•
If the filling equipment has CIP and SIP functionality, then the parts can either remain in the RABS
or parts of it can be assembled, followed by a validated SIP process, rendering the equipment and
filter (where applicable) sterile. (See PDA Technical Report No. 61: Steam in Place for additional
information.)
•
If the filling equipment needs to be manually assembled and has no SIP functionality, either
presterilized (e.g., gamma-irradiated) single-use assemblies are used, or alternatively, if the filling
equipment is sterilized by wrapping the clean items in suitable semipermeable packaging material
and sterilized in a moist-heat autoclave, followed by aseptic assembly inside the RABS. If items can
be directly unloaded into the cRABS (e.g., via RTP) or an aseptic transfer from the autoclave to the
RABS is guaranteed (e.g., via Grade A continuity, typically a process requiring rigorous controls),
different means of wrapping and/or dry-heat sterilization of the indirect product-contact items may
be applied. Where presterilized, the equipment should be fully assembled (as far as possible) to
minimize the need for aseptic connections.
Indirect Product Contact Parts
Indirect product-contact parts (e.g., stopper bowls, hoppers, tweezers), which have come into indirect
contact with the product , are to be sterilized and handled in a similar way to direct product-contact parts (1).
(See Question 6.1 for more detail.)
Machine Items that Break First Air
Machine items that break First Air (e.g., sensors, holders, brackets at the filling line or rotary table) should
be avoided where possible. This must be part of the initial design considerations for a RABS. Where a First
Air interruption is not avoidable, machine items should be moist-heat sterilizable. Where this is not
technically possible, sporicidal disinfection and a risk assessment of each individual part should be carried
out and included in the CCS (1). These parts should have both the highest-possible level of cleanability
(preferably with sporicidal agents) and the lowest-possible air interruption (e.g., fewer gaps/holes, be as
small as possible, ergonomically shaped). Inclusion in the EM program (e.g., surface sampling at the end of
manufacture) needs to be considered.
Miscellaneous Items that Break First Air
Miscellaneous items that potentially break First Air (e.g., forceps for removing stuck stoppers) must be
sterile and remain sterile during their intended use inside the RABS. Those items preferably can be sterilized
by moist heat and wrapped in semi-permeable packaging, or by dry heat in stainless-steel boxes (care should
be applied on appropriate closure of those boxes). Each individual unit should not contain more items than

=== PDF PAGE 90 ===
are reasonably needed for the completion of a batch or campaign. To avoid contamination of the sterile
items during their intended usage, suitable holders should be designed inside the Grade A space to avoid
reaching over the critical parts of the item with the RABS gloves.
Transfer of Items
Methods to transfer wrapped packages into a RABS depends on whether or not the RABS has automated
surface-decontamination functionality (e.g., VPHP). If this is the case, the outside surface of packages could
be surface-disinfected by the automated process after a transfer through a cleanroom area. If surface-
disinfection is purely manual, either an outer-pouch should be removed if the items were sterilized double-
wrapped (preferred) or a manual disinfection of the package/wrapping needs to take place when the items
enter the Grade A RABS.
Non-Product Contact Equipment and Parts
Non-product-contact equipment/parts (e.g., format parts such as vial guides) are not always suitable for
moist- or dry-heat sterilization. Those are parts formally not required to be sterile but must meet Grade A
conditions regarding surface cleanliness when inserted into the RABS. Those parts can either be disinfected
manually or with the help of automated surface-disinfection processes (e.g., VPHP or similar). When
removing the parts from the RABS (if stored outside the RABS without cleaning (i.e., for product dedicated
parts)) and before re-usage, the parts should be protected (e.g., by adding a protective pouch) while still
within Grade A. If parts are cleaned, they should be protected during storage and before re-usage.
Removal of Items from RABS
Once RABS doors are closed, the removal process of any items from the RABS (e.g., exposed plates or
package/wrapping) must not impact the unidirectional airflow protecting direct and indirect product-contact
surfaces. In addition, the removal should follow a unidirectional process where the flow of material into the
RABS is separated from the flow of items to be removed. For best practice in cRABS, RTPs placed at
different and suitable locations may be used to facilitate the transfer of material. Processing and waste-
removal flows should be considered during the equipment design and procurement phase, the placement of
access ports should be recorded in the equipment User Requirements Specification, and the rationale should
be documented in the Design Qualification and/or CCS.
Rationale
Transfer techniques into and out of the RABS are based on the type of material/accessory/equipment, its
cleanability and disposal. Either sterilization or surface decontamination is needed.

Q 7.1. What are the considerations for planning, designing, and executing a restricted
access barrier system (RABS) cleaning and disinfection program?
Recommendation
Before implementing a cleaning and disinfection program, a RABS cleanability assessment should be
performed. This assessment should include the suitability of material surfaces to be cleaned as described in
Question 1.2. In addition, cleaning accessibility in terms of ergonomics, the extent of reach required into the
RABS, and areas that need cleaning tools to reach should be considered. Hard to clean mechanical spaces
and surfaces in processing areas should have been eliminated by design as a basic GMP requirement (4). As
disinfection programs must include the use of an approved sporicidal, the materials of construction within

sporicidal disinfection must be defined in the CCS.

suitability and access of process equipment surfaces for cleaning and disinfection procedures.
Cleaning is a prerequisite to effective bio-decontamination processes. Initially, at start-up and after

Manual Cleaning and Disinfections of RABS
RABS require cleaning and disinfection to establish and maintain Grade A conditions. Manual processes for
these steps are described below.
Cleaning is the removal of soiling, residual product, and nonviable contaminants. The process is typically
manual, completed with wipes that apply cleaning agents and a physical removal (i.e., collection in a wipe)
of contaminants (1, 6).
Disinfection is a process of applying, via a qualified procedure, a laboratory-validated disinfectant that
provides an appropriate log reduction of bioburden to specified levels. All disinfection programs for RABS
should include a sporicidal agent (1, 6). Appropriate residue removal is required after cleaning using
purified/sterile water (e.g., WFI) or sterile isopropanol.

1.
Establishing control of the Grade A processing environment on first installation, after shutdown,

2.
Routine disinfection in production, which includes after open-barrier interventions and wiping

assessment and assurance that Grade A conditions are not compromised.

door disinfection before closing the RABS barrier.

Q 7.3. What considerations are required for qualification of the restricted access
barrier system (RABS) cleaning, bio-decontamination, disinfection, and sporicidal
disinfection process, and validation of the bio-decontamination and decontamination
process?
Recommendation
The qualification of the RABS cleaning and disinfection process is critical to ensuring a Grade A
environment within the RABS. The planning of the qualification should begin as early as possible prior to
implementation. A key output of the design phase of a RABS installation is a quality risk assessment
detailing the RABS physical attributes and usage scenarios that may have an impact on product quality. This
risk assessment then feeds into a validation plan of which cleaning and disinfection validation is a major
component.
There are different facets to the RABS cleaning and disinfection (bio-decontamination) and
decontamination validation process:
•
Establishing Grade A conditions for aseptic manufacturing
•
Applying cleaning validation for drug-product residue removal as a cross-contamination control
measure
Decontamination may be applied as a cross-contamination control measure and/or a measure to manage
operator exposure to toxic or biologically hazardous products. Where aseptic-containment is required to
process toxic or biohazard products, it may be more appropriate to apply isolator barrier technology because
of the inherent limitations of containment measures (e.g., air-overspill to the surrounding environment) in
RABS.
RABS Cleaning Process
The qualification of the RABS cleaning process should provide confidence of the:
•
Removal of any residue (chemical, including lubricants) or debris that would detrimentally impact
the effectiveness of the disinfecting agent to be used. The target should be visibly clean (1).
•
Reduction of bioburden before the disinfection process step, as determined by the end-user.
RABS Disinfection Process
The qualification and validation of the RABS disinfection process should provide confidence that the
disinfection procedure demonstrates its effectiveness in reducing any microbiological contamination within
the RABS to an acceptable level. The disinfection process should include:
1.
Use of a sporicidal agent, possibly in rotation with other disinfectant agents that together provide
broad spectrum efficacy (7). The selection and frequency of use should be based on EM data
(quantitative/qualitive), trends, cleanroom materials, and the efficacy of the disinfectant procedures
(2).
Note: Rotation of the disinfectants is not considered due to increasing microbial resistance but
because different disinfectants have different modes of action, and their combination can increase
the spectrum of efficacy. Rotation may also reduce occurrence of material damage.
2.
Use of sterile disinfectant agents for Grade A and Grade B areas.
3.
A target log reduction for bacteria, spores, and viruses (if applicable) (6, 20).

=== PDF PAGE 97 ===
4.
A method of application that assures surface coverage and wet contact time.
5.
A disinfection waste management procedure dealing with wipes, residual agents, and disinfection
tools.
6.
Any associated aseptic RABS hold-time qualification.
7.
Environmental control/EMPQ should be qualified and established before implementation of an
APS; confidence that the cleaning and disinfection process meets this objective of environmental
control is critical to GMP compliance (4).
RABS Decontamination Process
The qualification and validation of a RABS decontamination process applied post-production for product-

following are points to consider:
•
Cleaning methods may be manual (wiping) or more automated CIP systems (e.g., spray balls, spray
wands). In both cases, the target is to remove residual products from surfaces.
•
Cleaning validation includes surface-swabbing studies and chemical analysis to confirm removal of
product residuals to target levels considered as safe exposure to operators and/or not at risk of cross-
contaminating other products or subsequent batches.
•
Where more automated CIP systems apply, control system development will be required with
subsequent qualification.
WFI may be used as an agent for product-residual removal for treatment off-line:
•
Chemical agents that denature products in RABS may need a follow-up residue removal step.
•
If CIP systems are applied, sloping surfaces and “zero” dead-leg valves may be required to mitigate

Note: Disinfectant efficacy studies or validation studies should be conducted as well to demonstrate that the

Rationale
The primary purpose of a RABS is to provide people-operator and process-product-barrier separation so that
the risk of contaminating sterile product is mitigated to acceptable levels. As such, it is important to
determine the most appropriate bio-decontamination method for the RABS. The qualification and validation
of the bio-decontamination methods should consider the cleaning agents, the cleaning application processes,
and the associated processes and equipment risks. These requirements and processes should be documented
and proceduralized for repeatability and monitored to ensure continuous compliance.
Strong process understanding is the best basis for determining RABS validation requirements. Robust
monitoring and aseptic manufacturing processes will add to the confidence that the manufacturing process is
under a state of control. Decisions should be made based on the facilities process, control structure, and
process understanding. Based on those decisions, validation programs should be initiated and followed.
Decisions on the frequency of revalidation should be included in the bio-decontamination validation
program.

Topic 8. Aseptic Process Simulation
Q 8.1. What are the special considerations for aseptic process simulation (APS) in a
restricted access barrier system (RABS)?
Recommendation
A risk-based analysis and approach taken for APS are recommended for all aseptic processing technologies,
including RABS systems. By design, the APS should imitate routine manufacturing conditions and should
ensure that all critical steps are challenged. A RABS relies on the surrounding cleanroom environment to
maintain Grade A conditions for sterile processing. To maintain Grade A, the RABS routinely includes
cleaning and disinfection processes, in addition to sterilization processes for processing accessories and
equipment. By design, the RABS should only provide for open-door intervention during setup and, by
exception, during operations. The cleanroom qualification, operator training, cleaning, and disinfection
programs should all be qualified.
There are several key considerations that need to be established in support of APS, and they should include,
but are not limited to:
•
Cleanroom and clean air equipment, such as UDAF units, must be qualified according to the
required characteristics of the environment.
•
The RABS must be qualified to meet all requirements for aseptic processing including HEPA-filter
integrity-testing, pressure differential across the filters, air velocity testing, and AVS. AVS should
confirm unidirectional airflow that sweeps over and away from exposed products during processing,
without increased risk of contamination through turbulent airflow. Airflow visualization
qualification and monitoring should be applied at open-door interventions as a prerequisite to all
APS designs to ensure environmental-control conditions are qualified in readiness for an APS.
Note: APS cannot be used to justify poor design of equipment and processes.
•
Operator training and qualification extending to Grade A and Grade B aseptic gowning and aseptic
behavior training must be complete. Operators undertaking RABS cleaning and disinfection
programs should be qualified and operations should be implemented procedurally.
•
A list of authorized inherent and corrective interventions should be designed, defined, and
appropriately assessed and documented in the CCS to minimize the risk of contamination. Open-
door interventions should be a rare and exceptional occurrence. When required, the need to take
representative sterility samples should be considered, and the intervention should be effectively
demonstrated in an APS.
•
Qualified methods are in place for the transfer of materials that enter the cleanroom, and
subsequently the RABS, that do not compromise the cleanroom-surrounding environment or the
RABS Grade A processing zone.

=== PDF PAGE 99 ===
•
Risk assessments support the establishment of a comprehensive EM program, which should be
based on detailed knowledge of the process, facility, and equipment. Sampling locations, sampling
frequency and type of EM monitoring (e.g. active air, passive, contact plate) should be defined
based on risk assessment output.
The use of a “piggyback” APS can be considered as part of the overall APS design. A piggyback APS is
where an APS run is undertaken at the end of a routine single or campaign filling process and can be used in
support of validating an extended run or campaign length/duration. There are specific limitations associated
with a piggyback APS which, by design, does not provide for an appropriate simulation of activities and
variables that are only performed for the first lot of a campaign, for example, setup activities. Consideration
also needs to be given to potential interferences of residual product with the growth-promoting properties of
the media used in the APS. Consequently, a piggyback APS alone will not fulfill the validation requirements
to demonstrate an effective APS program. Rather, its use can be considered as part of an overall effective
APS program, which also must include an APS strategy that covers setup and other potential worst-case
scenarios.
The scheduling of piggyback APS in conjunction with traditional APS should be supported by a formal risk
assessment, for which the limitations of the piggyback approach and its use are considered.
Rationale
The planning of open- and closed-door interventions and simulation of worst-case criteria should be based
on risk management principles. The same criteria for the design of an APS as stated in PDA Technical
Report No. 22 (Revised 2011): Process Simulation for Aseptically Filled Products are also appropriate for
all RABS.
After establishing Grade A conditions inside a RABS, the barrier technology provides a physical and
aerodynamic barrier to contamination ingress to Grade A when the barrier is closed. The barrier is open at
setup where presterilized materials are transferred and are installed. At this time, both Grade A conditions
and the sterilized parts are at an increased risk for contamination and, thus, can compromise sterility.
If rare open-door interventions take place during aseptic manufacturing, there is also a risk escalation that
needs mitigation by design, qualifications, and procedural controls, and is subject to additional qualification
in an APS. Such in-process significant interventions require consideration for sterility samples to be taken
and should be included in the APS.
Simulation of the production activities is a necessity for designing the APS studies.

Q 8.2. What are the special considerations of the aseptic process simulation (APS) for
multiple shifts and multiple-day campaign production in the restricted access barrier
system (RABS)?
Recommendation
The duration of the APS should be based on risk assessment and sufficient to adequately challenge the
aseptic process. The duration should be long enough to allow simulation of the predetermined interventions
and activities and to fill the required units.
Where there are no risk-based time-related effects and where longer duration does not pose a risk to the
routine production process, there is no requirement that the APS should be equal to or longer than the
maximum production duration.
The following points need to be considered when creating an APS for campaign manufacturing:
•
Setup
•
Number and duration of interventions based on risk management principles to ensure that the APS
is representative of the routine manufacturing process and qualified interventions (type, frequency,
and duration)
•
Indirect product-contact parts remain installed for the duration for requalification of the campaign
and are subject to an aseptic hold time (the duration may need to be shortened based on the risk
assessment evaluation)
•
Replacement or leaving in place of direct product-contact parts (i.e., product filling needles), should
be representative of the routine manufacturing process exposure time
•
RABS gloves remain installed for the duration of the campaign
•
Cleaning/disinfection practices occurring inside the RABS during routine campaign manufacturing
should be simulated
•
Certain circumstances when performing an APS related to a production hold and/or interruptions
•
Taking of sterility samples after significant critical interventions, if justified
The approach to APS with a “piggyback” qualification is not considered a best practice as setup is missing
in the final APS and not representative of the full campaign.
Rationale
Contamination of an aseptic process is primarily an outcome of events rather than time. Therefore, the
duration of the APS should be sufficient to assess the performance of those activities identified in the risk
assessment as having the potential to introduce contamination.
The APS is not sensitive enough to uncover the flaws, weaknesses, or failures in the integrity of the RABS,
the environmental quality, or the ability of the personnel to perform activities over time.
The APS should challenge and confirm the effectiveness of these contamination control measures. This
means that the maintenance of environmental conditions should be assured and assessed by the RABS
system design and EM. Good process design and well-controlled activities required between the batches
within a campaign (e.g., fill needles or glove replacement), RABS gloves ergonomics, training and
qualification of personnel, assessment of human factors, and scheduling of breaks and rest periods for
operators are better tools for controlling the complexity of RABS campaign operations than extended APS
runs.

Open-door interventions (if required) in addition to the setup

Q 8.4. What is the best practice when performing the setup and assembly of a
restricted access barrier system (RABS) filling line?
Recommendation
There are two setup conditions for RABS: Initially, with an open barrier door, and then, readiness for
production operations with the barrier door closed where full EM is implemented and critical tasks
involving sterilized parts are completed, for example, positioning of filling needles (if single-use). As much
as possible, set-up should be performed with the doors closed so careful design and understanding of the
equipment is essential.
The open-door set-up process presents the highest risk of contamination when transferring in or assembling
sterilized parts or components.
Based on risk knowledge and the role First Air takes in preventing contamination ingress into the Grade A
zone, operators need to position themselves so protective airflow passes over body parts inserted into the
RABS (such as hands and sleeves) and into the surrounding cleanroom. Positioning of materials ready for
transfer should also consider protective airflow exiting the RABS Grade A zone and from the overhead or
over-open-door UDAF. AVS should demonstrate the appropriate airflow from the interior of the RABS to
the surrounding area.
Materials and parts should be presented for transfer at working position, typically on carts.
A secondary operator could support positioning parts and materials provided they do not compromise the
protective airflow.
Good aseptic technique (e.g., transfer of inner bag at the RABS interface by double-bag methods) is
required on open-barrier-door entry, and the operator’s reach should be limited to the required task without
touching other surfaces, critical or otherwise. Practices of hand disinfection prior to open-door interventions
should be based on the contact time recommended and qualified practices. (See PDA TR-62 for additional
information.)
During the intervention, EM sampling locations in the proximity of the intervention should be subject to
continuous EM (i.e., viable and nonviable). The surrounding cleanroom should also be monitored at the
door opening/operator interface. (See Topic 5.) Installation of sterilized RABS gloves after open-door setup
is commonly done as the final procedure. (See Topic 9.)
Before and at the time barrier doors are closed, the inside of the barrier door should be disinfected. The
RABS gloves should be drawn back so only the RABS glove port entry interface should require disinfection
(in some cases, covers may be inserted to prevent RABS glove contamination at open-door interventions).
With the barrier door closed, full EM should be implemented and should be ready for monitoring
throughout the production-readiness setup.
Installation setup of all critical parts requires good aseptic technique, possibly with the use of tools (e.g.,
tweezers, tongs) and throughout the procedure. First Air protection should not be compromised.
Rationale
As interventions are such a high-risk activity, best practice is needed in all cases to mitigate risks to an
acceptable level.

Q 9.2. How should interventions be qualified and approved for use in routine
production?
Recommendation
All interventions should be included in a documented risk assessment associated with a comprehensive
CCS. This documented risk assessment should consider the following risk factors:
•
Duration of the intervention (in minutes)
•
Complexity of the intervention (number of steps)
•
Proximity of the intervention to sterile components (equipment, product)
•
Exposure of personnel
Additional factors may be considered when assessing the risk associated with execution of an intervention.
The assessment must consider the use of sterile equipment and tools (e.g., forceps) to minimize impact on
the RABS environment when performing an intervention.
All qualified interventions should have the airflow visualization recorded and documented to ensure that the
impact of performing the interventions are thoroughly understood. This includes inherent and corrective
interventions that can be performed through RABS gloves and open-door activities. First Air principles shall
be included in this airflow visualization and must not be ignored during execution of interventions. Based on
mechanical or physical limitations, if proper First Air principles cannot be followed, it must be documented
within the risk assessment, and proper mitigations (e.g., material discard) must be established. It is
recommended that, as much as possible, mitigations be automated, ensuring proper execution each time the
intervention is performed. The inclusion of qualified interventions to be performed during routine
production should be included in the firm’s APS program.
Rationale
Following the process recommended above would ensure the inclusion of QRM principles through a
documented risk assessment to be included in a comprehensive CCS. The proper design, followed by risk
analysis and airflow visualization, will provide the appropriate understanding of the intervention to approve
its use, during routine production, and for execution in a firm’s APS program. The execution of
interventions, to be performed during routine production, are to be included in a firm’s APS program.

Q 9.3. Should interventions that disrupt First Air, as defined in EU Annex 1, be
permitted?
Recommendation
The placement of RABS glove positions and the aseptic operation should be designed to avoid the
disruption of unidirectional airflow above or in the proximity of exposed sterile product or critical surfaces.
Interventions should be designed in a manner that prevents the disruption of First Air to critical surfaces or
exposed product. If the intervention is occurring above a critical surface, sterile tools and/or equipment must
be utilized for execution of the intervention.
All interventions should be designed following appropriate aseptic techniques as recommended in EU
Annex I: Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal Products, FDA Guidance for Industry: Sterile Drug Products
Produced by Aseptic Processing-Current Good Manufacturing Practice, or PDA PTC-1. Intervention
design must consider equipment limitations and the placement of required equipment (e.g., EM) within the
RABS. The use of sterile equipment and tools (e.g., forceps) must be well thought out to not add further
risks to intervention execution.
Rationale
The process described above would ensure proper design of aseptic interventions and inclusion in the QRM
program through a documented risk assessment to be included in a comprehensive CCS. All interventions
shall be designed to meet First Air principles and include proper mitigation if those principles are impacted
due to mechanical or physical limitations. The proper design followed by risk analysis and airflow
visualization will provide the appropriate understanding of the intervention to approve for use, during
routine production, and for execution in a firm’s APS program.

=== PDF PAGE 106 ===
Q 9.4. Should contact between restricted access barrier system (RABS) gloves and
critical surfaces during setup or operations be permitted?
Recommendation
RABS gloves should not be permitted to contact critical surfaces during setup or during operation.
Portions of equipment would only be considered as a “critical surface” when coming into direct contact with
product or components. The handle and exterior surface of the stopper bowl are not “critical surfaces” and
may be contacted by RABS or operator gloves during setup.
Aseptic technique should be applied throughout the manufacturing process by trained and qualified
operators. (See Question 9.7 for more information on glove installation.) Written procedures are to be
established to avoid the risks of RABS gloves contacting direct or indirect surfaces and, if contact occurs,
what action(s) are to be taken.
Rationale
While the CCS for RABS gloves should maintain proper aseptic conditions throughout the campaign, there
is an inherent risk to the product when touching critical surfaces, as the environment and glove materials are
not sterile. Proper controls (e.g., process flow, equipment design) and use of sterile tools should be in place
to mitigate the need to touch critical surfaces.

=== PDF PAGE 107 ===
Q 9.5. What should be performed after open-door interventions?
Recommendation
RABS should be designed and operated with doors closed during aseptic processing; however, the process
specificities may require identification, qualification, and approval of interventions performed with the
doors open. Interventions performed with open RABS doors (e.g., manual setup) are to be minimized, and
mitigation controls must be identified and implemented.
When a RABS door must be opened, those interventions need to be assessed for risk of contamination
ingress. These interventions must be part of the approved list and must incorporate additional control
measures based on the risk of microbiological contamination that the intervention may produce.
Such additional control measures may include, but are not limited to:
•
Additional disinfection and/or sporicidal disinfection of nonproduct-contact surfaces, including the
interior of doors and glove ports exposed or touched during the activity.
Note: The firm is responsible for documenting and defining a validated agent as part of its CCS.
•
Identification of the appropriate units that are to be manually removed (i.e., discarded) as part of the
intervention.
Note: The firm is responsible for validating all automatic unit removal performed by the filling
machine.
•
Collection of sterility samples may be taken, if warranted in the CCS
•
Door access with recorded intervention alarms and an effective means of documenting the
following:
•
Start and end time of intervention
•
Description of the qualified intervention
•
Participants
•
Documentation of units removed (i.e., discarded)
•
Documentation of required EM
Note: The firm is responsible for determining the appropriate risk-based monitoring related to the
open-door intervention.
•
Monitoring of operator gloves and additional risk-based locations (e.g., forearms, chest, head, etc.),
immediately after completion of the open-door intervention. (See Topic 2 for additional EM
recommendations.)
Rationale
Personnel are a significant source of microbiological contamination and, therefore, pose a risk to product
sterility. Efforts to separate personnel from sterile products and product-contact surfaces reduce the risk of
microbiological contamination. Barrier systems, such as RABS, provide a physical separation between
personnel and the external environment and exposed product or product-contact surfaces, which helps
prevent microbiological contamination of the sterile product by personnel and the environment.
In the case of open-door interventions, there is no longer a physical separation; therefore, additional control
measures are required, as described in this section.

Q 9.6. How should interventions be performed?
Recommendation
Prior to performing an intervention, all product and empty containers are to be cleared, as applicable, then
the filling process is stopped. The operator(s) will then don the RABS glove in a slow and deliberate
manner, which should immediately be followed by a qualified visual inspection process. The operator(s)
then executes the intervention, as described in an approved procedure, following good aseptic practices and
the use of a sterilized tool, where appropriate, based upon the intervention.
Upon completion of the intervention, the operator(s) returns the sterile tool, where appropriate, to its
designated stand or location within the Grade A RABS. Prior to retracting the hands from the RABS glove,
the operator(s) will perform a second qualified visual inspection of the glove. The operator(s) then ensures
the glove is correctly placed to minimize stress and ensures the glove does not activate any light curtain,
present. The filler can then be reactivated to continue the manufacturing process. When open-door RABS
intervention is required, operators should work as a team, with a primary operator performing the task under
Grade A environment and a secondary operator assisting the primary operator in the intervention and to
ensure proper segregation.
This collaboration between primary and secondary operator(s) during Grade A open-door intervention aims
to prevent any contact between the primary operator's gloves and any surfaces exposed to the surrounding
environment (i.e., RABS door opening, material transfer). Intrusion of the primary operator and the duration
of the intervention into the Grade A RABS should be minimized.
Rationale
Each intervention needs to be performed using a sterile tool to minimize the likelihood of the RABS glove
contacting critical equipment and/or damaging the glove. Minimizing the impact to sterile components,
equipment, and sterile product, along with the RABS environment, is critical when executing an
intervention. Each firm needs to have a robust discard strategy, based on approved airflow visualization, to
discard product and open primary packaging components that have potentially been impacted when an
intervention is required. It is important to ensure the operator(s) understands and follows good aseptic
practices, as they can be a significant source of contamination. The integrity of the RABS gloves is an
important part of the comprehensive CCS to minimize hazard ingress; therefore, the RABS gloves need to
be inspected prior to and after every use. This process must be proceduralized, and each operator is to be
qualified to perform the inspection process. Each intervention needs to be performed using a sterile tool to
minimize the likelihood of the RABS glove contacting critical equipment and/or damage the glove.

Q 9.7. How and when should restricted access barrier system (RABS) gloves be
installed?
Recommendation
RABS gloves are sterilized before installation in the RABS barrier wall (glove port), and subsequently, they
must be wrapped and sterilized within an integral bag for protection against contamination through transfer
within the Grade B area. Prior to installation, RABS gloves are required to be sterile. Typically, this is
accomplished via autoclave or gamma-irradiation. There are multiple ways to install RABS gloves and,
while this document outlines three industry approaches, other options may be considered based on the
company’s equipment and risk assessment.
Note: In all the approaches listed below, there should be no contact of the RABS glove to any Grade A
surface with the exception of sterile tools.
Approach 1: RABS Door Closed
1.
RABS door is closed.
2.
Glove port inside is decontaminated by spraying and/or wiping with validated agents (e.g.,
sporicidals).
3.
Wrapped glove is introduced through the glove port; the glove remains covered.
Note: The wrapped RABS glove does not leave the UDAF (e.g., within cart) or UDAF zone of
protection using a UDAF cart, and it is transferred into the RABS under the UDAF zone of
protection.
4.
Upper part of the glove bag is opened using sterile tools (outside of RABS).
5.
RABS glove is connected by the attachment of the o-ring on the outside of the RABS door.
6.
Glove wrapping is aseptically removed inside the RABS.
7.
Glove remains inverted (i.e., turned “inside-out”).
8.
Glove wrapping is then taken out of the RABS by using the opening below the RABS doors for
oRABS. For cRABS another method must be used.

Q 9.8. Are restricted access barrier system (RABS) gloves treated with sporicidal agents
and/or otherwise disinfected during operations?
Recommendation
The use of disinfection, including sporicidal disinfection, should be defined within a CCS. In general, there
is no need to conduct a sporicidal disinfection of RABS glove(s) during aseptic operations.
Note: Disinfection is only required if the RABS gloves come in contact with nonsterilized surfaces.
RABS gloves should be installed, operated, and maintained in a manner in which they will not become
contaminated by exposure to the zone of protection outside the RABS (Grade B environment), by contact
with personnel during door opening, or by contact with nonsterilized surfaces within the RABS.
This requires that the RABS gloves be inverted (turned “inside out”) to the RABS outside when leaving the
RABS gloves with the operator hands (See Figure 1.4-1 for visual example of inversion). Leaving the
RABS gloves inserted and hanging down inside the RABS should be prevented as they may be
contaminated by direct contact with nonsterilized surfaces.
When there are door openings as part of setup or as a qualified intervention during a filling operation, the
RABS gloves should also be inverted and should not be directly exposed to the zone of protection outside
the RABS (Grade B environment).
RABS glove disinfection or sporicidal disinfection is only required if the RABS glove has come in contact
with potentially contaminated surfaces, which are locations below the working level, for example, for
interventions where an equipment- part must be fixed. The disinfection or sporicidal disinfection should be
performed by wiping; spraying disinfectants inside the Grade A RABS during filling should be prevented.
Rationale
RABS gloves are sterilized before installation and should be installed in a manner that prevents
contamination. (See Question 9.7.) Proper aseptic techniques should always be used when working with
RABS gloves during closed-door conditions. If the door is not opened, the RABS glove is not exposed to
the Grade B environment, and there is no contact with nonsterilized surfaces. There is no requirement for an
additional wiping, via disinfection or sporicidal disinfection, during operations. If a contamination risk
results as described above (e.g., RABS glove exposure in Grade B, contact with non-sterilized surfaces, etc.)
mitigation should follow the recommendations from Questions 9.4 and 9.5.
