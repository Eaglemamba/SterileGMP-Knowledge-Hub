## 1.0 Introduction

Reprocessing and reworking are tools that biological manufacturers can use when a manufacturing process
step has operated outside of its validated operating ranges. For reprocessing, rather than proceeding with the
next manufacturing process step, the product is rerun through the previous manufacturing process step
operated within the acceptable operating ranges. For reworking, the product is subjected to one or more
processing steps that are different from the established manufacturing process to obtain acceptable product.
In biological manufacturing, however, reprocessing, and especially reworking, should be considered only in
exceptional circumstances. Reworking can be considered during events such as critical equipment failures,
out of specifications of critical quality attributes of drug substance or drug product, and/or deviation from in-
process controls that impact quality of the drug substance or drug product (further conditions to consider are
stated in Section 5.0).
Reprocessing and reworking are deviations that are managed within the corrective and preventive actions
(CAPA) of the manufacturer’s quality management system. An essential prerequisite for the acceptance of a
reprocessing or reworking step is a clear indication of the root cause. Due to the size and complexity of
biological products, including the limitations in testing methods (especially measurement of functional
biological activity, i.e., potency), reprocessing, and especially reworking, are limited for biologicals. Thus,
they should be entered into with caution so as not to impact the product’s critical quality attributes (CQA),
and the costs and risks should be carefully evaluated and documented.
Effective reprocessing and reworking strategies should integrate supply chain resilience considerations,
including forecasting, inventory buffers, and manufacturing scheduling. Combining quality risk
management assessments (e.g., a FMEA assessment) with supply risk scoring can support balanced
decisions that protect both compliance and patient access.
Reprocessing and reworking also require regulatory authority approval before the manufactured product can
be released for human use. In limited cases, regulatory authority approval for reprocessing can be acquired
ahead of time by presenting a comprehensive change management plan (proactive approach detailed in
Section 3.6). This plan should explain the reason for reprocessing, such as refiltration or reconcentration due
to technical failures or mechanical breakdown of a chromatography column (1). It must also specify the
frequency and nature of the reprocessing procedures for potential events, as well as detail the steps for
validation and ensuring product comparability.

### 1.1 Purpose

PDA Technical Report No. 74 (Revised 2026): Reprocessing and Reworking of Biologicals (TR 74) presents
a strategy based on the experiences and practices of a PDA Technical Report Team representing a cross-
section of industry professionals. The intent is to provide guidance on the design, development, controls,

procedures, validation, regulatory submission, and implementation of reprocessing and reworking
procedures for the manufacturing of biologicals.
The information discussed represents what the authors consider a balance of scientific, regulatory, and
business considerations for the reprocessing of biologicals in particular but can also be applied to the
reworking of biologicals in general. It is highly recommended that manufacturers consult with the
appropriate regulatory authority for agreement on the strategies employed for any biological reprocessing or
reworking operation.

### 1.2 Scope

The previous version of TR 74 included only such biopharmaceutical products as recombinant proteins and
polypeptides. Because the principles of reprocessing and reworking are applicable across a wider array of
manufacturing processes, the scope has been expanded to include guidance on biological products. In
addition to recombinant proteins and monoclonal antibodies (both as innovators/novel and biosimilars),
biological products (2-4) encompass a wide range of other products such as vaccines and blood-derived
proteins.
The quality of biologicals is defined by the chosen production and manufacturing process. Minor changes in
the process can affect the quality of the drug product; therefore, the development of the manufacturing
process is of paramount importance for biologicals, including the implementation of any reprocessing or
reworking. Incorporating the topic of reworking further expands the scope of this technical report, but
because the reworking of biologicals is far more challenging than reprocessing, the authors emphasize that
extra caution should be taken.
The guidance in this technical report is general in nature, and the case studies illustrate how the general
principles may be applied. No attempt is made to encompass all types of unit operations for which
reprocessing or reworking could be considered.
This technical report is not intended to establish mandatory standards but serves as a single-source overview
that complements existing guidance documents listed in Section 8.0.
The principles discussed in TR 74 are applicable to both clinical and commercial manufacturing processes,
but the authors recognize that reprocessing and reworking during the clinical manufacturing of biologicals is
often more challenging than during commercial manufacturing. This is primarily because the understanding
of the product and the process is very limited, so extra caution is emphasized.

## 3.0 Reprocessing Considerations

Reprocessing (3, 9, 11-15), or repetition of an existing biopharmaceutical unit operation, requires a well-
designed process, supportive data, and validation to augment the existing manufacturing process.
Importantly, the supportive data must provide the manufacturer with a high degree of confidence
(assurance) that the drug substance or drug product produced by reprocessing meets the predefined quality
standards for the drug substance or drug product and is comparable to a nonreprocessed batch. The
reprocessed and reworked biological drug substances and sterile drug products should adhere to all CQAs
associated (including bioburden and endotoxins) to normal batch disposition and release. Enhanced testing
protocols are often required to assess the quality attributes of the reprocessed material, ensuring that it
remains consistent with the established manufacturing process.
Reprocessing in pharmaceutical manufacturing can occur under various scenarios that can be broadly
categorized as proactive and reactive approaches.
In GMP, a risk-based approach (6), to reprocessing is incorporated by systematically assessing and
managing potential risks associated with reactive reprocessing activities. This begins with identifying and
evaluating the nature of deviations and their potential impact on product quality and patient safety. By
prioritizing areas of greatest concern, manufacturers can focus resources and controls where they are most
needed. Decisions regarding reprocessing are made based on these risk assessments, ensuring that only safe
and effective practices are implemented.
For any event that requires reprocessing, the manufacturer must perform a formal root-cause investigation of
the unit operation excursion if the excursion if not within the design space. Additional development studies
may be required to supplement the root-cause investigation. Consideration of any proposed reprocessing by
regulators will likely be based on the root-cause analysis and the impact of reprocessing on product quality.

### 3.1 Proactive Approach

In a proactive scenario, the potential need for reprocessing is identified in advance through predetermined
criteria. The execution of the reprocessing activities occurs through a predefined concurrent validation
protocol with clear acceptance criteria or through previously validated procedures may be reviewed and
approved by applicable regulatory agencies. This approach includes:
1. Predefined Criteria: Manufacturers outline specific conditions or triggers that would
necessitate reprocessing, allowing for a structured response to an event that may require
reprocessing. Post reprocessing, there should be sufficient data to support a conclusion that
any given reprocessed batch of a drug substance or drug product is uniform and meets the
defined acceptance criteria.

----- Page 7 -----

2. Validated Procedures: Reprocessing is executed according to a validated procedure that
has undergone detailed review by the manufacturer’s quality unit. This concurrent
validation ensures that the process is capable of consistently producing a product that meets
established quality standards. The validation protocol should include the scope of the
manufacturing process that is being reprocessed, number of batches required, tests to be
performed, and acceptance criteria. The proposed acceptance criteria for all tests should be
appropriately justified. The concurrent validation approach should be described within the
Validation Master Plan. An example of reprocessing that might be specifically covered by a
Biological License Application (BLA) would be repeating viral filtration following a failed
filter-integrity test. Because viral filters occasionally fail, manufacturers may be approved
for refiltration of the protein drug substance or drug product (following successful
validation). The approach to validation should be consistent with a lifecycle approach to
validation, as discussed in the U.S. Food and Drug Administration Guidance for Industry:
Process Validation: General Principles and Practices and PDA Technical Report No. 60
(Revised 2026) Process Validation: A Lifecycle Approach (10).
When reprocessing has been prospectively validated, it is appropriate to provide the full
validation protocol and report in a regulatory submission and to follow the validated
process where indicated. When reprocessing is anticipated to undergo concurrent
validation, the protocolinclusive of initial starting conditions and appropriate acceptance
criteria to determine whether successful completion of a reprocessing step has been
achievedshould be provided.
3. Regulatory Guidance derived from Established Protocols: An essential prerequisite
for the acceptance of a reprocessing step is a clear identification of the root cause. For
biological products, these situations are usually restricted to some refiltration,
reconcentration steps upon technical failure of equipment, or mechanical breakdown of a
chromatography column. These steps should be appropriately described, validated, and
included in the regulatory submission. Such documentation should include the description
of conditions for which reprocessing could be applied and a demonstration that the
reprocessing step(s) does not impact the quality of the drug substance and/or drug product.
The sequence of steps detailed in Section 3.6 provides a roadmap for implementing
proactive reprocessing, ensuring that all necessary evaluations and approvals are addressed
before action is taken.

### 3.2 Reactive Approach

Reactive reprocessing occurs in response to unforeseen circumstances during the manufacturing process.
The same categories of data and decisions required for a proactive reprocessing approach apply to this
approach, except that less time and information may be available for performing assessments. As a result,
reactive reprocessing is better suited for steps that are simple (such as microbial or virus filtration after
failure of filter-integrity testing) and have a low likelihood of affecting product quality. The sequence of
steps discussed in Section 3.6 may be used to guide the approach to reactive reprocessing, starting with the
design of a reprocessing unit operation (see Section 3.6, Step 6).

----- Page 8 -----

Two distinct reactive reprocessing scenarios are described as follows:
1. Nonprospectively Validated Reprocessing at an In-Process Hold Point
a. In this scenario, a need arises for a nonprospectively validated reprocessing unit
operation while a product is held at an in-process stage. This may involve scenarios
such as freezing the product for an extended period.
b. During the hold period, manufacturers can develop and execute a plan to validate
the reprocessing step. This provides assurance that the product meets all the
required quality attributes subsequent to the product concurrent release.
2. Immediate Reprocessing During a Manufacturing Run
a. This scenario occurs when a reprocessing need emerges unexpectedly during a
manufacturing run. In such cases, the reprocessing step may need to be
implemented before its validation.
b. If a rapid design and implementation of the reprocessing option is feasible, it may
be executed "at risk." This means that the reprocessing can occur with the
understanding that supportive data must be generated and reviewed to ensure
product quality before the batch is released to the market.
Of the two reactive reprocessing scenarios described above, the second scenario (#2) carries the highest
degree of risk and is likely to be implemented only with relatively simple unit operations that affect a
limited number of CQAs, such as dead-end filtration for drug substance or drug product. Release of the
reprocessed batch using a reactive scenario should only be performed upon receiving regulatory approval.
(See Section 4.0 for strategies on regulatory submissions).
A proactive process design approach that accounts for reprocessing could apply to more complex (such as
ultrafiltration, diafiltration, concentration, chromatography, viral removal steps, and polishing) steps with
significant benefit to the manufacturer.
In principle, almost any unit operation may be considered for potential reprocessing as long as it is
demonstrated to consistently deliver a drug substance or drug product that meets the same specified quality
commitments of the standard manufacturing process. Reprocessing simple unit operations is relatively well
accepted and understood by regulators and the industry. Reprocessing a sterile drug substance and
reprocessing and reworking of sterile-biological batches requires critical attention to maintaining sterility,
endotoxin, and particulate controls. Reprocessing of nonsterile drug substances manufactured in low
bioburden facilities also requires special attention to be paid to microbial and particulate controls.

### 3.3 Reprocessing Frequency

A manufacturing process that requires frequent reprocessing is considered to be poorly controlled and not in
a validated state. While there is no formal definition of “frequent,” it would be reasonable to question the
state-of-control for a unit operation that requires reprocessing as often as 10% of the time (16). (Ten percent
is given here as an illustrative example and is not intended to be a prescriptive limit.)
It is important to distinguish between two types of reprocessing frequency:
•
Number of reprocessed unit operations within a batch
•
Number of batches that require reprocessing over a defined time period

Given that the Annual Product Review/Product Quality Review provides a one-year performance summary,
it may be appropriate to evaluate reprocessing trends within that annual timeframe. Under circumstances
where reprocessing becomes recurring or trends upward, a reassessment of the process design should be
strongly considered. If reprocessing is carried out frequently or reaches a specified percentage of
manufactured batches, it must be considered part of the normal process, described as such in the application,
and receives approval.

#### 3.3.1 Reprocessing Frequency per Batch

The frequency of reprocessing for the same batch at commercial-scale reprocessing should be restricted to a
maximum number of reprocessing events according to process development data for small-scale
reprocessing.
Validation of the reprocessing step should be supported by a scientifically justified number of
demonstrations runs. While no fixed minimum is mandated by regulatory agencies, performing at least two
independent runs is common practice to demonstrate reproducibility. The exact number should be based on
process complexity, risk assessment, and historical data to ensure the process consistently achieves
predefined acceptance criteria.
The reprocessing frequency is established by the manufacturer based on appropriate studies at either small
scale or same scale. Small-scale validation studies are commonly used to establish frequency for a
reprocessing event for a particular unit operation. These studies may be conducted before implementation of
a reprocessing approach at manufacturing scale. In a reprocessing event, product quality can change after
repeated run exposures during filtration or chromatography processing steps and may impact the stability of
long-term storage. There is no rule-of-thumb for frequency determination; the manufacturer must establish it
for each project based on its impact on quality attributes. For example, a simple dead-end filtration, typically
a maximum of three reprocessings, can be included in a small-scale reprocessing study. Some protein
molecules may not be stable for a second reprocessing; in that case, maximum reprocessing may be
restricted to only one reprocessing event.
These studies are used to obtain data on product quality attributes (e.g., aggregation) resulting from
exposure to physical/shear stress during repeated unit operations. Small-scale validation-study design should
be predictive of the commercial process. This will ensure that the data generated is relevant to large-scale.
Small-scale reprocessing studies must use the same protein solution for the repeated number of runs and the
samples to be withdrawn after each run. Product quality attributes of each run should be compared against
the initial and intermediate feed-material to understand the impact of multiple exposures and to determine
the frequency for the large-scale reprocessing event. All reprocessing events, including repeated runs, need
to be completed within the allowed chemical and microbial hold time for intermediate pools or scientifically
justified if there is any excursion from validated hold time.
When developing a proactive reprocessing step, a review of the development data and history may identify
process steps with inherent variability that may, on rare occasions, lead to a need for reprocessing (e.g.,
failing in-process control (IPC) assay results, filter integrity failure, difficult separation step). The variability
must be scientifically understood.

### 3.4 Continuation vs. Reprocessing (Repetition) of a Unit Operation

Most unit operations include some sort of in-process control measurement to determine whether the step
meets the objective (e.g., a concentration, pH). An adjustment to bring a product within acceptable limits is
not considered reprocessing and, instead, should be designed as a process “continuation.” Procedures should
account for continuation to perform an adjustment within defined limits. Without appropriate instructions,
continuations in processing may be erroneously considered as reprocessing and lead to procedural
deviations.
For example, during a step in the ultrafiltration-diafiltration operation, concentration is measured at different
volumes to check for the desired target concentration. If the concentration does not meet the target
specification, the process can be continued until the target specification is met, and it is considered a
continuation of the process, not reprocessing. Similarly, in the titration process, the addition of titrant to
achieve the desired pH or conductivity set point may be considered a continuation of the process. Likewise,
adding a buffer dilution to achieve the target product concentration is considered a continuation of the
process, not a reprocessing activity.
In the case of drug product manufacturing, when large-volume containers are thawed and only a partial
amount is used for filling, the remaining formulated drug substance may need to be refrozen for subsequent
use. This practice may be considered reprocessing if not included in an approved application. Supporting
freeze-thaw stability data from reduced- or-at-scale studies can help justify the impact on drug product
quality.

### 3.5 Quality Concerns During the Reprocessing of Biologicals

Protein aggregation is a key concern during manufacturing and quality assessment, particularly in the
context of reprocessing. Most therapeutic proteins are only marginally stable in solutions and are susceptible
to chemical instabilities (e.g., deamidation, oxidation, hydrolysis) as well as physical instabilities (e.g.,
denaturation and noncovalent aggregation).
During reprocessing, proteins may be exposed to various stress conditions. Process controls play a critical
role in mitigating the effects of destabilizing factors, such as pH, buffer components, and denaturants, that
can induce protein unfolding and subsequent aggregation.
Several stress factors during reprocessing can contribute to protein aggregation and instability. Key
considerations include:
•
Increased Viscosity During Reprocessing: Higher protein concentration can increase
viscosity, affecting subcutaneous formulation. Evaluate using rheology or dynamic light-
scattering.
•
Thermal Stress: Elevated temperatures (e.g., during viral inactivation) can promote protein
denaturation and aggregation. Effects are difficult to quantify and should be controlled.
•
High Ionic Strength: Use of high ionic strength eluents (e.g., in ion exchange or hydrophobic
interaction chromatography) may induce aggregation.
•
pH Sensitivity: Eluents with pH near or far from the protein’s isoelectric point can promote
aggregation.
•
Filtration Stress: Filtration processes may lead to aggregation due to membrane contact, air
bubbles, or shear stress. Ultrafiltration at high protein concentrations adds risk, especially
before filling or during buffer mixing.

----- Page 11 -----

•
Formulation Handling: Aggregation can be triggered by agitation, freeze-thaw cycles, and
prolonged storage of bulk solutions before formulation.
•
Air-Water Interfaces: Exposure to air-water interfaces during formulation can reduce protein
stability and initiate aggregation pathways. Visual inspection may be required.
Note: For factors affecting reworking, refer to Section 5.9.
Failure to manage these factors may result in out-of-specification (OOS) drug substance or drug product,
visible particulates, reduced storage stability, loss of efficacy, and potentially increased immunogenicity risk
(17, 18). Therefore, before performing any reprocessing, evaluating and understanding the potential impact
on product quality is essential. If a reactive reprocessing strategy is used, the sampling plan should include
specific analytical tests to demonstrate that reprocessing does not adversely affect the quality, safety, or
efficacy of the drug substance or drug product.
Note: For reprocessing, product release may occur in parallel with ongoing stability studies, since the
process remains within the validated manufacturing design space. For reworking, stability data must be
available prior to release to define retesting intervals, as the introduction of new steps is equivalent to a
modified synthesis with potentially higher risk (see Section 5.0).

Step 3: Review Development History
Review the design basis and development history for the existing unit operation to understand the designed
purpose of the proposed reprocessing steps, especially the control of CQAs. Leverage knowledge obtained
during the original development of the unit operation to ensure that reprocessing will address the designed
purpose of that step to consistently control specific product quality aspects (CQAs) (8, 9). Perform a
systematic assessment to identify which development studies (e.g., viral/impurity clearance, process
characterization, resin/membrane cleaning) should be repeated or augmented to support reprocessing.
Evaluate the assessment for any potential impact of reprocessing to subsequent processing steps.
Step 4: Identify Reprocessing Triggers
Identify the in-process testing or monitoring triggers that would indicate a need for reprocessing at a given
unit operation. It may be necessary to introduce a new in-process test(s) to detect failure modes identified in
the initial reprocessing risk assessment. The detection method for a failure mode that leads to reprocessing is
critical to the reliability of the overall process and to the success of a reprocessing step. The method must be
rapid enough to allow for timely decision-making before the opportunity to reprocess has passed. For
example, integrity testing of a 0.2 µm filter would be a better detection method to support reprocessing
because it can be completed in a few minutes or hours compared to microbial testing, which can take several
days to complete.
Step 5: Design Reprocessing Procedure
Begin the initial design with the proposed manufacturing-scale reprocessing procedure including all
equipment, process parameters, linkages to adjacent unit operations, pre-use/post-use procedures (e.g.,
equilibration/cleaning), and process controls. Give special attention to any procedure required to return the
process intermediate and equipment to an appropriate starting condition so that the reprocessing step can be
performed. This restoration step is designed to replicate the intermediate input feed-stream conditions (e.g.,
concentration, volume, pH, conductivity) and is not considered to be a rework step.
Step 6: Design Assessment
Consider the broader effect of the reprocessing design outside the scope of its specific unit operation; for
example, facility design, pre-/post-viral segregation, linkages to adjacent unit operations, draw on utilities,
and scheduling.
Step 7: Perform Quality Attribute Impact Assessment
Perform a risk assessment (6), to identify all quality attributes that may be affected by the proposed
reprocessing step. In particular, consider such issues as modes of degradation, critical process parameters
(CPP), exposure to product-contact surfaces, leachables from filters/single-use tubing, and adsorptive losses
that may be impacted by an additional processing step, intermediate hold point, or freeze-thaw cycle. It is
not expected that any new CQAs will be identified, but CQAs may take on a higher or lower overall risk
following reprocessing (8, 9, 20, 21). Consider whether the stability of the reprocessed batch should be
formally evaluated, especially upon the first instance of reprocessing.
Step 8: Reassess Criticality
Review existing criticality assessments conducted for process parameters, raw materials, equipment, in-
process controls, and other factors based on the standard process to determine whether any changes or
additions are required. The criticality may be influenced by the addition of a reprocessing step.

----- Page 14 -----

Step 9: Identify or Develop Analytical Methods
Identify or develop the analytical methods to monitor all relevant CQAs in intermediate feed-streams and/or
drug substance/product. Even though no new CQAs are likely to be identified to effectively develop and
validate a reprocessing step, additional analytical methods capable of monitoring all potentially impacted
CQAs in the relevant matrix may be needed to effectively develop and validate a reprocessing step.
Additional development and qualification may be needed to adapt methods to the reprocessed product. The
risk assessment (Step 7) should determine whether additional characterization methods are required to
establish comparability and acceptable product quality.
Step 10: Establish Comparability Criteria
Establish appropriate comparability criteria for all CQAs. Once CQAs and their corresponding analytical
methods are identified, it may be possible to develop the key comparability criteria to assess product quality
during the development of the reprocessing unit operation. All of the original drug substance or drug
product specifications must continue to be met following reprocessing. Verify that product quality at
intermediate points in the process meets original expectations or consider new criteria as appropriate. Use
development studies (discussed below in Step 11) when needed to demonstrate that viral or impurity
clearance downstream from the reprocessing unit operation is consistent with the original process.
Step 11: Design Scaled-Down Model
Design and qualify a scaled-down model, if applicable. To generate additional data, a scaled-down unit
operation model can be adapted from the previously qualified model of the standard process. The
requirements for a laboratory-scale model of a reprocessing step are the same as those used in the
development of a standard manufacturing process (i.e., design a scaled-down model that is representative of
the manufacturing-scale reprocessing unit operation).
Step 12: Obtain Representative Materials
Obtain representative materials for performing reprocessing at the reduced scale. If representative
intermediate feed-streams from manufacturing cannot be obtained, then it may be necessary to generate the
materials using a qualified scaled-down model of all, or a section of, the manufacturing-scale process. It
should also be possible to recreate the failure mode in the feed-stream in a representative manner (e.g.,
spiking with impurities). If the failure mode is based on operational checks, such as integrity tests, rather
than a product-quality excursion, then use of a standard intermediate feed-stream may be justified.
Step 13: Execute Developmental Studies
Plan and execute reduced-scale development studies, if required once the process design elements described
above in Step 4 – Step 12 are completed. The design of reprocessing development studies (e.g., using a
design-of-experiments – methodology or worst-case conditions) should result in an understanding of
robustness across appropriate critical and noncritical process parameter ranges. Evaluate any effects of the
reprocessing step on quality attributes using the selected analytical methods.
The design and scope of these studies should take into consideration all elements of the original
development studies related to the unit operation, including intermediate and drug substance/product
stability. Determine which prior development studies should be repeated and what additional studies (e.g.,
testing for new degradants) are needed. The data generated in reduced scale studies will become an
important element of regulatory submissions and may reduce the data needed from the manufacturing scale.

Step 14: Assess Development Data
Upon completion of the development studies, review the generated data to evaluate whether the scaled-
down process studies indicate the following:
•
All predetermined comparability criteria are met by the reprocessing procedure.
•
Process parameter operating ranges are confirmed.
•
Process intermediate, drug substance, and product stability are potentially impacted.
Step 15: Finalize Designs
Adapt GMP manufacturing-scale reprocessing procedures and equipment based upon the results of scaled-
down studies. For example, if the development studies described in Step 13 indicate that process parameter
ranges must be narrowed or shifted to ensure that all quality and comparability criteria are met, then it may
be necessary to modify the manufacturing-scale process design and control strategy to reflect this new
process understanding.
Step 16: Transfer Technology to Manufacturing
Execute technology transfer according to company policies and procedures. Transfer of the reprocessing
step to manufacturing should consider scale-up issues and implementation of associated GMP procedures.
For more complex reprocessing activities, an intermediate pilot-scale process or non-GMP “engineering
run” may also be beneficial to ensure a successful transfer into the GMP manufacturing environment (see
Section 3.8 for more information on contamination control strategies).
Step 17: Validation, Comparability and Stability Protocols
Write formal prospective process validation, comparability, and stability protocols for manufacturing-scale
reprocessing. Validation considerations include the number of lots of resin or other raw materials required,
the number of runs needed, and the additional nonroutine sampling and testing, beginning with
consideration of the original validation of the standard process. Additional validation and comparability
run(s) at manufacturing scale may be supported by the reduced-scale data generated during development. It
is unlikely that these protocols will be executed before the regulatory submission but, if they are, then
validation and comparability results could be included in the regulatory submissions discussed in Step 18
(10, 22).
Real-time stability studies are considered to be a holistic test of recombinant proteins and, therefore, are
often used to document that no changes to the protein have occurred. After validation of the reprocessed unit
operation and subsequent license approval, it may not be necessary for reprocessed lots to undergo further
stability testing. Ensure the expected stability studies after license approval are clearly stated in the
submission. Discuss stability requirements, which may vary regionally, with the appropriate regulatory
body.
Accelerated and/or stress stability studies for drug substances may be valuable tools for more rapidly
characterizing changes in a protein that may affect stability. In this case, a comparison of accelerated
stability data with non-reprocessed control materials, as well as with historical data, may be considered.
Step 18: Regulatory Submission
For approval of the reprocessing step, include the supportive scaled-down development data and prospective
validation/comparability/stability protocols in the submission (3, 8, 9). (The cited publications will be useful
references to support the submission.)

### 3.8 Impact of Contamination Control Strategy during Reprocessing

As discussed above, it is critical to ensure microbial controls are considered and protected during
reprocessing events. The reprocessed biological drug substances and sterile drug products should adhere to
all CQAs associated with bioburden and endotoxins.
During the reprocessing of a sterile biological drug substance and drug product, European Union (EU)
Annex 1 (20), provides general guidance that should be used in the design and control of facilities,
equipment, systems, and procedures used for the manufacture of all sterile products. The principles of
quality risk management (QRM) should be applied to ensure that microbial, particulate, and
endotoxin/pyrogen contamination is prevented in the final drug substance and drug products. QRM is also
applicable to low-bioburden biological intermediates and drug substances (20).
Critical aspects considered in QRM include, but are not limited to:
•
Process step where reprocessing occurs
•
Time of product exposure (open operations)
•
Air classification of the reprocessing work zone (where the product is exposed in absence of
closed system)
•
Particulate counts and bioburden trends
•
Training records of personnel carrying out reprocessing
•
Sanitization and disinfection procedures
•
Material movement and area segregation controls (e.g. CCS impact, pre/post viral areas)
•
Hold times during reprocessing
•
Line clearance before and after reprocessing
•
Cleaning and/or change over limits, in cases of multiproduct facilities
•
Filter validation studies (critical on operating ranges while handling concentrated protein
solutions)
•
Sampling techniques and number of samples taken for studies

The Quality Control Unit should establish a more comprehensive monitoring program for operators
involved in reprocessing operations, which are especially labor-intensive and require complex
manipulations and time criticality.
Processes, equipment, facilities, and manufacturing activities should be managed in accordance with QRM
principles to provide a proactive means of identifying, scientifically evaluating, and controlling potential
risks to quality. Where alternative approaches are used, these should be supported by appropriate rationale,
risk assessment, and mitigation and should adhere principles of CCS. A combination of QRM and a
contamination control strategy (CCS) should be established for a robust assurance of contamination
prevention during reprocessing. The CCS should be actively reviewed and, where appropriate, updated, and
it should drive continual improvement of the manufacturing and control methods. (See PDA Technical
Report No. 90: Contamination Control Strategy Development in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing for further
details (21, 24), Contamination Control Strategies (CCS) in Low Bioburden Biologic Drug Substance

## 4.0 Regulatory Considerations

Both reprocessing and reworking decisions require regulatory agency approval prior to release of the
affected batch(es) for use in humans. Section 4.0 provides regulatory agency guidance about what is
necessary to report and how to submit this information for approval. In addition, experiences and practices
from a PDA Technical Report Team representing a cross-section of industry professionals are provided in
the effective management of this communication.
For detailed information on reworking, see Section 5.0.

### 4.1 General

Some regulatory agencies provide clear guidance and details on submissions for these scenarios, while
others provide little to no direct guidance. In the latter case, the applicant should determine if reprocessing
or reworking can be executed under GMP control only. If in doubt, the wisest practice is to seek relevant
regulatory agency approval before considering reworking or reprocessing. For products manufactured across
global supply networks, harmonization of reprocessing/reworking strategies across sites is critical.
Regulatory submission timelines should be aligned with supply chain planning to avoid stock-outs and
ensure uninterrupted patient supply.
Regulatory considerations for several regions are presented in Sections 4.2–4.5. When intermediates are
manufactured by CDMOs, roles and responsibilities must be clearly defined. This includes identifying the
stage of processing, the nature of the biological intermediate, and evaluating the potential impact of
reprocessing or reworking. CDMOs should ensure transparent communication, data integrity, and timely
provision of information required for regulatory submissions.

national/local/regional rules. The stage of implementation of all ICH Guidelines is dependent upon when a

## 5.0 Introduction to Reworking

It is the primary responsibility of the Manufacturing Authorization Holder (MAH) of a biological drug
substance and/or drug product consistently meet all quality, safety, purity, and identity requirements. This
authoring team has strict recommendations on how and when such a reworking action should be considered
by the MAH. The ultimate approval of a reworked drug substance and/or drug product for human use is
solely at the discretion of the approving regulatory agency.
ICH and EU define reworking as “Subjecting an intermediate or API that does not conform to standards or
specifications to one or more processing steps that are different from the established manufacturing process
to obtain acceptable quality intermediate or API” (3, 38). During reworking, the nonconforming material is
subjected to a different manufacturing process to get a product that meets all the predefined acceptance
criteria and specifications for quality, safety, purity, and identity (QSIP).

### 5.1 Prioritization Tool for Reworking

Using the MoSCoW (Must-have, Should-have, Could-have, and Won’t-have, or will not have right now),
Prioritization Tool to evaluate reworking of a biological drug substance involves systematically categorizing
tasks or issues based on their criticality and impact (39).

----- Page 32 -----

This step-by-step guide outlines how to apply the MoSCoW method in this context, and Figure 5.1-1
illustrates the Patient-Centric Reworking Prioritization tool.
1. Gather Relevant Data and Stakeholders
− Collect all information related to the reworking process, including technical assessments,
quality data, regulatory considerations, and risk analyses.
− Assemble a cross-functional team comprising manufacturing, quality assurance, regulatory
affairs, and process development experts.
2. Define the Scope of Reworking
− Clearly specify which aspects of the biological drug substance are being considered for
rework (e.g., purification, formulation, in-process controls, final product adjustments).
3. Identify Rework Tasks or Issues
− List all potential rework activities, corrections, or modifications needed to meet quality and
regulatory standards.
4. Apply the MoSCoW Prioritization Criteria
− Categorize each task or issue into one of the following groups:
o Must have (M): Critical for human survival, compliance, safety, efficacy, or regulatory
approval. These are nonnegotiable and should be completed before proceeding.
o Should have (S): Immediate medical supply need; can be scheduled after Must-Have items.
o Could have (C): Desirable to support patient needs, especially those suffering with genetic
disorders, cancer, glaucoma, blood issues, and wherever critical to support health condition
of work force.
o Won't have (W): Tasks that are not necessary in the current scope or can be deferred.
5. Prioritize Reworking Activities
− Focus first on Must-Have tasks to ensure the reworked product meets all quality and
regulatory standards.
− Schedule Should-Have activities shortly afterwards, resources permitting.
− Consider Could-Have tasks, if time and resources allow, to enhance overall process
robustness.
− Leave Won't-Have tasks for future considerations or continuous improvement.
6. Document and Communicate
− Record the prioritization outcomes clearly, including rationales for each category.
− Communicate the priorities to all relevant teams and stakeholders to ensure aligned
execution.
7. Implement and Monitor
− Proceed with rework activities according to the established priorities.
− Monitor progress and adjust priorities if new information or issues arise.
8. Additional Measures
− Ensure compliance with GMP and regulatory requirements throughout the rework process.
− Use risk assessments to support the categorization, especially for CQAs.

### 5.3 Background to Manufacturing Changes

Reworking is an unexpected occurrence and is not preapproved as part of the marketing authorization. The
MAH’s Quality Unit should oversee investigations into a nonconforming (OOS) in-process drug, a bulk
process intermediate (final biological bulk intermediate), a drug substance of a single batch or lot to an
alternate manufacturing process during reworking decisions, prior to release to ensure that the details of
rework in question has been submitted to the regulatory agency and formal approval has been obtained. The
MAH’s Quality Unit should oversee investigations into nonconforming (OOS) bulk process intermediate/
drug substances from a single batch or lot. This oversight is crucial during reworking decisions prior to
release. The Quality Unit must ensure that the details of the rework have been reviewed and submitted to the
regulatory agency and formal approval has been obtained.
Changes that have a substantial potential to have an adverse effect on product quality (i.e., major variations)
require the manufacturer (applicant) to report the change to the FDA in a supplement to the approved BLA.
A PAS must be approved by the FDA prior to distribution of the product manufactured using the change to
protect the public from products whose identity, strength, quality, purity, potency, safety, or effectiveness
may be compromised (10, 33, 46).
If biological drug products are reworked, they must be reported in a supplement to FDA prior to
distribution, unless the reworking was done according to a procedure previously approved by the Agency.
The type of notification is based on the potential risk of the change having an adverse effect on the identity,
strength, quality, purity, or potency of the product as it may relate to the safety or effectiveness of the
product (24).

### 5.5 Application of Knowledge and Risk Assessments

ICH Quality Guideline Q10: Pharmaceutical Quality System was the first official global guideline known to
firmly position QRM and knowledge management (KM) as important enablers of an effective
pharmaceutical quality system (PQS). Specifically, QRM and KM jointly enable (i) achieving product
realization, (ii) establishing and maintaining a state of control, (iii) facilitating continual improvement, and
(iv) critical decision-making during the reworking strategy (57).
ICH Q9(R1) emphasizes the relationship between risk and knowledge, and it highlights the importance of
KM in managing risks. It places knowledge and KM repeatedly and explicitly at the core of all QRM
activities, further reinforcing the connection of scientific knowledge and risk in several other regulatory
guidance documents. Specifically, the definition of risk-based decision-making reads, “An approach to, or a
process of, making decisions that considers knowledge about risks relevant to the decision and whether risks
are at an acceptable level” (6).
The Risk Knowledge Infinity Cycle (RKI Cycle) establishes and promotes the following concepts:
•
Knowledge is both an input to and an output from QRM
•
Knowledge has an inverse relationship with risk
•
Knowledge should flow effortlessly and on demand to inform risk and risk-based decisions
•
The Cycle is continuous and perpetual
The RKI Cycle can be applied to support the goals of the PQS as defined in ICH Q10. A set of six principles
corresponding with six “nodes” along the Cycle has been developed to better guide the tangible connection
between QRM and KM practices as illustrated in Figure 5.5-1 (58).

*[Figure 5.5-1 Six Guiding Principles of the Risk Knowledge Infinity Cycle Framework]*

During the reworking of biologicals, rigorous risk management ensures that any changes or corrective
actions do not compromise critical quality attributes, safety, or regulatory compliance. Structured
assessments (e.g., FMEA within a quality risk management framework) prioritize controls and justify
decisions, while robust change control, deviation tracking, and CAPA link risks to measurable outcomes.
Effective knowledge management captures batch histories, tacit operator knowhow, prior rework learnings,

----- Page 42 -----

and real-time analytics in a shared repository. This enables cross-functional review and consistent, evidence-
based decisions that reduce variability and prevent repeat issues.
Knowledge
Prior knowledge is a critical tool to identify and justify quality during the critical planning, decision-making,
and execution of the OOS of the biological drug substance or a drug product to be reworked. Three forms of
knowledge are critical:
1. Internal knowledge of the manufacturer gained throughout the product lifecycle (including
development and characterization of biological drug substance and drug product) is the basis for
identifying the elements of CMC that are established conditions (EC) (52), and those elements
which are supportive information, historical development of products and/or similar processes,
proprietary technologies, modelling data, previous regulatory filings, regulatory inspections, pre-

2. External knowledge comes from public databases, literature surveys, journals, conferences, peer-
reviewed publications and monographs.
3. Knowledge application of scientific principles from biophysical sciences, pharmaceutical sciences,
biochemical engineering, material science, and application of principles of engineering for
evaluation of the structure-activity relationship of the molecule under investigation.

4. The evaluation of the risk to quality should be based on scientific knowledge and ultimately linked

5. The level of effort, formality, and documentation of the QRM process should be commensurate

### 5.7 Established Conditions

The implementation of ICH Quality Guideline Q12: Technical and Regulatory Considerations for
Pharmaceutical Product Lifecycle Management by different regulatory agencies will also enable the
recognition of ECs that require regulatory notification as well as those non-ECs that have been recognized
as not necessary to ensure product quality and, therefore, can be managed prior to implementation
notification (60). The amount of flexibility in a control strategy is based on the totality of product and
process understanding (e.g., prior knowledge, development studies) in the context of QRM principles
described in ICH Q9(R1).
A control strategy is designed to ensure that a product of required quality will be produced consistently. This
planned set of controls, derived from current product and process understanding, assures process
performance and product quality. The controls can include parameters and attributes related to drug
substance and drug product materials and components, capability of the verified mathematical models,
facility and equipment operating conditions, in-process controls, finished product specifications, and the
associated methods and frequency of monitoring and control. The control strategy is based on the
development of the CQAs which, in turn, have been based on thorough scientific work (4, 57).
Pharmaceutical development activities result in an appropriate control strategy of the ECs, which are critical
elements identified and proposed by the MAH to assure product quality and, therefore, would require a
regulatory submission if changes impacted any EC. ECs can include CPPs, critical material attributes
(CMAs), and CQAs as defined in ICH Q8(R2) (4).
In the event of reworking of a drug substance or drug product, CMC changes can occur. Thus, the ECs for
the manufacturing process should be defined based on enhanced product and process understanding,
considering all the relevant elements of the control strategy. To identify the ECs, an enhanced approach is
recommended with an increased understanding of interaction between the changed key and critical input
parameters along with the outputs, and the impact on quality attributes together with a corresponding control
strategy. Once ECs are identified for the material to be reworked, a detailed initial risk assessment must be
developed from the prior knowledge gathered to identify the potential risk to product quality associated with
changing the ECs and considering the overall control strategy.

### 5.8 Post-Approval Change Management Protocols

The PACMP tools described in ICH Q12 enable the planning and implementation of changes to ECs and/or
CPPs, and thereby to regulatory filings, in an efficient structured manner. The PACMP (or comparability
protocol) approved by the relevant health authority provides an agreement between the MAH and the
regulatory agency and describes the changes and level of detail complying to the complexity, major
variation, risk assessment, control strategy, and other pertinent elements. Once a PACMP is approved, when
the proposed study design or acceptance criteria changes, an update of the protocol should be submitted for
implementation as agreed upon in the original protocol. If changes are needed due to unanticipated or
unforeseen changes in plans, prior approval of a new or revised PACMP by the regulatory agency may be
required.

----- Page 46 -----

In the event of a reworking, the proposed comparability protocol (CP) for a modified manufacturing process
should sufficiently cover the intended change and provide an increased control when compared to the
existing process (61). A CP can be appropriately designed and/or drafted using a risk-based approach, with
an in-depth or a high-level knowledge of process and product understanding. A risk assessment should be
conducted on the potential drug substance and/or intermediate CQAs to be affected by parameter changes.
including unspecified parameters (12). Knowledge from previous developmental studies, small-scale
studies, and/or pilot studies can be critical to identify potential risks to quality and provide support in the
development of a suitable evaluation plan. The risk of adverse effects as a result of cumulative changes
during a reworking should be carefully identified, justified, and included in the supporting information for
the CP. The reworked material should be assessed with suitable structural characterization, analytical and
procedures. It should meet the criteria to demonstrate the lack of adverse effect, including immunogenicity
on the reworked product quality considering the impact of structural changes in drug substance and/or drug
product. Depending on the type and complexity of the drug substance, functional characterization and
additional studies should also be included.

### 5.9 Instabilities and Quality Concerns

Protein degradation (62), aggregation (18), poses significant challenges in the manufacturing and quality
control of biologicals, particularly during reworking processes. Given that, generally, these proteins are only
marginally stable in solution, they are susceptible to various forms of instability. Chemical instabilities like
deamidation, oxidation, and hydrolysis, alongside such physical instabilities as denaturation and
noncovalent aggregation, can compromise the quality and efficacy of the drug substances .
During an event of reworking, proteins will be exposed to stress conditions, such as elevated temperatures,
low pH, or hydrophobic surfaces, which might trigger conformational instabilities (23, 63, 64). Formulation
of drug substances at high concentrations can further encourage protein aggregation, impacting long-term
stability (55).
To mitigate these risks, purification steps are essential for removing impurities and aggregates. It is crucial
to implement appropriate process controls that manage pH, buffer components, and denaturants to prevent
protein unfolding, which can lead to aggregation. These controls are vital to ensuring the drug substance
meet specifications, maintains good appearance without visible particulates, retains stability during holding
and storage, preserves efficacy, and minimizes immunogenicity concerns.
Protein manufacturing and formulation are complex processes with several factors that can impact the
quality and stability of therapeutic proteins, such as:
•
Protein Concentration and Viscosity: During reworking, increased protein concentration
can lead to higher viscosity, which is problematic for formulations intended for subcutaneous
injections. Techniques such as rheology and dynamic light-scattering can be employed to
evaluate viscosity impacts and ensure optimal formulation characteristics.
•
Thermal Stress: Elevated temperatures, particularly during viral inactivation steps, can lead to
protein denaturation and aggregation. While it is challenging to quantitatively assess these
impacts, careful control of temperature conditions is necessary to maintain protein stability.
•
High Ionic Strength: The use of high-ionic-strength eluents in ion-exchange and hydrophobic
interaction chromatography may promote aggregation. Monitoring and controlling ionic
strength during these steps can minimize the risk.

----- Page 47 -----

•
pH Considerations: Eluents with pH values near the protein's isoelectric point, or far from it,
can lead to aggregation. It is crucial to optimize and maintain pH conditions throughout
processing to prevent destabilization.
•
Filtration Processes: Membrane contact, presence of air bubbles, and shear stress during
filtration can induce aggregation. Ultrafiltration, especially at high protein concentrations,
requires careful management to prevent aggregation, particularly before filling bulk containers.
•
Formulation Challenges: Bulk storage, freeze-thaw cycles, and agitation during formulation
processes can stress proteins and cause aggregation. Implementing gentle handling techniques
and optimized storage conditions can reduce these risks.
•
Air-Water Interfaces: Exposure to air-water interfaces during formulation can promote
aggregate formation by lowering the energy barrier for protein unfolding. Minimizing such
exposure and conducting visual inspections can help identify and address potential aggregation
issues.
•
Excipients in Formulation: Certain excipients can stress proteins and accelerate aggregation.
For instance, magnesium ions (Mg+2) have been noted for their potential to increase aggregation
rates in some formulations. Understanding and managing the impact of excipients is critical for
maintaining biological molecule stability.
•
Visible Particulates: It is critical to effectively monitor visible particulates after any
reworking. If visible particles are detected, it is necessary to implement detection measures,
conduct chemical identification, perform root cause analysis, and develop mitigation strategies
for maintaining safety and efficacy of the reworked biologicals.
•
Leachables and Extractables: Leachables and extractables typically pose minimal issues for
traditional cellulose-based depth filters (33, 46). However, they have been detected in various
sterilizing-grade filters, such as polyvinylidene fluoride, polyethersulfone, and mixed cellulose
ester. These filters demonstrate a complex pattern of destabilizing and stabilizing effects on the
physical and chemical stability of mAbs, including the formation of visible and subvisible
particles. The leachables and extractables may also exhibit "surfactant activity." Characterized
leachables and extractables in these filters include organic carbon, metals, inorganic salts, and
fibers. Since protein solutions undergo multiple chromatographic and filtration steps, most
extractables and leachables (65) should be removed during downstream processing.
By comprehensively addressing these areas through careful process controls, analytical techniques, and
optimization strategies, the risks associated with protein aggregation can be significantly minimized,
ensuring the production of high-quality therapeutic proteins. Understanding and controlling these factors are
key to maintaining the integrity and quality of biologicals throughout the stages of reworking.

### 5.11 Submission & Approval Timelines

Reworking a biological drug substance involves carefully revisiting and potentially modifying production
processes to ensure product quality, safety, and efficacy. The timeline for reworking such a drug substance
and/or drug product can vary significantly depending on several factors, including the complexity of the
product, extent of modifications needed, regulatory requirements, and manufacturing facility's capabilities.
Below are typical phases and approximate timelines:
1. Assessment and Planning (2–4 weeks)
− Evaluation of the current process and product
− Identifying reasons for rework
− Developing a detailed rework plan and risk assessment
2. Process Development and Optimization (4–12 weeks)
− Adjusting upstream or downstream processes
− Small-scale laboratory or pilot studies to validate modifications
− Analytical method development or validation if needed
3. Analytical and Stability Testing (4–8 weeks)
− Confirming product integrity, potency, and purity post-rework
− Stability testing to ensure product remains within specifications
4. Scale-up and Validation (8–16 weeks)
− Manufacturing at larger scale
− Process validation batches to confirm reproducibility
5. Regulatory Documentation and Submission (4–12 weeks)
− Preparing amendments or supplemental dossiers for regulatory authorities
− Addressing agency queries and obtaining prior approval
6. Manufacturing and Release (variable, typically 2–6 weeks)
− Final production run
− Quality release-testing and documentation

Total Estimated Timeline
Typically, reworking a drug substance can take approximately 6 to 12 months, but in some cases, especially
with complex modifications or regulatory hurdles, it may extend longer.
Factors Influencing Reworking Timeline
Several crucial factors that influence the timeline for reworking a drug substance include:
•
Regulatory Pathways: The need for prior approval or additional filings can significantly
affect timelines. Approval processes differ by region and can vary based on the scope of
modifications.
•
Extent of Process Changes: More extensive changes typically require deeper assessments
and validation efforts, which lengthens the timeline.
•
Availability of Raw Materials and Manufacturing Capacity: Supply chain considerations
and manufacturing capacity can cause delays, particularly if specific raw materials are scarce,

----- Page 51 -----

facility constraints exist, or there are changes in any equipment or introduction of single-use
systems.
•
Quality and Analytical Testing Turnaround Times: The time it takes to conduct necessary
quality and analytical testing can impact timelines, especially if retesting is required or if results
necessitate further adjustments.
•
Single-use Systems: If new single-use systems with greater process contact-surface area or
new polymers are introduced that encounter the drug substance during bulk dispensing
containing excipient buffers or final filtration  a detailed leachable and extractable impact
assessment will need to be performed before execution of reworking process (65-67).
•
Regulatory Agency Review Times: Variability in how long agencies take to review
submissions and respond can introduce uncertainties in the timeline.
Starting from the point of prioritization—the main reason to rework a drug substance t—the timeline for
reworking is highly variable and should be planned carefully, incorporating all development, validation, and
regulatory steps to ensure compliance and product quality. Consulting with regulatory authorities early in
the process can help streamline the timeline and clarify requirements.

### 5.13 Conclusions

In urgent situations, such as a global pandemic (e.g., COVID-19), wars, or natural disasters (e.g., Category 5
hurricanes, catastrophic earthquakes, major floods, meteor catastrophe), certain regulatory flexibilities or
deviations may be considered by the MAH to address critical supply issues, like immediate drug shortages.
However, reworking an OOS drug substance generally involves significant quality and regulatory
considerations, such as:
•
Regulatory Frameworks and Guidance:
− Regulatory agencies such as the U.S. FDA, EMA, and other agencies, offer guidance on
managing deviations, OOS results, and manufacturing challenges. In urgent situations, these
agencies may provide tailored guidance or emergency procedures. However, deviations from
approved specifications generally require proper justification, and thorough documentation.
•
Reworking of OOS Drug Substance:
− Reworking involves processing material that initially failed to meet specifications to bring it
into compliance.
− This process must be validated and scientifically justified, demonstrating that reworking
does not compromise product quality, safety, stability and efficacy.
•
Regulatory Approval and Documentation:
− Any decision to rework should be thoroughly documented, including risk assessments and
validation data.
− For licensed products, regulatory authorities often require that deviations or exceptions from
established processes be reported. Depending on the jurisdiction and the specific nature and
impact of the change, prior approval may be necessary. This ensures compliance with
regulations and maintains product quality and safety standards. It is crucial to follow the
guidelines and procedures set forth by relevant regulatory bodies to avoid potential
implications on the product's marketing authorization.
•
Risk Management and Justification:
− In emergencies or shortages, regulators may offer some flexibility, but the company must
ensure reworking does not compromise drug quality.
− A comprehensive risk assessment, incorporating knowledge management, should be
conducted to evaluate its impact on safety and efficacy.
•
Good Manufacturing Practice Compliance:
− Reworking must adhere to GMP standards, and the process should be validated.
− Proper change control procedures should be followed.
Reworking an OOS drug substance should never be treated as routine. However, in urgent situations such as
a pandemic or critical shortage, biotechnology companies may consider reworking if it is properly justified,
validated, and documented. They should proactively engage with regulatory authorities, possibly under
emergency use or contingency provisions, to ensure compliance and maintain product integrity.

#### 6.2.1 Column Packing

The resin utilized for chromatography is loaded into the column in a controlled manner to ensure a uniform
distribution while preventing the formation of channels that will affect the performance of the unit
operation. Commonly, a slurry containing resin in a process buffer is poured into the column. Once the resin
is settled, the integrity of the packaged column is confirmed. In the case of reprocessing, the integrity of the
column bed should be verified and confirmed prior to its use. Any issue affecting the bed should be solved
prior to reprocessing, considering resuspension and repacking of the resin if applicable.

#### 6.2.2 Cleaning and Sanitization

Full cleaning and sanitization of the column must be conducted in preparation for reprocessing. Cleaning
and sanitization will follow existing procedures and practices, including the regeneration of the resin. (These
elements are discussed further in (68, 69).)

#### 6.2.3 Performance

Performance of the chromatography step must be monitored according to the routine established procedures
and must satisfy predefined acceptance criteria. The criteria should include, but not be limited to, the
operational conditions (e.g., asymmetry, height equivalent to the theoretical plate, and/or transition analysis)
and quality attributes (65).

#### 6.2.4 Regeneration and Lifecycle

The resin must be regenerated consistent with established procedures and must satisfy the acceptance
criteria defined and validated. The use of the resin for a reprocessing activity will be within the validated
number of reuses if the reprocessing activity is not associated with a malfunction of the chromatography
column and resin.

#### 6.2.5 Validation and Filings

Validation of reprocessing will be performed under a prospective protocol. Detailed guidance for validation
activities of chromatography processes for the purification of proteins is provided in PDA TR 14 (68). In the
event that changes are required to an existing filing, guidance is provided in PDA Technical Report No. 38:
Manufacturing Chromatography Systems Post-Approval Changes (ChromPAC): Chemistry, Manufacturing,
and Controls Documentation.
In this example, a protocol for prospective validation of reprocessing at the manufacturing scale was written
and approved; however, the potential of a concurrent validation could be considered if the frequency for
reprocessing did not justify a prospective protocol. The protocol required a single GMP run at
manufacturing scale, including all routine in-process and release testing as well as additional nonroutine
testing, for example, analysis of HCP, acidic variants, aggregate levels, fragmentation, oxidation, and
potency in reprocessed material. The protocol also required placing the resulting drug substance in the
stability program. The protocol acceptance criteria for nonroutine testing were based on the comparability
criteria established in Section 6.4.3 and demonstrated at small-scale. (See Section 6.5.6.)
Yet, a full prospective validation approach may not be feasible or practical for reprocessing in all cases.
Instead, a concurrent validation strategy may be more appropriate, allowing the manufacture and testing of
products and only reprocessing when and if necessary. In the case of concurrent validation, the acceptance
criteria shall be aligned with the approved specifications for the process.

#### 6.5.2 Reprocessing Triggers

Based on a review of the development history and the prior risk assessment, an HCP critical in-process test
following the AEX step was designed and implemented to serve as the trigger for an investigation (i.e., if
HCP is >100 ng/mg). The test results were available within eight (8) hours of sampling. The requirement for
this additional eight-hour hold time was considered when designing the reprocessing step, as described in
Section 6.4.3 and Sections 6.5.6-6.5.9. The introduction of this IPC test was a permanent process change
that affected all production batches and not just the reprocessed batches. The investigation procedure was
defined in an SOP. If the investigation confirmed the positive result, then the reprocessing step would be
initiated.

#### 6.5.3 Reprocessing Design

The design of the AEX reprocessing step took into consideration the issues described in Section 6.3.1,
Section 6.3.2, Section 6.5.1, and Section 6.5.2. Since the step operated in flow-through mode, the AEX
eluate was considered to be suitable as a feed-stream for AEX reprocessing without any adjustments. Prior
development studies reviewed in Section 6.5.1 showed that all ranges for feed-stream conditions expected
for the reprocessing operation were evaluated and shown to produce drug substance with acceptable quality.
In addition, studies for the reprocessing step demonstrated that the AEX column—after being stripped,
cleaned, and re-equilibrated—would readily remove HCP to well below acceptable levels. All other aspects
of the design of the reprocessing step, including operating conditions, buffer solution, equipment sizes, and
others were similar to the design of the original AEX step.

#### 6.5.4 Design Impact Assessment

After completing the design of the reprocessing step, the broader impact of the design on manufacturing
operations was assessed. Since the AEX is a flow-through step, no adjustment to the buffer composition was
required. The protein concentration was expected to be 30% lower than the original feed-stream but still
within acceptable limits. The AEX load concentration is not considered a CPP.
A summary of the design impact assessment is included in Table 6.4.1-1 in the “Other” category. The two
most significant areas of concern identified were Buffer/Solution Supply and Equipment Cleaning. The
maximum hold time for AEX eluate had to be increased by 48 hours to allow for completion of the HCP
IPC test and to complete preparation of equipment and buffers in the event that the reprocessing trigger
occurred. An evaluation of the manufacturing-scale microbial hold studies supported the longer hold time.

Clear rationale for reprocessing (e.g., reason for reprocessing)

Proper management of the column and resin

## 7.0 Appendix II: Case Study — Filtration

Filtration is among the more likely unit operations to be subjected to reprocessing; therefore, it is discussed
separately in this case study. The complexity and quality impact of filtration reprocessing (referred to hereon
as refiltration) tend to be low compared to other unit operations, such as the chromatography example
discussed in Section 6.0 (Appendix I).
The most common refiltration scenarios would include bioburden-reduction filters and virus-reduction
filters, as further elaborated in Section 7.2 and Section 7.3. Reprocessing of tangential flow filtration is
more complex and would tend to follow a framework more similar to chromatography (as described in
Section 6.0). Refiltration for nonproduct-containing solutions, such as media and buffer, is generally
acceptable and could be desirable (e.g., due to supply constraints on certain ingredients), in which case,
potential impact (such as adsorption) should also be evaluated. For most refiltration steps, additional
nonroutine product and stability testing of the drug substance and drug product may not be required.
•
For refiltration of an in-process bioburden-reduction filter that is used throughout the process,
routine release and hold-time stability data may be adequate to support validation of
reprocessing.
•
Refiltration may also occur during bulk drug substance filling, where the final container was
found compromised (e.g., bioprocess container (Single Use systems) found to be leaking).
Reprocessing at that point may require additional validation, such as pooling of filtered and
unfiltered bulk drug substance and additional bioburden-testing.
•
Validation of reprocessing using a viral filter could be performed using routine product-testing,
assuming the hold-time data is adequate and viral segregation is maintained.
Scenarios where product quality is compromised, such as if the drug substance or drug product exceeds a
bioburden specification, if unexpected precipitation is observed in the drug substance or drug product, or if
viral segregation is compromised, then reprocessing is unlikely to be an option.
The following case studies are based upon the experience of the PDA Technical Report Team members.

### 7.1 Case Study 1: Proactive Drug Substance Refiltration

#### 7.1.1 Case Study 1: Issue

The manufacturer validated the process for filling a liquid biologic drug substance into the drug substance
containers (e.g., carboys, single-use bags). Prior to validating the filling process, a failure modes analysis
was performed to identify potential failures that could occur during the filling process. Three potential
failures were identified:
•
Mechanical failures during bulk fill (e.g., structural failure such as leakage in the feed vessel,
pinholes in the feed tubing, and/or single-use bags, post-use filter-test failure, failure in the
filling apparatus)
•
Out-of-range results of protein concentration (e.g., higher than specification, which requires
further dilution with formulation buffer and refiltration) and/or spiked excipients (e.g., lower
than specification polysorbate, which requires additional spiking and refiltration)
•
Bioburden limits exceeded the action limit but were less than the specification

#### 7.1.2 Case Study 1: Considerations

Up to three full-scale refiltration validation runs would be performed against a preapproved protocol
capturing the failure mode, of which at least one run evaluated the failure mode of interest. Each individual
refiltration protocol would be performed on a separate lot of drug substance. Samples from the validation
runs would then be tested according to the specification to confirm meeting release criteria, and if necessary,
placed on GMP stability for the shelf life of the drug substance. The resultant data package would provide
additional confirmation that refiltration had no impact on product quality at release as well as on stability.

#### 7.1.3 Case Study 1: Resolution

The results generated during the validation would then be filed in the BLA. The application would indicate
that refiltration could only be performed once and that it must occur within a specified time period
supported by the validations (e.g., validated hold stability). The application should specify that the Quality
Assurance Unit must be notified and provide approval of refiltration. The refiltration procedure could be
deemed as acceptable for approval as part of the license.

### 7.2 Case Study 2: Proactive Virus-Removal Refiltration

#### 7.2.1 Case Study 2: Issue

Post-use integrity test failures of a normal-flow virus-retentive filter are a known failure mode affecting lot
release. Refiltration of the virus-filtered pool through a new integral virus filter can be performed to ensure
the validated clearance of virus; however, this reprocessing procedure has the potential to impact product
quality due to extended processing times, for which the product load and/or pool stability should be
considered.

#### 7.2.2 Case Study 2: Considerations

Small-scale validation studies can be performed using a representative scaled-down model to assess the
potential impact to product quality resulting from refiltration of virus-filtered pools. For these studies,
samples of virus-filtration load can be obtained from different lots at manufacturing scale. Each of these

----- Page 76 -----

virus-filtration loads should be refiltered at laboratory-scale through two sets of new, integral virus filters to
represent worst-case. The validation studies can help to confirm that refiltration had no impact on product
quality.

#### 7.2.3 Case Study 2: Resolution

Results from the small-scale study of refiltration through a virus-retentive filter were reported in a regulatory
submission. Validation of the refiltration unit operation would be completed for one full-scale batch and
would include data on product yield, quality, and stability. Upon completion of the validation study and
submission of the data, the refiltration procedure would become an amendment to the license.

PDA Technical Documents (Technical Reports, Points to Consider, Technology Guides, etc.) are global
consensus documents, prepared by member-driven teams (listed on inside front cover) comprised of content
experts, including scientists and engineers working in the pharmaceutical/biopharmaceutical industry,
regulatory authorities and academia. The viewpoints and strategies presented in this document are those of
the authors as independent subject matter experts and do not necessarily represent the policies and practices
of their respective companies.
While in development, PDA Technical Documents are subjected to a global review by PDA members and
other topic-specific experts, often including regulatory officials. Comments from the global review are then
considered by the authoring team during the preparation of the final working draft. The level of expertise of
the team and those participating in the global review ensure a broad perspective reflecting best thinking and
practices currently available.
The final working draft is next reviewed by the PDA Advisory Board(s) that sanctioned the project. PDA’s
Advisory Boards are: Science Advisory Board, Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products Advisory Board,
Biopharmaceutical Advisory Board, and Regulatory Affairs and Quality Advisory Board. Following this
stage of review, the PDA Board of Directors conducts the final review and determines whether or not to
publish the document as an official PDA Technical Document.
