# PDA Technical Report No. 87 (2021): Current Best Practices for Pharmaceutical Glass Vial Handling and Processing

## 1.0 Introduction

Glass has long been one of the first materials fabricated by humankind to contain valuables—from
perfumes, oils, and potions to the most technologically advanced. Glass has many properties that make
it an appropriate packaging material for parenteral drugs, including:
• Rigidity and durability
• Stability and compatibility with filling and shipping processes
• Ease of cleaning and sterilization
• Gas impermeability and maintenance of container-closure integrity
• Compatibility with a wide range of drug products
• Optical transparency and clean appearance
• Ease of drug product inspection
Like other packaging materials, glass is not always the appropriate packaging material. Not all glass
types are the same in composition and properties. Based on the glass type, some drug formulations can
react with glass, particularly those with a high pH or certain buffers.
Beyond this consideration is the fact that glass can break. Glass breakage can result in processing down-
time, an increased number of production interventions, injury, increased particulate load, and loss of
product. Ultimately, patient safety can be put at risk due to a loss of container-closure integrity (possibly
including sterility breaches), lost or improper doses, or injury from glass shards and particulates.
The resistance of glass to breakage is less a property of the material, as glass is inherently strong, but
more about the condition of its surface. For glass to break, both a stress and a flaw must be present at
the same place and at the same moment. As will be explained in this technical report, stress, whether
from an applied load, impact, or thermal shock, is generally temporary and the glass container will
retain its initial integrity and strength. Flaws, however, are permanent once induced.
As glass is handled through its manufacturing processes and subsequent pharmaceutical processes, it is
vulnerable to surface damage. Best handling practices can preserve the inherent strength of the glass,
whereas poor glass handling practices can significantly reduce the practical strength of glass containers.
Surface damage induced early in the manufacturing process may manifest itself as failure later in the
process or in the field as the container is subjected to subsequent stresses.

### 1.1 Purpose and Scope

The purpose of this technical report is to provide a technical understanding of glass, its strength, and
its limitations, and to provide guidance in best handling practices for glass vials throughout the phar-
maceutical process—receipt, storage, preparation, filling, stoppering, freeze-drying, and capping. This
document focuses on glass vials, where surface damage may occur, and where excess stresses may be
applied during processing and handling. Many of the principles discussed here can be applied to the
handling of glass syringes, cartridges, ampoules, and other glass container types.
This report also reviews typical pharmaceutical glass handling processes and identifies areas of concern.
Points to consider are presented for line, material, and equipment enhancements as well as guidance
and practical approaches for using procedural controls to better employ existing equipment.

### 3.1 New Equipment Design, Installation, and Qualification

In order to optimize the glass handling process for a specific application, equipment design, installa-
tion, and qualification should be conducted as a joint activity of the pharmaceutical manufacturer, ma-
chine suppliers, and manufacturers of the product’s primary and secondary packaging. The full range
of dimensional variability and consideration of critical dimensions required for optimal container
handling must be accounted for in equipment design. Factory and site acceptance testing should be
conducted to evaluate all vial sizes in order to demonstrate suitability (4-5). Process parameters, such
as line speed, dwell times, and change parts, should also be evaluated. Both the maximum and mini-
mum speeds should be evaluated to assess the impact on glass breakage, pinch points, torque limits on
star wheels and screw feeds, and pressure measurements (Figure 3.1-1).

### 3.2 Considerations of Component Design and Variation

During the qualification process, several aspects of the components should be considered:
• Container dimensional variability (lot-to-lot or vial-to-vial)
• Component stack-up tolerances (vial + stopper + seal)
• Container design appropriate for the equipment to be used
• Technical drawings, appropriately detailed with all specifications that could impact performance
of the vial in the equipment (e.g., contact points on the rails, star wheels, screw feeds, grippers),
including specifications on shoulder angles, radii, concentricity, and roundness
Aligning measurement techniques among and within the pharmaceutical company, equipment suppli-
ers, and component suppliers is very important.

### 3.3 Real-Time Stress Monitoring

New technologies, such as vial diagnostic drones, are available that can measure line pressure and shock
events and transmit these data live during fill and packaging operations or in transit (Figure 3.3-1).
These real-time data acquired for line and vial sizes can diagnose problem areas and enable corrective ac-
tions to be taken. The data can be used to adjust and optimize conveyor speeds, format part tolerances,
and improve robotic pickups to reduce the mechanical stress that leads to glass damage events. These
technologies can also be used in machine design and acceptance testing.

## 4.0 Risk Assessments

An effective risk management process can reduce glass handling hazards during initial process devel-
opment and design and also when evaluating process changes. Further, risk management can be used
to discover the root cause of glass breakage events and make informed decisions during investigations.
A risk management program can support regulatory discussions and inquiries, ultimately providing
regulators with higher confidence in the pharmaceutical manufacturer’s capabilities to address these
types of events. The scope of the risk management program, specific to glass handling, should include
the full lifecycle of the glass container including the manufacture and shipment from the supplier
to filling, packaging, and distribution to the customer. There are several helpful documents available
to support risk assessment activities, such as ISO documents, ICH guidelines, and PDA technical
reports (6-9).
Risk assessment comprises identification of potential risks, analysis of the probability of occurrence,
evaluation of potential impact of the risk, and prioritization for action. Risk assessments can be quanti-
tative or qualitative in nature. A quantitative assessment, in which a numerical value or range of values
describing the level of risk, may be used. Or, a qualitative assessment, using relative categories such as
“high,” “medium,” and “low,” may be used to characterize the risks. In either case, the definition and
rationale for each category should be clearly defined.
A risk assessment is performed by a cross-functional team that includes the appropriate SMEs and/
or responsible persons to assure accurate analysis. Multiple data sources are used to support the as-
sessment including, among other material, process maps, procedures, manufacturing floor experience,
frequency data, deviation and complaint history, and detection capability. These inputs ensure the
accuracy of the risk analysis, so they must be robust.
Subsequent to risk assessment, risk controls are considered and applied where appropriate to eliminate
or reduce risks identified by the risk assessment. The amount of risk control (i.e., risk reduction or
elimination) should be proportional to the significance of the risk and should be assessed based on a
benefit-to-risk analysis.
The resulting risks, mitigating activities, and level of risk acceptance must be effectively communi-
cated. Risk communication and review requires appropriate documentation as well as communica-
tion of the risk decisions to the persons authorized to accept the risk and direct response strategies
for risk mitigation and follow-up (e.g., functional experts and area or operations heads). Risk re-
sponse activities need to be reviewed periodically to confirm ongoing compliance effectiveness and
proactively reevaluated when new process, equipment, or industry changes influence or alter the
underlying risk profile.
Many tools are available that can be used to employ effective risk management. Table 4.0-1 provides a
list of tools typically used with a brief description and potential applications for each.
One effective method used to assess the risk inherent in a process specific to glass handling is the Fail-
ure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA). This method is widely used by many industries, including
the pharmaceutical industry. Although its original and intended use had been to proactively identify
unacceptable risks prior to failures occurring, the FMEA has also been used successfully in a reactive
fashion to evaluate processes for most likely root causes of failures after they occur.
The FMEA method is based on the theory that the risk of any particular failure is a composite of the
severity, frequency, and probability of the detection of that failure. Cross-functional team participants,
with a diverse knowledge of the process, compile an exhaustive list of potential failures and, taking
into account the known attributes of the process, thoroughly analyze each one. This output is then
evaluated to ascertain if the risk is acceptable or unacceptable. Mitigation activities are identified and
completed for those risks deemed unacceptable.

An FMEA should be performed during the initial design and development of the product. Thus,
critical elements of the container design can be identified to ensure product quality including, but
not limited to, considerations for processing steps such as filling, blast freezing, lyophilization, and
final storage conditions. The glass breakage FMEA may be performed in conjunction with a FMEA
for other types of potential failures. This can confirm the original FMEA and provide a benchmark
risk assessment for production. An FMEA should be performed during evaluations of changes to the
process as a specific step of process change control. The existing FMEA should be modified and used to

ascertain if risk will change due to the planned process change. Any increase in risk due to the planned
change should be mitigated prior to implementation. A periodic review should be completed to con-
firm ongoing effectiveness of controlling risk levels and identify opportunities for further mitigation
through development of new technology or advanced procedures.
The specific glass-handling hazards to address as part of an FMEA should include glass particles or
fragments that could enter a container from a glass breakage event, and/or events or risks resulting in
breakage or a cracked container. The scope of the FMEA should cover as much of the lifecycle of the
glass container as possible, including supplier manufacturing and shipment as well as receipt, filling
and packaging, and final distribution.
During the risk analysis, potential failure modes specific to the glass-handling hazards are identified and the
potential causes and current controls are captured. The risk associated with specific failure scenarios is estimat-
ed using a risk priority number (RPN). An RPN is determined for each scenario based on the severity of the
failure effect, the frequency of occurrence of the potential cause, and the detection and mitigation (preven-
tion) capability that exists based on the controls currently in place. The definition and rationale for rankings
used to complete each element of the RPN should be clearly outlined and agreed upon by the FMEA team.
Multiple sources of information are employed to determine the RPN. Process maps and procedures that
outline the relevant process steps, and the equipment associated with them, are used to ensure that all relevant
areas are captured in the evaluation. In addition, and importantly, observations on the manufacturing line
are conducted as part of a complete process review of the manufacturing floor or location of the process and
equipment. Pictures and videos gathered, either during the process review or from previous efforts, are useful
for evaluating the severity, frequency, and detection of each failure mode. Specific glass-breakage frequencies,
locations, and root causes, as well as investigation history, are used to further inform the FMEA. Visual in-
spection capabilities throughout the process should be outlined to provide information relative to detection.

### 5.1 Packaging for Transport and Storage

The glass-handling process begins at the glass vial manufacturer, where the pack-out configuration
must be specified to minimize the risk of damage or breakage during transit or shipment from a suppli-
er to a pharmaceutical firm. These configurations should be standardized for each container type and
size: shrink-wrapped module type with glass-to-glass contact or a tray with or without cell partitions or
separators. The number of containers that are held in a module or tray and their arrangement is typi-
cally driven by the infeed of the pharmaceutical processing equipment. The packaging format is also
sized to maximize pallet space. Where modules are used, ensure proper packing and shrink-wrapping
of the module (e.g., vial alignment, tightness of shrink-wrap) and avoid flexing and module-to-module
impact during handling which may cause glass-to-glass abrasive damage.
The remaining aspect of the pack-out configuration should include a review of such factors as: the set-
back of the load from the edge of the pallet; module-stacking configuration; need for corner protection
and straps; need for slip sheets between layers; method of securing the load to the pallet; and total
height of the pallet. Double-stacking of pallets, especially during transit, should be avoided.

### 5.2 Integrated Line with Multiple Transfer Points

To ensure the pharmaceutical processing equipment is designed appropriately, collaboration with ma-
chine manufacturers is recommended. Where equipment from different machine manufacturers is
used, ensure that each machine is properly integrated into the line. An appropriate risk assessment
should be conducted during the machine design and line integration. Typical transfer points at the
pharmaceutical company that should be evaluated include belt transfers, guide rails, dead plates, screw
feeds, star wheels, and pick-and-place devices.
Vials can be automatically or manually pushed from a tray onto an accumulation table, where they
are transferred to an infeed screw. To ensure the proper feeding of vials into the screw, proper spacing
between vials, which is determined by belt speed before the screw, is important. The number of vials
that back up prior to the screw should be kept to a minimum to reduce glass-to-glass contact between
vials that could potentially cause damage.
Machine designers endeavor to remove potential negative impacts to the vial; however, not all risks
can be removed. With the transport of vials, abrasion to the body is always a risk; therefore, proper
materials should be used to reduce friction on the bottom, heel, and body of the vial. The greatest risk
in vial damage comes during the transition from infeed screw to star wheel or from star wheel to star
wheel (or inspection turret). Proper timing of the star wheels is critical, as is precision machining of
size-change parts and low-backlash gearing or clutches. If the vial is not handled smoothly, with care
taken to reduce impact forces, the vial body may experience excessive stress, which can cause breakage,
cracks, or other damage.
A risk assessment should be conducted to properly design the process and materials for collecting
downed vials that are ejected from the line if they may be reintroduced into the line. The assessment
should determine the appropriate drop height, the amount of cushion or padding required, the fre-
quency of emptying the eject bin, and the level of inspection required prior to reintroducing the vials to
the line. The edges of the eject bin should be rounded to avoid sharp edges that could damage the vials.

#### 5.2.1 Accumulators

Accumulators are used between segments of a filling line where the throughput speeds of the specific
units of equipment may vary or be intermittent, or where one unit may be more likely to experience
periods of down time (e.g., after depyrogenation and before filling, or after stoppering but before cap-
ping). Accumulators are also used for loading and off-loadings trays at the beginning, during transfers,
or at the end of line (e.g., after lyophilization and before capping, or after terminal sterilization but
before labeling).
Accumulators may be of several types, including rotary tables (Figure 5.2.1-1), push (back pressure),
and bi-flow.

##### 5.2.1.1 Key Parts of the Process and Potential Impact on the Containers

The accumulation of vials often leads to abrasion damage to the glass surface. This happens especially
where large numbers of vials are tightly gathered and the vials continue to revolve and rotate against one
another, or where one or more vials become trapped in an eddy of the flow and remain for a long pe-
riod of time. This type of damage is typically evidenced by circumferential scuff or scratch rings. These
rings are usually observed at contact areas just below the shoulder and above the heel of the vial (Figure
5.2.1.1-1). While every surface discontinuity affects glass strength, the significance of these, relative to
the potential for container failure, should be evaluated through the process described in Section 5.4.
The type of damage shown in Figure 5.2.1.1-1 may occur wherever rotary tables are used, but it is
most prevalent when these tables are used immediately after depyrogenation. Depyrogenation dehy-
drates the glass surface, eliminating its natural lubricity and exacerbating friction damage. In addition
to damaging the glass surface, visible and subvisible glass particulates may be generated in the process.

##### 5.2.1.2 Points to Consider

As good general practice, the table should be properly sized for the planned line speeds and large enough
so that it is not loaded to more than one-third of its capacity. Additionally, directional flow control devices
should be engineered to prevent eddies (opposite circular flows) in the flow of vials.

and limiting the number of vials allowed to accumulate on the table to a practical minimum (i.e., a
number of vials that allows for the process to proceed efficiently yet avoids overcrowding and damage
to the vials) is recommended.
Throughput speeds should be as consistent as possible for each segment of the line, reducing the need
for accumulation. When this is not possible, the line should be regulated (i.e., sections stopped and
started) to minimize accumulation. This may be accomplished with procedures and training or by
integrating the control systems of the various segments of the line. Accordingly, the movement of vials
on the table should also stop if the line itself is stopped. This will preclude excessive accumulation of
vials onto the table.
If turntables or accumulation tables must be used, they should be designed to minimize the momen-
tum of the containers (e.g., low speed settings) in order to reduce the level of forces between contain-
ers, thus reducing the occurrence of abrasions in the glass and generation of the glass particles. Using
low speed settings would also reduce the level of fallen containers at transition points.

### 5.3 Vial Washing

In parenteral packaging, glass vials are typically washed prior to being sterilized for filling. Vial washing
is conducted in order to ensure removal of such materials as bacterial toxins, chemical substances, and
particulates so that the product meets expectations of identity, purity, quality, and safety.

#### 5.3.1 Washing Machines

Washing machines are available in various designs, the most common being linear tunnel and rotary
(Figure 5.3.1-1). Vials are washed with either a diving spray nozzle or a stationary, nonpenetrating
spray nozzle. When using diving spray nozzles, where a needle enters the vial and sprays the vial from
the bottom up, there is a risk of needle strikes on the bottom of the vial, the inside wall, or the finish
of the vial that could result in a bent needle, glass damage, and particulate generation (glass chips).
The risk of vial strikes increases when multiple vials must be aligned with an array of needles for simul-

*[Figure 5.3.1-1 Diving Nozzle Rotary Washing Machine]*

taneous washing. The process parameters, including needle depth and centering, must be adapted for
varying vial sizes or designs (10-11). Needles should be routinely inspected for straightness to prevent
needle strikes. Some newer washing machines can be equipped with needle strike alarms to aid in risk
management.
For washers that use buckets to hold the vials, inverted or double-stacked vials (two vials within the
same bucket) are vulnerable to breakage due to needle strikes. Automated systems that detect and eject
upside-down vials or detect double-stacked vials can be incorporated to mitigate this risk. Manual
intervention procedures to address vial orientation issues may be needed for some washing systems.
Automatic tray loader/unloaders, conveyers, and infeed-outfeed systems keep human interaction to a
minimum.
For rotary washers, water jet pressure should be moderated to ensure vials are not forcibly ejected from
the grippers by an excessive jet stream.
In limited applications, batch washers are used to wash vials one tray or module at a time. Loading
plates and corresponding washing needles must be matched to the respective tray configuration and
vial size to avoid glass damage or breakage during loading. A visual check and preventive maintenance
plan should be in place to identify bent or damaged needles that may pose a risk for needle strikes dur-
ing washing. Care should be taken during loading and unloading to prevent glass damage or breakage
(e.g., when inverting the vials from the loading plate onto a stainless-steel tray).

### 5.4 Dry Heat Sterilization and Depyrogenation

Dry heat sterilization and depyrogenation of the vials occurs immediately after washing and is usually
accomplished using dry heat. Dry heat ovens may take the form of tunnels or batch ovens. The ovens
often serve as the transfer portals from the preparation area into the aseptic area. Vials are usually ster-
ilized at ≥ 160 °C for up to two hours and depyrogenated at approximately 325 °C for at least thirty
minutes, but the times and temperatures can vary depending on the specific process requirements
(12–13).

These two processes may increase the coefficient of friction (COF) of the glass surface and make the
vials more susceptible to abrasion. This is of particular concern if the vials will be subjected to glass-to-
glass or glass-to-metal contact, such as on a rotary accumulation table. This type of friction damage is
typically evidenced by scuff rings or scratches on the vials (Figure 5.2.1.1-1). This damage is usually
observed just below the shoulder and above the heel of the vials. Though small and relatively minor in
appearance, scratches and scuffs can potentially contribute to breakage under sufficient mechanical or
thermal stresses further downstream. In addition to damaging the glass surface, visible and subvisible
glass particulates may also be generated in the process. The longer the time the vials are subjected to high
temperatures, the more profound the increase of the COF. In a case where vials are in contact with one
another, one vial may bond with the surface of another vial, causing them to stick together. When these
vials come apart, distinct vertical marks will be apparent (i.e., stuck marks) (Figure 5.4-1). These marks
may act as stress concentrators and fracture origins as the vials are subjected to subsequent loads during
downstream processing or handling.

#### 5.4.1 Continuous Convection Tunnel

After the air-drying process, the glass vials (either in single file or in rows) pass onto a conveyor belt
over a dead plate. Back pressure from the continuous loading of the belt pushes the material onto the
conveyor.
Ideally, the conveyer belt should be operated in a “pull mode” and run at a slightly higher speed than
the feed rate from the washer in order to maintain a small space (e.g., ~1 mm) between the rows of
vials. This transition is sometimes supported by a pushing device to complete the transfer from the
washer to depyrogenation tunnel.
Maintaining a minimal downward transition of the vials is recommended to reduce the probability of
damaging the heel region.
Vials are then moved through the convection tunnel on the conveyor at a predetermined rate. Tun-
nels typically include three heat zones: a preheat zone, a hot zone (where the glassware is exposed to
sufficient temperature for sufficient time to effect sterilization/depyrogenation), and a cooling zone
(Figure 5.4.1-1). Depyrogenation guidelines are presented in PDA Technical Report No. 3 Validation
of Dry Heat Processes Used for Depyrogenation and Sterilization (12).

*[Figure 5.4-1 Stuck Marks]*

*[Figure 5.4.1-1 Typical Depyrogenation Tunnel Layout with a Preheat Zone (1), a Hot Zone (2), and a Cooling Zone (3)]*

The vials are then pushed out of the tunnel, using slight back pressure, across a dead plate, and onto
the next stage of the process by an accumulator.

#### 5.4.2 Points to Consider

As best practice, containers should be dry prior to exposure to depyrogenation temperatures (Zone 2
in Figure 5.4.1-1), as residual moisture may attack the interior surface of the glass causing etching or
glass corrosion (12).
In the event downstream processes come to a stop, it should also halt additional vials from entering
the tunnel to prevent overloading, while allowing the in-process batch to complete the validated depy-
rogenation cycle.
Viewing windows are recommended so the entire depyrogenation area can be observed for evidence
of such overcrowding as containers riding on top of the bulk material and fallen or broken containers.
Although the glass structure is not impacted directly by the temperatures used for depyrogenation,
leaving the containers in the tunnel beyond the validated depyrogenation process time is not recom-
mended (see Section 5.5).
The temperatures achieved during depyrogenation remove the protective water skin from the contain-
ers, leaving them more vulnerable to surface damage, such as scratches. As a result, the exiting contain-
ers may be more prone to damage in general and have a higher COF than prior to depyrogenation (see
Appendix I, Section 8.4).

#### 5.4.3 Batch Depyrogenation Oven

Another method for dry heat sterilization and depyrogenation is the use of a depyrogenation oven,
which allows for processing of smaller rather than continuous processing. Trays of vials are loaded into
the oven on racks and depyrogenated according to a validated cycle. The trays are typically unloaded
under a Grade A (ISO 5) unidirectional airflow and then transported to the vial-filling line. Due to
the absence of the protective water skin, consideration should be given during loading, unloading,
and transport to limit movement of the glass vials within the stainless-steel trays to prevent damage
or breakage.

### 5.5 Vial Filling

Drug product is added to vials at a filling station. Vials are transferred to the filling station after wash-
ing and depyrogenation by a conveyor belt, with a star wheel or by a gripper that picks up the vial by
the neck. Vials are filled with a filling needle by either a fixed nozzle or a diving nozzle apparatus. Once
filled, the vials move along the line to the stoppering station where they are stoppered either by direct
placement (where the stopper is picked up by vacuum) or by a rotating wheel.

#### 5.5.1 Impacts

Vials transported by means of a conveyor belt experience glass-to-glass contact as they move along the
line, which can result in abrasions to the glass. Vials that are transported by a star wheel or by gripper
have glass-to-glass contact only at the vial gate, where they accumulate, and where damage can occur.
For filling lines that use grippers (i.e., pick and place) to move vials, the gripper configuration and the
amount of pressure exerted on the vial are critical and should be compatible with the vial neck size.
Ideally, grippers should be constructed of plastic rather than metal.
Needle installation and alignment should be verified at set-up. A needle strike may occur when a filling
needle is misaligned with the vial being filled; the needle strikes the top of the vial and can chip the
vial and/or bend the needle. Needles can also become bent when striking vials that have offset finishes.
Damage in the lip region of the vial can go unnoticed through inspection as the stopper and seal can
mask the damage. Regular inspection during the production batch should assess misalignment of and
damage to filling needles. Vial attributes such as leaners (slanted bottoms), bent or offset finishes, or
small inner diameter, can increase the risk of needle strikes.
During weight checks, vials can be damaged or broken when transferred from the filling line to the
weigh station. Vacuum-assisted vial grippers can reduce the incidence of damage but can also be im-
pacted by particulate matter if the vials are not adequately clean.

#### 5.5.2 Points to Consider

The filling process can be improved to further reduce damage to the vials by replacing the metal with
plastic throughout the line. Damage to the vial due to needle strikes can be reduced by changing the
stainless-steel filling needles to disposable plastic. In contrast to the impact from a metal needle, a
plastic filling needle would fail or bend before the vial would break.

### 5.6 Stopper Placement

At the stoppering station, the stoppers can be placed on the vials either directly (pick-and-place) or by
a rotating wheel. The risk of incurring damage during the stoppering process is very low as there is no
glass-to-glass contact during stoppering. Any breakage observed during stoppering is most likely due
to damage that occurred upstream.

### 5.7 Lyophilization

Lyophilization, or freeze-drying, is a process through which water is removed from the liquid solution
by freezing the product, subliming the ice, and desorbing residual water to low levels. Vials are filled
with liquid product and stoppers are partially seated into the vials on the filling line prior to accumu-
lation for the lyophilizer-loading operation. The vials are loaded onto the freeze-dryer shelves, either
manually using bottomless trays, or via an automated mechanical loading system. Upon reaching the
specified end conditions of the process, the stoppers are fully seated into the vials with the hydraulic
shelf-stoppering system. Vials are then unloaded from the chamber. Manipulation of the vials during
the loading, stoppering, and unloading processes, in addition to the freeze-drying process itself, can all
be potential sources of glass vial handling issues.

#### 5.7.1 Loading and Unloading

During the traying, transfer, loading, and unloading process, glass-to-glass and glass-to-metal interac-
tions occur similar to other vial manipulation operations, such as filling and capping. Vials are typically
conveyed by sliding across stainless-steel surfaces and then nested with other vials in a close-packed con-
figuration, experiencing forces applied from surrounding vials during movement. Vials could be accu-
mulated onto individual trays and transferred manually to the freeze-dryer or transferred by automated
loading systems. Typical automated material-handling systems transfer and load the entire lyophilizer
shelf, row by row, or by some other indexing method. The tooling used to manipulate these vials should
be compatible with the vial size and type. Material-handling methods with significant glass-to-glass and
glass-to-metal contact can lead to small scratches, checks, or cracks, creating a weakened region in the
glass that can potentially lead to breakage at that location when stresses are exerted.
Over time and repeated use, cleaning, and sterilization, wear on the bottom surface of the tray can
become evident and warping of the transfer ring may occur, leading to “pinched” vials when nested. In
turn, the prominence of glass defects can increase the risk of container failure due to increased friction
and limited mobility.

#### 5.7.2 Lyophilization Process Concerns

The potentially significant stress imposed on the glass from the expansion of product during the freez-
ing and drying steps is a concern. As the product freezes, crystals form and the product can expand,
often upward from the bottom of the vial, but also outwardly and down from the product surface
toward the bottom. The extent of this effect will vary depending on the specific product formulation.
Although this expansion would not initiate glass breakage by itself, the risk to vial breakage increases
with the presence of a defect, such as a blemish due to scratches in the vial from glass-to-glass and
glass-to-metal abrasion. Vial design, especially in the heel area, may also have an impact on breakage
during expansion. With a large fill volume, internal forces are increased significantly. Figure 5.7.2-1
and Figure 5.7.2-2 show examples of glass fractures and lensing, respectively, that can occur during
the freeze-drying process. Other product effects can also impose stress on the container (14).

#### 5.7.3 Seating the Stopper

Final seating of the stopper occurs within the lyophilizer chamber at the end of the lyophilization
process, when the shelves are either lowered or raised to fully insert the partially seated stoppers. The
suspended shelves most often are driven by a hydraulic ram (Figure 5.7.3-1). Pressure exerted by the
hydraulic system is set initially for the most demanding need, such as 2 cc or 3 cc vials with a 13 mm
finish. Such small vials have greater density across the shelf surface area and require the greatest pres-
sure (force per unit area) to fully seat the stoppers. In order to apply a uniform pressure to each vial
across the shelves, the shelves should be level and planar. If there is any significant variation to pressure
distribution, the force on the vials can be localized significantly in one area, which could lead to glass
breakage.
One area to assess is the container-to-container height variation; stoppering forces will be concentrated
to any vials that may be slightly taller than the surrounding vials. The difference in vial-stopper height,
in some instances, has been sufficient to result in broken vials. When a partial shelf-load of vials left at
the end of the filling operation are being placed on the lowest shelf, there must be a sufficient number
of vials, and they must be evenly positioned across the shelf to accommodate the pressure evenly. Fail-
ing to evenly distribute the vials across the shelves can lead to cocked shelves and distortion of the shelf
suspension hardware. In addition, for container-closure systems with a poor stopper fit or misplaced
stopper, cocked closures upon stoppering can lead to excessive force and, potentially, broken vials.
Some breakage observed in a lyophilizer may not be due to improper handling but may be attributed
to vial nonconformities such as leaners, rocker or uneven bottoms, or an improperly designed or
formed bottom radius.

#### 5.7.4 Points to Consider

Once risks for glass handling defects and breakage have been assessed, based on material-handling
conditions and variables for the specific product and container configuration, there are systems that
can be implemented to reduce the risk. The material of construction on contact surfaces, such as
guide rails on the filling line during material-handling operations, has an impact. The condition of the
shelves, conveyors, trays, and rings should be flat, level, and smooth, and monitored on a predeter-
mined schedule for adequate maintenance. Vial pushers, indexers, and other vial manipulators should
be compatible with the system. Using plastics for vial contact surfaces and eliminating pinch points
can help alleviate glass-to-glass or glass-to-metal friction (stickiness). The container-closure industry is
making strides in decreasing the risk of vial defects; for example, preloaded vials, already indexed into
tubs, can limit glass-to-glass and glass-to-metal interaction.
The height of the container-closure system should have an allowable tolerance range specified to pre-
vent significant variation across a shelf and unequal forces. Thermal stresses should be limited, where
possible, during the lyophilization process. Product expansion behavior during the solidification pro-
cess should be known and potentially limited, by limiting fill height in the container, altering the ratio
of crystallizing to noncrystallizing components, or considering alternate freezing methods, such as
controlled nucleation, which initiates nucleation of ice from the liquid surface.

### 5.8 Capping

Vial capping is the last step in the aseptic process to fully seal a parenteral vial. The primary seal of a
parenteral vial packaging system is the interface of the elastomeric stopper flange and the land sealing
surface on the crown of the vial. Capping is the process of sealing the vial by compressing the flange of
a rubber stopper against the top surface of the glass vial, forming the primary seal, and then crimping
an aluminum ferrule around the stopper and vial finish. Sufficient vertical load force is necessary to
optimally compress the stopper, achieve sufficient seal tightness, and assure container-closure integrity.
A side load may also be imparted when the metal is crimped (Figure 5.8-1).

#### 5.8.1 Impacts

Regardless of the sealing method, the stress created in the glass from these loads of an optimized cap-
ping process (< 130 N force) should be well below that where breakage would be expected (see Appen-
dix I, Section 8.3.4 for further discussion on conditions influencing breakage). Breakage, however,
is sometimes observed at the capper. Glass failure at capping is likely the manifestation of significant
surface damage caused to the vial earlier in the process.
Improper set-up of the capper or excessive vial variability (e.g., offset necks, bent necks, large necks,
leaners, rocker bottoms, laps (folds) in the neck) (Figure 5.8.1-1) could cause the neck to come in
contact with the sealing rail or disc, causing surface damage to the neck of the vial that may propagate
into fractures (Figure 5.8.1-2).
Transfer of vials into and out of the capper usually consists of a combination of belts, screw feeders,
and star wheels. Transfer mechanisms are discussed in Section 5.2.

#### 5.8.2 Points to Consider

To manage the capping process, an appropriate combination of set-up parameters and change-parts
must be determined for each container-closure combination. That the sealing disc or rail will not
contact vials within normal variability must be assured, and detailed capping equipment set-up pa-
rameters must be established and documented for each package combination. The parameters should
be based on achieving sufficient stopper compression to achieve acceptable container-closure integrity
without excessive force. Component variation and stack-up tolerances should be considered in estab-
lishing the capper parameters.

Offsetting (top and bottom) springs or dampers are employed to compensate for total height variation.
Additionally, automated inspection systems are used to remove out-of-specification components, vials
missing stoppers, or misplaced stoppers.

### 5.9 Terminal Sterilization

Terminal sterilization is performed once the vial is stoppered and capped to assure the sterility of
the product (15). Moist heat, which is applied in the sterilizer (oven) by spraying hot water on the
product, is the method used for parenterals in vials. The process uses pressure, temperature, and time
parameters. The loading pattern of the units in the chamber should be uniform to ensure proper heat
distribution.
Vials that are in contact with one another in the sterilizer may bond with the surface of another vial,
causing them to stick together. When these vials come apart, the stuck marks may act as stress concen-
trators (See Section 5.4).
Breakage of vials during terminal sterilization is most likely due to an upstream incident. The practical
strength of the glass may have been reduced, thus reducing the burst pressure of the vials.

### 5.10 External Washing

Transitions that convey the glass containers into and out of the external washing unit operation (e.g.,
star wheels, screw feeds) must be aligned and synchronized. Also, a slight downward slope or short
vertical step downward should be employed when transitioning to or from conveyors and dead plates.
When grippers are used to convey the glass container through the external washer, proper alignment of
the grippers and the pressure applied must be set to avoid introduction of glass defects or glass break-
age. Feedback mechanisms or a sensor to detect proper functionality of the grippers should be used to
prevent such glass breakage. The water and air pressure and their direction should be set appropriately
to avoid glass-handling issues.

### 5.11 Inspection

Filled containers should be inspected individually for contamination or other defects (1). Inspections
can be carried out using three methods: manual, semi-automated, or automated. The sorting of ac-
cepted or rejected vials should follow the recommendations outlined in this section.

#### 5.11.1 Manual Inspection

Manual inspection consists of a human inspector handling the vials directly. Typically, an inspector
manipulates a vial under a calibrated light source and in front of both white and black backgrounds
to enable proper inspection (Figure 5.11.1-1) (5,16,17). During the inspection process (as with all
manual processes), proper and careful handling should be employed to avoid damaging the vials. The
accept-or-reject decision is based on the inspector’s knowledge and training.

#### 5.11.2 Semi-Automated Inspection

Semi-automated inspection also uses a human operator to make accept-or-reject decisions; however, the
vial handling is done with automation. Typical semi-automated inspection machines include a roller
conveyor for vial transportation and spinning of the vial, while also providing proper lighting and mir-
rors that allow the inspector a full view of the container. Handling the vials within a semi-automated
inspection machine should limit glass-to-glass contact and minimize impact during transitions.

#### 5.11.3 Automated Inspection

Automated inspection can be performed using both sensor-based and camera-based systems and, depend-
ing on the process, can incorporate different handling designs. Appropriate, container-specific change
parts and set-up are necessary to ensure proper handling, inspection, and prevention of additional damage.
For example, the base attachments and spindle caps on machines using inspection turrets are designed
in such a way that the vial is held in place during the spin process to prevent the vial from being thrown
from the turret (“spin-out”) (Figure 5.11.3-1). Vials are sorted depending on the results of the inspection
process. For ejected vials, limit glass-to-glass contact and minimize impacts during transitions as the ejected
vials may be reinspected manually or reintroduced into the automated inspection process.

#### 5.11.4 Inspected Vial Ejects

Vials that fail an initial inspection may be reintroduced into the line. Acceptance criteria should be
defined, and a risk assessment should be conducted to determine the level of reinspection required.

Controlled eject lanes are preferred over eject bins to avoid damaging the vials. If eject bins are used,

#### 5.11.5 Points to Consider

Two upgrades are recommended to improve the handling of glass vials during the inspection process:
puck-handling and direct-spin system. With puck-handling, after the vial is fed through an infeed
screw and star wheel, it is placed on a specialized puck. This puck is then transported throughout the
machine carrying the vial. The advantage of this system is the reduced interaction between machine
and glass. The machine passes the puck from the star wheel to an inspection turret and back to the star
wheel without any additional forces exerted on the glass vial.
The direct-spin system provides an individual servo motor for each location on the pre-spin or inspec-
tion turret. That allows increased control of the spin speeds of the vial, optimizing the machine for a
larger range in containers and reducing the risk of vial “spin-out”.

### 5.12 Off-Loading

Off-loading is the collection of finished product from the line. When transferring the vials into interim con-
tainers prior to labeling, materials, and practices similar to those outlined in Table 3.0-1 should be employed.

### 5.13 Vial Labeling

Typically, labeling does not occur in-line following the final container inspection. Vials are often pack-
aged for transport (within a facility or to a remote location) and care should be taken to prevent dam-
age from glass-to-glass contact, vibration, and other shocks. Trays are unloaded manually onto the
labeler in-feed; then, a combination of star wheels and/or scroll feeds orient and singulate the vials for
labeling. As with other processes, vials downed during labeling should be handled as recommended in
Section 3.0. Once the label is applied, the vials are transferred to the label inspection unit.

### 5.14 Secondary Packaging

Secondary packaging is the final step in the pharmaceutical process, one that provides the glass vial
with extra protection from rough handling during distribution and storage. Folding cartons, made of
cardboard or coated paperboard, are often used. The carton should be properly sized, designed in a way
that prevents glass breakage or damage, and made of materials with a lower friction factor and shock
absorption. For multi-vial packs, the inner compartment of the carton should be designed so that the
packaging prevents glass-to-glass contact.

#### 5.14.1 Points to Consider

Newer carton designs are available with double-walled construction and cushioning that protect
against severe impact and provide crush resistance. Cartons with an integrated partition should be
suitable for both manual and automated packaging lines. Pre-glued edges can support the cover, pre-
venting pressure on the vials during stacking. Additionally, labels that add an extra layer of protection
are available for certain applications (e.g., cushion or double-thickness labels).

### 5.15 Finished Product Storage and Handling

The remaining aspect of the pack-out configuration, how the vials will be stored and handled during
distribution, presents a number of factors that should be considered, such as:
• Set-back of the load from the edge of the pallet
• Carton-stacking configuration
• Need for corner protection
• Need for slip sheets between layers
• Method of securing the load to the pallet
• Total height of the pallet
Double-stacking of pallets is generally not recommended and should be avoided, especially during transport.

## 6.0 Considerations for Preventing Additional Damage

New technologies and product developments are available to further mitigate the risk of damage to vi-
als. A brief overview of some of the options are provided in this section. This list is not comprehensive;
however, and each company should evaluate its needs and requirements for including any additional
damage-prevention solutions.

### 6.1 Ready-to-Use Vials

As an alternative to bulk vial delivery, suppliers now offer vials that are prewashed or ready-to-use (prewashed
and sterilized). The responsibility for proper glass handling and packaging during the washing and steriliza-
tion processes shifts to the supplier of the ready-to-use vials. After the supplier washes the vials, they are placed
in nests and tubs, and the packaging units are then sealed with a protective barrier (e.g., high-density poly-
ethylene) (Figure 6.1-1). This method prevents glass-to-glass contact and allows vials to be used in aseptic
manufacturing processing without unpacking for washing, sterilization, filling, and/or lyophilization.

### 6.2 Robotic Automation

Robotics offer alternatives to traditional glass-handling processes by reducing glass-to-glass contact
and providing consistent glass manipulation. Precision pick-and-place units can replace infeed screws
and accumulation tables.

When using robotics, the handling materials, contact points, timing, forces exerted upon the vials, vial
configuration, positioning, and orientation should all be carefully considered.

### 6.3 Outer Surface Treatments or Coatings

Glass containers may be subject to abrasion during conveyance on production lines and during trans-
portation, resulting in scratching and bump checks that may adversely affect their mechanical resistance.
Strengthening the outer surface and/or applying a lubricating layer can mitigate possible damage.
The COF between bare glass surfaces is very high. Vials are typically sterilized by dry heat depyrogena-
tion prior to entering the filling line, which removes water and most lubricious organics and produces
a surface with a COF ranging from 0.9 to 1.0 (18). Under these conditions, glass-to-glass contact
should be minimized. To mitigate particle generation and any subsequent damage, glass may be coated
to reduce the COF.
In some molded-glass containers, the exterior surfaces may receive up to two coatings to reduce dam-
age and particle production. The first is applied to the container exterior as tin chloride prior to anneal-
ing, forming a thin, tin-oxide film. The second coating is applied after annealing and is organic. The
tin-oxide coating helps improve the adhesion of the organic to the glass surface, and the combination
improves the mechanical scratch- and damage-resistance of the container’s outer surface (19).
For tubular containers, a low-COF polymer chemical-composition coating can be applied to the sur-
face. These types of exterior coatings can persist through the pharmaceutical processes, such as washing
and depyrogenation, and maintain a low COF (< 0.5 under a 30 N load) (20).

### 8.1 Coefficient of Thermal Expansion

Most materials expand and contract upon heating and cooling, respectively. The extent of expansion
is dependent on the specific glass chemical composition. This expansion can be measured using tech-
niques such as push-rod dilatometry and is expressed as the change in length per temperature unit,
otherwise known as the coefficient of linear thermal expansion (CTE). The CTE for a glass is generally
reported as mean value over a specific temperature range (e.g., the slope of a linear fit over a defined
temperature range to a plot of expansion as a function of temperature is equal to the CTE). The higher
the CTE, the more the material will expand when heated and, conversely, the more it will shrink when
cooled. ASTM E228 and ISO 7991 provide guidance on measuring the CTE of glass (4-5).
Table 8.0-1 shows the different glass types used in parenteral packaging and their corresponding
CTEs. The CTE is also a main factor in determining the resistance of glass in a flawless, nonstrength-
ened state to sudden temperature changes. Increasing resistance to thermal shock is correlated with
decreasing CTE. The relationship between CTE and the temperature difference that can be endured
for different glasses is illustrated in Figure 8.1-1.
Soda-lime silicate glass with a wall thickness of 0.5 mm can withstand a maximum rapid temperature differ-
ence of approximately 110 °C without cracking. Standard borosilicate glass of the same thickness can endure
greater temperature differences. Borosilicate glasses having a CTE of 5.0 10-6 K-1 and 3.3 10-6 K-1 with a
wall thickness of 0.5 mm can withstand approximately 220 °C and 340 °C rapid temperature differences,
respectively, and are generally more resistant to thermal shock than soda-lime silicate glass. Due to the fact
that glass has poor thermal conductivity, a thinner wall thickness is recommended for thermal resistance
while a thicker wall will withstand greater mechanical loads. These calculations are theoretical, however, and
refer to glass in a flawless state. In practice, glass is never in a flawless state; therefore, the presence of surface
flaws may be more significant than the CTE alone in determining thermal shock resistance (6).

### 8.2 Stress Inside the Glass

#### 8.2.1 Melting and Solidification Behavior of Glass

The transition of a solid crystal to molten liquid occurs instantaneously at fixed melting temperatures,
T . In contrast, solid glass does not possess a melting temperature. Instead, the viscosity of glass chang-
m
es continuously as a function of temperature when going from the solid state to the liquid state. This
behavior can be illustrated using the Volume-Temperature (V-T) diagram shown in (Figure 8.2.1-1).
In order to form a typical glass, a molten liquid must be cooled below T without crystallizing, thereby
m
becoming a supercooled liquid. The point at which the slope of the V-T curve deviates from the su-
percooled liquid line marks one boundary of the “glass transformation range” (also called the “glass
transition range”). The intersection point of lines extrapolated from the solid and liquid sections of
the glass V-T curve is one definition of the glass transition temperature, T. Other descriptions for T
g g
exist within the glass community, generally within the context of the experimental technique used to
measure T such as dilatometry, calorimetry, or viscometry.
g
The T of a glass is dependent on chemical composition and is of practical importance in glassmak-
g
ing. In particular, the T lies between the annealing point and the strain point. The annealing point is
g
approximately located at the upper temperature boundary of the glass transformation range and can
be broadly defined as the temperature at which a majority of residual stress within a glass is dissipated
within several minutes. The strain point is approximately located at the lower temperature boundary of
the glass transformation and can be broadly defined as the temperature at which a majority of residual
stress within a glass is dissipated within several hours. In addition, the strain point marks a limit for
structural rearrangement of the glass (i.e., residual stress will not be removed to any significant extent
once a glass is cooled below the strain point). Knowledge of these temperature points are used to design
annealing schedules that remove a majority of forming-induced stress within a glass article.

important types of stress in the pharmaceutical packaging handling process: tensile stress and compres-
sive stress (Figure 8.2.2-1). If the atomic positions are changed by an external force (i.e., a tensile or
compressive force), the atoms will attempt to return to their original, optimal distance by counteract-
ing the external force.

##### 48.2.2.1 Thermal Stress During Container Manufacturing

During the formation of a container, the raw material is melted, and a tube is drawn and cut into
lengths. The glass has a low, even stress distribution at this point in the process. Heat must be applied
again to the glass tube when forming a container (Figure 8.2.2.1-1). Forming the container employs
temperatures of 1000 °C or greater, which causes stress at the bottom and at the opening of the con-
tainer while the rest of the tubing remains cool. While heating up one part of a glass tube, the atoms
being heated increase their oscillation. As the heated part is cooled down quickly after the forming
process, the atoms do not have sufficient time to rearrange to their former structure. Rather, they are
stuck in the position they occupied at that time, and both tensile and compressive stress is created
(Figure 8.2.2.1-1).

##### 8.2.2.1.1 Exposure to Cold Temperatures

Glass will be exposed to temperatures of approximately -40 °C to -80 °C during lyophilization and
of -150 °C to -196 °C for cold storage, including cryopreservation. The lower the CTE, the more
stable the glass is in terms of thermal shock resistance (see Section 8.1), but the rate of change must
be extremely high for thermal shock breakage. Use of glass with lower CTEs may reduce the breakage
risk due to rapid rate of change; however, surface defects still play the major role in glass breakage.
Breakage during exposure to cold temperatures are primarily due to flaws present in the glass and the
outward pressure of the internal materials, not thermal stress (8). The fewer defects there are on the
bottom area and area of drug contact, the less likely the glass will break. Section 5.7.2 discusses stress
due to mechanical expansion of the drug product during freezing. The stress due to expansion of the
product should not be confused with thermal stress. Vial breakage during the lyophilization process is
usually due to the mechanical stress from the expanding solution rather than thermal stress. Likewise,
the thermal stress does not contribute to breakage when glass is immersed in liquid nitrogen, but the
sole cause of the failure is the outward expansion force of the frozen material.

### 8.3 Mechanical Strength of Glass

#### 8.3.1 Ductile versus Brittle Materials

Along with thermal stress, physical load can introduce a great amount of stress onto glass. Breakage
will occur if the physical load is greater than the practical strength of the container.
Materials are generally differentiated into two failure mode categories: ductile and brittle. Ductile
materials, like metals (and some polymer materials), deform under tensile stress; that is, they undergo
a plastic deformation (changing their shape) rather than breaking immediately. During plastic defor-
mation, the molecules in the material are able to move. This provides the material’s ability to bend
before breaking.
Brittle materials break before the molecules in the material can move sufficiently to support bending.
The molecules are literally torn apart in place. Brittle materials, such as glasses, are unable to undergo
plastic deformation and therefore, break abruptly.

For brittle materials like glass, practical strength depends on such parameters as geometric aspects of
the glass piece, flaws inside and on the surface of the glass, residual stress inside the glass, and the inte-
rior and exterior mechanical loads on the glass. An equation to determine practical strength for brittle
materials, developed by A. A. Griffith, is shown in Figure 8.3.2-1 in a simplified manner (9). The
stress intensity factor is the estimation of how the size and geometry of the flaw amplifies the applied
tensile stress at the tip of the flaw.

### 8.4 Adsorption Layer on the Glass

When glass is freshly heated or cleaned, the surface reacts with adsorbates from ambient air (e.g.,
water, hydrocarbons) that then saturate the surface. Figure 8.4-1 shows a schematic of the reaction of
water with a glass surface. Initially, the water is only adsorbed, and a monolayer is developed. After a
few weeks, however, the water diffuses deeper into the surface (10). This adsorption layer acts like a
cover on the glass and protects it from scratches, and the more developed layer can better protect the
container from scratches. This protective layer can be removed by heating the glass (e.g., depyrogena-
tion oven); a monolayer will evaporate more quickly under dry air/heat conditions. As the kinetics of
reestablishing the protective layer are very slow; submerging the glass in water will not accelerate the
reaction.
This phenomenon of building up and evaporating the protective layer is equally valid for all glasses.
This also infers that scratches and sticking behavior is a matter of glass handling rather than the com-
position of glass itself.
The water layer not only protects the glass from scratches, but it also plays a significant role as a lu-
bricant, decreasing the friction between glass containers or between glass and metal. This property
becomes especially important during the filling process as it can limit the number of scratches that the
containers may receive, potentially allowing an increase in the speed of the filling line.

### 8.5 Chemical Strengthening

Chemical strengthening by ion exchange creates a residual compression layer on the inner and outer
glass vial surfaces that resists the applied tensile stresses required to propagate flaws. It can also hinder
minor to moderate surface damage, blocking the routes along which cracks could otherwise propa-
gate and, thereby, prevent breakage. Chemical strengthening can be performed on aluminosilicate,
borosilicate, or soda-lime silicate glass containers with sufficient available sodium ions to increase
their mechanical strength. Because the glass tubing is heated in the container formation process, any
chemical process must be performed on the final container, not the glass tubing. To avoid thermal
shock, the empty glass containers are gradually heated to the bath temperature before immersion in a
potassium melt (e.g., KNO ) where an ion exchange reaction takes place. In this reaction, sodium ions
(Na+) from the near-surface regions are exchanged for potassium ions (K+). Potassium ions are larger
in diameter than sodium ions (K+’s radius is roughly 0.38 Å larger than that of Na+), leading to com-
pressive forces that let the glass withstand higher external loads. At the end of the designated immer-
sion time, the containers are removed from the bath, allowed to cool, undergo thorough salt-removal
process, and are then dried. This surface compressive stress creates an additional barrier to prevent
the formation and propagation of checks as well as suppressing the creation of visible glass particles.
Chemical strengthening also improves the overall robustness of the vial, reducing the probability of
breakage from high-stress loading and particulate generation from fragmentation.
Applying an additional protective coating to the outer surface of the glass container, as discussed in
Section 6.3, will lower its COF and minimize surface damage during frictive sliding contact.

## 9.0 Appendix II: Investigations

Production personnel must be aware when a failure occurs. Any failure should be immediately investi-
gated; this may include conducting a risk assessment to determine the cause and the corrective action
steps to be taken. These steps should be detailed in a standard operating procedure (SOP) and the cor-
rective actions taken should be documented. Production personnel should be well trained in proper
glass handling, conducting initial-failure assessments, sample collection, and sample preparation for
fracture analysis.
Certain risks must be well understood when glass damage or breakage is observed. Risks go beyond the
immediate failure of a broken or damaged container, potentially including subsequent failure of addi-
tional containers and possible glass particulate contamination in adjacent containers on the line. Steps
should be taken to mitigate any risk by isolating and evaluating adjacent vials before batch release. If
glass fragments are observed on or near the line, it is crucial that all the other remnants be found and
collected. The objective is to ensure that no vials—chipped, damaged, or containing glass particles—
continue to be processed that pose a potential risk to subsequent steps or harm to the patient.
In order for breakage to occur there must be a:
• Force applied to the item to generate stress in the glass
• Flaw to act as the origin of the fracture
The general aim of fracture analysis is to identify the:
• Type and magnitude of force that was applied to the container
• Type and severity of the defect at the fracture origin
• Cause of the breakage, whether due to
– Defective container that failed at normally expected levels of applied force
– Excessive force applied to a commercial-quality container

### 9.1 Initial Investigation

Initial evaluations by production personnel can often help identify the root cause(s) of the failure. Ob-
servations can include the breakage pattern, line location, and relative size and number of fragments;
these comments can provide important clues as to the cause of the breakage, for example:
• A chip observed near or around the filler, or on a belt or accumulator just beyond the filler, often
indicates a filling needle strike on the mouth of the vial.
• Numerous small fragments or a complex fracture pattern observed often indicates the breakage was due

to unusual excessive forces on the container; small fragments could also occur when a primary fragment
(such as a chip) is subsequently crushed by another applied load. The excessive force may be due to
misaligned transfer mechanisms, a pinch point, a mispositioned container, or an out-of-spec container.
• A simple fracture pattern or just a few large fragments observed indicates a low-energy break, pos-
sibly a manifestation of a significant flaw or damage that occurred upstream.
Sometimes, the only corrective action needed is a simple line adjustment and a determination that
the correct change parts are in place. Other times, more extensive corrective actions may be needed.
Appendix 1, “Glass Breakage Investigation Worksheet and Checklist,” provides a simple means for
operators to record relevant information about any vial breakage they observe that will be valuable to
the initial evaluation of the failure.
Performing a detailed analysis of the breakage using fractography (fracture analysis) may be the best
way to determine or confirm the exact cause of a failure (1,2). A qualified fractographer can identify
the point of failure origin, providing clues to the type of surface defect and the cause, including the
material at contact. Fractography can also estimate the force that caused the failure. Fractography
requires that all, or as many of the fragments as possible, be collected for evaluation along with the
portion of the sample containing the fracture origin. As much as possible, fragments from different
individual vials should not be comingled; they should be treated carefully so as not to create secondary
defects on the broken glass. The collection and preservation of the samples should be conducted so as
to prevent any additional damage that may make the analysis more difficult or may obscure the true
cause of the breakage. Samples should be packaged carefully and properly identified. Depending on
the nature of the product and the containers, collecting samples of the units prior to, and following
the breakage to compare to the broken unit may help in determining the root cause (e.g., needle-strike
marks on vials following the broken unit, marks on vial necks from the capping process).

### 9.2 Root Cause Analysis

Proper analytics and root-cause determination can help identify and address systemic failures in the
glass handling process. If no trained fractographer is onsite, there are fractography services available to
outsource analysis of breakage samples. For further information on defects, including those that may
lead to breakage, refer to PDA Technical Report No. 43 (Revised 2013): Identification of Nonconformities
in Molded and Tubular Glass Containers for Pharmaceutical Manufacturing (3).
In cases where the breakage is witnessed or the cause is known, fracture analysis may provide further
information regarding the mechanics of the failure. However, if the cause of fracture is not known,
before adjustments are made to the line handling, it is very important to perform a thorough fracture
analysis to identify the exact cause of failure.
• Simple breakage patterns with a few large fragments sometimes suggest a significant flaw in the
container; the source of the flaw needs to be identified in order to be corrected. If the flaw at the
origin is related to handling, the damage source(s) must be isolated and eliminated. If the flaw is
related to forming or glass-melting, then the glass manufacturer must correct the problem.
• Complex breakage patterns with many small fragments suggest a high-energy break due to exces-
sive applied stresses. As such, a thorough review of the line would be recommended, and adjust-
ments should be made to reduce the magnitude of any forces found to be excessive.
To mitigate future incidents of glass breakage, root cause analysis may include:
• Fracture Analysis – Conducted by a qualified fractographer
• Supplier Evaluation – Review for atypical levels of incoming defects, dimensional conformity, etc.
• Incoming Supplies Inspection – Review alignment with supplier testing procedures and criteria
• Equipment and Handling – Assess areas prone to breakage, including glass-to-glass contact, glass-

to-metal contact, high back-pressure zones, transitions, and others; high-speed cameras are often
used to assess glass breakage sources on the line
• Line Clearance Procedures – Remove all open containers that could be impacted during a glass
breakage event and put in place robust line clearance procedures
• Training – Assess employee training and ensure that operations staff are trained to understand the
criticality of their response to glass breakage, factors that contribute to glass breakage, and the im-
pact on customers if defects are passed downstream
• Metrics – Review glass breakage frequency and locations of impacted lots
• Visual Inspection – Review visual inspection process and defect rates; visual inspection methods
should have defined capability for removal of glass defects and glass particles
• Packaging and Distribution – Review downstream processing for potential issues

### 9.3 Corrective and Preventive Actions

Corrective and preventive actions (CAPA) should be defined as part of the glass breakage investigation.
A list of typical CAPAs would include:
• Collaborate with supplier to eliminate the source of incoming defects and ensure high-quality supply
• Remediate sources of glass breakage found on the line, upstream and/or downstream
• Install active monitoring through a walk-down of the line or continuous operator-monitoring
• Enhance line-clearance procedures to eliminate the potential impact of glass-breakage events
• Install shielding to limit the impact of glass-breakage events
• Train operators, supervisors, and all staff to reinforce the importance of proper glass handling and
the appropriate response to glass-breakage events
• Improve detection methods (visual inspection)
