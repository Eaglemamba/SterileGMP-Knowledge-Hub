# PDA Technical Report No. 88 (2022): Microbial Data Deviation Investigations in the Pharmaceutical Industry

## 1.0 Introduction

Microbial data deviations are microbial test results that fall outside the product specification or ac-
ceptance criterion established in a drug application, drug master file, official compendia, good manu-
facturing practice regulations, or internally by the manufacturer. This includes, but is not limited to,
results generated during environmental and water monitoring as well as pharmaceutical ingredient,
in-process, and finished product testing. The validity of the test results is supported by a documented
laboratory and manufacturing investigation, where applicable.
The information presented in this technical report was compiled by a team of experts representing
both industry microbiologists and regulators and reflects current industry best practices. The mem-
bership of personnel from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) on the task force does not
imply Agency endorsement. Feedback from industry and regulatory professionals was evaluated and
incorporated into the technical report to ensure that the suggested methods, terminology, and prac-
tices for conducting microbial data deviation investigations reflect sound science and current tech-
nological approaches. This technical report is not intended to establish any mandatory or implied
standards. Published references supporting the recommendations presented are cited in the text and
listed in Section 7.0 References.
Definitions for the terms used throughout this report are provided in the Glossary. When available,
the same or similar technical definitions have been used as those included in the FDA or Interna-
tional Conference on Harmonization (ICH) guidelines. To avoid ambiguity, the task force members
believe that use and definitions of these terms should also be consistent and standardized when incor-
porated into company-specific standard operating procedures, validation protocols, and regulatory
submissions.

### 1.1 Purpose

The FDA issued a Guidance for Industry: Investigating Out of Specification (OOS) Test Results for Phar-
maceutical Production in 2006 in response to the U.S. versus Barr Laboratories decision, which is
applicable to laboratory testing results for products regulated by the Center for Drug Evaluation and
Research (CDER) (1, 2). The FDA guidance does not extend to microbiological failure investigations,
however. Consequently, PDA developed this technical report to provide approaches, specific to the
pharmaceutical industry, for investigating various types of microbial data deviations based on current
scientific knowledge and modern testing technologies. The term “microbial data deviation” is preferred
to “out-of-specification” or “out-of-limit result” in this technical report, as most microbiological tests,
especially in a sterile product manufacturing facility, are in-process tests, not finished- product tests
that must meet a regulatory-approved specification. For simplicity and consistency, the term “devia-
tion” will be used throughout this document.

### 1.2 Scope

Each of the three sections of this document focuses on distinct areas where microbial data is collected
and inspected for deviation investigations and with the following designated responsibilities:
Roles and Responsibilities—Focuses on personnel, especially microbiologists, in the labora-
tory investigations and cross-functional teams responsible for manufacturing investigations.

Conducting Laboratory Investigations (Phase I)—Provides additional details on how an
investigation may be conducted to determine that the microbial test was valid and what is the root
cause of a failure when the result is deemed invalid. Subsections focus on different microbiologi-
cal tests conducted, with points for consideration in the final determination of the investigation.
Conducting Manufacturing Investigations (Phase II)—Illustrates where a microbiologist,
acting as a subject matter expert (SME), can contribute to the evaluation of personnel activities,
environmental changes, utilities, materials, and process-related events and can assist in determin-
ing the probable cause of the deviation and the most appropriate corrective and preventive actions
(CAPA).
If needed and when appropriate, Phase I and Phase II investigation activities can be conducted in
parallel.
The roles of other contributors to deviation investigations required for effective root cause analysis,
such as representatives from manufacturing, process engineering, and quality assurance, will also be
briefly described. The composition of the investigation team will depend on the nature of the inves-
tigation.
This technical report promotes a lifecycle approach, but greater emphasis is placed on laboratory and
manufacturing investigations of marketed drug products. The approaches described may be suitable
during research and product development and the manufacture of clinical supplies, as well as the
manufacture and testing of regulatory-approved products. The microbial tests discussed in this techni-
cal report include microbial attribute testing of nonsterile products, components, sterility testing, bac-
terial endotoxin assay, mycoplasma testing, environmental and personnel monitoring, and microbial
monitoring of utilities including pharmaceutical-grade water and compressed gases.
Microbial assays of antibiotics and vitamins and the detection and enumeration of protozoans, vi-
ruses, and prions are not included in the scope of this report. Additionally, batch disposition is
separate from the outcome of a microbial investigation report. This document is directed specifically
to pharmaceutical manufacturing; however, the industry practices may be suitable for deviation in-
vestigations in the biological, consumer health, medical devices, and cosmetic industries. In addition,
these can be extended to contract manufacturing and testing organizations supporting pharmaceuti-
cal companies.
While this document provides a framework illustrating how deviations and the resulting investigations
might be conducted, each company is responsible for developing their internal policies and procedures
suitable for its specific needs that also meets current GMPs.

## 3.0 Microbiologist Roles and Responsibilities

### 3.1 Laboratory Microbiologist Responsibilities

The principal responsibility for achieving accurate laboratory testing results resides with the analyst
performing the microbiological test. Recognition of potential practices that could create invalid results
should be an integral part of the laboratory analyst’s training. The analyst is responsible for performing
all testing according to standard operating procedures (SOP) and in-house documentation practices.
The analyst should only use qualified, calibrated, and well-maintained laboratory instruments and
equipment and approved growth media, diluents, and reagents.
The analyst should check the product test results for compliance with their specifications before dis-
carding test preparations, whenever practical. If any potential or suspected laboratory errors occurred
during testing, the analyst must discontinue testing immediately and notify their laboratory manage-
ment. The analyst, with guidance and instruction from the laboratory management, must collect and
retain all test samples, reagents, consumables, paperwork, and any other relevant materials related to
the affected assay, even if the test is deemed invalid, to aid the laboratory investigation. If errors are
clear and indisputable, such as atypical appearance of a test sample (e.g., unexpected particulate matter
present), damaged test container, test sample spillage, use of wrong pipetting volumes, missing dilu-
tion steps, aseptic handling missteps, or use of the wrong testing medium, the analyst should immedi-
ately document the event. When in doubt, the analyst may consult with the laboratory management
to confirm that the test should be discontinued.
The analyst should not knowingly continue a test that they expect to invalidate at a later time for an
assignable cause (i.e., analyses should not be completed for the sole purpose of seeing that a passing
result can be obtained when obvious errors are known). In addition, the analyst is responsible for as-
sisting management with the laboratory investigation.

### 3.2 Microbiologist Role in a Manufacturing Investigation

The key to successful manufacturing investigations of microbial testing failures with pharmaceutical
ingredients and finished drug products is the inclusion of an experienced microbiologist as an SME
on the manufacturing investigation team. This microbiologist may work in the quality control (QC)
microbiology testing laboratory at the manufacturing site, the quality control unit (QCU), product
development, or a corporate microbiology support group. Ideally, the microbiologist should have a
comprehensive knowledge of product formulation and specific manufacturing operations, obtained
from frequent shop-floor visits or surveillance of the overall process. For smaller organizations, with-
out internal microbiological expertise, the microbiologist may be an outside consultant. To be truly
effective, the SME must be a trained microbiologist with broad experience in microbiological investi-
gations. Furthermore, it would be advantageous if the SME is familiar with the actual product impli-
cated in the failure. Without this expertise on the investigation team, it is less likely that the root cause
of the failure can be determined, and the investigation may be hampered or severely limited.
The microbiologist should be engaged in the investigation as soon as is practical, as microbiological
investigations are always time-sensitive.
The microbiologist should be part of the first step in an investigation, such as performing a walk-
through of the manufacturing area, to become oriented and identify situations and events that are
nonroutine and may be directly linked to the deviation. To be successful, the microbiologist must
have access to all related testing results, have the cooperation of manufacturing managers, supervisors,
operators, and support personnel, and have the authority to interview staff, inspect processing equip-
ment, take investigatory microbial samples, and review change control and batch records. In some
instances, access to customer complaint records may be necessary. For failures related to purchased
pharmaceutical ingredients, it is highly desirable that a microbiologist join any audit team visiting the
ingredient supplier to conduct an investigational review.

Systematic, proactive risk assessments for assessing the overall aseptic manufacturing process and con-
trols have been proven helpful in identifying potential weaknesses in advance, and may mitigate the
occurrence of a deviation, for example, sterility failures and endotoxin or environmental monitoring
(EM) excursions (12).

### 3.3 Microbiology Laboratory Management Responsibilities

Upon notification by a laboratory analyst of a suspected laboratory error, the laboratory management
must conduct an objective assessment to determine if an error has occurred. Data should be reviewed
promptly to ensure the contemporaneous memory of events and to determine if the results might be
attributed to an isolated or systematic laboratory error. In the absence of laboratory error, a confirmed
valid test could indicate problems in the manufacturing operations; however, all facts need to be evalu-
ated before predetermining a cause. The correct compliance assumption is that the results are valid
until proven otherwise.
Laboratory management should ensure that the probable cause is supported by evidence, using root
cause analysis tools when appropriate (e.g., 5 Whys, Ishikawa/fishbone diagram). Laboratory manage-
ment should promptly notify the QCU and any impacted departments of the suspected deviation and
inform them that a laboratory investigation has been initiated and when it might be completed.
If testing is conducted by a contract testing laboratory, the quality agreement between the contract
testing laboratory and the manufacturer should clearly assign responsibilities, include a timeline, and
specify the requirements of the laboratory investigation.

### 3.4 Subject Matter Expert Responsibilities

SMEs can support the investigation by providing technical assistance related to the assay methods and
technologies utilized, designing any investigational testing, and providing general scientific guidance,
as needed. This expert could have greater experience and scientific background related to the assay or
manufacturing process from within the company. Hiring a qualified outside consultant may also be
an option.

### 3.5 Quality Control Unit Responsibilities

The QCU oversees all aspects of the microbial deviation investigation. A QCU representative or other
approved personnel would be responsible for coordinating with the cross-functional team involved in
supporting the investigation, including personnel from manufacturing, facilities, calibration, valida-
tion; approving any laboratory retesting plans; and approving the laboratory and manufacturing inves-
tigations. The QCU also would be responsible for overseeing quality agreements made with contract
testing laboratories and for ensuring that microbial deviation investigations are adequately addressed.

## 4.0 Phase I: Conducting the Microbiology Laboratory Investigation

The first phase of a microbial data deviation (deviation) investigation is an initial assessment of the results
reported by the microbiology laboratory. The purpose of the laboratory investigation is to establish the
validity of test results, thereby confirming that a deviation has occurred. If a laboratory error has occurred,
which would invalidate the test, the QCU would approve retesting and/or resampling of the pharmaceuti-
cal ingredient or drug product/drug substance. Some compendial microbiology testing chapters have lim-
ited information to address how to handle sample deviations and investigations (13-14). This technical re-
port will discuss additional factors for microbiological evaluation of sterility, bacterial endotoxin, and other
microbiological testing conditions for invalidating test results or allowing an initial test to be repeated.
Pharmaceutical QC microbiology laboratories vary in size and complexity. These factors may impact
the level of experience with dealing with deviations in terms of the training, experience, and academic
background of the analysts and laboratory management.
Contrary to the belief that microbial failure investigations are usually inconclusive, experience has demon-
strated that the causes of the microbial contamination are determinable through robust investigations con-
ducted by teams that include an experienced microbiologist. In the absence of a definitive cause, the most
probable cause(s) can be determined based on the available information and follow-up observations, ad-
ditional testing, and batch-record review. To avoid a confirmation bias, the team must be willing to reject
their original hypothesis about the probable cause in light of the evidence subsequently collected. If possible,
as many questions should be asked as to why the probable cause is not correct as are asked about why it is
correct. Hindsight, where the successes of past microbial failure investigations often are exaggerated and may
incorrectly influence the direction of the current investigation, is another bias that must be recognized and
avoided. However, a history of conducting investigations will enhance the success with current investigations.
When no obvious laboratory errors are noted, the laboratory management must initiate a laboratory
investigation promptly and must complete it as soon as is practically possible in accordance with
regulatory expectations and company policies and procedures. Accurately recording the sequence of
activities may prove helpful in determining root cause during an investigation. Some tests, such as
microbial identification, may require more time and may delay completion of the laboratory investiga-
tion. Figure 4.0-1 presents a generalized fishbone diagram to be used as guidance for how to evaluate
the inputs of microbial testing during root cause analysis.

If a laboratory error (Phase I investigation) cannot be found that invalidates the results, the original
test results are presumed to be correct. The next step, then, is to conduct a Phase II investigation,
including review of manufacturing batch records and other facility documents available to find the
possible cause of the deviation or trend that may have occurred during manufacturing. Specific points
to consider might include:
• If an analyst thinks a mistake has been made in any part of the testing, including sample prepara-
tion, the assay should be terminated, and the justification documented.
• Analysts should be instructed to neither destroy nor discard the sample, sample-preparation dilution
tubes, or reagents, when possible, until the final results are known, confirmed, and released. Compar-
ing them with the product specification may be an important part of the laboratory investigation.
• Caution should be used in conducting investigational assays. They are not considered retests,
but a response to the deviation and should not be used to release material. A laboratory SOP
should precisely describe the procedure(s) that must be followed if a deviation is observed. Such a
document eliminates unplanned decisions regarding the appropriateness of investigational testing,
retesting, or resampling. When appropriate, retesting can be conducted according to established
compendial directives.
• Investigational testing provides the laboratory an opportunity to assess the hypotheses regarding
possible reagent or analytical failures in testing; it frequently aims to identify and change a variable
(e.g., analyst) from the first failed test. Investigational testing should be well-defined and justified,
preapproved, and well-documented. Values generated from the investigational test may provide a
clue as to the actual or potential product contamination or a mishandling of a positive control or
a tube mix-up. The purpose of performing investigational tests is not to reverse the original devia-
tion, but to gain information regarding a potential failure, so a more effective effort can be made
to pinpoint the root cause and implement any necessary CAPAs.
Fully documenting each step in the lab investigation is critical. Laboratory management should de-
termine not only the reliability of the individual test result obtained, but also the significance of the
deviation. If the deviation is verified to be correct, they must determine what that represents about the
laboratory quality assurance program and what impact the final report may have on other product lots.
Laboratory management should continue to monitor for developing trends.
General points to consider during the investigation phase include:
• Having all QC challenge microorganisms genotypically and phenotypically profiled may be ad-
vantageous so a library is available to compare product isolates in the event of cross-contamination
during testing.
• If growth is detected on the negative control media, these isolates should be identified for inves-
tigative purposes.
• The two-fold variance described in USP <61> and <1111> as the maximum acceptable count ap-
plies only to approved specifications in cfu/g or mL, expressed as a power of 10 (15, 17).
• Nonsterile products tested at one or two product dilutions for bioburden (e.g., 1:10, 1:100) may
need to be retested at higher product dilutions to obtain a countable number of colonies, if the
highest product dilution tested yields a result too numerous to count.
• Due to the variability of microbiological results, the investigation should not be limited to the
specific batch but extended to multiple batches, excipients, or APIs over a selected period of time.
• Include historical trend data for the assay, controls, and any additional factors that may support
the investigation hypothesis and/or investigational results.

### 4.1 Microbial Identification

Identifying the microorganism(s) implicated in product failure is a critical step in a laboratory investi-
gation and, subsequently, in the manufacturing investigation. Knowledge of the source of the micro-
organism may lead to a possible route to determine which microorganism contaminated the product.
Microbiological identification systems are based on different analytical methodologies, with their in-
herent limitations, and the databases related to the system. Identification is accomplished by matching
characteristics (genotypic, phenotypic, or proteotypic) to an established standard (reference) microor-
ganism, such as a strain-type in the database. If a microorganism is not included in the library or da-
tabase of the system, it will not be identified. Therefore, pharmaceutical manufacturers should review
the breadth of the database of the identification system they plan to use and its applicability to their
use requirements.
As stated in USP <1113> Microbial Characterization, Identification and Strain Typing, the hierarchy of
microbial identification errors of impact, in descending order, is (1) misidentification to genera, (2)
misidentification to species, and (3) no identification (16). Incorrect identifications could lead to a
faulty investigation, inappropriate CAPAs, and ill-advised product disposition. For example, a mis-
identification may result in a nonsterile drug product being released when it contains an objectionable
microorganism. If the in-house identification system cannot identify the isolate or if there is doubt
regarding the identification, it should be sent to a contract testing laboratory for confirmation; other-
wise, a misidentified microorganism will always be carried in the records.
The need for microbial identification is specifically cited in USP <62> Microbiological Examination of
Nonsterile Products: Tests for Specified Microorganisms. This chapter presents a requirement for confir-
matory identification tests for organisms that grow on selective or diagnostic media and demonstrate
defined morphological characteristics (18). As a related aspect, USP <71> Sterility Tests allows for
invalidation of the test if, after identification of the microorganism(s) isolated from the test, the con-
tamination may be unequivocally ascribed to faults with respect to the material and/or the technique
used in conducting the sterility test procedure (discussed further in Section 4.2.1) (13).
Taking a polyphasic identification approach is recommended, that is, one that assembles and assimi-
lates many levels of information from molecular, physiological, morphological, serological, or ecologi-
cal sources to classify a microorganism. Some vendors of identification instruments encourage micro-
biologists to forgo the preliminary characterization steps, such as colony description, Gram reaction,
cellular morphology description, and/or diagnostic screening. Forgoing these steps is not recommend-
ed as it often necessary to assess the microorganism identification against all available information to
determine if the identification makes sense. For example, Salmonella typhi is unlikely to be isolated
from a cleanroom, but this pathogen could be present in an animal-derived ingredient.
Another consideration is that many phenotypic identification systems require the preliminary charac-
terization of the isolate for the selection of the appropriate identification kit. An incorrect Gram stain
may result in a misidentification and flawed investigation. Criticality of this routine test should not
be overlooked.

#### 4.2.1 Sterility Testing Investigations

If a laboratory sterility test result used for release testing of a pharmaceutical or biological drug product
or a medical device indicates microbial growth in the microbiological growth medium (i.e., turbidity,
pellicle formation, precipitation, or flocculent growth) during the incubation period and is subsequently
confirmed via subculture and/or Gram reaction, it raises the question: Was the observed growth a result
of a sampling error, laboratory error, or a true product contamination? This scenario with a finished
product is commonly referred to as an ‘out of specification’ (OOS) test result.’ Due to the unique nature
of viable microorganisms, unlike chemical analytes, microbiologists also describe these aberrant results
as a ‘microbiological data deviation’ (MDD) The response to this presumptive failure to meet USP <71>
requirements is to investigate the source of the microbial contamination in order to confirm the validity
of the test results. The purpose of the investigation is to confirm that the test results were obtained while
utilizing good laboratory practices, including proper aseptic technique, appropriate equipment and re-
agents, and sterile assay media/components within an appropriate Grade A/ISO 5 environment.
Deviations in equipment, procedures, or personnel performance that require corrective action should
be discovered during the investigation. If the positive sterility test is determined to be valid, an inves-
tigation will be conducted to determine the root cause of the manufacturing practice that caused the
tested product lot to become contaminated. If no definitive root cause is determined, then the manu-
facturing corrective actions might be based on the probable causes identified and the measures defined
to prevent recurrence of such an event. The decision on the disposition of the batch needs to consider
that, as no analytical cause was identified, the batch might be nonsterile. If a laboratory investigation
concludes that errors or events caused the test contamination to come from the laboratory environ-
ment or met other compendial invalidation criteria, the sterility test result would be invalidated, and
the sterility test would be repeated with the same number of units and quantity of product.
USP <71> requires that the sterility test media be visually examined for evidence of microbial growth
at intervals during the incubation and at its conclusion. This evidence may present itself as turbid-
ity throughout the media, a pellicle growing on the surface of the soybean-casein digest medium
(SCDM), growth at the interface between the aerobic and anaerobic portions of the fluid thioglycol-
late medium (FTM), as floccular growth within the media, or as precipitation at the bottom of the
media. The mode of growth should be documented within the sterility test report.

Although not specifically recommended in USP <71>, microscopic examination of suspected micro-
bial growth and subculturing the contents of test media container/enclosed membrane canisters are
common industry practice. The sampling of the media must be done aseptically to avoid additional
microbial contamination. Incubation of the original container must complete the full 14-day period
described in USP <71> in order to ensure discovery of slower growing microorganisms. Aliquot re-
moval from the canisters (or sterility test vessel used for direct inoculation) can be challenging when
using a sterile Pasteur pipette (or sterile syringe) as it is difficult to assure the same subsequent culture
growth conditions that may be needed in the event of an investigation. For example, the proximity
of the growth within the media may be diagnostic as to the type of microorganism(s) that had been
recovered. Secondly, it is not advisable to disperse the macroscopic growth within the media, especially
for FTM because the aeration of the media may be fatal for the presence of strict anaerobes.
Subculturing to isolate the suspected microbial contaminant may include:
• Transferring up to 10 mL of the test media into fresh SCDM and FTM and incubating the media
for at least 14 days at 20-25 °C and 30-35 °C, respectively. The subculture time of incubation
should mimic the time period that was necessary for the original time of incubation in order to
optimize microbial recovery required by the targeted species (for example, some environmental
fungi require the lower temperature range and are inhibited by the higher temperature range).
• Streaking out from the test media onto soybean-casein digest agar (SCDA) and Sabouraud dex-
trose agar (SDA) and incubating aerobically at 30-35 °C for SCDA and 20-25 °C for SDA, until
microbial growth is obtained and/or,
• Alternative enrichment medium (i.e., Columbia blood agar) may also be used for subculturing
from the primary SCDM and FTM to support slow growing fastidious microorganism.
The medium and mode of growth within the medium will provide insight as to whether the microor-
ganism is a strict anaerobe, facultative anaerobe, or strict aerobe and what subculturing method would
be most appropriate. Any isolates should be identified at least to species and, if available, strain-typed
to aid the follow-up investigations with isolates recovered from the manufacturing or material biobur-
den. If subculturing fails to recover the microorganism, then a direct Gram stain on an aliquot of the
suspect microbial growth should be performed. If no microbial presence is detected with the direct
Gram stain, the turbidity may be a result of product-media precipitation or an artifact of the media
after prolonged incubation (for example, the low percent of agar in the FTM can precipitate out of
solution and causes medium turbidity).
Careful review and documentation of the microbial appearance within the SCDM and the FTM test
tubes or canisters before any inadvertent jostling or shaking This is especially true when the product
may potentially make the test media turbid or cause a precipitation (e.g., suspensions or liposome-
based drug products). These observations should be recorded in the sterility test report or, if available,
photomicrographs can be taken with a camera mounted on the microscope.
In the absence of the isolation of a microorganism on subculture of the test media and/or no micro-
scopic evidence of the presence of microorganisms, the deviation sterility test failure is not confirmed
and, therefore, the product passes the test.
USP <71>, which has been harmonized with the European Pharmacopoeia and Japanese Pharmaco-
poeia, is frequently used as the method of choice for finished product release and is cited in many
regulatory applications (19, 20). The section entitled “Observation and Interpretation of Results”
describes the four acceptable conditions that may be used to invalidate the initial test result and allow
for retesting:
1. The data of the microbiological monitoring of the sterility testing facility show a fault (Table 4.2.1-1)
2. A review of the testing procedure used during the test in question reveals a fault (Table 4.2.1-2)
3. Microbial growth is found in the negative controls (Section 4.2.1.1)

4. After determination of the identity of the microorganisms isolated from the test, the growth of
this species (or these species) may be ascribed unequivocally to faults with respect to the material
and/or the technique used in conducting the sterility test procedure (Section 4.2.1.2)
If evidence of microbial growth is found, the product to be examined does not comply with the test
for sterility unless it can be clearly demonstrated that the test was invalid for causes unrelated to the

#### 4.2.2 Bacterial Endotoxins Testing Investigations

Compendial bacterial endotoxins testing (BET) detect and/or quantify lipopolysaccharide cell membrane
components derived from the outer-cell membrane of Gram-negative bacteria that are most commonly
responsible for human pyrogenic responses. This method is more comparable to chemical assay than to the
traditional methods used for the detection and enumeration of microorganisms in pharmaceuticals, bio-
pharmaceuticals, and medical devices. For this reason, the enzyme cascade-based Limulus amoebocyte lysate
(LAL) BET has unique analytical requirements that may affect the reliability of the test results. USP <85>
Bacterial Endotoxin Tests describes the procedures for performing both the gel clot and spectrophotometric
methods for detecting and quantifying bacterial endotoxins. Because the methods are based on enzymatic
components and principles, incubation conditions (e.g., temperature and time) are important, and labora-

tory analysts should have a technical understanding of the potential inhibition and enhancement of the
product matrix on the product/lysate mixture. Another critical factor is the measurement and, if necessary,
the adjustment of the product–lysate mixture to fall within the required pH range as stated in the lysate
instructions for use. If the pH is not within the specified range, the endotoxin test results can be significantly
altered from the true results (usually lower). Therefore, it is important that analysts understand the details
of the compendial test and are trained to recognize how changes in the procedures may cause erroneous
results is imperative (14). Section 4.2.2 describes general approaches for investigating BET deviations and
provides guidance and references that contain detailed protocols on how to conduct investigations.
A deviation may be due to one of the following from a partial list of reasons:
• Error during product dilution (excessive volume transfer during serial dilution preparation), in-
correct volume (too little or too much) during reagent rehydration, misplacement of handled test
tubes causing a control standard endotoxin (CSE) serial dilution for positive control preparation
and inhibition and enhancement results to be incorrect, mislabeling of test tubes, or a test tube
mix-up between product and CSE serial dilutions
• CSE cross-contamination caused by pipette tips (or pipets) used repeatedly
• Automated pipettors or bulbs contaminated with aspirated endotoxin solution because of im-
proper use or not being cleaned routinely
• Working on a lab bench with a nearby splash sink that creates water aerosols (tap water commonly
contaminated with Gram-negative bacteria)
• Analyst not performing the test according to the prescribed method
• Reagent(s) or equipment not adequately depyrogenated or not handled aseptically with pyrogen-
free water
• Unexpected occurrence during the analysis that interrupted the completion of the gel clot (e.g.,
table vibration from draw closure or fan vibration in air-circulating stand-up incubators)
• Glucans, at high enough concentrations, present in the test material may cause false positives
• Contaminated water baths used for incubation of gel clot test tubes
Section 4.2.2.1 compiles a number of factors important to BET that, if not performed correctly, may
culminate in a faulty analysis.

##### 4.2.2.1 Compendial Bacterial Endotoxins Test

When faced with an endotoxin LAL OOS result it would be best to start with a review of the manda-
tory requirements and controls in the harmonized compendial chapter, USP <85>. As with any testing
used to release products, performing these key analytical steps and documenting them accurately and
contemporaneously as required by company SOPs and the lysate manufacturer’s instructions for use is
essential. These records will prove critical in the event the laboratory management or auditors review
or question the validity of the results. The harmonized compendial chapters include specific consider-
ations for addressing BET analytical anomalies:
• In the section for INTERPRETATION of the harmonized gel-clot method, when a positive result
is found for only one duplicate of Solution A (product only), the test should be repeated. In the
repeat test, the preparation under test complies with the test if a negative result is found for both
replicates of Solution A. The preparation does not comply with the test if a positive result is found
for one or both replicates of Solution A. However, if the preparation does not comply with the
test at a dilution less than the maximum valid dilution (MVD), the test may be repeated using a
greater product dilution, not exceeding the MVD.
• Along with each gel-clot Limit and Quantitative Test, a 2 λ positive product control, a 2 λ positive
control in BET water, and a negative control with just BET water should be included.
• For the Photometric Quantitative Techniques, the absolute value of the correlation coefficient, r,
must be 0.980 for the range of endotoxin concentrations used.
• For the Photometric Quantitative Techniques, to be considered free of factors that interfere with
the assay under the conditions of the test, the measured concentration of the endotoxin added

to the sample solution must be within 50%–200% of the known added endotoxin concentra-
tion after subtraction of any endotoxin detected in the solution without added endotoxin. Note:
Knowing how to interpret the value of the positive product control (PPC) is very important when
reading either under or over the allowed range. Even an assay within the allowable PPC range may
still give false test results, which may be caused by the natural endotoxin in the test sample and the
spiked CSE combining to give an acceptable value for this control.
• Additional suggestions on how to investigate bacterial endotoxin deviation can be found in
USP <1085> Guidelines on the Endotoxins Test, in the section entitled “Out-of-Specification Re-
sults and Retesting Considerations” (21).
• For those laboratories responsible for performing BET on sterile implantable devices or combina-
tion products (one that includes a medical device), specific guidance is provided in USP <161>
Medical Devices–Bacterial Endotoxin and Pyrogen Tests (22). For medical device testing, careful
attention to the compendial requirements should be given to performing the bacterial endotoxin
extraction step from the device products.

##### 4.2.2.2 FDA Guidance

When performing a deviation investigation for results associated with BET, the 2012 FDA Guidance
for Industry: Pyrogen and Endotoxin Testing: Questions and Answers may offer valuable insights as to
some regulatory expectations (23). In general, the guidance suggests that the sampling plan used by
firms should consider the potential presence of bacterial endotoxin contamination in raw materials,
in-process materials, and the finished product. Specifically, they should consider aspects of the manu-
facturing design, including consistency of a manufacturing process, impact of in-process hold-times,

#### 4.2.3 Antimicrobial Effectiveness Testing Investigations

The test methods and acceptance criteria for different dosage forms are defined in USP <51> Anti-
microbial Effectiveness Testing. In general, the test is only applicable to aqueous, multiple-use sterile
and nonsterile drug products that may be subject to microbial contamination during use. USP <51>
defines aqueous as a water activity (A ) of more than 0.6. Products with A < 0.6 will not support the
w w
growth of microorganisms and may be demonstrably self-preserving. The test confirms the effective-
ness of the antimicrobial preservative system and/or other supporting physicochemical attributes to
meet product category criteria. Although a drug product would be required to meet the test criteria
during development and to be demonstrated as effective in a long-term stability study, the test is
a shelf-life specification, but not a release requirement. In contrast, the preservative concentration
required within the formulation to meet the product category criteria at release and at expiry is a
product specification included in the regulatory submission. Typically, USP <51> is looked upon as a
stability-indicating pass/fail test, where the results in terms of log reduction of the challenge organisms
at specified time intervals are evaluated against the log reduction requirement for the product category.
Given the inherent variability of the test method, marginally preserved products have a potential to
fail at one or more time intervals in long-term stability studies supporting marketed products (27).

#### 4.2.4 Mycoplasma Testing Investigations

Mycoplasma contamination is a serious concern when using mammalian, plant, and insect cell cul-
tures in the development and production of cell-derived biopharmaceutical products for human use.
These products may include viral vaccines, monoclonal antibodies, growth factors, replacement thera-

pies, and gene and cell therapies. Contamination of cell culture ingredients and cell lines represent a
potential patient safety risk as well as an economical loss in terms of reduced product yields, rejections,
and recalls. To minimize these risks, master and working cell lines or banks, cell culture ingredients,
inoculum expansions, process materials, and finished products are routinely screened for mycoplasma.
The most common contaminating mycoplasma species (95%), in descending order, are Mycoplasma
orale (human cell lines), M. hyorhinus (porcine cell lines), M. arginini (bovine cell lines), M. fermentans
(human cell lines), M. hominis (human cell lines), and Acholeplasma laidlawii (bovine cell lines).

#### 4.2.5 Biological Indicators Investigations

Biological indictors (BI) are used during the validation of moist heat, dry heat, and gaseous steriliza-
tion processes and during vapor-phase hydrogen peroxide decontamination cycles. BIs may also be
used for monitoring routine cycles to support the release of products that are used by the pharmaceuti-
cal industry. Additionally, a BI can be used as a load monitor to support parametric release of moist-
heat sterilized products in the U.S. Typically, they consist of high numbers of resistant bacterial spores
with characterized calibrated D-values that are included in sterilization loads, retrieved, and cultured
to demonstrate the lethality of their destruction during the sterilization cycle.
It is an expectation that a company will conduct confirmatory testing of the population and purity stated
in the certificate of analysis of the incoming BI lot and growth of the cultured spore strip. Low counts may
reflect the incomplete recovery from the matrix supporting the bacterial spores, whereas the growth of a heat-
resistant spore-forming bacteria like Geobacillus stearothermophilus from a BI is most likely caused by contami-
nation of the laminar flow hood and/or test supplies from positive controls in the testing environment (30).
Probable causes of failure would include gross mishandling, insufficient exposure to the sterilization
parameters, and higher BI D-values that result in a lower probability of destruction in the sterilization
cycle. Other points to consider include:
• Were the BIs stored and handled prior to use, as recommended by the manufacturer?
• Were the BIs incubated in accordance with regulatory expectations and manufacturer instructions?
• Were the BIs used in the manner prescribed by the manufacturer?
• Was the appropriate BI used for the product being tested (e.g., ampoule BI used for liquid products)?
• In cases where the BI is directly inoculated into a solution or onto a substrate, what is the D-value
as used in that form?
PDA Technical Report 51 Biological Indicators for Gas and Vapor-Phase Decontamination Processes:
Specification, Manufacture, Control and Use provides further guidance on this topic (31).
4.2.6 Aseptic Process Simulation Failures (Media Fills)
Proper design and execution of aseptic process simulations can be found in PDA Technical Report 22: Pro-
cess Simulation for Aseptically Filled Products, FDA Guidance for Industry: Sterile Drug Products Produced by
Aseptic Processing—Current Good Manufacturing Practice, PIC/S Annex 1: Manufacture of Sterile Medicinal
Products, Guide to GMP for Medicinal Products, and ISO 13408-5 (3, 5, 32, 33). Any media-filled contain-
ers that, upon inspection, score as “turbid” during the incubation period should be examined by a microbi-
ologist and transferred to the microbiology laboratory for investigation. The growth of microorganisms in
culture medium may include any slight haze, pellicle formation, floccular amassment, or precipitation. The
first task, prior to opening the container to subculture the media, is to establish if the container is flawed in
any manner that would compromise the sterility of the medium. Damage may take the form of inadequate
crimps to a stoppered vial, leakage around the container closure, or chips or cracks in the container that

could threaten integrity. Any damage should be adequately recorded and, if possible, supplemented with
photographs. Involving packaging experts in the laboratory investigation who can evaluate the extent of
damage may be helpful, for instance, in performing physicochemical integrity testing to confirm that the
damage would result in microbial ingress. The media fill may be inspected for damaged/nonintegral units,
and those units should be discarded. The rationale for excluding these units from the final run tally should
be fully documented and the deviation should be explained in the media fill report. (3, 34).
In the absence of obvious damage, the contents of a turbid container would be aseptically sampled and
subcultured with appropriate solid media and incubated at 20–25 °C and 30–35 °C for three to seven
days. The contents of the media fill containers may be examined microscopically for the presence of
microorganisms. Any isolates should be identified at least to species and, preferably, strain-typed to aid
the follow-up investigations. Guidance may be found in USP <1113> (16).

#### 4.2.8 Utilities Monitoring

##### 4.2.8.1 Pharmaceutical-Grade Water Systems

The purpose of the laboratory investigation is to determine whether a definitive source of sampling
and/or laboratory error contributed to the root cause of the deviation. This section addresses the initial
and extended laboratory investigations associated with samples collected from pharmaceutical-grade
water systems, such as purified water and water for injection (WFI).
Microbiological testing of pharmaceutical water systems, including the source water (i.e., potable wa-
ter) and purification steps, typically consists of total viable microbial count, BET, and/or a test for the
absence of coliforms, depending on the grade of water and any local regulatory requirements. After
a pharmaceutical water system is successfully validated, both the system and the source are subject
to periodic microbial monitoring using standard methods. Action limits and alert levels should be
established to recognize adverse trends in the water system. These alert levels or action limits should
be based on historical data, the intended purpose of the sampling plan, as well as the recommended
compendial or regulatory limits. In general, specifications are not set for water systems.
In a well-designed, well-operated pharmaceutical water system, sanitized by maintaining a minimum
turbulence level and elevated temperature (> 65 °C) or periodic heat or chemical sanitization, test
results above the action limit should be very rare.
Several events can signal that a laboratory investigation should be conducted:
• A single test result that exceeds an action limit
• Multiple test results that exceed an alert level, thus indicating an adverse trend
• Isolation of microorganisms of concern based on the dosage forms manufactured in the facility
The cross-functional team conducting the laboratory investigation should review the collection and
microbiological testing of the subject water sample to determine any potential sampling and labora-
tory errors. The data collected should include the sampling location, sample containers, sampling
technique, and any alert level or action limit excursions associated with the sampling location, loop, or
water system, and the operators collecting the water samples.
If this investigation does not identify a definitive source of laboratory error to explain the deviation
result, the laboratory should immediately notify the owner of the utility system, operational users, and
the QCU.

##### 4.2.8.1.1 Evaluation of Sample Handling

The methods used for preparing, storing, and attaching hoses and sampling apparatus to the sample
ports are critical. Regulatory guidance recommends that each point of use should be flushed using the
same flushing procedure used in the manufacturing process (35).
The potential for changes in the microbial population in samples should be limited by restricting the
holding time before testing and controlling the storage temperature. If the samples cannot be tested
within two hours, they should be transported and stored at refrigeration temperature for a maximum
of 24 hours. In situations where even 24 hours is not possible (such as when using off-site contract
laboratories), it is particularly important to qualify the microbiological sample hold times and storage
conditions. (See APHA/AWWA/WEF Standard Methods, USP <1231>, EMA 496873, and Japan: G8
for guidance (28, 36-38). Prior to removing the test aliquot, all samples should be adequately mixed
to ensure a homogeneous mixture is achieved. Any parameters for storage and transportation of water
samples that are different from standard methods should be qualified to ensure the samples are not
negatively affected, resulting in potentially invalid results.

##### 4.2.8.1.2 Evaluation of Test Methods

Review of all materials associated with the testing and their appropriateness for the grade of water
tested is required in a laboratory investigation. Sterility of the membrane filters, sample containers, pi-
pettes, and utensils used to perform testing must be suitable for their intended use and must conform
within sterilization dating. Also, all diluents and reagents used must be suitable (such as sterile and/or
endotoxin-free) and within their expiry dating.
Any deviation from these requirements must be evaluated and investigated. Confluent microbial
growth, in contrast to discrete colonies on the membranes or pour plates, may be indicative of equip-
ment contamination. The position of the analyst’s head, hands, and arms in the path of the airflow
(preferably in a laminar flow hood with horizontal air flow) over the test materials may also be a source
of microbial contamination (personnel-related considerations are discussed further in Section 5.2.1).
The identity of the microorganism(s) associated with the investigation may also reveal information
about the source of contamination. The most common water isolates are Gram-negative, nonferment-
ing, rod-shaped bacteria historically described as pseudomonads, which may be indicators for a biofilm
formation within the water system. The recovery of organisms that do not thrive or survive under the
low ionic strength/low carbon conditions found in a pharmaceutical water system may suggest con-
tamination of the sample or insufficient control or flushing of the sampling apparatus and/or hoses
attached at the port. Gram-positive cocci commonly found on the human skin, such as Staphylococ-
cus epidermidis, can be indicative of potential sampling contamination, while Gram-positive spore-
formers, such as Bacillus species or the fungus Cladosporium species, can be indicative of potential
environmental contamination.

##### 4.2.8.1.3 Evaluation of Trending Data

Reviewing the historical test data will help determine if an adverse trend is associated with individual
points-of-use, loops, and water systems. This information will be also useful for the manufacturing
investigation.

### 4.3 Phase I: Conclusion and Next Steps

Evaluation and questions related to the Phase I deviation investigation must use strong, evidence-
based data. The investigation may not be definitive but may suggest probable cause(s). The Phase I
investigation commonly concludes with one of three possible outcomes:
1. Events uncovered during the investigation may be responsible for the deviation and present a
clear root cause that is directly responsible for the deviation. Examples could show contamination
due to failure to follow SOPs, use of poor aseptic techniques during either sampling or testing, a
recognized laboratory error, or the use of nonsterile testing materials. There may be a direct link
between isolate identification and the same contamination from the testing environment. In these
cases, the test may be considered invalid.
In the case of invalid tests, the Phase I investigation would conclude without advancing to the
Phase II manufacturing investigation, or where Phase I and Phase II investigations were under-
taken concurrently, the cancellation of the Phase II investigation would be warranted. Depending
on the sample type and source of contamination, resampling or retesting may also be considered
as long as the QCU agrees. CAPAs must be implemented (preferably prior to any retesting or
resampling), however, in order to remediate the original cause of the invalidation.
2. While the investigation might have revealed issues, there may be insufficient evidence to directly
correlate the investigative findings to the deviation. For example, if the EM conducted during the
testing showed microorganisms present but the organisms were a different genus and species than the
contamination within the sample, the environmental results may cast doubt on the sample, but they

would not invalidate the original result. Thus, the Phase I laboratory investigation would be found
inconclusive, not providing sufficient evidence to invalidate the result, and the result is deemed to be
valid. The investigation would then proceed to a Phase II manufacturing investigation.
3. The results of the investigation did not show any evidence of testing failure. In other words, it can be
clearly demonstrated that all procedures were followed, analysts were properly qualified to perform
the testing, and all materials, equipment, and testing environment were considered appropriate for
the test. The calculations for reporting data and all final results were correct and verified. Therefore,
the deviation is confirmed and deemed valid, so the investigation must proceed to Phase II in order
to determine the source of contamination from the processes used during manufacturing.

## 5.0 Phase II: Manufacturing Investigation

Once laboratory error has been eliminated or found inconclusive through the Phase I investigative
process, the investigation focuses on the manufacturing facility, personnel, processes, and materials. A
manufacturing investigation includes, but is not limited to, several principal elements:
• The cross-functional investigation team must represent all departments responsible for handling
the deviated sample whether it is a pharmaceutical excipient, in-process intermediate, drug sub-
stance, or drug product. This component is essential to conducting an effective investigation. The
QCU or another suitable individual may lead the team, which should include representatives from
manufacturing, plant engineering, process validation, maintenance, and quality control. In a mi-
crobiological-related investigation, the contribution of a microbiologist on the team will be critical.
• The QCU should have overall responsibility for managing or approving the outcome of the manu-
facturing investigation and for notifying upper management of the investigation findings and
batch disposition.
• Each investigation should be thoroughly documented and include a time for completion, as speci-
fied by company policies and procedures. Microbiological-related investigations are complex and
may not be able to be completed within the specified time period; in these cases, an SOP-governed
mechanism should be in place for extending the completion date. This extension must be justified
and supported by the QCU.
While additional assessments can be performed if required by company policy or procedure, every
manufacturing investigation should include the following actions:
• Determine all batches in scope. Does the initial scope of the investigation need to be expand-
ed based on laboratory findings? Batches under investigation should not be released.
• Determine the definitive or probable cause (root cause analysis). The investigation must
be comprehensive, especially if the probable cause may not be identified. When conducting the
root cause analysis, all aspects must be considered, and a thoroughly objective approach must be
taken to evaluate the cause of the deviation. Identifying the probable cause is critical. It determines
the type and degree of CAPAs implemented, which affects the likelihood of the deviation recurring.
• Determine if this is a recurrence of a previous deviation or a first-time incident. Is there
a pattern of microbial contamination? Were previous CAPAs effective?
• Determine the potential impact. What impact might this incident have on other batches
manufactured in the facility? This impact assessment may include pharmaceutical ingredients,
manufacturing equipment, or other processing lines in the same facility, other sites within the
company that are manufacturing the same or related products, and distributed product.

• Determine the level of risk. What risk might be posed to a person taking the product? What
effect could there be on the stability, safety, and efficacy of the product over the shelf life of the
product?
• Identify CAPAs required and develop a plan for implementation. Create a list of all the
possible solutions that would prevent the deviation from recurring and select the most effective
changes for implementation.
• Clearly identify the deviation. State what the deviation is and how it exceeded the limits. Be
as specific, simple, and concise as possible.
• Decide what, if any, manufacturing should cease during the investigation. Depending
on the nature of the deviation (e.g., high microbial counts, damage to facility, microorganisms
identified), the team should consider recommending that manufacturing in the affected area
should cease until remediation (e.g., extra cleaning and sanitization, more environmental sam-
pling) has been completed and before the investigation is closed. All actions taken to demonstrate
containment of the deviation should be documented.
• Maintain clear communications. Interview personnel involved and revise the investigation as
needed. Provide routine updates to management and ensure that members of the team are well
informed and kept up to date on the progress of the investigation.
• Inspect the area where the deviation occurred. Locate the deviation on a plan of the facility
and look at surrounding areas that might contribute to the microbial contamination. Consider
personnel and material flows, and nonroutine events, as well as temperature, humidity, and space
pressurization excursions.
• Look for trends. Review prior related investigations to assess. Is there an adverse trend—same
location, common personnel involvement, recurring microorganisms, root cause? If an adverse
trend is identified, act promptly to remediate the problem.
• Evaluate data. A thorough review of production or batch records should be conducted to deter-
mine if falsification or incorrect recording of data from either intentional or unintentional human
error during the time of recording results occurred (35).
While SOPs are crucial to effectively manage an investigation, other tools can provide valuable assis-
tance in identifying possible causes of the deviation, such as brainstorming, fishbone diagram, FMEA/
HAACP, cause-and-effect analysis, and the 5-why technique.

### 5.1 Root Cause Analysis

When writing a manufacturing investigation, use of root cause analysis tools are recommended (36).
One common approach is the use of a fishbone diagram (Figure 5.1-1); it can be used to ensure that
all inputs that may have contributed to the deviation are adequately considered and assessed as a po-
tential root cause.
Thinking through the entire scope of factors that may have influenced the deviation is very important,
as is recording each potential root cause identified in the investigation, even it is determined not likely
to have been a contributing factor. For example, if pharmaceutical ingredients and packaging compo-
nents were assessed and were determined to not have caused the deviation, the investigation should
reflect that information. This demonstrates that the investigator performed due diligence in examining
the potential impact of these materials as a source of contamination and subsequently ruled them out
as a potential root cause.
The categories of contributing factors to be considered in a root cause analysis may vary based on the
process involved. They can also be modified depending on the situation, for example, the dosage form,
facility design, or nature of the deviation itself, location within the facility, sample type, classified area
level, or involvement of personnel. The investigating team is responsible for ensuring that all contrib-
uting factors have been assessed so that no potential root cause of the deviation is overlooked.

### 5.2 Phase II: Specific Points to Consider

#### 5.2.1 Sterility-Positive Investigations

Should the laboratory confirm that a positive sterility result is valid, and no fault was attributed to the
laboratory and actual testing of samples submitted for analysis, the lot of product or material should be
considered nonsterile, and a comprehensive investigation into the source of contamination is required.

Commonly, this is a collaborative effort between the QCU, laboratory, and production along with any
additional SMEs relevant to the manufacturing facilities and ancillary equipment. In order to guide
this step of the investigation, both scientific and regulatory considerations are provided to incorporate
into your investigative strategy.
All sterility test isolates should be identified to the species level, and the data should be reviewed for
any potential linkages to organisms isolated from the production environments, personnel who par-
ticipate in the manufacturing process directly or indirectly by handling of the sampling containers
throughout the process, and any in-process bioburden testing. If the same species of microorganism
was recovered from EM or supporting activities during batch manufacturing or testing, these colonies
should be identified to the strain-type level. Using the same microbial identification platform for both
the sterility test isolate(s) and any isolates recovered from the production areas is advisable to eliminate
potential differences in software databases if using two different microbial identification platforms.
Useful regulatory guidance for conducting a sterility test failure investigation for an aseptically filled
product are the 2004 FDA guidance on sterile drug products and some insightful chapters in the
PDA book Microbial Risk and Investigations (3, 41, 42). Experience has shown that the investigational
approach for aseptically filled sterile products will be significantly more complex than investigating a
sterility test failure associated with terminal modalities. The following table provides a partial list of
suggested questions to consider when evaluating sterility assurance data for a suspect lot of product
under review during a Phase II manufacturing investigation.

#### 5.2.4 Evaluation of Confirmed Mycoplasma Contamination

Investigation should be conducted in a timely manner as mycoplasma contamination can interrupt
production and prove costly to the firm.
Common sources of mycoplasma contamination are master and secondary cell lines, raw materi-
als, and growth medium that has not been rendered sterile using a 0.1 µm filtration step. Attention
should be given to investigating these materials. The authentication, acquisition, banking, passage, and
cultivation history of the master cell bank and working cell lines should be reviewed, with particular
emphasis on certificates of analysis, certificates of origin, and the reported mycoplasma test results.
Testing additional samples from the working cell bank may be advisable. Human contact or contami-
nated cell culture media and reagents may contaminate cell banks, and a new working cell bank would
need to be established in order to continue production.

Cell culture media is usually made mycoplasma-free by heat treatment, irradiation, antibiotic use, or
0.1 µm retention filtration. That these procedures are fully validated and documented should be verified.
The media, equipment sterilization, and handling of inoculum production must be as well controlled
as large-scale cell culture. The manufacturing batch records should be reviewed thoroughly, especially
noting manufacturing deviations and staffing changes. If available, retained production materials could
be tested using a rapid method to trace the potential source of the mycoplasma contamination (29).
When the mycoplasma are of human origin (Mycoplasma orale, M. fermentans, and M. hominis), human con-
tamination may likely play a role in the failure. Additional consideration should be given to species of myco-
plasma that are of animal or nonanimal origin (Acholeplasma laidlawii, M. hyorhinis, M. synoviae, and Spiroplas-
ma) as they may be indicative of contamination of specific growth media or additive growth components (46).

#### 5.2.5 Component (Container Closure) Investigations

Some of the major causal factors of microbial contamination in primary packaging components that
threaten the sterility assurance of sterile drug products include the container (e.g., vial, cartridge, flex-
ible container, or syringe) and closure (e.g., syringe cap and plunger, stopper, seal). Specific microbial
tests have the potential to fail as a result of a defective container or closure system, such as the biobur-
den test, sterility test, process simulation media fill (turbid vial), container closure/seal integrity test,
and BET. Test failures can occur during initial component validation or revalidation testing, compo-
nent release testing, in-process monitoring, finished-product release testing, or stability testing. The
severity of the microbial test failure may depend on where the container–closure failure occurred. In
every case, a thorough review of the components, filling, and closure operations should be performed.
Involving the supplier early in the investigation is the best plan, as they can help provide data from the
manufacturer. A complete history will be needed that covers manufacture supply, storage, preparation,
sterilization, use, and final finished presentation in order to successfully complete the investigation.

##### 5.2.5.1 Pharmaceutical Containers

Vials, cartridges, and syringes used for sterile products are regarded as primary containers. Each presenta-
tion has unique characteristics that must be considered during a microbial failure investigation. Upon
container receipt, a 100% inspection is not possible, both because of the destructive nature of functional
testing, the size of the incoming shipments, and the general reluctance to return quality samples to inven-
tory. Thus, acceptable quality-level attribute inspection plans are widely used for packaging components.
After the initial testing of packaging components for bioburden, endotoxin, and physical characteristics, a
defect inspection and certificate of analysis review is performed. Dimensional tests are important because
the necks of containers and closures form the barrier or seal that prevents microbiological and other con-
tamination ingress. Containers that are out of round, have seam or form defects, or have cracks can present
paths of ingress. Molded glass can be traced to a specific mold at the factory to identify recurring defects.
The initial concern is the physical state of the shipping containers, so an inspection for gross damage should
be performed. Shrink wrapping trays of containers will stabilize the shipment, prevent damage, and reduce
particulate contamination. Shipping cartons that are received infested, wet, crushed, or torn should be
rejected. Containers used during production of a product with a failed microbial test should be reviewed.

##### 5.2.5.2 Pharmaceutical Closures

The most common closure for a sterile injectable product filled in a glass vial is an elastomeric stopper
and crimped aluminum seal. Syringes most commonly include a shielded needle and are sealed using
the rubber septum in the syringe plunger without a crimp seal. For cartridges, a combination crimp
seal is generally employed. Each presentation has unique challenges but, generally, the investigation
should follow the same path as that for containers. Special consideration should be given to the semi-
permeable nature of elastomers. A review of the sterilization process validation also may be useful.
Containers and closures can be purchased raw and unprocessed, ready-to-sterilize, and ready-to-use.
These different preparations should be considered during an investigation.

##### 5.2.5.3 Component Contamination

Post-manufacture microbiological and environmental contamination can include nonviable particles, mi-
croorganisms, and bacterial endotoxins. Viruses are generally not a concern with nonanimal raw material
used to manufacture packaging components. In most cases, the high-heat extrusion process associated
with glass and elastomer manufacture is microbiologically unfavorable; however, environmental controls
in the manufacturing plant are often inadequate to maintain these conditions during sorting and packag-
ing for shipment. Downstream processing is required and must include rigorous processing controls.

##### 5.2.5.4 Container–Closure Integrity

Physical deformation, damage, and out-of-tolerance dimensional specifications of both containers and closures
can result in sterile products becoming nonsterile through microbial ingress. Emphasis should be given to man-
ufacturing set-up, in-process controls, and container–closure testing at release and in stability programs. Failure
of the container–closure system and the inability to maintain microbial integrity should be fully investigated.

#### 5.2.6 Environmental Monitoring

The pharmaceutical industry has many controlled and/or classified environments in which products
are manufactured, and the key to the success of an EM investigation is understanding these require-
ments and the possible sources of contamination. Out-of-level monitoring results can come from the
following types of environments:
• Classified cleanrooms for sterile product manufacturing
• Controlled or classified rooms for nonsterile product manufacturing
• Controlled or classified rooms for low bioburden product manufacturing (e.g., biologics)
• Laminar flow hoods, restrictive access barrier systems (RABS), and isolator systems used for
product manufacturing or finished-product release testing
This section will cover the potential sources of contamination, and points to consider when investigat-
ing out-of-level EM results. Eliminating the potential sources of contamination of the manufacturing
environment must be established by risk assessment during facility and program development. Mea-
sures for control, however, can be implemented based on the following key concepts:
• Minimize the entry of contaminants by incorporating good facility design, proper layout,
efficient material and personnel flow, and sound supplier controls
• Reduce contamination during the manufacturing process using well-developed process and
equipment controls
• Establish and maintain proper hygiene and PPE practices
• Institute appropriate aseptic techniques and cleanroom behaviors
• Assure a state of control through routine EM and analysis

##### 5.2.6.2 Manufacturing Process Evaluation

The purpose of an EM program is to determine the adequacy of the environmental controls and the
aseptic technique of personnel in the manufacturing area. A well-maintained and well-operated facility
requires good facility design, operation, and maintenance; functional HVAC systems; temperature and
humidity control; supply of HEPA-filtered, laminar flow air; proper movement of materials, equip-
ment, and personnel; and a sound facility cleaning and sanitization program. If these requirements
are not maintained, control of the environment will be lost, and an EM excursion will occur. The
microbiologist on the investigation team must be experienced in EM and understand manufacturing
operations. Questions commonly asked during the manufacturing portion of an EM investigation are
outlined in Table 5.1-1.
Loss of environmental control in the cleanroom should be considered a potential source of microbial
contamination. Excursions in temperature, differential pressure gradients, humidity, nonviable par-
ticulate levels, and appropriate cleaning and disinfection activities can contribute to microbial con-
tamination in the cleanroom environment.
Microbial contamination can enter the manufacturing facility, including cleanrooms, in a variety of
ways; in fact, the most common cause of fungal spore and bacterial endospore contamination in clean-
rooms are items that enter the cleanroom. Some customary items include tools, processing equipment,
communication devices, boxes, carts, cleaning and sanitizing equipment, and batch records. Card-
board and other cellulose-based materials known to shed particles should never enter GMP-compliant
manufacturing areas. Items entering critical aseptic processing areas should first be double-wrapped,
unwrapped, and wiped down with a sporicidal agent.
The age and maintenance level of facilities can affect contamination rates in the cleanroom (50). Ceil-
ing tiles, HEPA-filter units, cleanroom flooring, materials of construction, drainage systems, and duct
work are areas of concern in aging facilities that may need to be kept on a routine schedule for repairs.
Drains in manufacturing facilities require routine disinfection with a sporicidal agent to prevent mi-

##### 5.2.6.3 Resampling or Investigational Monitoring

The need for additional monitoring to determine root cause (to confirm or exclude hypotheses with
regards to the root cause) should be considered early in the investigation to avoid losing time and to
ensure that microbiological results will be representative of the conditions.
Based on the type of manufacturing facility, the frequency for routine environmental monitoring
should be considered and a decision should be made as to whether resampling or investigational moni-

locations and locations found as important during facility walk-throughs. If the isolate identified as

#### 5.2.8 Utilities Monitoring

If the initial laboratory investigation (Section 4.0) does not find a conclusive source of a laboratory er-
ror that explains the deviation, deviations related to pharmaceutical water systems should be examined.
Water is used in many ways in pharmaceutical manufacturing, and the required grade of water varies de-
pending upon how it is used. Compendia define the minimum quality attributes that each grade of water
must meet and may specify its method of preparation. Pharmaceutical water systems consist of several
components, including a water supply, a robust purification system that should consistently produce water
of the intended quality under a range of conditions, and the storage and distribution systems that assure
the water continues to meet all required quality attributes until it is delivered to the process. Together, the
design, installation, and qualification of the water system, as well as the procedures for its operation, use,
and maintenance, ensure the system consistently performs as intended and in a state of control.
The cross-functional team charged with investigating any deviation related to a water system should
be familiar with the potential risks associated with its design, installation, operation, use, and main-
tenance. This team also needs to understand both the value and the limitations of data derived from
system qualification and monitoring.
A monitoring plan should define the locations and frequency of sampling and the attributes for test-
ing. The plan should encompass the entire system, from the water supply to the points of use, and
relevant points in between on the generation, storage, and distribution systems. The sampling plan
should reflect the results of intensive sampling during qualification, recent historical data, risk factors,

and mitigations. Monitoring points should be carefully selected to ensure they provide the intended
information regarding control of the system, including the information needed to support the initial
investigation of a deviation at that point or at other points on the system.
Routine monitoring of the water generation, storage, and distribution systems verifies that they continue
to operate in a state of control, while monitoring at the points-of-use evaluates the quality of water deliv-
ered to the process. Points-of-use should be regularly sampled and tested (typically off-line) for chemical
attributes. These chemical measures can be a useful reference when conducting an investigation of a devia-
tion. Measurements taken before and after unit operations, such as carbon beds, deionizers, UV lights, and
reverse osmosis system, can provide data to assess whether they continue to function as expected.
Microbiological monitoring typically involves manual sample collection from a storage tank, a site on
the loop, or at a point of use; the transport, storage, and analysis of the sample; and, ultimately, the
recording, interpretation, and reporting of results. Each step should be carefully designed to minimize
potential errors and to ensure the reported result accurately reflects the condition of the material
sampled. In addition, several monitoring systems are available for at-line monitoring of microbial at-
tributes and, as with other instruments for the sampling and measurement of critical attributes, these
should be properly qualified. For additional guidance on monitoring plans, refer to PDA Technical
Report No. 13 Fundamentals of an Environmental Monitoring Program (4).
The approach to investigating the root cause of a deviation on a pharmaceutical water system should be
guided by the location and timing of the sample and by the test involved. If multiple deviations of the
same type and level are observed, a cause that affects the entire system is more likely. If the deviation is
not consistent with results from other contemporaneous samples, causes related to the sample point or
to sampling or testing may be suspected. Each water system deviation is unique, and its investigation
may consider factors related to the design, installation, operation, maintenance, change history, and
use of the system. The monitoring history, including past deviation investigations, related CAPAs, and
typical microbial flora of the system, will provide useful references.
In the case of a deviation for bioburden, the identity of the organism recovered is essential informa-
tion. Recovery of organisms that thrive in water and form biofilms may suggest one set of causes,
whereas recovery organisms that are not compatible with the water system may suggest another set of
causes, possibly related to environmental contamination.
Potable water is the starting ingredient for both purified water and WFI. Standards set by regional
authorities, at minimum, require potable water to be free of coliforms and to contain bioburden at a
specified level (e.g., ≤ 500 CFU/mL per USP <1231>), and they may define additional requirements.
Purified water must meet the compendial limits for conductivity, total organic carbon, and bioburden
(≤ 100 CFU/mL). WFI must meet the same physical and chemical criteria, as well as more stringent
microbiological criteria for bioburden (< 10 CFU/100 mL) and bacterial endotoxin (< 0.25 EU/mL).
The source and method of delivery of potable water can introduce significant risks to control of the water
system. Potable water quality may be affected by seasonal variations due to runoff of melting snow or pe-
riods of low precipitation. Agricultural or industrial runoff may enter surface water sources or aquifers. An
aging infrastructure may increase the chance of contamination entering the water supply via a damaged
water main (50). Likewise, water delivery via truck introduces a variety of risks that may vary from day to
day and truck to truck. The sampling and analysis plan for the potable water supply should consider the
risks related to the source and method of supply and should consider, in advance, the information needed
to investigate a deviation found in a sample from the potable water supply or from the system downstream.
Risks related to the design and installation of the water system should be identified and remediated
prior to qualification testing. System boundaries should provide an effective barrier to microbial con-

tamination, including microbial retentive filters on holding tanks and the use of appropriate elasto-
mers. Dead legs, as well as poor welds and other irregular surfaces, increase the risk of biofilm develop-
ment. Recent design standards specify that pipes branching off the main recirculation loop should not
exceed 1.5 times the pipe diameter, and the use of valves with no dead legs is now common.
Investigation of a deviation should include a review of system operations, including control of physi-
cal parameters, and results of nonmicrobiological monitoring of the system. Operational controls for
storage and distribution systems typically include parameters such as temperature, flow rate, and pres-
sure, and can show whether the system remained flooded or flowing and at the required temperature.
Additional parameters may be defined for pretreatment stages such as carbon beds, deionizers, and re-
verse osmosis systems. On-line, at-line, and off-line measurements of physical and chemical attributes
such as hardness, conductivity, and total organic carbon, provide near continuous data on chemical
attributes. They also allow prompt notification of an unexpected shift in performance, which can be
helpful in investigating deviations. The rate of water usage affects the stagnant time of water in the
system and may be related to the root cause.
The investigation of a deviation may include a review of routine and corrective maintenance records.
Invasive maintenance adds contamination risks, so measures to mitigate these risks and return the
system to service should be prepared in advance, whenever possible. Water system maintenance should
ensure the system remains integral and continues to operate in a state of control. A well-designed
program assures that the system boundaries continue to provide an effective barrier that prevents mi-
crobial contamination. Measures should include periodic replacement of key parts, such as elastomers
and vent filters on holding tanks, as the failure of these elements can open the system to the external
environment. The investigation of a deviation should also consider the potential for wear and damage
to durable parts such as piping. Pipes may thin over time, and damage can occur due to unplanned
events such as hydraulic shock. Water leaks should be repaired promptly to reduce the opportunity for
contamination; however, the investigation of a deviation should recognize that not all leaks, a small
leak of cooling water through a cracked heat exchanger tube, for example, are easily detected.
Practices for operation of the sample or use port should be considered, especially if the deviation is not
consistent with other results. Flushing prior to sampling (and use) is important to physically remove the
opportunistic organisms found on hoses, sampling apparatus, or the sample port. Use procedures should
ensure that dead legs are not created through the use of valves between the water system and points
downstream. Hoses should be removed when not in use and should be stored in a free-draining position.

##### 5.2.8.1 Compressed Gasses

Compressed gasses used during the manufacturing and testing of pharmaceutical products are air, ni-
trogen, oxygen, and carbon dioxide. Inappropriate design and piping for distribution of these gaseous
materials can introduce unwanted microorganisms. Pharmaceutical-grade compressed air is treated
to remove moisture, and chemical residue like lubricants and point-of-use sterilizing-grade filters are
employed. The introduction of uncontrolled water moisture or inadequately filtered compressed gas,
coupled with poorly designed pretreatment and sanitization processes of the gas delivery systems, can
adversely affect the system and lead to product contamination. The types of methodologies used for
testing the microbial presence within these systems are important because when finished pharmaceuti-
cal products are found adulterated, the eventual investigation by the QCU will need to find the source
of the contamination or, at least, eliminate the possibility that it was from a compressed gas.
Even though these utility systems have been qualified to perform as designed, the materials used dur-
ing their construction may contain unexpected component defects (i.e., poor welding or incomplete
passivation), as well as presenting potential ingress of grease and oils used for equipment lubrication.
In addition, the metals and plastics may deteriorate over time, causing breaches or leaks in the plumb-
ing materials or in components connected to the pipes (e.g., gauges, shut-off valves).

## 6.0 Conclusions

The investigation of microbial monitoring and testing deviations is a critical activity in the pharma-
ceutical industry that is not adequately covered by the FDA Guidance for Industry: Investigating Out
of Specification (OOS) Test Results for Pharmaceutical Production (1). This technical report provides
industry best practices related to microbial investigations to eliminate that gap in guidance.
These investigations are operationally divided into two phases—Phase I laboratory investigations and
Phase II manufacturing investigations—with different teams responsible for each activity. Because of
the importance of timeliness in microbial investigations, the manufacturing investigation may be initi-
ated prior to completion of the laboratory investigation.
The objective of the laboratory investigation is to confirm that the sampling and testing was conducted
correctly, and the reported result was valid. The investigational team is usually limited to the microbi-
ologist conducting the test, the laboratory management, an SME with knowledge and experience with
the test, and a representative of the QCU.
The major points to consider in a laboratory investigation are:
• Integrity of the test sample used in the microbial test
• Educational background, skill, and experience of the microbiologist conducting the test
• Testing and environmental and personnel monitoring history associated with the microbiologist
• Suitability of the equipment, supplies, media, diluents, and reagents used for the test
• Environmental controls, including microbial testing facilities, laminar flow hoods, isolators, and
PPE associated with the test
• Reading of plates, scoring of microbiological broth, recording of data, accuracy of calculations,
and review and reporting of the results
• Microbial identification of the isolate implicated in the deviation
Once the validity of the microbial test is confirmed, a multifunctional team is assembled to conduct the
manufacturing investigation related to the failure. Representatives of the microbiology laboratory, manu-
facturing, engineering, maintenance, and process validation may be members of the manufacturing inves-
tigation team usually led by the QCU. To increase the chances of a successful investigation, an experienced
pharmaceutical microbiologist familiar with manufacturing operations is usually added as an SME.
The major points to consider in a manufacturing investigation are:
• Unit operation and location within the facility directly related to the failure
• Possible contribution of surrounding support areas
• Possible contribution to the failure of deviations in environmental controls, i.e., space pressurization,
airflow patterns, temperature, humidity, power outages, material flow, facility cleaning, and sanitization
• Possible contribution of pharmaceutical ingredients, ingredient water, in-process materials, pack-
aging components, and utilities
• Deviation in critical manufacturing steps, as documented in the batch record
• Recent change controls
• Related in-process bioburden, environmental, and personnel monitoring deviations
• Interviews with operators, laboratory management, and production managers as to the possible
cause of the failure
• Follow-up microbial monitoring and testing
The investigation reports must adequately document the team findings. If the root cause of the out-of-
limit or out-of-specification microbial test result cannot be definitively established, the most probable
cause or causes must be presented. Investigating out-of-trend results may help identify laboratory and/
or manufacturing issue before they become out-of-specification results. To prevent the recurrence of
these microbial failures, a CAPA plan must be implemented, with responsible individuals reporting
their progress on the plan to the QCU and their management.
