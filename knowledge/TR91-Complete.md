# PDA Technical Report No. 91 (2023): Post-Approval Change Management and Implementation for Biologics and Pharmaceutical Drug Products — A User's Guide

## 1.0 Introduction

The complexity of global post-approval change/variation (PAC) processes and the time required to receive
their approval presents a challenge for industry when it is working to implement needed changes and op-
erating with a focus on continual improvement. Industry benchmarking shows that receiving approval for
a PAC requiring global approval can take between two and five years, and the time it takes from original
submission can vary from six months to several years depending on the jurisdiction and review pathway
(1). For new products, where the amount of commercial process or product data at the time of the initial
submission is limited, this timing can significantly impact the ability to implement necessary process im-
provements identified as more data becomes available. In specific cases, when a change is needed to ensure
the product remains safe, efficacious, and of high quality or provides additional capacity based on high
product demand, the global approval timing can result in drug product shortages.
The current regulatory pathway to implement change is highly complex. For each jurisdiction in
which a product is licensed, the respective regulatory agency typically requires a notification, based
on its risk reporting category, or it must preapprove certain PACs for certain products. Differences
in country- and region-specific requirements for PAC filings, filing procedures, and associated time-
lines can often make simultaneous implementation of changes problematic, necessitating a product
be tested or produced using different processes based on where it will be distributed. Companies must
consider this factor when deciding to implement a change.
These challenges were highlighted during the COVID-19 pandemic, as companies continued to man-
ufacture lifesaving products despite the extreme strain on supply chains for raw materials, components,
and manufacturing supplies. As alternative sources of supply or alternative materials were identified,
the complexity of the global regulatory PAC process presented a significant challenge to product sup-
ply, highlighting the need for a harmonized approach to PAC implementation.

### 1.1 Purpose

This technical report expands on the latest concepts and tools for improving PAC management, as pub-
lished in ICH Q12. It provides a practical guide for the pharmaceutical industry on how to implement
PACs within the framework of an effective pharmaceutical quality system (PQS) and in alignment with
ICH quality guidelines. It recognizes the challenges that the limited amount of global harmonization
among regulators presents with respect to PACs and attempts to provide some insight on how industry
can work with regulators to improve in this area. Though TR-91 aligns with ICH Q12, it also provides
guidance for non-ICH regions. It suggests how to implement the tools described in ICH Q12 and pro-
vides examples of the concepts that can facilitate aligned industry understanding for PACs.
TR-91 highlights the benefits of some of the tools described in ICH Q12 that are already available in
certain jurisdictions, specifically, PAC management protocols (PACMPs, or comparability protocols) and
established conditions (ECs). These concepts were acceptable for use by several regulatory agencies prior
to ICH Q12, that is, ECs and comparability protocols by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)
(11,12) and comparability protocols by the European Medicines Agency (EMA) (13) and WHO (4,5).
Section 10.0 (Appendix 1) and Section 11.0 (Appendix 2) provide examples of PACs common in indus-
try; these are presented as representative frameworks for consideration, presented as hypothetical PAC-
MPs (or comparability protocols) that can be leveraged for similar changes. These examples, although not
all-inclusive, do offer insights into the types of documentation and the scientific justification that may
be required for implementation. They are intended to be illustrative, but they do portray how negotia-
tion with regulators, where existing requirements may be disproportionate to risk given either knowledge
gained through development or process experience, could be demonstrated using a PACMP approach.

### 1.2 Scope

The information in this technical report focuses on chemistry, manufacturing, and controls (CMC)
changes that can be applied to PACs for both small-molecule and large-molecule pharmaceutical products.

## 3.0 Background and Landscape for PACs

The importance of PAC and the need to overcome these myriad challenges has led many international
organizations, including the Parenteral Drug Association (PDA), to develop recommendations and
proposals to address the situation:
PDA established the Post Approval Change; Innovations of the Availability of Medicines (PAC iAM)
program that has developed numerous papers, including this technical report.
The European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations and International Federation
of Pharmaceutical Manufacturers and Associations have issued position papers on PAC, calling for
regulatory convergence and proposing a relevant framework (1, 2).
One Voice of Quality for PAC, an initiative sponsored by the chief quality officers of more than 30
global pharmaceutical companies, has been developing risk-based recommendations to help reduce
the global complexity associated with PACs (3).
The World Health Organization (WHO) has provided guidance, suggesting pathways and require-
ments for PACs (4, 5).
The International Council for Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Hu-
man Use (ICH) has continued its push for harmonization by publishing Quality Guideline Q12: Tech-
nical and Regulatory Considerations for Pharmaceutical Product Lifecycle Management, which focuses
specifically on PACs (6), while building upon established ICH Quality Guidelines Q8-Q11 (7–10).
The ICH Q12 guidance, which describes key concepts necessary to streamline the PAC process, is rec-
ognized as a significant step forward. In addition to the implementation of ICH Q12, there are areas
where work is still needed to achieve full harmonization, including:
• Agreement on the change categories among regulatory authorities
• Harmonization on filing procedures and timelines within ICH regions
• Inclusion of non-ICH regulatory authorities to converge on the application of these principles
By fully implementing ICH Quality Guidelines Q8–Q11, manufacturing firms can develop an effec-
tive internal PQS sufficient to optimize the management of PACs. Demonstrating the effectiveness of
the PQS would allow a reduction in the regulatory reporting burden for changes with a lower level of
risk, as they would be managed through a firm’s PQS, by:
• Ensuring ICH Q8–Q12 are implemented and leveraged as building blocks for PAC management
• Working with regulators to drive consistent reporting categories
• Developing a robust PQS system
• Enhancing transparency and trust between companies and regulators to identify when a firm’s PQS
system is sufficient to allow certain types of lower-risk change to be implemented without reporting
In keeping with ICH Q10 and Q12 guidances and with a robust PQS system in place, PACs requiring
prior approval by regulatory authorities would be limited to changes assessed as having the potential to
have a major impact on product quality and/or patient safety. Minor-to-moderate changes would be
made with notification to the regulatory authorities, and low-risk changes would be managed under a
firm’s PQS. The benefits of this paradigm shift are depicted in Figure 3.0-1.

## 4.0 Key Elements of a Pharmaceutical

Quality System for PAC Management
An effective PQS is the foundation upon which PAC management and implementation are established.
PQS effectiveness in relation to risk-based change management is a core concept within the ICH Q12
framework. Several PQS elements are needed to establish effective PAC management: management
review, a corrective action and preventive action (CAPA) program, a change management system, and
process performance and product quality monitoring (3). Importantly, knowledge management and
quality risk management are key enablers of a firm’s PQS.
Having an established PQS that is executed effectively and consistently within a firm will provide con-
fidence to the regulatory agency that changes are supported by data obtained through the application
of patient-centric, scientific, risk-based principles. A well designed and effective PQS is an essential
foundation for trust between regulatory authorities and industry that will facilitate harmonization on
the categorization of changes. It also should allow more PACs to be managed under the firm’s PQS
without requiring prior review and approval by regulatory agencies (3).
When using contract manufacturing organizations (CMO) or contract development and manufactur-
ing organizations (CDMO), a firm needs to ensure that appropriate contractual agreements are in
place for PACs occurring at the CMO or CDMO and that they maintains an effective PQS (16). The
same principles for lifecycle management and reporting responsibility apply.

### 4.1 Management Review

A firm’s senior management is responsible for ensuring that appropriate systems and processes are in
place to manage changes effectively. In accordance with ICH Quality Guideline Q10: Pharmaceutical
Quality System, these responsibilities include:
• Defining and communicating roles within the change management system
• Ensuring timely and appropriate communication to address potential quality issues
• Providing adequate resources to ensure that changes are made based on sound scientific and risk-
based judgments
• Maintaining awareness of the long-term product lifecycle management strategy and ensuring
that appropriate controls are in place
• Developing and fostering the desired quality culture at all levels throughout the firm to safe-
guard the patient’s access to high-quality medicines

Quality culture is a set of behaviors, beliefs, and values of an enterprise that serve as a guide to decision-
making. In the ideal state, a quality mindset and quality-focused behaviors are embedded into all func-
tions of daily work and result in positive patient outcomes. In an organization with a well-established
quality-culture environment, employees not only follow quality guidelines but consistently see others
taking quality-focused actions, hear others talk about quality, and understand the importance of qual-
ity in everyday activities (17).
For sustained effectiveness, the PQS needs to be embedded alongside a firm’s culture of quality for it to
properly perform its function and be considered reliable. Management plays an important role in nurtur-
ing key elements of a mature quality culture, including a focus on continuous improvement, demonstra-
tion of leadership, communication of a common vision, and investment in employee development and
education. Training is essential to ensure a proficient and knowledgeable quality-oriented workforce.

### 4.2 Corrective Action and Preventive Action Program

A properly functioning CAPA system identifies not only when actions are needed as a result of an event
that has occurred, but also operates proactively to improve based on weaknesses identified prior to an
event occurring. Ideally, the majority of all actions would be preventative. The CAPA system is often
where the initial need for change is identified and where the PAC process begins. Having a strong CAPA
program is a critical component of any PQS system, which will lead to better process understanding,
process control, and determining when action is needed.
An effective CAPA program monitors and manages unintended risks and consequences of PACs for
internal and outsourced operations. It should enable identification of root cause(s) so that appropriate
actions can be taken to correct problems and prevent their recurrence. The program should also moni-
tor and verify the effectiveness of any CAPAs associated with PAC initiatives.

### 4.3 Change Management System

While most changes made have no impact on the regulatory dossier, the importance of having a system
that appropriately evaluates changes and their impact on the manufacturing processes and product
cannot be emphasized enough. The change process must be able to adequately evaluate the change,
the level of risk to the process and product, and the need for a PAC to be filed. Tools, such as those
described in ICH Q10 and Q12 and in the PIC/S Recommendation: How to Evaluate/Demonstrate the
Effectiveness of a Pharmaceutical Quality System in relation to Risk-based Change Management, focus on
the relevance of risked-based change management.
The PIC/S recommendation provides a checklist to evaluate the effectiveness of a firm’s PQS in rela-
tion to risk-based change management and acknowledges that the level of effort and associated docu-
mentation should be commensurate with the level of risk the change entails. This, in turn, should
be used to justify the categorization and filing requirements for that change (18). To ensure that the
underlying risk level is well understood, the change management process needs to be robust enough to
capture the impact of any proposed change. Therefore, it must support a knowledge-based assessment
and review of the change, ensuring that the final scientific justification supports the ultimate filing
decision to the applicable regulatory agency.
A structured, well-organized change management process is essential for a successful PAC process.
It must cover critical quality attributes (CQA), critical process parameters (CPP), product lifecycle
management (PLCM), and control strategy and include creating ECs as appropriate, among other
elements (see Section 4.1). Such an approach ensures that relevant quality aspects are considered, and
possible quality impacts are thoroughly assessed, documented, and communicated to relevant stake-
holders, including regulatory bodies.

### 4.4 Process Performance and Product Quality Monitoring

A comprehensive program to monitor the manufacturing process performance and product quality
should be in place at every manufacturing facility to ensure that the process continues to perform in
a state of control and in keeping with the validated state. A sound monitoring plan allows a firm to
establish the baseline process performance and detect early warnings of atypical process performance.
Having some process monitoring data before and after the change is an essential component of the
overall qualification of any change. In essence, product and process monitoring results serve as the
scientific basis for improvement of the manufacturing process and the control system. Typically, after
a process change is put in place, monitoring frequency is increased to confirm that the process contin-
ues to perform as expected. For example, documentation of any deviations from trend limits and/or
adverse trends with respect to process, in-process, release, and/or stability testing parameters should be
carefully assessed in line with the quality system. These deviations should be investigated for root cause
and evaluated to ensure the change has no adverse impact on product quality.
Effective change management is fundamental to ensuring that PACs are controlled in an environment
characterized by data-driven, scientific, and risk-based assessments. In an effective change manage-
ment environment, all aspects of the product and process are managed to ensure that there are no
unintended consequences and that product quality and process performance are maintained in a state
of control.

### 4.5 Quality Risk Management and Knowledge Management

Quality risk management (QRM) and knowledge management (KM) are key enablers of an effective
PQS. An organization that strives to have a strong, quality-oriented culture will use QRM and KM to
guide the identification and proactive management of PACs. PDA Technical Report No. 54: Implemen-
tation of Quality Risk Management for Pharmaceutical and Biotechnology Manufacturing Operations pro-
vides a comprehensive description of the QRM elements and tools aligned with ICH Q9. In addition,
annexes to TR-54 supply examples of QRM application for manufacturing processes and equipment
qualification that may be relevant for PAC management (19).
An effective QRM system provides a sound framework that ensures systematic and proactive risk-
based and data-driven decision-making is being employed for all PACs. QRM should enable innova-
tion by allowing faster implementation of changes that reduce the risk of quality failures and manufac-
turing problems and improve process capability. An effective QRM system can also separate changes
that require regulatory reporting or notification from changes that can be managed completely within
the PQS. Effective QRM enables a firm to demonstrate an understanding of the existing production
process and of the risks, associated impacts, and mitigation of those risks in that process.
An effective KM system leverages historical and recently identified product and process knowledge,
including innovation and technology advancements. It also considers knowledge gained from process
monitoring, deviations, trends, complaints, recalls, annual product or product quality review, audits,
inspections, and management reviews. Effective KM ensures that the right knowledge is used by the
right people at the right time, allowing for effective risk-based decision-making and formal product and
process risk assessments. A systematic, transparent, and well-understood KM system ensures that changes
are based on scientific rationale and can facilitate implementation of identified changes accordingly.
Several FDA, Health Canada, and WHO guidance documents on CMC changes in a post-approval
situation demonstrate how product and process knowledge and effective QRM can be leveraged to
establish lesser reporting categories for changes (4, 5, 11, 20). FDA consistently states in guidances its
intent to apply the principles of quality risk assessment and risk management in accordance with ICH
Q9 during their review. The Agency recognizes that, in order to keep pace with the many advances in
quality management practices in manufacturing and enable it to more effectively allocate its limited

regulatory resources, implementing a risk-based approach for regulating pharmaceutical manufactur-
ing is necessary (21). Accordingly, one FDA guidance allows that changes demonstrated to have a
minimal impact to a product can be filed as an annual reportable item (22). Likewise, the Health
Canada and WHO guidances, which provide conditions to be fulfilled for specific change types to
help guide firms on how to assess risk and categorize changes accordingly, include the option of an-
nual notification for minimal impact. In light of this, the principles of KM and QRM can be equally
applied in both ICH and non-ICH regions and should be recognized by global regulatory authorities.

for a new desired state. Regulatory tools should be used to enhance the management of these PACs
in a way that encourages innovation and continuous improvement. Doing so may help a firm avoid
time-consuming, iterative, and expensive processes and, instead, encourage smooth, straightforward

### 5.1 Established Conditions

EC is an important term and concept in evaluating change and, while ICH Q12 works to bring more
definition to the term, the concept has been in place for some time.

#### 5.1.1 Background

ICH Q12 defines ECs as legally binding information considered necessary to assure product quality.
Consequently, any change to an EC necessitates a submission to the regulatory authority. ICH Q12,
which the FDA officially adopted in May 2021, further considers ECs to be the elements of the phar-
maceutical development activities that result in an appropriate control strategy (23).
Previously, FDA defined ECs as the description of the product, manufacturing process, facilities and
equipment, and elements of the associated control strategy, as defined in an application, that assure
process performance and quality of a product (11). Japan has a similar concept, called “approved mat-
ters,” that refers to an item of information described in a dossier for an application that was approved
by the regulatory agency (14). Health Canada also leverages a similar concept, requesting that market
authorization holders file and maintain a Certified Product Information Document. That document
constitutes part of the approval package and provides an accurate record of key quality information
for the product proposed for marketing at the time the approval is issued. Thereafter, it serves as an of-
ficial reference document during post-approval inspections and PAC evaluations performed by Health
Canada (20). Hence, the expectation of regulators to assess changes for the potential impact on prod-
uct quality, safety, and efficacy has always existed.
Changes that could potentially have a major impact on product quality usually have the highest reporting
category. As a term and a concept, EC may guide both the firm and the regulators in predefining the pa-
rameters that are more likely to impact the product. ICH Q10 describes the control strategy as “a planned
set of controls, derived from current process and product understanding, that assures process performance
and product quality,” which may include CPPs and CQAs (9). Some elements of the control strategy may
be managed in the applicant’s PQS and not necessarily detailed in the dossier or application. Others may
be detailed in the dossier but considered to be supportive information rather than ECs.
In the ICH Common Technical Document (CTD), ECs are already defined in Module 3: Quality.
As per ICH Q12, all regulatory dossiers contain a combination of ECs and supportive information.
Although identification of ECs with appropriate justifications might allow for increased flexibility in re-
porting PACs, explicitly identifying ECs for a given product is not necessarily mandatory. If the market
authorization holder chooses to identify ECs, they should clearly identify the elements of Module 3 that
they consider to be ECs and those they consider to be supportive information. The rationale for the ECs
should remain in the appropriate CTD section. Maintaining a tabular summary of the ECs by section
and providing those in the dossier, explicitly naming them as such, is recommended (24). Another tool
for defining and tracking ECs is the use of a PLCM strategy document; the PLCM document should
be maintained in the firm’s change management system and in CTD Module 3.2.R, as per ICH Q12.
In addition to explicitly identifying ECs, the firm should propose the type of filing that will be pro-
vided should a future change impact an EC. Once a submission is approved and the ECs are agreed
upon by the firm and the regulatory agency, they are considered legally binding, and any changes to
the EC must be reported to the regulatory agency. The reporting mechanism or filing type may vary,
however, and the supporting data for a change may be part of the subsequent filing or may be main-
tained in the firm’s PQS (see Section 5.2).

#### 5.1.2 Benefits of Defining Established Conditions

ECs can be defined as part of the initial product development or through a review of product knowl-
edge obtained over the product’s lifecycle. They should be limited to the elements found essential to
controlling the quality of a drug product based on scientific and risk-based justification. Careful evalu-

ation of the product and manufacturing process will assist in focusing on the parameters critical to
quality control, which will reduce the number of non-value-added PACs that require prior approval by
regulatory agencies. ECs will depend on the complexity of the product and the capabilities inherent to
the manufacturing process and manufacturing site. ECs may vary by dosage form and drug substance
but, fundamentally, they should not vary by region or country. Regulatory convergence across jurisdic-
tions or regulatory reliance under mutual agreement is required to fully realize the benefit.
Clearly identified and justified ECs in an application ensure that the firm and the regulatory agency are
aligned on the criticality of a process parameter or quality attribute. As a result, there should be agree-
ment on the necessity for subsequent submissions and the filing category for changes to any given EC.
While a change to an EC will require a filing with a regulatory agency, the type of filing can depend on
the risk assessment and the process and product knowledge available. The risk may be product- and/or
process-dependent and, importantly, how the risk is assessed is dependent on the effectiveness of the
PQS in place. Ultimately, the same type of change may require different filing strategies for different
products. This further highlights the importance of product and process knowledge and an effective
PQS to fully realize the benefits of identifying ECs. For example, if the risk is considered low because
the change will have little or no impact on product quality, the change may be made through a notifi-
cation; if the risk is considered high due to a potentially high impact on product quality, prior approval
of the change may be required. The documentation of these assessments and supporting data must
live in the firm’s PQS. When a prior approval supplement or variation is required, a PACMP may be
leveraged to align the planned supportive studies and expected outcomes. This would lower the filing
category and reduce the review scrutiny for the submission, which allows for implementation of the
change more rapidly and efficiently (see Section 6.0).

uct Lifecycle Management illustrate how development approaches can impact product understanding
and, ultimately, the identification of ECs (25).
Sections 10.0 (Appendix 1) and 11.0 (Appendix 2) provide examples of ECs and the PACMPs devel-
oped to revise them.
In line with ICH Q10, if a firm can leverage the utility of a robust PQS system—one that incorporates
KM and sound quality risk and change management strategies—the firm can gain increased flexibility
to change ECs as more process and product knowledge is developed. A robust PQS will also enhance
the firm’s understanding of the product and its manufacturing process which, over time, will allow the
firm to critically analyze ECs and reevaluate their potential impact on product quality. That critical
evaluation will afford the firm the potential to remove ECs and negotiate lower filings for changes as-
sociated with existing ECs. This will then facilitate process and facility improvements.
In the current environment, firms and regulatory agencies frequently experience a lengthy submission
and approval process. By predefining ECs and gaining regulatory agency agreement, firms can reduce
the number of PACs that require regulatory agency submission or approval. The filing categories will
be reduced for those changes that do require submission. This would both enable faster implementa-
tion of changes to the drug product and mitigate drug shortages related to lifecycle management. As
they support continual improvement, PACs can result in decreased product variability, reduced supply
shortages, and increased manufacturing efficiency, as well as facilitate the introduction of innovation.

#### 5.1.3 ECs versus Non-ECs and Change Management

All changes are managed through the firm’s change management system, that is, within the PQS, as
illustrated in ICH Q12. This guidance suggests that any parameter that needs to be controlled to en-
sure product quality (i.e., a CPP or process parameter where impact on quality cannot be reasonably
excluded) should be considered an EC. ECs are intended to provide understanding and agreement

between the firm and the regulatory agency on the parameters necessary to assure product quality;
these parameters can change with knowledge gained throughout the lifecycle of the product. The focus
should be on understanding and identifying ECs and appropriately linking them to an agreed-upon
post-marketing plan and regulatory oversight. As such, process parameters, which should be assigned
based on criticality to the process and its control strategy, can be considered ECs and can be defined
with regulatory agencies based on process and product knowledge. Yet, not all data and/or parameters
described in a filing will necessarily be ECs; they may be detailed in the dossier but only as supportive
information. To define the changes that may require a regulatory agency filing, it is critical both to
have explicitly defined ECs and to understand what elements are not. Ultimately, appropriately identi-
fying ECs (and therefore non-ECs) can be a useful tool for harmonization in regions not participating
in ICH when assessing the impact of PACs.

### 5.2 Post-Approval Change Management Protocols

The PACMP tool described in ICH Q12 enables planning and implementation of changes to ECs
and/or CPPs, and thereby to regulatory filings, in an efficient and structured manner. The concept of
the PACMP is not new. The FDA and EMA have accepted comparability protocols for several years,
and WHO guidances discuss the use of comparability protocols as a tool to help expedite the distri-
bution of product post-change (4, 5, 12, 13). The PACMP (or comparability protocol) approved by
the relevant health authority provides an agreement between the firm and the regulatory agency and
describes changes with a level of detail complying to the complexity of the change, including risk as-
sessment, control strategy, and other pertinent elements. Where possible, a description in the original
protocol of the types of changes that might be considered allowable, and/or how they will be com-
municated to the agency post-approval, should be included. Once a PACMP is approved, when the
proposed study design or acceptance criteria changes, an update of the protocol should be submitted
for implementation as agreed upon in the original protocol. If changes are needed due to unantici-
pated or unforeseen changes in plans, prior approval of a new or revised PACMP by the regulatory
agency may be required.
A firm’s processes may include internal guidance for filing initial market registrations, including tem-
plates for PACMPs. In general, PACMPs can include a description of the product’s current state, the
change to be made, the ECs and/or the product quality attributes that will be affected, the defined
testing and acceptance criteria to establish comparability of the changed product, and the proposed
category for filing the change.
Typically, a PACMP describes a single change or a group of related changes that may be made at the
same time, for example, a scale-up of a purification process that involves larger equipment and associ-
ated changes to process parameters. In addition, PACMPs can be used to describe the same change or
changes being implemented across multiple products, such as a new drug product container–closure
system being implemented across a portfolio of products. In this case, the same PACMP could be filed
for multiple applications, and subsequent submissions to provide the final data could be filed for each
individual application, including a combination of product-specific and non-product-specific data.
Contents for PACMPs may vary from one firm to another and from one type of change to another,
but as a best practice, all PACMPs should contain the following elements:
• Description of the current state and the proposed change as well as a justification or scientific
rationale for the change (e.g., issues or disadvantages with the current state and benefits or ad-
vantages with the proposed state)
• Proposed submission category or submission type for the subsequent filing that includes the
data, clearly indicating which sections of the registration will be impacted
• Detailed list of change(s)

• Risk assessment (i.e., risk-based approach) to explain the potential impact of changes to product quality
• Study protocol describing how the potential impact(s) would be assessed to adequately demon-
strate comparability of the product quality before and after the change
• List of predefined acceptance criteria that must be met before implementing the change
• Validation or qualification of the changed method/process/equipment to demonstrate that all
acceptance criteria are met, as required, based on the risk level of change to the product quality
• Additional process characterization and validation studies at small-scale, as required, based on
the specific change or, minimally, an assessment to confirm that the existing process characteriza-
tion and validation studies adequately support the new process conditions
• List of post-change commitments, if any, including any stability studies to demonstrate acceler-
ated and/or long-term stability of the product with the proposed change
• Anticipated timeline for implementation of the change
• Description of how the approved PACMP will be modified, if needed, due to additional knowl-
edge gained during the actual execution
PACMPs may only be accepted by a limited number of regulatory agencies; however, harmonization
of these best practices across ICH regions and some non-ICH regions would greatly improve the po-
tential for using use these tools to facilitate the efficient implementation of PACs.
PACMPs may save firms considerable time and resources to effectively manage changes and provide
regulators greater insight into planned changes, which would facilitate the review of post-change filings.
This could alleviate constraints in the global supply chain, enabling a continuing supply of drug prod-
ucts to patients in a timely manner. Global acceptance by regulators, with a defined mechanism in local
jurisdictions for submission of PACMPs, is needed to fully realize the benefits that PACMPs can offer.
Examples of PACMPs are provided in Section 10.0 (Appendix 1) and Section 11.0 (Appendix 2).

### 5.3 Product Lifecycle Management

The PLCM can be described in a strategy document that describes how the firm will:
• Monitor the process and product attributes to ensure that both remain within the validated
state in accordance with the product control strategy (e.g., continued process verification)
• Manage and report changes to ECs
• Execute PAC filings that are planned as part of the product lifecycle
A PLCM strategy document may include the following sections:
• Summary of the product control strategy, e.g., a description of the CQAs for drug substance
and drug product and the controls in place for raw materials, excipients, process steps, and final
drug substance and drug product that ensure CQAs are maintained within acceptable ranges
• A table describing the CTD sections that contain ECs, the ECs in each of these sec-
tions, and the reporting categories that will be used if those ECs change
• PACMPs that may be used for future changes
• Post-approval commitments from the initial registration or subse-
quent supplements and the timelines for completion
A PLCM strategy document may provide an effective tool to help manage the commercial lifecycle of
a product. It can be filed with the initial registration, or it may be filed as a post-approval variation.

The document would also prove helpful in the firm’s records as a repository of the commitments to the
regulatory agency that must be maintained throughout the commercial lifecycle. The PLCM strategy
document can also be used to communicate to the regulatory agency the elements of the registration
that will be maintained during the commercial lifecycle, as well as the intended reporting categories
and timing estimates. As post-approval variations are submitted, an updated PLCM should be in-
cluded that describes the updated ECs, control strategy, reference to PACMPs, post-approval CMC
commitments, and any other pertinent information about the change.

## 6.0 Global Lifecycle and PAC Management

### 6.1 ICH Regions

The impact of ICH in harmonizing guidelines and aligning ICH member regions has provided sig-
nificant advantages in both the PLCM and the management of PACs. The continuing challenge is the
wide diversity of expectations of non-ICH regions in both the content and timing of PAC submission
approvals. Some common characteristics leveraged in ICH regions that enable efficient PACs include:
• Clear guidance for filing changes, including reporting categories based on risk
• Acceptance of science- and risk-based approaches for developing data to support PACs,
such as bracketing and/or matrixing of process validation and stability batches
• Potential acceptance of limited stability data when justified, such as three or
six months of real-time data and three months of accelerated data, to sup-
port major changes, and less or no stability data for minor changes
• Well-defined timelines for review and approval of PACs, in the range of six months
for a major change and shorter periods for minor to moderate changes
• Relatively fewer requests for additional documentation not included in CTD Module 3, such as
declarations or GMP certificates, although the latter may be needed for addition of a new facility

### 6.2 Non-ICH Regions

Regions not participating in ICH frequently observe fewer of the characteristics that enable PACs
to be managed consistently across multiple regulatory authorities. Examples seen in some non-ICH
regions include:
• Lack of available post-approval guidance
• No or very limited provision for a differentiation in filing level, such as allowing for providing
notifications rather than submissions for lower-risk changes or recognizing risk-based scientifi-
cally valid strategies, such as bracketing or matrixing of process validation and stability data
• Requirements for extensive amounts of drug product stability data to support even minor
changes or drug substance changes, for example, up to the full shelf life
• Requirements for extensive ancillary documentation to support PACs,
such as declarations, certificates of various types, and legalizations
In 2020, PDA completed a survey of industry on this topic. According to survey results, half of the
respondents indicated that the authorities that take the most time to approve prior-approval supple-
ments require more than a year to grant such approval. Some markets were reported as having ex-
tremely long review times for changes, for example, five years. Some markets add extra requirements,
such as implementing product changes immediately upon that country’s approval, which is not always
feasible given the manufacturing schedule or lag in approval by other jurisdictions. The survey also

indicated some jurisdictions may not allow for the simultaneous review of multiple changes, instead
requiring each change to be submitted for agency review individually, in sequence (26). These types
of requirements make it extremely difficult to file global PACs, which can make it very challenging to
initiate or even propose process improvements. In some cases, this can impact product supply.
When considering the supply chain, extended approval times for products with limited expiry dating
can create significant challenges, as it may not be possible to build up sufficient inventory levels. For
example, a process or facility change that requires modifications that, when made, no longer allows for
the product to be manufactured using the original process. Once the change is made, the inventory of
product using the original process is set and, if approval takes an extended time (in some cases years),
the chance the inventory will be depleted or will expire before the change is fully approved increases
significantly, impacting product availability and affecting the patient. The WHO guidances on bio-
therapeutics and vaccines provide good examples of changes and apply a risk-based approach to PACs,
similar in format to the Health Canada Post-Notice of Compliance (NOC) Changes: Quality Document
(20). The WHO publications are intended to serve as a guide for establishing national requirements
for the regulation of PACs to biotherapeutic products and vaccines. In jurisdictions that do not of-
fer their own guidance, these guidelines can be helpful in applying a risk-based approach to the PAC
requirements and approval timing. In these cases, the guidance(s) should be referenced during the
PAC process. Also, the WHO PAC guidelines strongly encourage national regulatory authorities to
establish requirements commensurate with their own regulatory capacity, experience, and resources
and to work toward minimizing any negative impact on access to approved products (4,5). National
regulatory authorities are encouraged to consider establishing procedures for the expedited approval of
changes based on previous expert review and approval of the same changes by the national authorities
of the other countries where those products are licensed or, alternatively, based on the decision of a
recognized regional regulatory agency.
Where there is a potential for the timing of change approval in non-ICH regions to impact product
supply, it is highly recommended that the firm contact those regulatory agencies early to alert them to
the potential issues and request their assistance.

## 7.0 Legacy Marketed Products and ICH Q12

One of the challenges with the changing global regulatory landscape relates to marketed products, that
is, products that have been approved and are in distribution. The full benefits of the ICH Q12 PAC
processes may initially only apply to products that are newly submitted in jurisdictions that apply ICH
guidelines. Consequently, for many older products already on the market, there may be a prolonged
period to implement ICH Q12 PAC principles, which may be impacted by the inclusion of specific
information in the approved filing. For example, identifying which items should be called out as ECs
could present a challenge and could require considerable time and resources to accomplish.
To take full advantage of ICH Q12, some type of modernization of the regulatory filing around the
CMC sections may be needed.
Regardless of how they embrace ICH Q12 concepts, in ICH regions, firms will have the option to
develop and submit a list of ECs to regulatory agencies in a prior-approval submission. This would
allow the firm to conduct a full or partial assessment and possibly remove parameters currently in the
filing that, based on product knowledge, do not require a submission to change. This could reduce

the filing category of some of the parameters, for example, moving from prior approval to notifica-
tion. The firm could also use a prior-approval submission to change from a parameter-based EC to
performance-based conditions. This could be done for the entirety of Modules 3.2.S and 3.2.P or only
for specific sections.
The potential advantage to adding ECs to a marketed product is that more historical data and a bet-
ter understanding of the product exists for marketed products than would have been available when
the initial marketing authorization was submitted. That product history and knowledge can be used
to demonstrate that the changes made to certain parameters and quality attributes are low risk and,
therefore, should be allowed notification-only status or could be managed solely within the PQS. To
reduce the regulatory burden of PACs, the historic manufacturing and characterization data would
have to be submitted to justify the risk assessment scientifically. This data should be submitted in the
development sections of the filing (e.g., Modules 3.2.S.2.6 and 3.2.P.2) to support the proposed ECs
and reduced filing category. If these data are submitted in sufficient detail, once the ECs are approved,
future PACs could be implemented more quickly.
In most cases, prospectively filing a prior-approval submission to add ECs would not be efficient unless
it is submitted along with a planned major change. Otherwise, the ECs would have to be approved
before a change could be made without needing a submission, which would further increase the regu-
latory burden. Additionally, regulatory agencies in different jurisdictions may not agree to the same
ECs, which would add to the complexity of the maintenance of ECs by the firm.

## 8.0 Recommendations for Harmonization

(Categorization of Changes)
In the current regulatory environment, industry benchmarking shows that it can take up to five years
to have a single change approved across all affected countries. Current guidances from regulatory
agencies, including the FDA, EMA, WHO, and Health Canada, attempt to clarify how changes are
to be evaluated, when they should be reported, and if prior approval from the agency is required to
implement the change (4, 5, 11, 13, 20). Based on the severity of the effect of the change to the ECs
or product quality and, hence, to the safety and efficacy for patients, the changes are generally classified
in three categories:
• Do and Record (Lowest Risk) — manage within the PQS (e.g., Health Canada Level IV)
• Do and Tell (Minor to Moderate) — notification (e.g., Type IA or Annual Reportable)
— Tell, Wait and Do (some Moderate) — (e.g., Type IB or CBE-30)
• Tell, Wait and Do (Major) — prior approval (e.g., Type II or Prior Approval Supplement)
The EU’s variation regulations and post-authorization guidance and FDA’s scale-up and post-approval
change (SUPAC) guidance documents provide specific details on the scientific data that should be
submitted and how it should be presented for specific PACs (27-31). The requirements are consistent
with the potential impact of the change on the quality, safety, and efficacy of the product similar to the
underlying concepts adopted by the FDA in its Guidance for Industry: Changes to an Approved NDA
or ANDA (32).
Specific scientific documentation requirements and approval timelines can vary greatly among juris-
dictions, however.

To allow the industry to continue to modernize and make the needed improvements in both quality
and productivity in a timely fashion, action is needed in many of the non-ICH regions. Establishing
new regulations or modifying existing regulations to introduce the three mechanisms for change—PQS
management, notification, and prior approval—that align with ICH Q12 and other global regulatory
expectations is recommended. To fully realize the benefits of ICH Q12, a certain level of regulatory
convergence and harmonization is needed. With respect to categorization of changes, this convergence
and harmonization is necessary for global movement towards the model provided in Figure 3.0-1.

### 8.1 Harmonized Definitions and Consistent Requirements for Approval of

Changes
The categorization of changes depicted in Figure 3.0-1 as major, moderate, or minor throughout all
regions would greatly benefit both regulators and manufacturers globally by providing clear, consistent
guidelines.
Having a consistent set of requirements in place would allow firms to prepare a single scientific jus-
tification for their PACs that can be submitted to all regulatory agencies for approval. Specifically,
better definition of the requirements for the approval of a major change would clarify data-reporting
expectations. With this clarity, firms could better plan for major changes. With this convergence and
harmonization using the ICH framework, regulatory agencies also could provide guidelines on the
requirements for comparability that include a time-period requirement for stability data for each of the
three categories of change. Applying the same requirements across ICH and non-ICH regions would
also benefit patients.

### 8.2 Mutual Recognition and Consistent Review Times of PACs in ICH Regions

Most often, product applications are filed across multiple regions. If the classification of changes and
requirements for justification of a PAC were consistent throughout all ICH regions, a firm would be
able to submit the approval evidence of a change from one ICH member to other ICH members,
minimizing the repetitious review time for subsequent approvals. If member countries mutually rec-
ognize the approval of a PAC, filing the change in one ICH member country could then be sufficient
to obtain approval in all ICH regions.
Similar concepts could be applied in non-ICH regions. Acceptance of changes approved by recognized
regulatory agencies in ICH countries based on sound science- and risk-based principles would result
in a lower regulatory burden worldwide. This is an established practice, for example, with issuing
Certificates of Pharmaceutical Product. Many jurisdictions accept a Certificate from one jurisdiction
and leverage the approval from the Certificate-issuing country to alleviate the regulatory burden in the
country accepting the Certificate.

anticipate the timeline for approvals of the PAC, allowing for improved planning, faster introduction
of innovative technologies, and timelier availability of improved products to patients.

## 9.0 Conclusion

PACs and the continuing work to harmonize the processes used by the regulatory authorities to govern
them is a key enabler to fully implementing a continual improvement model that ensures access to
state-of-the-art, high-quality medicines and prevents or mitigates supply disruptions. The principles
outlined in ICH Q12 provide the pathway to harmonization.
If implemented globally, the principles set out in ICH Q12, including PACMPs, can lead to more
consistent reporting requirements, with the level of reporting based on the risk associated with a spe-
cific change. This, in addition to increased alignment of regulatory authority processes and approval
times, can enable a more rapid implementation of innovative technologies and improvements in the
manufacturing process.

## 11.0 Appendix 1: Replacement of Traditional

Adventitious Agent Detection Methods with Rapid,

### 11.1 Background

Changes to adopt new and improved technologies for test methods can pose a challenge to the supply
chain as this type of change typically requires regulatory agency filings and, sometimes, prior approval.
The use of testing methods to detect adventitious agents in biologics manufacturing is a necessity and
is required by regulators globally. During the manufacturing process for biologics, manufacturers may
use eukaryotic expression systems, which can be prone to microbial contamination. Consequently,
these processes require strict microbial control strategies, including testing for such adventitious agents
as mycoplasma, Leptospira, and viruses.
Current adventitious agent test methods, however, pose numerous limitations. Assays based on poly-
merase chain reactions (PCR) offer the potential to mitigate these limitations and offer even better
overall detection of adventitious agents. Biologics manufacturers are actively exploring this area, and
current research indicates that PCR-based testing is not only scientifically acceptable, but also accepted
by regulators.

PCR-based test methods for Mycoplasma sp. present certain advantages for microbiological control—
better sensitivity, faster turnaround of results, reduced sample handling, and fewer test failure issues
(e.g., due to sample matrix effects)—and timely batch release for biologic drug substances.

#### 11.1.1 Description and Rationale of Proposed Changes

The proposed change(s) should be described in sufficient detail to enable a regulatory agency to evalu-
ate the relevancy and adequacy of the PAC strategy, such as supporting data and change categorization.
Including information on the basis and rationale for the change(s), where applicable, is recommended.
The differences from the data that have already been approved should be highlighted clearly (prefer-
ably in a tabular format, as illustrated in Table 11.1.1-1).
Currently, mycoplasma testing of bulk harvest is performed according to established compendial
methods—the culture method (broth and agar cultivation procedure), and the indicator-cell culture
method (cultivation using an adequate cell line followed by a DNA-staining procedure) (1-2).
In this case study, the firm plans to introduce an alternative PCR-based method for the detection of
Mycoplasma sp. in a product’s bulk harvest. This PCR method would be used to perform mycoplasma
testing on unprocessed bulk harvest samples as an alternative mycoplasma test method. The existing,
registered test for mycoplasma (compliant with both European and U.S. pharmacopeias) will remain
in the application.

While this limited specificity and detection limit/sensitivity is a current challenge for the development

adventitious agents. The mode-of-action of some NMEs is based on cellular cytotoxicity. Cytotoxicity-
induced cell death not only kills the oncogenic target cells but also the permanent cell lines used as
indicator cells for the traditional in vitro detection methods. Therefore, the testing sample needs to
be diluted (product-dependent up to 1:10,000), resulting in very low sensitivity of the traditional in
vitro detection methods. Whether or not this situation will be tolerated by regulators in the future
is questionable; however, regulators have published guidance documents recommending PCR-based
methods for specific target organisms.
Another example demonstrating the acceptance of PCR-based detection methods by regulators can
be found in the FDA’s Q&A on Current Good Manufacturing Practices, Good Guidance Practices. Here,
the Agency raised the question of whether the microbial control strategies of biologics manufactur-
ers are capable of detecting unusual microbiota such as Leptospira. The FDA recommends the “use of
validated PCR methods (e.g., as an investigative tool) for rapid screening and detection of spirochete
bacteria” (5).
To address the potential limitations of the traditional detection methods discussed in Section 11.1.2,
several companies have introduced multiyear programs, intending to replace traditional cell- and
growth-based detection methods (for microbial contaminants, e.g., mycoplasmal contaminants, or
specific virus strains, such as MMV) with new, fully automated PCR-based methods. These new meth-
ods include purification of the target nucleic acid and real-time PCR amplification and detection
systems.

Automated PCR-based testing methods for adventitious agents, in contrast to traditional indicator-
cell and growth-based methods, detect contaminants in five to eight hours of analytical time. Thus,
biopharmaceutical companies can isolate the contamination to upstream and harvest equipment
without contaminating downstream equipment. No additional or subsequent processed lots using
the contaminated downstream equipment are secondarily contaminated. Consequently, decontami-
nation efforts are less extensive, resulting in reduced facility shut-down times. In general, PCR-based
adventitious-agent detection methods significantly reduce reaction times, minimize the impact of any
potential contamination on the product supply, and ensure an uninterrupted supply to patients.
Higher throughput and higher reliability are additional benefits of PCR-based methods as they can be ex-
ecuted on fully automated robotic platforms without the need for manual intervention. This avoids safety,
health, and environmental hazards, including such ergonomic issues as repetitive strain injury. Other bene-
fits include cost savings, improved productivity, and increased agility and flexibility in the testing network.

### 11.3 Comparability Protocol Strategy Overview for the Replacement of the

Traditional Mycoplasma Detection Methods with a PCR-based Method
According to Ph. Eur. Mycoplasma 2.6.7 and Ph. Eur. Nucleic Acid Amplification Techniques 2.6.21,
the following parameters should be evaluated for a qualitative assay by the validation program (1,6):
• Specificity— ability to unequivocally assess target nucleic acid in the presence of other (poten-
tially interfering) components that may be expected to be present
• Detection Limit/Sensitivity— lowest amount of target nucleic acid in a sample that can be
detected, but not necessarily quantitated as an exact value
• Robustness— measure of its capacity to remain unaffected by small, but deliberate, variations
in method parameters and provide an indication of test method reliability during normal usage
• Comparability Study— comparison of the detection limits of the alternative method and of-
ficial methods; however, specificity should also be considered
The validation program could be split into two sections:
• Generic Method Validation— evaluation of all the parameters listed above
• Product-Specific Method Validation— use of different lots of the specimen to be tested with
a limited number of sensitivity experiments to evaluate whether the PCR-based detection method
is unaffected by the specific sample matrix (test for interfering or inhibiting properties of the
sample matrix or method suitability test) and by lot-to-lot-variability of the sample to be analyzed
The experimental data generated during the generic method validation program, as well as the data of
the product-specific method validation(s) method suitability test(s), would be included in the submis-
sion package.

#### 11.3.1 Conditions that Need to be Met Prior to Implementation of the Change:

Acceptance Criteria for the PCR-Based Mycoplasma Detection Method

##### 11.3.1.1 Acceptance Criteria for the Generic Method Validation

##### 11.3.1.1 Specificity

The ability of the PCR-based mycoplasma detection method to detect a large panel of mycoplasma
species should be demonstrated using characterized reference panels (e.g., reference strains). Moreover,
as the primers will likely detect other bacterial species, the potential cross-detection should be docu-
mented in the validation study.
Based on the results of this validation of specificity, if a gap in the specificity of the method is identified
(such as detection of nonmycoplasmal bacteria species), an appropriate strategy must be proposed in
the validation study to allow interpretation of positive results on a routine basis. For example, a second
test may be performed using an alternative method without this specificity gap or by using an official
method.

##### 11.3.1.2 Detection Limit/Sensitivity

A positive cut-off point should be determined for the PCR-based detection method. The positive cut-
off point, as defined in Ph. Eur. 2.6.21, is the minimum number of target sequence copies per volume
of sample that can be detected in 95% of test runs.
Acceptance criteria for the detection limit/sensitivity are defined as follows:
• If the alternative method is proposed to replace the culture method, the PCR-based mycoplasma
detection system must be shown to detect 10 CFU/mL for each relevant mycoplasma test species
chosen.
• If the alternative method is proposed to replace the indicator-cell culture method, the nucleic
acid amplification techniques (NAT) system must be shown to detect 100 CFU/mL for each
mycoplasma test used.

##### 11.3.1.3 Robustness

To determine whether the assay remains unaffected by deliberate variations caused by different lots of
reagents and solutions, three lots of selected critical PCR assay ingredients are used for the PCR-based
mycoplasma detection assay.
Using any of these combinations, the PCR-based mycoplasma detection assay must be able to detect
at least 10 CFU/mL of any of the mycoplasma reference preparations (e.g., M. orale).

##### 11.3.1.4 Comparability Study

In the comparability study, the PCR-based alternative method should be performed in parallel with
the official methods to evaluate simultaneously the detection limit/sensitivity of both methods using
CHO cultures inoculated with different spike levels of mycoplasma reference strains.
The detection limit/sensitivity, as well as the specificity of the PCR-based mycoplasma detection meth-
od, should be comparable or superior to the traditional mycoplasma detection methods.

##### 11.3.1.5 Acceptance Criteria for Product-Specific Method Validation

To evaluate whether the test specimen possesses inhibitory or interfering properties on the PCR-based
mycoplasma detection method, the parameter detection limit/sensitivity should be examined with
three different lots of the test specimen (method suitability test).
The detection limit/sensitivity of the PCR-based assay determined in the generic method validation
must be confirmed using a defined set of mycoplasma species for different lots of the test specimen.

PURPOSE
The intent of this validation is to demonstrate that the mycoplasma PCR assay is suitable for the
detection of mycoplasma species in preharvest cell-culture fluid (PHCCF) samples. It also fulfills the
requirements given in Ph. Eur. 2.6.7 for an alternative NAT-based method proposed as an alternative
to the indicator-cell culture and the culture methods for the detection of mycoplasmas. According

the culture method should be performed.
The presence of mycoplasma DNA in the sample is determined by a 400-500 bp target sequence that is am-

DESCRIPTION OF PLANNED CHANGES
Generic Validation of the Mycoplasma PCR Assay
The validation of the mycoplasma PCR assay follows the criteria described in Ph. Eur. 2.6.21 and in
the ICH Q2(R1) guideline for a qualitative test. According to the Ph. Eur., the criteria specificity,
sensitivity/detection limit, and robustness should be addressed.
Specificity
The specificity of the assay will be tested with a large panel of the mycoplasma strains listed in the “Ma-
terials and Methods” section, as well as with other bacterial species that might be cross-detected in the
assay. The bacterial species listed in Materials and Methods were chosen for this study due to the close
phylogenetic relationship to mycoplasma strains. CHO cells will be spiked with mycoplasma strains as
noted. These spiked cells will be examined as a sample in the mycoplasma PCR assay.
In addition, CHO cells will be spiked with a serial dilution of the Gram-positive bacteria to concentrations
of 106–102 CFU/mL. These spiked cells will also be examined as a sample in the mycoplasma PCR assay.
Acceptance Criteria
Acceptance criteria of the test assay must be met for all positive and negative controls.
An amplicon of 400-500 bp must be detectable for all mycoplasma strains tested.
No acceptance criterion is defined for the cross-detection of the Gram-positive bacteria.
Sensitivity/Detection Limit
The detection limit of the mycoplasma PCR assay is the lowest number of CFU in a sample that can be
detected but not necessarily quantitated. To determine the detection limit for each mycoplasma strain, three
independent 10fold dilution series will be tested. The mycoplasma strains chosen will represent all strains
required in the NAT validation chapter (Ph. Eur. 2.6.21), as well as M. orale, M. salivarium, and M. hominis.
According to the data published by McGaritty et al., the five strains—M. hyorhinis, M. orale, M. argi-
nini, M. fermentans, and A. laidlawii—account for more than 95% of all isolates found in mammalian
cell cultures (11).

More recent publications by Timenetsky et al. and Uphoff & Drexler confirm the data published by
McGarrity et al. In addition, both M. salivarium and M. hominis have been described as sources of my-
coplasma contamination in mammalian cell cultures. These literature citations support the relevancy
of the selected validation strains to industry contamination risks (12-14).
The determination of the sensitivity/detection limit of the mycoplasma PCR method will be per-
formed using the mycoplasma strain stocks listed in the Comparability Study section below. For the
calculation of the different mycoplasma spike concentrations, the titer before freezing will be used.
To determine the detection limit for each mycoplasma strain stock, three independent runs (dilution
series) will be prepared on different days with eight test replicates for each dilution, giving a total num-
ber of 24 test results. In each dilution series, CHO cells will be spiked with 10, 5, 1, 0.5, and 0.1 CFU/
mL. The spiked CHO cells will then be analyzed as samples in the mycoplasma PCR assay. If the cut-off
point is reached for one organism, no further testing of the higher dilutions will be evaluated. The cut-
off point is the minimum number of CFU/mL of sample that can be detected in 95% of the test runs.
If necessary, additional spike concentrations will be used.
Acceptance Criteria
Acceptance criteria of the test assay must be met for all positive and negative controls.
If the mycoplasma PCR assay is proposed as an alternative to the compendial methods (culture meth-
od and indicator-cell culture method), the Ph. Eur. claims that the alternative method can detect
10 CFU/mL for each mycoplasma strain mentioned. Therefore, for each of the strains tested for the
detection limit, a band of 400-500 bp must be present in no fewer than 23 of 24 test results (=95%
cutoff) up to a concentration of at least 10 CFU/mL.
Robustness
In order to examine the robustness of the ready-to-use PCR mycoplasma detection assay, CHO cells
will be spiked with M. orale master stock at concentrations of 1000, 100, 10, 1, 0.1, and 0.01 CFU/
mL. The spiked CHO cells will be analyzed as samples in the PCR mycoplasma detection assay. The
tests will be carried out independently from each other by one analyst on three different days.
The PCR mycoplasma detection assay was developed for use with two models of thermocycler. Each
thermocycler uses different mechanisms for heating and cooling; hence, both thermocycler models will
be included in the robustness study.
To determine whether the assay is affected by different lots, two lots of the commercial kit will be used
to perform the experiments.
Acceptance Criteria
Acceptance criteria of the test assay must be met for all positive and negative controls.
A band of 400–500 bp must be present up to a concentration of 10 CFU/mL in all three test runs.
The detection limit determined for all three test runs must be comparable (difference between the test
runs of not more than 1 log step).

Comparability Study
To demonstrate equivalency between the compendial test methods (culture method and indicator-cell
culture method) and the mycoplasma PCR assay, comparability studies will be performed using the
mycoplasma reference strain stocks.
To demonstrate comparability between the indicator-cell culture method, the culture method, and the
mycoplasma PCR assay, 15 mL of a CHO-K1 culture will be spiked with the following mycoplasma
strain stocks to a concentration of 100 CFU/mL:
• M. hominis ATCC 23114 • M. pneumoniae NCTC 10119
• M. orale ATCC 23714 • M. salivarium ATCC 23064
• M. arginini ATCC 23838 • M. fermentans ATCC 19989
• A. laidlawii EDQM reference strain (15) • Sp. citri ATCC 27556
• M. hyorhinis EDQM reference strain (10)
• M. hyorhinis ATCC 29052
(fastidious strain)
The spike concentration will be determined using the titer of each of the mycoplasma strain stocks
before freezing.
Based on this initially spiked sample (100 CFU/mL), a serial 10fold dilution will be prepared using
unspiked CHO-K1 cell culture as diluent, leading to samples spiked with 100, 10, 1, 0.1, 0.01, and
0.001 CFU/mL. The spiking will be performed in triplicate.
After spiking, these samples will be subjected to all three different mycoplasma detection methods in

spike concentration in which mycoplasma can be detected in all three runs.
The detection limit of the three mycoplasma detection methods should be comparable.

## 12.0 Appendix 2: Introduction of a New

Isolator System for Aseptic Fill

### 12.1 Background

Appendix 2 describes the introduction of improved technology for aseptic filling and the related assess-
ment of the impact to currently approved biological products. This example refers to the upgrade from
a legacy conventional-filling operation to an isolator technology for toxic, biologically active, geneti-
cally modified, cell-based, and/or protein therapeutics. The introduction of the new isolator aims to
enhance the quality of the product and reduce microbial, particulate, and chemical contamination as
well as increase the sterility safety level and/or operator-safety environment in the case of active biolog-
ics or genetically modified organisms.

### 12.2 Current Regulatory Filing Considerations—Improvements to Drug

Product Aseptic Filling

#### 12.2.1 U.S. Filing Considerations

With respect to certain biologics, the FDA Guidance for Industry: Chemistry, Manufacturing, and Con-
trols Changes to an Approved Application: Certain Biological Products suggests that changes impacting
the transfer of manufacturing of an aseptically processed sterile drug substance or drug product into a
new or renovated aseptic processing area within the currently licensed building would be considered
a prior approval supplement (PAS). Likewise, the addition or modification to an approved filling line
that affects sterility assurance would be considered a PAS (1).

In the 1997 FDA Guidance for Industry: Changes to an Approved Application: Biological Products, “Section II.
Changes under §601.12(b) – Changes requiring supplement submission and approval prior to distribution

Major construction, or changes in location, involving or affecting environmentally controlled
manufacturing or related support areas, such as ***
• aseptic processing areas;
• modifications to support systems with significant potential to affect air, water, or steam quality;
• installation of a new HVAC system involving or affecting environmentally controlled manu-
facturing or related support areas; or
• modifications to an existing HVAC system that supplies aseptic processing areas (2).
In general, the process impact of changing the drug product filling process to replace the filling unit

The needed space may be reduced, as an isolator would have a Grade A chamber that can be placed in
a Grade C or D space, making a Grade B background no longer necessary. In addition, other adapta-
tions would be needed to accommodate material and airflow accessibility.
FDA offers general information about aseptic processing in Guidance for Industry: Sterile Drug Products
Produced by Aseptic Processing – Current Good Manufacturing Practice and sets high-level expectations
for industry in Guideline for the Submission of Documentation for Sterilization Process Validation in Ap-

FDA and other regulatory authorities look for in aseptic filling processes; however, neither provides

PURPOSE
This post-approval change management protocol (PACMP) describes the strategy and approach for
demonstrating the introduction of an isolator as an improvement to an aseptic filling suite for Ourfa-
voritebiologic (OFB).
This protocol outlines the proposed studies, test methods, and acceptance criteria that will be used to
assess the impact on OFB drug product when manufactured using the new isolator. The data from the
proposed studies will demonstrate that the quality of the OFB drug product is at least comparable to
the historical OFB drug product and that the new isolator is acceptable for manufacturing this product.
SCOPE OF CHANGE
Sterile drug product manufacturing is accomplished in various ways; traditionally, ISO-5 laminar air
flow (LAF) units with manual operations have been widely used. As the sterility of the product is of
the greatest concern, manual operations always have disadvantages over isolated systems. The overall
objective in the aseptic filling process is to attain the highest sterility assurance level by using an iso-
lated system. Currently, this site uses ISO-5 LAF conventional filling technology, an open restricted
access barrier system (RABS). To switch from LAF to any other isolated system for the filling of pro-
tein therapeutics as sterile solutions may require an extensive overhaul of the manufacturing process;
however, the increased control over the following critical areas (in order of importance) plays a major
role in maintaining the quality of the product (11):
• Microbial contamination
• Particulate contamination
• Chemical contamination
Isolated systems available in the industry to exercise that control are:
• Closed RABS (access by glove ports)
• Isolators (access by glove ports with automated decontamination)
• Aseptic work cell with robot (gloveless systems)

process are illustrated in Figure 12-1 using a fishbone structure.
A thorough assessment was completed with the three options (i.e., RABS, isolator, aseptic work cell)
weighted against product impact, production needs, facility capabilities, and business needs. Moving
forward with the isolator technology was deemed best suited for meeting the needs of the business and
ensuring product quality.

BACKGROUND
Current Drug Product Manufacturing
The aseptic manufacturing process for OFB consists of sterile filtration, aseptic filling, capping or clos-

for OFB are shared with other sterile liquid products. All products are manufactured on a campaign
basis so that only one product is present in the manufacturing or filling suite at a time. A validated
changeover procedure is implemented prior to the initiation of a new campaign.
All OFB product-contact manufacturing equipment is dedicated to OFB. Product-contact materi-
als are primarily stainless steel and silicone tubing, and other product-contact materials are glass and
acrylics. Elastomers used in the manufacturing process (e.g., gaskets, tubing) meet the specifications of
USP Class VI, as defined by USP <88> Biological Reactivity, In Vivo, and 21 CFR 177.2600 – Rubber
Articles Intended for Repeated Use (12, 13).
Lot Size(s)
There are two lot sizes for OFB: 450 L and 1250 L. The yield can vary from 50,000–225,000 vials and
135,000–625,000 vials, respectively. All products filled on this filling line use the same lot sizes and
are supported by media fills spanning these ranges.

Container Closure Integrity Testing
When new container–closure components or a change to the filling process that may impact container
closure are introduced, container–closure integrity assessments are performed. In order to confirm
comparability following the introduction of the isolator, container–closure integrity will be tested
to support this change. Container and closure system integrity testing includes performing validated
physical or chemical container and closure system integrity tests (e.g., bubble tests, pressure/vacuum
decay tests, trace gas permeation/leak tests, dye penetration tests, seal force or electrical conductivity
tests, and capacitance tests) or microbiological container and closure system integrity tests (e.g., micro-
bial challenge or immersion tests). Such tests may be more useful than sterility testing in demonstrat-
ing the potential for product contamination over the product’s shelf life or dating period. The con-
tainer and closure system integrity tests for OFB will be designed to detect a breach of the container
and/or closure system prior to product contamination.

Currently Approved Gowning Procedures
Personnel entering the manufacturing areas gown and degown according to specified requirements
following approved written procedures. Different levels of gowning occur according to the environ-
mental controls and to the type of operation being carried out in the manufacturing rooms. Gown-
ing garments might include safety shoes, shoe covers, hair cover, facial hair cover (as needed), safety
glasses, coveralls, booties, sterile gloves, sterile sleeves, or sterile mask in line with written procedures.
Personnel are required to wear gloves prior to handling any cleaned, sanitized, or in-process produc-
tion equipment.
An external laundry service may be used for cleaning and/or sterilization of reusable coveralls and
uniforms, where applicable.
Cleaning Procedures
The facility is cleaned and sanitized using primary disinfectants and sporicidal agents at defined inter-
vals to control mold, bacteria, and spore-forming microorganisms. All manufacturing areas are cleaned
and sanitized as required in written procedures, and all cleaning and sanitization activities are docu-
mented. The firm periodically reviews the data obtained from environmental monitoring to determine
if further modifications to the facility cleaning program are warranted. Bioburden is verified by two
testing methods: Contact surface samples and airborne samples for viable particulates.
Change-Out Procedures
The currently approved and the proposed filling line will be used on a campaign basis across several
protein therapeutics, all filled into glass vials in a multiproduct facility. Filling needles, gloves, all tub-
ing, connectors, and glass containers are single-use and replaced for each campaign. The manufactur-
ing area is dedicated to the production of only one product at any one time, with complete cleaning
and changeover between products.
Throughout the manufacturing process, appropriate measures are established to ensure the absence
of product contamination. Other methods used to prevent contamination include facility design fea-
tures, environmental monitoring, dedicated equipment, room air classification, air pressure differen-
tial, flow of personnel, materials and waste, and cleaning procedures.
With respect to the currently approved and proposed filling process, there are no reusable direct product-
contact materials. Multiuse product-contact equipment is cleaned following validated processes, and the
cleaning effectiveness is verified by total organic carbon methods. Process equipment is sterilized into the
aseptic core or proposed isolator by autoclave sterilization or dry-heat depyrogenation processes.
The product-contact components in the formulation (carboys, stir bars, sampling materials, filters, and
transfer tubing) are sterilized prior to use and are single-use, disposable materials. All product-contact
components in the filtration system (tubing and filters) and in the filtrate receiving-vessel (glass carboy,
tubing, and carboy ports) are sterilized prior to use and are single-use, disposable materials.
The currently approved process uses a diaphragm pump, and proposed filling lines use a peristaltic
pump system. The product-contact components (tubing, connectors, and filling nozzles) are sterilized
prior to use and are single-use, disposable materials. Parts of the filling machine and other equipment
that cannot be sterilized by thermal methods are sanitized by wiping external surfaces with approved
and validated germicidal or sporicidal agents. Such chemical sanitization procedures are restricted to
equipment that cannot be sterilized by other means and to equipment that is not in direct product
contact or in the direct process path.

Media Fill Program
A media fill is one part of the validation of an aseptic manufacturing process. Media fills are performed
every six months and after any changes to the aseptic fill suite are made. Using a sterile microbiological
growth medium in place of the drug product, media fills are designed to simulate the filling process to
ensure the performance of the aseptic manufacturing procedure. Microbiological growth medium is
used in place of the drug solution during media fills to test whether the aseptic procedures are adequate
to prevent contamination during actual drug production.
The media fill program is designed to evaluate the aseptic assembly and operation of the critical (ster-
ile) equipment, qualify the operators and assess their technique, and demonstrate that the environ-
mental controls are adequate to meet the basic requirements necessary to produce a sterile drug by
aseptic processing. Data collected from media fills are compared to predefined acceptance criteria and
run against an approved protocol. The media fills cover the vial sizes specified for OFB (i.e., 3 mL, 6
mL, and 10 mL), ensuring that both the smallest and largest sizes are subject to the media fill.
To assess the impact of the new isolator on the filling suite, media fills will be completed with the new
equipment according to the procedures described here and as part of the comparability and PPQ as-
sessment of this change. The viable monitoring requirements during the filling period and the micro-
bial monitoring results for the media fills pre- and post-change will be provided.
DESCRIPTION OF PLANNED CHANGES
A new isolator will be introduced into the manufacturing suite used for the manufacture of OFB.
When the new isolator is introduced, the filling line will also be completely replaced using a new pump
that has greater throughput and accuracy.
Isolator
The isolator is a “Separative Device” that will provide assured protection by using physical and dynam-
ic barriers to create separation between operation and operator; it comes supplied with HEPA-filtered
air and will be decontaminated in a quantifiable and highly reproducible manner. When the system is
closed, it uses only decontaminated interfaces (11, 14).
This change will introduce new technology: a stainless steel–glass isolator and positive pressurization.
This design facilitates both aseptic and containment applications; however, it will be used primarily in
aseptic processing with the goal of excluding contaminants from the isolator. Construction materials
will be mechanically and chemically resistant (i.e., stainless-steel frame, glass). The operator interface
will require gloves, gauntlets, and appropriate gowning. The aseptic core will continue to be a HEPA-
filtered air supply with vertical, unidirectional air flow (open) and pressure differentials of no more
than 100 Pa. Pressure differentials between the exterior and the interior of the isolator will be moni-
tored continuously and integrated into the existing automated process control system.
Sterilization-in-place will be implemented for contact parts, such as fluid pathways. In areas where this
cannot be performed, all parts will be autoclaved to achieve sterilization and aseptically assembled pri-
or to processing. Parts of the filling machine and other equipment that cannot be sterilized by thermal
methods are sanitized by wiping external surfaces with approved and validated germicidal or sporicidal
agents. Such chemical sanitization procedures are restricted to equipment that cannot be sterilized by
other means and to equipment that is not in direct product contact or in the direct process path.
A full description of the proposed isolator will be available in the PPQ protocol.

The scope of this change is limited to the isolator, which consists of a sterilization tunnel, filling ma-
chine, and capping machine that will have the capability of processing 3 mL, 6 mL, 10 mL vials of
sterile, liquid, injectable drug products. Autoclaves and support utilities are excluded from the scope of
this change. (See Table 12-6 for the impact to process.) The PPQ will be designed according to USP
<1208> and will be validated before its use in sterility testing as part of a batch release (15). To verify
that the isolator system and all impacted equipment are suitable for aseptic processing, the following
validation studies will be completed: Factory acceptance testing (FAT), site acceptance testing (SAT),
installation qualification, operational qualification, and performance qualification. All changes will be
assessed in the PPQ, which will confirm that all identified CPPs and CQAs of the drug product are met.

PROCESS DEVELOPMENT AND CHARACTERIZATION STUDIES
Laboratory Pump Studies
The equipment used for the new isolator will be comparable to that used in the current aseptic filling
suite. The new pump could potentially impact the product quality due to shear forces. This laboratory
study was performed to evaluate the pump and establish its operating parameters; the result is sum-
marized here. These studies demonstrated that the new pump will not negatively impact the product
quality and identified the filling parameters. The pump settings identified in these studies were used
in the Engineering runs.
The new isolator will use peristaltic filling pumps (currently a diaphragm pump system is used). A
laboratory-scale study, using a pump representative of the one used for the new isolator, was performed
to optimize the filling set-up and pump parameters and to evaluate the performance of the pumps
about fill volume accuracy, precision, and impact on product quality.
Studies were initially conducted using the OFB placebo solution to identify the pump settings that
would optimize the fill volume accuracy and precision. These conditions were then tested using OFB
drug product solution to ensure that the addition of OFB does not affect those parameters and to
evaluate its quality. The equipment and optimized set-up of the pump configuration resulted in en-
hanced process control capability for the fill volume compared to the pumps and pump configuration
used in the current aseptic fill suite. Further optimizations of the pump settings were carried out dur-
ing Engineering runs.
To assess the potential impact of shear forces on product quality during the filling, the same OFB drug
product solution was pumped through the pump set-up, including filling needles, three times. After
the third pump cycle, samples of the OFB drug product solution were analyzed against the CQAs. In
addition, testing was performed to evaluate the generation of ≥2 µm and ≥5 µm subvisible particles.
This data generated from the proposed peristaltic pump was compared to data generated from the cur-
rently approved diaphragm pump.

Cleaning Validation
The PPQ will capture all cleaning validations through the new filling process.
Isolator Chamber Decontamination
The isolator system ensures a defined reduction of microbial contamination on the surfaces of the iso-
lator chamber using hydrogen peroxide. H₂O₂ decontamination cycles for the isolator in the syringe
and vial lines were developed by defining the parameters that ensured a sterility assurance level of 6-log
microbial reduction.
For the decontamination cycle, Geobacillus stearothermophilus with a population of at least 106 pores/
carrier are placed at defined locations, representing worse-case locations, within the isolator chambers.
An assessment of potential impact to product and exposure to low levels of ozone and/or hydrogen
peroxide will be completed.
Media Fills
Three media fills (aseptic process simulation) will be performed to requalify the filling process, the
equipment, and the aseptic process following the introduction of the isolator. The media fills will be
executed under protocol and serve to validate the aseptic process as a whole, using the sterile compo-
nents, controlled room, and filling equipment to fill sterile media. The media fill validation will be
simulated at all steps: from introduction of components from the sterilization tunnel to sterile filtra-
tion of the drug substance (using media), to preparation of the containers and closures and the filling,
stoppering, and sealing of the drug product (media). The three media fills will bracket all vial sizes
(3 mL, 6 mL, and 10 mL), container sizes (neck diameters), fill speeds, and filling time durations.
Air Visualization Studies
Smoke studies, or airflow visualization tests, will also be conducted during the qualification of the new
isolator for aseptic filling. The smoke studies are conducted to confirm airflow direction in the cleanroom.
Full details of all validation and qualification exercises will be included in the PPQ protocol.

Justification of Change
The introduction of the state-of-the art isolator for aseptic filling is considered necessary to enhance
the current GMP standing. Currently, the site utilizes a conventional filler with an aseptic Grade A
core within a suite with a Grade B background.

Contamination may take the form of particulates, microorganisms, or endotoxins. Human operators,
as a source of both viable and nonviable particles, pose the greatest risk to product contamination
during aseptic processing. Introducing the isolator filling technology significantly reduces these risks:
• Number of process operation steps, which will reduce the potential for an error that ultimately
could lead to the distribution of a contaminated product
• Need for intervention(s), that is, any manual or mechanical manipulation of the sterilized drug,
components, containers, or closures prior to or during aseptic assembly that poses the greatest
risk of contamination
The isolator will also create a physical barrier, lending protection to the product, container, and clo-
sure, preventing exposure to the environment (i.e., currently “open” process steps in the LAF filler).
Use of the improved filling system will further enhance protection of the product from human contact.
Upon approval of this PACMP and with successful execution of the studies (see “Comparability and
Process Validation”), introduction of the proposed filling line is considered a process improvement. All
data will be provided post-change as a Notification.
