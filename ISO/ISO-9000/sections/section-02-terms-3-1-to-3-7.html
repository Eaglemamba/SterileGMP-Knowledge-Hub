3.1

Terms Related to Person or People  |  人員相關術語

ISO 9000:2015 Clause 3.1 — Defines roles and engagement within the quality management system

Original Text — Source Definitions  |  原文定義

3.1.1 top management  （高層管理者）

Definition: Person or group of people who directs and controls an organization (3.2.1) at the highest level.

Note 1: Top management has the power to delegate authority and provide resources within the organization.

Note 2: If the scope of the management system covers only part of an organization, then top management refers to those who direct and control that part.

Note 3: Common term in Annex SL for ISO management system standards.

3.1.2 quality management system consultant  （品質管理系統顧問）

Definition: Person who assists the organization on quality management system realization, giving advice or information.

Note: The QMS consultant can also assist in realizing parts of a quality management system (3.5.4). ISO 10019:2005 provides guidance on distinguishing competent from non-competent QMS consultants.

3.1.3 involvement  （參與）

Definition: Taking part in an activity, event or situation.

3.1.4 engagement  （投入）

Definition: Involvement in, and contribution to, activities to achieve shared objectives.

Note: Engagement goes beyond mere involvement — it implies active contribution and shared ownership of outcomes.

3.1.5 configuration authority / configuration control board  （組態授權機構）

Definition: Person or group with assigned responsibility and authority to make decisions on configuration (3.10.6). Relevant interested parties within and outside the organization should be represented. [SOURCE: ISO 10007]

3.1.6 dispute resolver (DRP-provider context)  （爭議解決人）

Definition: Individual person assigned by a DRP-provider (3.2.7) to assist the parties in resolving a dispute (3.9.6). Examples include staff, volunteers, and contractor personnel. [SOURCE: ISO 10003]

Commentary & Pharma Application  |  解析與製藥應用

Top Management in Pharma: QP vs. ISO 9000 定義

ISO 9000 defines "top management" functionally — whoever has authority over the QMS scope, can delegate, and provides resources. In pharmaceutical manufacturing, this typically maps to the Site Director or VP of Operations.

However, pharma has a uniquely regulated role with no direct ISO equivalent: the Qualified Person (QP) under EU GMP Annex 16. The QP holds legal authority to certify batch release — authority that cannot be fully delegated upward or downward. This creates a dual-authority structure:

  • ISO "top management" → Site Director (business + QMS authority)
  • Regulatory "top authority" → QP (batch release authority, personal legal liability)

In ISO audits, the question "who is top management?" matters because ISO 9001 Clause 5 places specific obligations (leadership, commitment, policy) on top management. Ensure roles are clearly documented.

Involvement vs. Engagement — 為什麼這個差異重要？

Think of it this way: a bystander who watches a fire drill is involved (present, taking part). A fire warden who leads the drill, checks headcounts, and reports findings is engaged (contributing, driving outcomes).

In pharma quality culture, ISO 9001:2015 explicitly requires engagement of people — not just their physical presence in training sessions. This shift is deliberate: the 2015 revision moved away from "procedure compliance" toward genuine participation and ownership.

For GMP purposes: an operator who signs a batch record is involved. An operator who flags a process deviation before it becomes an OOS result is engaged.

ISO 9000 Person-Related Terms at a Glance 術語速覽

  • Top management (3.1.1) — Directs and controls at highest level / 最高層指揮控制者
  • QMS consultant (3.1.2) — External advisor; competence governed by ISO 10019 / 外部顧問
  • Involvement (3.1.3) — Taking part / 參與活動
  • Engagement (3.1.4) — Contributing to achieve shared objectives / 積極投入並貢獻共同目標

Terms Related to Organization  |  組織相關術語

ISO 9000:2015 Clause 3.2 — Defines the organizational entities that interact within a quality system

Original Text — Source Definitions  |  原文定義

3.2.1 organization  （組織）

Definition: Person or group of people that has its own functions with responsibilities, authorities and relationships to achieve its objectives (3.7.1).

Note: Includes sole-trader, company, corporation, firm, enterprise, authority, partnership, charity, institution, or part/combination thereof — public or private, incorporated or not.

Note: Annex SL common term for ISO management system standards.

3.2.2 context of the organization  （組織環境）

Definition: Combination of internal and external issues that can have an effect on an organization's approach to developing and achieving its objectives.

Note 1: Can relate to products, services, investments, and behaviour towards interested parties.

Note 3: Also referred to as "business environment" or "organizational environment" or "ecosystem of an organization".

Note 4: Understanding the infrastructure (3.5.2) can help define context.

3.2.3 interested party (stakeholder)  （利害關係人）

Definition: Person or organization that can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by a decision or activity.

Examples: Customers, owners, people in an organization, providers, bankers, regulators, unions, partners, or society — including competitors or opposing pressure groups.

3.2.4 customer  （顧客）

Definition: Person or organization that could or does receive a product (3.7.6) or service (3.7.7) that is intended for or required by this person or organization.

Examples: Consumer, client, end-user, retailer, receiver of product/service from an internal process, beneficiary, purchaser.

Note: A customer can be internal or external to the organization.

3.2.5 provider / supplier  （提供者 / 供應商）

Definition: Organization that provides a product or service.

Examples: Producer, distributor, retailer or vendor of a product or service.

Note 1: A provider can be internal or external. Note 2: In a contractual situation, a provider is sometimes called "contractor".

3.2.6 external provider / external supplier  （外部提供者）

Definition: Provider (3.2.5) that is not part of the organization.

Examples: Producer, distributor, retailer or vendor of a product or service external to the organization.

3.2.7 DRP-provider (dispute resolution process provider)  （爭議解決程序提供者）

Definition: Person or organization that supplies and operates an external dispute resolution process (3.4.1). Generally a legal entity separate from the organization and complainant. [SOURCE: ISO 10003]

3.2.8 association  （協會）

Definition: Organization consisting of member organizations or persons. [SOURCE: ISO 10003]

3.2.9 metrological function  （計量職能）

Definition: Functional unit with administrative and technical responsibility for defining and implementing the measurement management system (3.5.7). [SOURCE: ISO 10012]

Commentary & Pharma Application  |  解析與製藥應用

"Interested Party" in Pharma — Who Counts? 利害關係人：製藥業的完整清單

ISO 9000's definition of interested party is deliberately broad: anyone who can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves affected by your decisions. For a pharma CDMO, this includes:

  • Regulatory authorities — FDA, EMA, PMDA, TFDA; can shut down manufacturing
  • Marketing Authorization Holder (MAH) — client who owns the product license
  • Patients — ultimate end-users; cannot perceive a problem until harm occurs
  • Healthcare providers — prescribers, pharmacists who depend on product quality
  • Internal workforce — operators, QC analysts exposed to risk
  • Investors / shareholders — exposed to recall, enforcement, reputational risk
  • Society / environment — affected by pharmaceutical waste, water discharge

ISO 9001:2015 Clause 4.2 requires identifying and understanding the needs of relevant interested parties — a more expansive obligation than ICH Q10's "customer focus."

Customer vs. Patient — 顧客與病患的差異

In a CDMO business model, the customer (3.2.4) is the MAH who pays for manufacturing. But the end-user — the patient — is not the paying customer.

ISO 9000 captures this complexity: "consumer, client, end-user, retailer... beneficiary" are all potential customers depending on context. In pharma: the MAH is the direct customer; the patient is the end-user; the healthcare provider is an intermediary. All three have different "requirements" (3.6.4) the QMS must satisfy.

This distinction matters enormously in QMS audits. When a non-conformance harms a patient who never directly contracted with you, regulatory liability still applies — because the patient is a recognized interested party.

Provider vs. External Provider — CDMO Context 外部提供者在合約製造中的應用

ISO 9000 distinguishes provider (3.2.5) from external provider (3.2.6). A CDMO is an external provider to the MAH. Within the CDMO, raw material suppliers, contract testing labs, and qualified subcontractors are also external providers.

ISO 9001:2015 Clause 8.4 places specific controls on externally provided processes, products and services. This directly maps to GMP Annex 7 (outsourced activities) — both require qualifying and monitoring external providers as part of the QMS.

Context of the Organization — PESTLE Analysis 組織環境分析

Clause 4.1 of ISO 9001:2015 requires identifying internal and external issues. Common frameworks used to map "context of the organization" include PESTLE (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental). For pharma:

  • Political: FDA enforcement climate, import alerts
  • Legal: country-specific GMP legislation, data integrity requirements
  • Technological: PAT adoption, continuous manufacturing trends
  • Social: patient access, drug shortage risks

Terms Related to Activity  |  活動相關術語

ISO 9000:2015 Clause 3.3 — Defines quality management activities including the critical QA/QC distinction

Original Text — Source Definitions  |  原文定義

3.3.1 improvement  （改善）

Definition: Activity to enhance performance (3.7.8).

Note: The activity can be recurring or singular.

3.3.2 continual improvement  （持續改善）

Definition: Recurring activity to enhance performance.

Note 1: Management review of establishing objectives and finding opportunities for corrective/preventive action is a continual process through use of audit findings and conclusions, analysis of data, management reviews, or other means — generally leads to corrective action or preventive action.

Note 2: Annex SL common term. Original definition modified by adding Note 1.

3.3.3 management  （管理）

Definition: Coordinated activities to direct and control an organization.

Note 1: Management can include establishing policies, objectives, and processes to achieve these objectives.

Note 2: "Management" sometimes refers to people; avoid this ambiguity — prefer "top management" (3.1.1) for the person sense.

3.3.4 quality management  （品質管理）

Definition: Management with regard to quality.

Note: Quality management can include establishing quality policies and quality objectives, and processes to achieve these quality objectives through quality planning, quality assurance, quality control, and quality improvement.

3.3.5 quality planning  （品質策劃）

Definition: Part of quality management focused on setting quality objectives and specifying necessary operational processes and related resources to achieve the quality objectives.

Note: Establishing quality plans can be part of quality planning.

3.3.6 quality assurance (QA)  （品質保證）

Definition: Part of quality management focused on providing confidence that quality requirements will be fulfilled.

3.3.7 quality control (QC)  （品質管制）

Definition: Part of quality management focused on fulfilling quality requirements.

3.3.8 quality improvement  （品質改善）

Definition: Part of quality management focused on increasing the ability to fulfil quality requirements.

Note: Quality requirements can be related to effectiveness, efficiency, or traceability.

3.3.9 configuration management  （組態管理）

Definition: Coordinated activities to direct and control configuration (3.10.6).

Note: Configuration management concentrates on technical and organizational activities that establish and maintain control of a product or service and its product configuration information throughout the lifecycle. [SOURCE: ISO 10007]

3.3.10 change control  （變更管制）

Definition: (Configuration management context) Activities for control of the output after formal approval of its product configuration information. [SOURCE: ISO 10007]

3.3.11 activity  （活動）

Definition: (Project management context) Smallest identified object of work in a project. [SOURCE: ISO 10006]

3.3.12 project management  （專案管理）

Definition: Planning, organizing, monitoring, controlling and reporting of all aspects of a project, and the motivation of all those involved to achieve the project objectives. [SOURCE: ISO 10006]

Commentary & Pharma Application  |  解析與製藥應用

KEY: QA vs. QC — The Most Misunderstood Distinction in Pharma 製藥界最常混淆的概念

ISO 9000 provides precise, unambiguous definitions:

  • QA (3.3.6) = Part of quality management focused on providing confidence that requirements will be fulfilled — prospective, systemic, preventive.
  • QC (3.3.7) = Part of quality management focused on fulfilling quality requirements — operational, testing, verification of actual results.

Pharma example:

  • QA writes the SOP for sterility testing, validates the test method, and audits that the lab follows it → providing confidence the system works.
  • QC runs the sterility test on each batch, reads results, and reports pass/fail → fulfilling the test requirement on each unit.

A clean room that is built and validated correctly (QA activity) is different from the environmental monitoring swab taken today (QC activity). Both are needed. Neither replaces the other.

Quality Management as the Umbrella 品質管理是大傘

Think of quality management (3.3.4) as an umbrella with four spokes:

  • Planning (3.3.5) — deciding where to go / 策劃目標
  • Assurance (3.3.6) — checking you have the right map / 提供信心
  • Control (3.3.7) — navigating correctly at each step / 執行確認
  • Improvement (3.3.8) — making the next trip better / 持續改善

All four are parts of quality management — not alternatives to each other. In pharma QMS organization charts, QA and QC departments handle different spokes of the same umbrella.

Continual vs. Continuous — ISO Uses "Continual" Deliberately 持續 vs. 連續：ISO的有意選擇

"Continual improvement" (3.3.2) means recurring cycles — improvement activities happen repeatedly over time but may have gaps between cycles. The PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) cycle is the classic model.

"Continuous" would imply uninterrupted, non-stop activity — like a continuous manufacturing process running 24/7 without pause. ISO 9001 deliberately uses "continual" because:

  • Improvement projects have start and end points (CAPA closure, change control completion)
  • Between improvement cycles, the system may be stable (not actively improving every second)
  • "Continuous" is reserved for process descriptions in chemistry/engineering contexts

GMP auditors sometimes confuse these terms. The correct ISO 9001 language is always "continual improvement."

Change Control in Pharma — 3.3.10 Mapped to GMP 變更管制對照

ISO 9000's "change control" (3.3.10) aligns directly with GMP change control systems. In both frameworks:

  • Formal approval precedes any change to a controlled output
  • Product configuration information (specifications, methods, processes) requires documented control
  • Configuration management provides the systematic framework

For pharma: change control applies to master batch records, validated processes, analytical methods, equipment qualifications, and facility configurations — all elements of "product configuration information" in ISO 9000 terms.

Terms Related to Process  |  過程相關術語

ISO 9000:2015 Clause 3.4 — Defines processes, projects, procedures, and outsourcing

Original Text — Source Definitions  |  原文定義

3.4.1 process  （過程）

Definition: Set of interrelated or interacting activities that use inputs to deliver an intended result (output, product, or service).

Note 1: Whether the intended result is called output, product, or service depends on context.

Note 2: Inputs to a process are generally outputs of other processes; outputs are generally inputs to other processes.

Note 4: Processes are generally planned and carried out under controlled conditions to add value.

Note 5: A process where conformity of the resulting output cannot be readily or economically validated is a "special process."

3.4.2 project  （專案）

Definition: Unique process consisting of a set of coordinated and controlled activities with start and finish dates, undertaken to achieve an objective conforming to specific requirements, including the constraints of time, cost, and resources. [SOURCE: ISO 10006]

Note 2: In some projects, objectives and scope are updated and the product/service characteristics defined progressively.

3.4.3 quality management system realization  （品質管理系統實現）

Definition: Process of establishing, documenting, implementing, maintaining and continually improving a quality management system (3.5.4). [SOURCE: ISO 10019]

3.4.4 competence acquisition  （能力獲取）

Definition: Process of attaining competence (3.10.4). [SOURCE: ISO 10018]

3.4.5 procedure  （程序）

Definition: Specified way to carry out an activity or a process.

Note: Procedures can be documented or not.

3.4.6 outsource (verb)  （外包）

Definition: Make an arrangement where an external organization performs part of an organization's function or process.

Note 1: An external organization is outside the scope of the management system, although the outsourced function or process is within the scope.

Note 2: Annex SL common term.

3.4.7 contract  （合約）

Definition: Binding agreement.

3.4.8 design and development  （設計與開發）

Definition: Set of processes that transform requirements for an object into more detailed requirements for that object.

Note 1: Requirements forming input to design and development are often the result of research and can be expressed in more general terms than the output requirements. Generally defined in terms of characteristics.

Note 3: Qualifier can be applied to indicate nature (e.g. product design and development, service design and development, or process design and development).

Commentary & Pharma Application  |  解析與製藥應用

Process Model: Input → Transformation → Output 過程模型

ISO 9000's process definition establishes the fundamental model used throughout ISO 9001 and all ISO management system standards:

  • Inputs — typically outputs of preceding processes (materials, information, energy)
  • Activities — the interrelated, value-adding work that transforms inputs
  • Outputs — intended result: product, service, or other output
  • Controls — procedures, specifications, regulations that govern the transformation
  • Resources — people, equipment, infrastructure, environment

In aseptic fill-finish, a single manufacturing process (e.g., vial filling) has inputs (sterile bulk drug product, stopper, vial), activities (filling, stoppering, capping), controls (SOP, process parameters, environmental conditions), resources (isolator, filling machine, operators), and outputs (filled vials ready for inspection).

Special Process — 無法完全驗證輸出的過程

Note 5 to process (3.4.1) introduces the concept of a "special process": one where conformity of the resulting output cannot be readily or economically validated.

In GMP, this concept maps directly to aseptic processing: you cannot 100% test sterility of every vial without destroying it. Process validation (APS, media fills) provides the evidence that the process reliably produces sterile product — even though the output cannot be fully verified after-the-fact.

Special processes require heightened controls: qualified operators, validated processes, controlled environments, and continuous monitoring — all elements of PDA TR22 and Annex 1.

Outsource (3.4.6) — Scope Boundary 外包的範圍邊界

ISO 9000 clarifies a critical boundary: the external organization performing outsourced work is outside the QMS scope, but the function or process itself remains inside the scope.

Real-world pharma translation: if a CDMO outsources analytical testing to a CRO, the CRO is not part of the CDMO's QMS. But the testing activity — its accuracy, turnaround, OOS handling — is very much within the CDMO's QMS responsibility. ISO 9001:2015 Clause 8.4 and GMP Annex 7 both require the organization to control outsourced activities as if they were internal.

Procedure (3.4.5) — Documented or Not? 程序：可以不書面化？

ISO 9000 explicitly notes that procedures can be documented or undocumented. ISO 9001:2015 no longer mandates the "six mandatory procedures" required by the 2008 version. Instead, it requires "documented information" where needed to support operations.

In contrast, GMP regulations (EU Annex 1, FDA Aseptic Guidance) still require extensive written SOPs. The practical takeaway: ISO 9001 sets a minimum floor; pharma GMP requirements set a higher bar. A pharma company's QMS must satisfy both — and GMP typically wins on procedural documentation requirements.

Commentary & Pharma Application  |  解析與製藥應用

QMS — Not Just the Manual 品質管理系統不等於品質手冊

A common misconception: the QMS is the quality manual, or the set of SOPs. ISO 9000 defines QMS (3.5.4) as "part of a management system with regard to quality" — i.e., all the interrelated elements (structure, roles, processes, documentation, resources) that work together to achieve quality.

The quality manual is one documented information artifact within the QMS. The QMS itself encompasses:

  • Organizational structure and responsibilities
  • All processes from design to delivery
  • Infrastructure, work environment, resources
  • Documented information (policies, procedures, records)
  • Monitoring, measurement, audit, and improvement activities

When an FDA inspector asks "show me your QMS," they want to see the whole system in operation — not just a folder of SOPs.

Work Environment (3.5.5) in Pharma — Beyond Temperature 工作環境不只是溫度

ISO 9000 explicitly includes psychological and social factors in work environment: recognition schemes, occupational stress, ergonomics. This is more holistic than GMP's typical focus on physical environment (temperature, humidity, particle count, lighting).

Why does this matter? Operator error is a leading cause of GMP deviations. An overworked, stressed, or unrecognized operator in an aseptic area is a contamination risk factor. ISO 9001:2015 Clause 7.1.4 explicitly requires managing the environment to support conforming products — and that includes the human environment.

Metrological Confirmation (3.5.6) — Calibration Lifecycle 計量確認：校準的全過程

Metrological confirmation is more than a calibration sticker. ISO 9000 defines it as a set of operations including:

  • Calibration or verification of the instrument
  • Adjustment or repair if out of tolerance
  • Subsequent recalibration after adjustment
  • Comparison with metrological requirements for the intended use
  • Required sealing and labelling

Key distinction: metrological requirements (the instrument's accuracy specification) are distinct from product requirements (the spec on the product being measured). A pH meter calibrated to ±0.05 pH may or may not be fit for a process requiring pH ±0.1 — the metrological confirmation process determines fitness for intended use.

Policy → Vision → Mission → Strategy 方針、願景、使命、策略的層次

ISO 9000 provides definitions for organizational direction concepts that are often loosely used:

  • Mission (3.5.11) — Why we exist / 我們存在的理由
  • Vision (3.5.10) — Where we aspire to be / 我們想成為什麼
  • Strategy (3.5.12) — The plan to get there / 實現願景的計畫
  • Policy (3.5.8) — Top management's stated intentions within the QMS / 高層對品質的承諾聲明

In pharma quality culture, quality policy must be aligned with the mission and vision — not treated as a standalone compliance document. A CDMO whose mission is "delivering safe medicines reliably" should have a quality policy that explicitly supports that mission.

Terms Related to Requirement  |  要求相關術語

ISO 9000:2015 Clause 3.6 — Defines requirements, quality, conformity, nonconformity, and related concepts

Original Text — Source Definitions  |  原文定義

3.6.1 object / entity / item  （客體 / 實體 / 項目）

Definition: Anything perceivable or conceivable.

Examples: Product, service, process, person, organization, system, resource.

Note: Objects can be material, non-material, or imagined (e.g. future state of the organization). [SOURCE: ISO 1087-1]

3.6.2 quality  （品質）

Definition: Degree to which a set of inherent characteristics of an object fulfils requirements.

Note 1: The term "quality" can be used with adjectives such as poor, good, or excellent.

Note 2: "Inherent" — as opposed to "assigned" — means existing in the object.

3.6.3 grade  （等級）

Definition: Category or rank given to different requirements for an object having the same functional use.

Example: Class of airline ticket and category of hotel in a brochure. Note: When establishing a quality requirement, the grade is generally specified.

3.6.4 requirement  （要求）

Definition: Need or expectation that is stated, generally implied or obligatory.

Note 1: "Generally implied" means it is custom or common practice for the organization and interested parties that the need or expectation is implied.

Note 3: A qualifier can denote a specific type: product requirement, quality management requirement, customer requirement, quality requirement.

Note 5: It can be necessary for achieving high customer satisfaction to fulfil an expectation even if it is neither stated nor generally implied nor obligatory. Annex SL common term.

3.6.5 quality requirement  （品質要求）

Definition: Requirement related to quality.

3.6.6 statutory requirement  （法定要求）

Commentary & Pharma Application  |  解析與製藥應用

Nonconformity vs. Defect — Legal Distinction Matters 不符合 vs. 缺陷：法律意義的差異

ISO 9000 draws a sharp distinction:

  • Nonconformity (3.6.9) — Non-fulfilment of any requirement. A broad term: a missing SOP signature, a temperature excursion, a failed sterility test — all are nonconformities.
  • Defect (3.6.10) — Nonconformity related to an intended or specified use. Narrower, and carries legal liability implications: product/service liability, recall, patient harm.

In pharma: a visible particle in an injectable is both a nonconformity (fails specification) and a defect (fails intended use — safe injection). A process that runs 2°C above the validated range may be a nonconformity without necessarily being a defect — depending on risk assessment of impact on product quality.

Why it matters: product liability claims hinge on whether a product had a "defect." ISO 9000 aligns the vocabulary with legal frameworks.

Statutory vs. Regulatory Requirement — Two Layers 法定要求 vs. 法規要求

ISO 9000 distinguishes these carefully:

  • Statutory requirement (3.6.6) — Set by a legislative body (parliament, congress). Example: the US Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act; EU Directive 2001/83/EC on medicinal products.
  • Regulatory requirement (3.6.7) — Set by an authority mandated by that legislative body. Example: FDA's 21 CFR Parts 210/211 (Current GMP regulations); EMA guidance; WHO GMP guidelines.

The law creates the framework; the regulatory authority writes the technical rules. Both are obligatory. ISO 9001 Clause 8.2.2 requires capturing both types of applicable requirements when determining product and service requirements.

Quality Definition — "Degree to Which" 品質的程度化定義

ISO 9000's definition of quality (3.6.2) is deliberately graduated: "degree to which a set of inherent characteristics fulfils requirements."

This means quality is not binary (good/bad) but a continuum. A product can have high quality (fully meets all requirements), adequate quality (meets critical requirements but has minor deviations), or poor quality (fails key requirements).

In pharma: a drug product's quality encompasses safety (no harmful impurities), efficacy (correct potency), and purity (correct identity, absence of contamination). Each characteristic contributes to the overall "degree" of quality fulfillment.

Traceability in Pharma — GMP Alignment 可追溯性的GMP應用

ISO 9000's definition of traceability (3.6.13) covers three dimensions for products:

  • Origin of materials and parts → raw material certificates, supplier lot numbers, chain of custody
  • Processing history → batch records, equipment logs, environmental monitoring records, operator IDs
  • Distribution and location after delivery → distribution records, serialization, cold chain documentation

EU Falsified Medicines Directive and US Drug Supply Chain Security Act (DSCSA) requirements align directly with this three-dimensional traceability model — extending it to the serialization and patient-level tracking of finished medicines.

Requirement (3.6.4) — Stated, Implied, or Obligatory 要求的三種形式

ISO 9000 identifies three types of requirements that together define what the QMS must satisfy:

  • Stated — Explicitly documented in contracts, specifications, regulatory filings
  • Generally implied — Common practice or custom that both parties take for granted (e.g. a drug product must not contain gross particulate contamination even if not specified in every contract)
  • Obligatory — Legal / regulatory requirements that apply regardless of what the contract says

Note 5's addition is particularly significant: to achieve high customer satisfaction, you may need to fulfil expectations that are neither stated, implied, nor obligatory. This is the basis for "customer delight" programs and proactive quality improvement beyond minimum compliance.

ISO 9000:2015 Clause 3.7 — Defines objectives, output, product, service, performance, risk, and effectiveness

Original Text — Source Definitions  |  原文定義

3.7.1 objective  （目標）

Definition: Result to be achieved.

Note 1: An objective can be strategic, tactical, or operational.

Note 2: Objectives can relate to different disciplines (financial, health & safety, environmental) and apply at different levels (strategic, organization-wide, project, product, and process).

Note 3: Can be expressed as an intended outcome, a purpose, an operational criterion, a quality objective, or by words such as aim, goal, or target.

Note 4: In the context of QMS, quality objectives are set by the organization, consistent with the quality policy, to achieve specific results. Annex SL common term.

3.7.2 quality objective  （品質目標）

Definition: Objective related to quality.

Note 1: Quality objectives are generally based on the organization's quality policy.

Note 2: Quality objectives are generally specified for relevant functions, levels and processes in the organization.

3.7.3 success  （成功）

Definition: (Organization context) Achievement of an organization objective.

Note: Success emphasizes the need for balance between economic/financial interests and the needs of interested parties such as customers, users, investors, people in the organization, providers, partners, interest groups, and communities.

3.7.4 sustained success  （持續成功）

Definition: Success over a period of time.

Note 1: Sustained success emphasizes the need for balance between economic-financial interests and the social and ecological environment.

Note 2: Relates to the interests of all relevant interested parties.

3.7.5 output  （輸出）

Definition: Result of a process.

Note 1: Whether an output of the organization is a product or a service depends on the preponderance of the characteristics involved. Example: a painting for sale in a gallery = product; a commissioned painting = service. A hamburger in a retail store = product; ordering in a restaurant = service.

3.7.6 product  （產品）

Definition: Output of an organization that can be produced without any transaction taking place between the organization and the customer.

Note 2: The dominant element of a product is that it is generally tangible.

Note 3: Hardware is tangible and its amount is a countable characteristic (e.g. tyres). Processed materials are tangible and their amount is a continuous characteristic (e.g. fuel, soft drinks). Software consists of information regardless of delivery medium.

3.7.7 service  （服務）

Definition: Output of an organization with at least one activity necessarily performed between the organization and the customer.

Note 1: The dominant elements of a service are generally intangible.

Note 3: Service can involve activities on a customer-supplied tangible product (car repair), intangible product (tax return), delivery of an intangible product (knowledge), or creation of ambience (hotels, restaurants).

3.7.8 performance  （績效）

Definition: Measurable result.

Note 1: Performance can relate to either quantitative or qualitative findings.

Note 2: Can relate to the management of activities, processes, products, services, systems, or organizations. Annex SL common term.

3.7.9 risk  （風險）

Definition: Effect of uncertainty.

Note 1: An effect is a deviation from the expected — positive or negative.

Note 2: Uncertainty is the state, even partial, of deficiency of information related to, understanding or knowledge of, an event, its consequence, or likelihood.

Note 3: Risk is often characterized by reference to potential events and consequences, or a combination of these.

Note 4: Risk is often expressed as a combination of the consequences of an event and the associated likelihood of occurrence.

Note 5: The word "risk" is sometimes used when there is the possibility of only negative consequences. Annex SL common term.

3.7.10 efficiency  （效率）

Definition: Relationship between the result achieved and the resources used.

3.7.11 effectiveness  （有效性）

Definition: Extent to which planned activities are realized and planned results are achieved.

Note: Annex SL common term.

Commentary & Pharma Application  |  解析與製藥應用

Risk = Effect of Uncertainty — Including Positive Risk 風險包含正面偏差（機會）

ISO 9000's definition of risk (3.7.9) is strikingly different from everyday usage. Key points:

  • Risk = "effect of uncertainty" — it is an effect, not the source of uncertainty itself
  • Effects can be positive or negative. A positive deviation from expected outcome is an opportunity
  • ISO 9001:2015 explicitly requires organizations to address both risks and opportunities (Clause 6.1)

Contrast with ICH Q9: ICH Q9 (pharmaceutical risk management) focuses primarily on threats to patient safety — harm, hazard, probability of harm, severity of harm. ICH Q9 risk = potential for negative outcomes.

ISO 9000's broader definition captures both sides: a new analytical technology might present uncertainty (risk), but if outcomes exceed expectations, it's an opportunity. Risk-based thinking in ISO 9001:2015 is explicitly bidirectional.

Product vs. Service vs. Output — Pharma Is Both 產品 vs. 服務 vs. 輸出：藥廠兩者兼具

ISO 9000 distinguishes:

  • Output (3.7.5) — Generic result of any process
  • Product (3.7.6) — Output that can be produced without direct customer transaction; tangible dominant
  • Service (3.7.7) — Output requiring at least one activity performed with the customer; intangible dominant

For a CDMO: manufactured drug product = product (tangible, produced without patient interaction). Contract manufacturing services = service (requires interaction with the MAH customer throughout — tech transfer, batch review, release discussion). The same CDMO provides both products and services simultaneously.

This distinction matters for ISO 9001:2015 Clause 8.2 (Requirements for products and services) — different controls apply to each.

Effectiveness vs. Efficiency — 有效性 vs. 效率

A critical distinction often collapsed in practice:

  • Effectiveness (3.7.11) = Did we achieve the planned results? (Doing the right things) / 計畫的事做到了嗎？
  • Efficiency (3.7.10) = What was the resource cost of achieving those results? (Doing things right) / 用了多少資源去做？

Example: A CAPA that successfully prevents recurrence of a GMP deviation is effective. If it took 200 person-hours to close, it may not be efficient. ISO 9001:2015 Clause 9.1 requires monitoring both: KPIs for quality system performance should capture both dimensions.

For pharma quality culture: you can have high effectiveness with low efficiency (gold-plating CAPAs) or high efficiency with low effectiveness (rushing to close CAPAs without fixing root causes). The ideal is both.

Quality Objectives — SMART Requirements in ISO 9001 品質目標的設定

ISO 9000 defines quality objectives (3.7.2) as objectives related to quality, consistent with the quality policy. ISO 9001:2015 Clause 6.2 adds specific requirements for quality objectives — they must be:

  • Consistent with the quality policy
  • Measurable (where practicable)
  • Taking into account applicable requirements
  • Relevant to conformity of products/services and enhancement of customer satisfaction
  • Monitored, communicated, and updated as appropriate

For pharma sites: typical quality objectives include batch right-first-time rates, OOS rates, deviation closure timeliness, CAPA effectiveness, audit findings per cycle. These must cascade from quality policy to process-level targets.

Performance (3.7.8) — Measurable Results Across All Dimensions 績效：可量測的結果

ISO 9000's definition of performance as "measurable result" is deliberately broad — it applies to management activities, processes, products, services, systems, or organizations. This underpins ISO 9001:2015's performance evaluation requirements (Clause 9):

  • Monitoring and measurement of processes (process performance)
  • Customer satisfaction measurement (customer-facing performance)
  • Internal audit findings (system performance)
  • Management review inputs (organizational performance)

For pharma quality dashboards: process capability (Cpk), yield, OOS rate, deviation rate, and complaint rate are all "performance" data in ISO 9000 terms — measurable results that reflect the quality management system's effectiveness.

QA/QC Distinction — Use ISO Language in Audits

When regulators or ISO auditors ask "what is your quality assurance?" they expect you to describe system-level activities that provide confidence in quality — not just describe the QA department. Use ISO 9000's precise language: QA = providing confidence; QC = fulfilling requirements.

Risk Language — Context Matters

In ICH Q9 discussions (pharmaceutical risk management, FMEA, hazard analysis), "risk" means threat to patient safety — probability × severity. In ISO 9001:2015 discussions (QMS risk-based thinking, Clause 6.1), "risk" includes opportunities. Clarify which framework applies before discussing "risk management."

The CDMO Dual Identity

A CDMO simultaneously operates as:

  • Organization (3.2.1) with its own QMS, policies, objectives
  • External provider (3.2.6) to the MAH customer
  • Customer (3.2.4) to its raw material suppliers
  • Provider of both products (3.7.6) and services (3.7.7)

ISO 9000's organizational framework captures all these roles simultaneously — which is why ISO 9001 certification is relevant for CDMOs beyond GMP compliance alone.

Effectiveness vs. Efficiency in CAPA

Apply both metrics to CAPA management:

  • Effectiveness: Did the CAPA prevent recurrence? (measure: repeat deviation rate)
  • Efficiency: How many person-hours / days to close? (measure: CAPA cycle time)

A quality system that is only effective but not efficient is costly. One that is only efficient but not effective creates repeat deviations. Both metrics should appear in management review.
