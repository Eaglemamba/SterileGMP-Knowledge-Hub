INTRODUCTION
Gases, such as compressed air, nitrogen, and atmospheric gases that contact sterile components, containers and closures, and product
contact surfaces of processing equipment, such as tanks and piping, must be sterile to prevent contamination of pharmaceuticals and
biopharmaceuticals. The sterilization of gases is typically accomplished by passing the gas through one or more sterilizing-grade membrane
«lters. The «ltration process must be designed to ensure that the «ltered gas has been sterilized.
GAS VERSUS LIQUID STERILIZING FIL
TRATION
Sterilizing Filtration of Liquids 〈1229.4〉 contains information that is applicable to this chapter, including but not limited to sterilizing-grade
«lters, retention mechanisms and factors affecting retention, validation, integrity-testing principles and methods, and pre«ltration bioburden
control. The signi«cant differences between liquid and gas «ltration will be discussed in this chapter.
MEMBRANES FOR GAS STERILIZATION
Filters used for sterilization of gases are commonly hydrophobic membranes, although hydrophilic membranes can be employed where
membrane wetting during use is not an issue. Wetting may be a factor with «lters used for autoclave venting and where vent «lters are
required to maintain the sterility of tank headspace. Microporous «lter membranes that have a pore-size rating of NMT 0.2 µm, which meets
the ASTM International standard (see F838-15a Standard Test Method for Determining Bacterial Retention of Membrane Filters Utilized for
Liquid Filtration), are suitable for the sterilization of gases; in fact, these «lters are considerably more retentive for gases than for liquids (1).
Additionally, the «lters must be chemically compatible with the gas to be «ltered and physically compatible with the «ltration conditions (e.g.,
pressure differentials and ¬ow rates).
RETENTION MECHANISMS
The primary retention mechanisms of membrane «lters used in gas sterilization are size exclusion, impaction, diffusional interception, and
electrostatic attraction. Size exclusion is effective for par ticles (microorganisms) that are too large to pass through the pore structure of the
«lter membrane and is essentially independent of the velocity of gas ¬owing through the «lter. Retention mechanisms other than size
exclusion can be effective for par ticles smaller than the pores they encounter. Retention due to impaction, diffusional interception, and
electrostatic attraction is dependent on par ticle size, ¬ow rate, and relative humidity. For example, par ticle retention e­ciency due to
impaction is propor tional to the mass of the par ticle; heavier par ticles have a greater momentum than lighter ones and are therefore more
likely to contact pore walls and subsequently be retained by the «lter during tor tuous ¬ow. In contrast, the capture e­ciency of diffusional
interception is inversely propor tional to par ticle size and velocity. Smaller par ticles in¬uenced by Brownian motion can exit the streamlines,
contact the «lter matrix, and be retained, and lower velocity increases potential contact time and improves capture e­ciency. Electrostatic
attraction of par ticles is reduced by a high relative humidity in the «ltered gas. Adsorption, which can be impor tant in liquid «ltration, is not a
retention mechanism for gases.
VALIDATION
Validation of the processes used in sterilizing «ltration for gases, in general, may be divided into two par ts: sterilization of the «lter and its
housing, and the ability of the «lter to remove microorganisms from the «ltered gas.
Sterilization of the «lter and its housing has been addressed speci«cally in 〈1229.4〉 and Sterilization-in-Place 〈1229.13〉, and generally in
Sterilization of Compendial Ar ticles 〈1229〉.
Membrane «lters meeting the requirements of ASTM F838-15a are suitable for sterilizing «ltration of gases in terms of microbial retention.
Liquid bacterial challenge testing represents a worst-case condition for sterilizing gas «lters because the retention e­ciency in liquids is
lower than in gases (2).
INTEGRITY TESTING
Integrity testing requirements and techniques are discussed in 〈1229.4〉. The hydrophobic «lters typically used for gas «ltration can be
integrity tested using bubble-point, diffusive-¬ow, and pressure-hold tests that use a liquid of low surface tension to completely wet the pore
structure of the «lter membrane. Additionally, integrity testing can be performed by measuring the membrane’s resistance to wetting with
water as a function of pressure. Water intrusion testing is an example of this type of integrity testing.
