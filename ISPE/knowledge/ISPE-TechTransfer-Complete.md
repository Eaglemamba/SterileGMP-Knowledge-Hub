# ISPE Good Practice Guide: Technology Transfer (3rd Edition): Planning, Execution, and Quality Aspects of Pharmaceutical Technology Transfer

## Chapter 0: Introduction & Key Terms (Ch 1) (p1-p20)

# Chapter 1: Introduction, Background & Key Terms

    

    

ISPE Good Practice Guide: Technology Transfer (3rd Edition) | p1–p20

    

        ISPE Tech Transfer 3rd Ed. | p7

        

Transfer of manufacturing processes and analytical procedures between facilities or laboratories is a necessary part of pharmaceutical development and commercialization. Technology transfers may utilize the outputs of process and method development activities and/or documentation of established processes and methods. Knowledge of the product and the manufacturing process is the basis for transfer to a different location (which could be a different site, country, or facility on the same site) where a process or analytical procedure will be operated.

        

This Guide focuses on how technology transfer can be achieved.

        

            Technology transfer is defined in ICH Q10 paragraph 3.1.2 as follows:  
  

            "The goal of technology transfer activities is to transfer **product and process knowledge** between development and manufacturing, and within or between manufacturing sites to achieve **product realisation**. This knowledge forms the basis for the manufacturing process, control strategy, process validation approach and ongoing continual improvement."
        

        

Technology transfer projects may take place at various points during the product lifecycle. Successful transfers depend on robust project management processes combined with appropriate product and process understanding. They require **partnership, cooperation, and coordination** between the sending and receiving units to ensure successful and efficient completion, such that the receiving unit can manufacture, test, and release a safe, efficacious, and quality product comparable to that of the sending unit.

        

Technology transfer projects are dependent on the development and characterization of robust processes that allow consistent and predictable operation of these processes. Similarly, development of robust analytical methods enables timely transfer of methods. These are particularly important aspects; if processes are not well developed and sources of variation are not well known, e.g., due to incomplete or inadequate process/product knowledge, then technology transfer will not be robust.

        

This Guide has been designed to present industry good practices for successful and efficient execution of technology transfer projects and to achieve a balance between risk management and cost effectiveness while aligning with applicable regulatory expectations, as described in ICH Q7, Q8(R2), Q9, Q10, and Q11 and other regulatory documents.

        

The impetus for technology transfer varies and may be based on several factors:

        

            
- Progression in a product development lifecycle, from development through scale-up to commercialization

            
- The need for additional manufacturing capacity driven by increased demand or risk mitigation

            
- The need to streamline capacity when volumes fall, e.g., at patent expiry, when products change commercial status from novel to generic, etc.

            
- Strategic requirements to relocate business units, e.g., regional economic advantages, regional requirements for local manufacture, global expansion in different regions of the world, etc.

            
- Identification and qualification of local analytical facilities for testing of intermediates and finished products to meet regional regulatory requirements

            
- Dual sourcing strategies to mitigate demand variability or to reduce sourcing risks from manufacturing sites

            
- Ensuring adequate turnaround times for analytical laboratories

            
- Utilization of virtual organizational structures and increasing use of third parties

## Chapter 1: Planning, Success Criteria & TT Phases 1–3 (Ch 2–3a) (p21-p40)

# Chapters 2–3a: Planning, Success Criteria & Technology Transfer Phases 1–3

    

    

ISPE Good Practice Guide: Technology Transfer (3rd Edition) | p21–p40 ISPE

    

        

This chapter provides guidance on what to consider for a transfer, including establishing success criteria, prior to the formal formation of the full technology transfer team. Guidance provided is applicable to drug substance, drug product, and analytical procedure technology transfers.

        

A technology transfer typically starts with an agreement (referred to as a charter in Chapter 3) normally formalized or on a contractual basis between a sending unit and a receiving unit for the transfer of, e.g., a developed process or a commercial process or an analytical method.

        

The success of a technology transfer project will be largely dependent upon the skill and performance of individuals assigned to the project from both the sending unit and the receiving unit. It is, therefore, critical that a clear objective for any technology transfer project be developed. It is also critical that a project team comprised of individuals from both the sending unit and the receiving unit is established and that there is a precise understanding of each team member's role and responsibility prior to initiation of the technology transfer project.

        

            "Skill alone will not ensure a successful technology transfer project. Once a project has been identified and a team chosen, a clear and realistic project implementation plan is required to guide the project, manage expectations, and handle the inevitable deviations and changes that may present themselves during implementation."
        

        

Along with that plan comes the need to consider the relationship between the various tasks associated with a successful technology transfer. It should be clearly understood that each technology transfer assignment is unique; therefore, it is impossible to provide a generic technology transfer plan.

**3.4** Operational Readiness

        

The goal of the operational readiness phase is to achieve a state of readiness required to move into the qualification phase, as described in Section 3.5. Both the process and methods are required to be in a state of control prior to entering qualification phase. A critical review of the operational readiness should be performed and formally documented.

        

Analytical methods are required to be qualified prior to full scale process qualification in order to be able to measure process performance. In some cases, method qualification may be concurrent with process qualification; in this case, the risk to the overall qualification risk must be documented and accepted by management.

        

Closure of actions defined by gap analysis and corresponding risk assessments drive the requirements to complete this phase.

        

Operational readiness activities required may include:

        

            
- Additional process or method development

            
- Equipment installation, modification, or re-validation

            
- General commissioning, qualification and validations as needed (refer to ISPE Baseline® Guide: Commissioning and Qualification)

            
- Engineering runs

            
- Procurement

            
- Training runs or training of staff

        

        

Having identified gaps and risks, then subsequently prioritizing activities, technology transfer operational readiness may include the following:

        

            
- Facility modifications identified in risk identification/gap analysis, including appropriate equipment validations

            
- Testing of full scale manufacturing equipment to ensure proper function, e.g., mixing studies, buffer or water runs, cleaning studies, etc.

        

        

In the case of cleaning, it is recommended that a comprehensive review of the requirements (e.g., soil types, required cleanability limits, and methods of detection) are compiled into a planning document. It is highly likely that changes in equipment type, scale-up, and process optimization implemented during the transfer will require new cleaning and monitoring methods, sample types, and corresponding analytical method qualifications.

        

As noted in Sections 2.2 and 3.3.1, effective knowledge management is crucial throughout all the previous phases. Operation readiness requires generation of documentation at the receiving unit required to implement and control changes necessary for implementation of the transferred process and/or methods. Diligence in document review and reconciliation in this phase is of paramount importance prior to proceeding to the qualification phase.

        

Examples of required documentation are as follows:

        

            
- Flow chart of process steps
                

                    
    - Quantities of all materials and stoichiometry of the starting materials, intermediates, and reagents

                    
    - Specific processing conditions (e.g., times, temperatures, and pressures)

                    
    - Sequence of activities (e.g., order of addition of reagents)

                    
    - Expected theoretical yield and output weight/volume of product

                    
    - CPPs, i.e., factors impacting product quality

                    
    - Permitted tolerance ranges for key parameters (e.g., yield impacting parameters)

                    
    - Detail of other materials and by-products generated and their fate

                    
    - Mass balance information

                

            

            
- Development and approval of receiving unit manufacturing instruction documents (batch records, SOPs, automation recipes, etc.)
                

                    
    - A clearly defined structure for review and approval of the manufacturing documents should be agreed upon by the sending unit and receiving unit, including expectations for duration and number of reviews. A well-organized system for tracking and managing edits throughout the process should be used by the teams.

                

            

            
- Implementation of small scale laboratory models at receiving unit
                

                    
    - As part of the transfer, small scale models of some or all the unit operations may be transferred to the receiving unit. These can be used for process training before large scale runs begin, for additional process development work identified in the risk identification/gap analysis, and can also be used for efficient troubleshooting during the initial large scale runs.

                    
    - A suggested practice is to use the small scale model in a "satellite" mode alongside the initial large scale runs with the same buffers or media. This can help to troubleshoot problems that may be observed at scale by providing a control for some of the parameters.

                    
    - An overall assessment comparing the small scale runs to the production runs should be documented or referenced in a campaign summary report.

                

            

            
- Process training
                

                    
    - Training should be conducted by SMEs prior to the start of the GMP campaign. It may be helpful to have some elements of this training led by personnel from the sending unit where they may have more extensive process experience. It also may be useful to have personnel from the sending unit as support staff during operation of first full scale batch.

                

            

            
- Models
                

                    
    - Various models can be used by companies to develop pharmaceutical products. The rigor and documentation associated with the technical transfer of such models should be commensurate with the complexity and risk associated with their implementation and intended use.

                    
    - Where models are established only for the purpose of understanding a process and its unit operations, it is usually sufficient to transfer the model as part of the knowledge base for the product, as these models are not part of ongoing control decisions during production. Where models are established as elements of a control strategy for ongoing production, consideration should be given to scale, sampling, and instrument dependencies as applicable.

                    
    - In addition, procedures to calibrate, verify, maintain, if applicable validate, and update models should be established, as appropriate to the nature of the model being used.

                

            

            
- Creation of real time processing models and data trending template

            
- Identification of in-process controls for monitoring

            
- Cleaning requirements per process step, including required analysis and forward processing criteria

            
- Generation of sample plan

        

        

In a GMP environment, some of these activities may require additional risk assessments and change controls as a part of the activity.

        

Additionally, the operational readiness phase of the technology transfer project will include the generation of the documentation and/or electronic systems that will be used to execute the technology at the receiving unit. This activity should be supported through the information and deliverables that were included as a part of the technology transfer package generated in the pre-transfer planning phase.

        

The operational readiness phase of the technology transfer is complete when the process and analytical procedures are qualified. The sending and receiving units should be confident in the receiving unit's ability to successfully execute analytical procedures or manufacturing processes.

        

Following successful completion of the technology transfer operational readiness phase, the project will move into the process/procedure qualification phase of the project.

## Chapter 3: Analytical Method Transfer (Ch 4) (p53-p66)

# Chapter 4: Technology Transfer of Analytical Methods

    

    

ISPE Good Practice Guide: Technology Transfer (3rd Edition) | p53–p66

    

        

ISPE TT Ch.4

        

This chapter highlights those aspects of a technology transfer process specific to analytical methods.

        

The goal of an analytical method technology transfer is to ensure that knowledge from the sending unit is translated into an effective analytical method control strategy (that supports the overall product control strategy) in the receiving unit. When a receiving unit is required to perform an analytical method transfer, the company should execute an SOP to ensure:

        

            
- Consistent practices are followed

            
- Exchange of knowledge between the sending unit and the receiving unit is clear and robust

            
- Data produced by the receiving unit is fit for purpose, equivalent to data from the sending unit, and defensible from both quality and regulatory standpoints

        

        

Method transfer may be required for several reasons, including:

        

            
- Initial introduction of an analytical method into a unit that has not previously executed an equivalent method

            
- Introduction into a manufacturing facility by a development group

            
- Transfer from a manufacturing facility or development facility to another

            
- Transfer into a third party testing unit

        

        

The level of effort, formality and documentation of the method transfer, and any testing (performed to demonstrate the success of the transfer) should be commensurate with the criticality of the respective analytical data. For example, companies may not want to spend the same amount of time on transfer specific activities for products in the early versus late stage of its lifecycle. Late stage method transfers should be rigorously performed in order to ensure success of the method transfer, in support of product release and/or inclusion of the data into regulatory registration filings.

As a drug product transitions through each stage of the clinical program and a higher degree of confidence in the clinical profile of the product materializes, the strategy should shift to focus upon commercialization of the dosage form. This should include defining process scale-up and transfer plans to the intended commercial manufacturing site.

        

This activity traditionally occurs between the phase 2 and phase 3 clinical stages, in order to ensure phase 3 clinical and pivotal stability supplies are produced within a commercial facility. This also can be advantageous in supporting lifecycle validation activities for the product and helping to build manufacturing experience in a commercial facility.

        

In addition, there may be a need to transfer a drug product to a new commercial manufacturing site post-registration and launch. Although more information may be available while transferring a commercial product (filed release specifications, CPPs, etc.), the general knowledge needed and steps to successfully transfer a product remains the same.

# Appendix 2: Biologics Case Studies (Part 2) & Conclusions

    

    

ISPE Good Practice Guide: Technology Transfer (3rd Edition) | p104–p112

    

        ISPE Tech Transfer | p104–106

        

The first two case studies are interrelated in that they involve two products, one CMO company, and three CMO sites. The first case study describes a product in early phase 2 development that held significant clinical potential to move to a pivotal study. With the advent of breakthrough therapy designation and accelerated product development, the timelines for process optimization and the timeframe for gaining manufacturing experience to achieve a robust and reproducible manufacturing process is significantly reduced. Therefore, the process was transferred from the client's internal early phase manufacturing site to a US-based commercial CMO (site A) fairly early in clinical development.

        

This CMO was selected for the following reasons:

        

            
- Commercial experience

            
- Appropriate scale – 2000 L single-use bioreactors (SUB)

            
- Available capacity – manufacturing slots available with ~12 months lead time

            
- Good compliance history – FDA inspected as well as multiple other regulatory agency inspections

            
- Technical skill/capability

            
- Cost competitive

        

        

The client's internal manufacturing train was 250 L and 1,000 L, so transfer and scale-up to a 2,000 L scale was deemed low risk. However, manufacturing at the client site was performed with a low productivity cell line. Development of a new higher productivity cell line and establishment of a new Master Cell Bank (MCB) was nearing completion at the start of the technology transfer. Limited development work was performed at the client site and technology transfer was initiated prior to having completed full process development. Utilization of a Research Cell Bank (RCB) for initiation of technology transfer was also required since the MCB was not available yet.

        

Changes were made to the downstream process to enable facility fit and to improve process efficiency. A focused development and optimization approach leveraging CMO expertise and experience was taken for the downstream process to enable timely transfer of the process to GMP manufacturing. Changes in the downstream process included addition of a pH treatment step, change in depth filters, change in sequence of operations (Sartoclear® P filtration step moved from post to pre-SP-Sepharose® column chromatography), change in viral filter, and removal of a dilution step prior to loading the SP-Sepharose® column. An engineering run was performed to shake down the process and ensure process changes were effective and successfully scaled up to the manufacturing scale (2,000 L).

## Chapter 8: Appendix 4: Engineering Considerations (p123-p134)

# Appendix 4: Engineering Considerations for Technology Transfer

    

    

ISPE Good Practice Guide: Technology Transfer (3rd Edition) | p123–p134

    

        ISPE TechTransfer 3rd Ed.

        

Transfer of engineering knowledge can be an important consideration for transfers, whether for single or multiple sites. Importantly, it should be an ongoing two-way exchange of information as the intricacies and experience gained may enhance process understanding and product quality.

        

In the context of modern biopharmaceutical manufacturing, engineering knowledge can cover a wide range of information, including activities and information related to facilities/equipment design and maintenance through to chemical and process engineering knowledge and decisions related to manufacturing activities.

        

This chapter focuses on those activities that do not fall within the scope of process development or product realization as such activities should be captured within the development activities and control strategy for the process.

        

Overall, it is the success of the transferred process and delivery of product quality (drug substance or drug product meeting the requisite CQAs) that determines whether a transfer is successful. Well developed products and processes will identify critical aspects of processes that support achievement of consistent and reliable manufacturing; where these are of significance, they will form part of the control strategy.

        

Engineering controls are critical to maintenance of CPPs and can be key to detection and/or management of potential process influence from material attributes. Additionally, engineering requirements and specifications are important risk mitigations in processes — for example, where materials compatibility and cleanability is required to be assured by control of materials of construction, welding, surface finishes etc; or where instrument suitability is managed by instrument selection and calibration.

        

It is important that technology transfer planning clearly differentiates the critical engineering aspects of processes and that these decisions are clear to engineering groups at the receiving unit as well as their suppliers. If engineering teams and suppliers are disconnected from this information, this situation can lead to lack of focus and inefficiency or, worse, may result in failure to achieve the required manufacturing outcomes or delays, which lead to potential shortages in critical products.

        

Engineering information may include:

        

            
- Details and information on the design, fabrication, or procurement of process equipment or supporting utilities:
                

                    
    - Equipment drawings and specifications, including materials of construction compatible with process solutions and geometry

                    
    - Details of critical measuring instruments, including range/capabilities

                    
    - Physical layout or arrangement and integration of equipment

                

            

            
- Details on the types of equipment to be used for running or supporting a process

            
- Arrangement for equipment transfer, such as equipment transfer plans and commissioning/qualification methodology

            
- Control programming/logic and configuration information

            
- Maintenance and setup instructions

            
- Equipment service history

            
- Cleaning process development information

            
- Details of engineering studies that are of importance to determining process benchmarks for equipment or processes

        

        

Proper technical assessment needs to be carried out across GxP and GEP (Good Engineering Practice) systems to ensure that key knowledge in these areas is captured to avoid delay or complications with technology transfers.

        

            "Engineering information may include tacit knowledge that exists within an organization in systems and locations outside of the mainstream production control systems — often assumed to exist within qualification and validation files; however, the outputs of the engineering knowledge are frequently the only portion that is visible, not necessarily the process to get to that point."
        

        

There are three possible broad scenarios in terms of knowledge transfer:

        

            
- **Scenario 1:** Sending unit transfers the process and like-for-like equipment as part of the project. Knowledge around use and behavior of the equipment related to the product and process under transfer is the focus.

            
- **Scenario 2:** Sending and receiving units use the same or similar equipment (either new or existing) but that exists separately on both sites. Experience of operation, recommended configuration, and details of setup are required.

            
- **Scenario 3:** Sending and receiving units use similar but not identical equipment, where the receiving unit may need to procure or operate equipment to achieve the process. The product and process outcomes are important; the means of getting there operationally may differ.

        

        

The scope of a technology transfer often includes the use of plant automation and/or some level of enterprise level information technology (such as manufacturing execution systems). Specialist engineering resources should be identified for replication or implementation of suitable automation and/or network infrastructure; particular attention should be given to risks to data integrity and cybersecurity.
