The use of multiproduct facilities for the production of pharmaceutical products is necessary to allow products to be brought to patients in a timely and cost-effective manner. Doing this safely requires an understanding of the products, the facilities, the processes, and the equipment and the risks posed by this combination. The major risk of concern within shared facilities is the risk of cross-contamination.

        

New GMP guidance for the EU came into effect in 2015 that requires a risk management process to determine if products can be safely manufactured in shared facilities. This new GMP requirement left industry unsure how to perform and document these risk assessments.

        

This Baseline® Guide provides a scientific risk-based approach based on ICH Q9 to manage the risk of cross-contamination to maintain product quality and operator safety. This allows the selection of the appropriate risk control strategies to be implemented on a case-by-case basis to maintain an appropriate balance between assurance of product quality and worker safety. This Guide also provides examples of risk assessments and the summary report.

        

This second edition of the Guide is updated to include the new EU GMP requirements as well as additional information for cleaning, HVAC, and examples to assist the reader.

        

During the development of the first edition of this document, the FDA and EMA were actively involved and other regulatory agencies reviewed the document prior to publication. For the second edition there were discussions with the EMA to ensure that the Guide did not contradict the new GMP requirements and again other regulatory agencies, including the FDA, reviewed the document prior to publication.

## Quality System Requirements

            

        

    

    

        

            

                

### 3. Quality System Requirements

                

A well-defined quality system should consist of governance documents which are complemented by a concise set of tactical or procedural requirements. The overarching documentation matrix should provide the basis of consistency required for the creation of a sustainable quality system model which enables the ICH Q10 life cycle approach across the GxP regulated functions within an organization.

                

### 3.1 Application to Cross-Contamination

                

The key concepts discussed in this Guide should be used as a framework from which to develop standards, tools, and approaches to manage the risk of cross-contamination.

                

A case by case assessment should be performed of the risk factors that the concurrent manufacture of several products may cause. Risk assessments should be performed to properly understand the implications for both IH and GxP. While each is normally considered separately, to prevent any significant imbalance, the implications of decisions taken during an assessment for IH should be checked against those for GxP, and vice versa.

## Suggested Quality Policies / Standards for Managing CC Risk

            

        

    

    

        

            

                

### 3.1.1 Suggested Quality Policies / Standards for Managing the Risk of Cross-Contamination

                

While many aspects for managing the risk of cross-contamination are covered in various quality documents such as cleaning and cleaning validation, line clearance, etc., it is suggested that overarching quality policies, standards and SOPs are put in place to manage the risk of cross-contamination that tie together these various aspects.

                

Other quality systems that are impacted or related to managing the risk of cross-contamination include:

                

#### 1. Technology Transfer (New Product Introduction)

                

Firms should have a well-documented procedure for assessing potential products prior to introducing them into the facility. This should include:

                

                    
- A mechanism that triggers an assessment of the potential cross-contamination risks prior to bringing the product/compound into the facility.

                    
- The hazards of the product(s) should be identified typically via an ADE monograph.

                    
- Cleaning process development prior to introducing the product into the facility.

                    
- Analytical methods sensitive enough to meet the required acceptance criteria.

                

                

#### 2. Risk Management

                

The Risk Management quality system should provide a common understanding of risk and how to assess it. Senior management should provide guidance on how to determine acceptable and unacceptable risk. It is suggested that this be established at the corporate level so that there is a common understanding across the organization.

                

#### 3. Change Control

                

It is suggested that the change control process includes a trigger for risk review when the change may impact the risk of cross-contamination. Such changes may include: new product introduction, the ADE value of product, maximum daily dose for products, equipment changes, facility changes including changes to HVAC or water quality/pressure, process changes, or procedure changes.

                

#### 4. Corrective and Preventative Action (CAPA)

                

An outcome of the risk assessment activities may be to implement some risk reduction controls. The CAPA system may be used to track the risk mitigation measures until implemented and confirmation of performance is complete.

                

#### 5. Product Quality Review

                

Results from the risk management process may become part of the product quality review as appropriate. In addition, requalification or monitoring programs should also provide an indication that the risk of cross-contamination is in a controlled state.

                

#### 6. Training

                

It is important that basic GMP training for all relevant staff includes practices that are designed to avoid cross-contamination as well as understand how cross-contamination occurs. Staff involved in managing the risk of cross-contamination should have further training in both how to avoid cross-contamination and quality risk management.

### 3.2 The Logic Diagram

                

A logic diagram is a simple tool that can be used to guide a team through a process to determine the adequacy of existing controls or the need for dedicated facilities. (Section 3.6.1 should be read in conjunction with the flow diagram in Chapter 14.) Determining the need for dedicated facilities should be performed by a multidisciplinary team, so that all required areas are addressed appropriately.

                

The logic diagram is divided into two, addressing both GMP/Regulatory logic and IH logic. Both are equally important as they deal with aspects of risk associated with the different populations that may be exposed to the hazard under review:

                

                    
- **Left side** — product (patient risk)

                    
- **Right side** — worker (personnel risk)

                

                

Where dedicated facilities are implemented for business reasons, the risk assessment should clearly state that this is a business decision and not a cross-contamination issue.

## Considering the GMP / Regulatory Factors

            

        

    

    

        

            

                

### 3.2.1 GMP / Regulatory Factor — Logic Chain

                

#### 3.2.1.1 Gathering Hazard Information (e.g., Health Based Criteria)

                

Both the IH and GMP criteria are drawn from common toxicological or clinical data sources. The ADE value is normally the health-based criterion used in assessing GMP concerns related to cross-contamination. The OEL is normally the health-based criterion used in assessing airborne product for worker protection.

                

#### 3.2.1.2 Specific Requirements for Handling Product in a Dedicated Facility

                

"*Is there a written regulation that would preclude the co-manufacture of this product with other non-related materials?*"

                

The usual example is the beta-lactam class of antibiotics where 21 CFR 211.42(c)(d) requires separate facilities. The updated EU EudraLex Vol. 4 Section 3.6 requires dedicated facilities if:

                

                    
1. "the risk cannot be adequately controlled by operational and/or technical measures"

                    
2. "scientific data from the toxicological evaluation does not support a controllable risk (e.g. allergenic potential from highly sensitizing materials such as beta lactams)"

                    
3. "relevant residue limits, derived from the toxicological evaluation, cannot be satisfactorily determined by a validated analytical method."

                

                

Additional reasons to dedicate include: agency inspection consequence, internal QA recommendation, unique manufacturing technology, or high sales demand utilizing all capacity.

                

#### 3.2.1.3 Determining an ADE

                

"*Can an ADE be determined?*"

                

If the scientific data supports the determination, then an ADE value should be determined and passed on to other stakeholders. If the information does **not** support the determination of an ADE value, then a dedicated facility is required. This is where the beta-lactam family of products would be addressed.

                

#### 3.2.1.4 Detection of Residue Limits

                

"*Can relevant residue limits be detected?*"

                

Compounds that can be denatured/degraded/deactivated require studies to ensure the effectiveness of the process to render the residues safe. If analytical methods **cannot** be validated to the appropriate level, then a dedicated facility is necessary.

                

#### 3.2.1.5 Technical and Operational Controls

                

"*Do technical and operational controls manage the risk?*"

                

Each of the modes of cross-contamination should be assessed to determine whether technical and operational controls can manage the risk. Care should be taken when assessing manual operations and the controls in place to detect manual errors.

## Considering the Health and Hygiene Factors (Personnel)

            

        

    

    

        

            

                

### 3.2.2 Health and Hygiene Factors (Applicable to Personnel)

                

#### 3.2.2.1 Gather Hazard Information

                

The OEL is normally the health-based criterion used in assessing airborne levels of API from bulk or finished product for protection of workers.

                

#### 3.2.2.2 Assess Exposure Potential

                

Risk is a function of both hazard and exposure. Several factors should be considered:

                

                    
- **Who is at risk?** — Workers at the line, maintenance personnel, general personnel, QA personnel when sampling

                    
- **What factors influence exposure?** — Dusty powder? Liquid? Closed or open process? Training level? Routine or frequent task?

                    
- **What is the exposure pathway?** — Inhalation, ingestion, or skin absorption?

                    
- **When is exposure most likely?** — Sampling, process breakdown, clean down, or product changeover?

                

                

#### 3.2.2.3 Control of Exposure

                

"*Are the exposures adequately controlled?*"

                

Review available information: adverse health effects reported? Monitoring data available? Similar processes in other facilities? Controls should be commensurate with the risk and consistent with manufacturing process requirements (e.g., engineering controls must not make interventions or cleaning difficult).

                

#### 3.2.2.4 Process Changes

                

"*Is elimination, substitution, or process change possible?*"

                

Examples where this has been effective:

                

                    
1. Removal of a solid recovery step between stages in a chemical process

                    
2. Change to a wet granulation process from a direct blend process

                    
3. Addition of a film coat to a tablet to reduce exposure during packing

                    
4. Formulate as a liquid/semisolid in a capsule rather than a conventional tablet or powder filled capsule

                

                

#### 3.2.2.5 Selection of Engineering Controls

                

Once risk factors have been investigated, control measures can be considered. Factors include: cost, feasibility, ergonomics, ease of cleaning, and failure mode operation (what happens if the control measure fails).

                

#### 3.2.2.6 Level of Exposure Risk

                

"*Is the exposure risk still too high?*"

                

Is it reasonable to add in a secondary system, such as an extract system? Is routine use of PPE acceptable for short-duration activities? Discuss with operating personnel, plant management, and HSE specialists. Operators may prefer PPE if a rigid barrier makes a process step ergonomically difficult.

                

#### 3.2.2.7 Emergency Recovery

                

"*Is emergency recovery required?*"

                

Considerations: what will need to be recovered? What supporting infrastructure is required — PPE, change areas, showers, storage for PPE, separate air supply to service PPE?

                

#### 3.2.2.8 Secondary PPE

                

"*Is secondary control or PPE required?*"

                

The rationale for secondary PPE should be well understood and documented: is it provided to supplement a primary control measure, or for emergency use only?

## Risk Assessment

                

ICH Q9 three-step framework — identification, analysis, evaluation | p37–38

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Chapter 4

                    

Risk assessment as described in ICH Q9 encompasses three steps: risk identification, risk analysis, and risk evaluation. Each of the three steps helps to answer the three fundamental questions:

                    

                        
- What might go wrong?

                        
- What is the likelihood (probability) it will go wrong?

                        
- What are the consequences (severity)?

                    

                    

The risk assessment steps may be considered the most important aspects of an overall quality risk management process. If the risks are not identified, analyzed, and evaluated properly, the decisions on how to control the risk cannot be made adequately.

                    

The criteria for judging acceptability of risk for cross-contamination and operator exposures differ, although GxP and IH (Industrial Hygiene) considerations are linked because they share the same underlying toxicological data as the starting point from which they are both derived. In addition, the application and interpretation of the criteria in the risk assessment process will be different.

                    

Part of the risk assessment process is to consider if there are realistically any plausible pathways that might give rise to an increased risk of exposure. For example, when considering airborne transfer, the following risk questions should be asked:

                    

                        
- Can cross-contamination from an airborne pathway occur? Specifically, can product A be directly distributed via the air stream or resuspended back into the air (after being deposited) in a room/suite/facility, and then settle or get redeposited directly into product B or onto contact parts?

                        
- Can this occur in sufficient amount that the ADE for product A might be exceeded should a patient be administered the now contaminated product B?

                        
- Is it plausible for product that has been deposited on equipment or adjacent work surfaces to be redispersed into the air stream and present an exposure risk for operators via inhalation?

                    

                    

Similar questions should be asked to understand the risks associated with mix-up, retention, and mechanical transfer.

                    

At the conclusion of the risk assessment, risk should be expressed in quantitative or qualitative terms depending on the data available from the three steps. This information should then be used in the decision making to accept or reduce the risks under the risk control steps.

## Definition of Hazard

                

Active pharmacological, toxicological, microbiological properties | p37–38

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Section 5.1

                    

Hazard is defined in ICH Q9 as a potential source of harm. ICH Q9 also includes the severity of harm in the definition of risk. Within the context of this Guide, hazard describes the inherent property of a compound to produce adverse effects, e.g., in patients that may be exposed to the compound as a trace contaminant in another pharmaceutical product.

                    

The compounds typically considered include:

                    

                        
- APIs (Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients)

                        
- Process intermediates

                        
- Solvents

                        
- Excipients

                    

                    

Other compounds that may need to be considered include impurities, byproducts, and degradants. Each compound has its own inherent ability to cause adverse effects (i.e., toxicity) — effects that may be well documented in the case of the API, but may be unknown for other compounds.

## Hazard Continuum and Prioritization

                

From no hazard to poorly characterised hazard — OEB/OEL estimation | p38–39

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Section 5.2

                    

Depending on the hazardous properties of an individual compound, resulting from its mechanism-of-action and potency, health-based limits such as the ADE values can span several orders of magnitude. It is preferable to derive health-based values for all pharmaceutical compounds, regardless of potency, to ensure the protection of patients.

                    

Similarly, there is a need to focus attention on those compounds with the lowest health-based exposure limits because these may be the compounds that are the most difficult to control, and the consequences of overexposure may be the greatest.

                    

As part of an overall risk management approach, it is useful to have a qualitative tool to help determine the need for, and to prioritize, comprehensive quantitative risk assessments. This tool should ideally incorporate the severity of the effect/impact on quality of life, reversibility of the effect and likelihood of death.

                    

Criteria for identifying hazardous drugs and prioritizing risk assessments include:

                    

                        
1. Genotoxic compounds that are known to be or highly likely to be carcinogenic to humans

                        
2. Compounds that can produce reproductive and/or developmental effects at low dosages*

                        
3. Compounds that can produce serious target organ toxicity or other significant adverse effects at low dosages*

                    

                    

*For example clinical doses <1 to 10 mg/day or animal doses <0.1 to 1 mg/kg/day

                    

The use of Occupational Exposure Limits (OELs) and Occupational Exposure Bands (OEBs) can be used to estimate ADE values for the purpose of prioritization in risk assessments. Extrapolating an OEL to an ADE value, in its simplest sense, is accomplished by:

                    

ADE (µg/day) = OEL (µg/m³) × 10 m³

                    

Many companies assign new compounds to an OEB, or equivalent designation corresponding to airborne concentration ranges, to provide guidance on safe handling before sufficient data are available to establish a numerical OEL.

## Establishing Health-Based Exposure Limits (HBEL)

                

Foundation of risk-based manufacturing — hazard identification + dose-response + limit derivation | p40

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Section 5.3

                    

The foundation of a risk-based approach for manufacturing pharmaceutical products comprises:

                    

                        
- The identification of hazards

                        
- Assessment of dose-response relationships

                        
- Establishment of health-based limits

                    

                    

The identification of hazards and assessment of dose-response relationships are collectively referred to as hazard characterization.

                    

Having an understanding of how a compound can cause harm, and at what dose, is crucial to evaluating risks and deciding on appropriate exposure controls.

                    

The approach used to derive ADE values is consistent with a number of guidance documents on setting health-based exposure limits (e.g., WHO 2005). The EMA guidance document on setting Permitted Daily Exposure limits (PDEs) based on the ICH Q3C(R6) guidance for residual solvents utilizes a similar set of adjustment (assessment) factors. The PDE is essentially synonymous with the ADE.

                    

In practice, the methods used to derive health-based exposure limits may differ slightly, but it is the responsibility of the organization to ensure that all relevant sources of uncertainty and variability are addressed, regardless of which adjustment factor covers them, and that their recommended limits are scientifically defensible and adequately protective.

## Data Collection and Analysis

Original Text — Section 5.3.1

                    

For hazard identification, a formal review of all available relevant animal and human data should be performed for each compound. This review should be done by a qualified toxicologist. This is an individual that has many years of experience setting health-based limits such as ADE values and OELs, preferably in the pharmaceutical industry. Specific training in toxicology is necessary, whether at MS or PhD level, but degrees in related fields such as pharmacology or medicine may be sufficient when coupled with appropriate specific training and experience.

For non-proprietary compounds, all relevant information on the potential hazards of the material should be obtained through reliable sources. Standard toxicology and pharmacology reference texts should be consulted and a literature search (e.g., on PubMed) should be performed.

5.3.2

            

                

## Identification of the Critical Effect

                

First clinically significant adverse effect as dose increases | p41

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Section 5.3.2

                    

The purpose of a hazard evaluation is to identify all possible hazards associated with a compound and to rank them according to their severity. When combined with a dose-response assessment, the critical effect can be defined. This is typically the first clinically significant adverse effect that is observed as the dose increases.

                    

The critical effect should be used for the derivation of an ADE. Ideally, the critical effect should be derived from studies for the same route that the ADE value will be used to evaluate. Extrapolation from data for other routes may be necessary if relevant route specific data are not available.

                    

Exposures below the NOAEL for the critical effect should not, by definition, lead to other effects, as these will only occur at higher doses.

                    

The reviewing toxicologist should determine which of the effects reported at low dosages is the critical effect. It may be necessary to evaluate several endpoints, which may involve different sets of adjustment factors. A justification should be made considering the final ADE value, which is based on the most sensitive effect relevant to humans.

                    

Due to differences in the route of exposure, the toxicological profile of the compound in question and the potential for local effects at the site of contact, the critical effect may be different for patients and operators.

                    

Professional judgment is required to discern between biological effects that may be physiological or adaptive changes or subclinical pharmacological effects of the drug, which would have associated Lowest-Observed-Effect Levels (LOELs). These biological effects should be distinguished from clinically significant effects which are considered adverse effects.

                    

Clinically significant effects should be considered adverse effects for anyone not under the supervision of a physician prescribing this drug for a specific medical condition. These would have associated Lowest-Observed-Adverse-Effect Level (LOAELs). Quite often, the LOAEL for these effects is used as the Point-of-Departure (PoD) for derivation of the ADE value.

## Dose-Response Assessment

                

Threshold for systemic toxicity vs. no-threshold for genotoxic carcinogens | p41–42

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Section 5.3.3

                    

During the hazard characterization process, all effects potentially attributed to a compound should be documented. Each effect, whether considered adverse or not, has a dose-response relationship. In general, as the dose increases, the incidence and severity of the effect increases.

                    

For systemic toxicity endpoints, there is a dose below which no effects are expected. This is often referred to as the threshold for toxicity.

                    

For some endpoints such as cancer, due to direct damage to DNA, it is often assumed that a threshold does not exist and some effects (e.g., mutations) are possible at low doses. In this case, it is necessary to determine the acceptable level of response for the effect (e.g., a 1/100,000 excess cancer risk). It should be noted that this may be viewed as a conservative approach given that there are DNA repair mechanisms that may reverse the mutations and immunosurveillance mechanisms that prevent cancers from developing.

## Establishment of the NOAEL or Benchmark Dose

                

NOAEL definition, LOAEL limitation, BMD as mathematical alternative | p42

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Section 5.3.4

                    

For most toxicity endpoints, there is a clear dose-response relationship for the critical effect. The aim is to define the NOAEL for the critical effect, which may be used to derive the ADE. The application of adjustment factors (see Section 5.3.5) results in ADE values that are unlikely to produce any undesirable compound-related effects.

                    

It may not be possible to define the NOAEL for some toxicology data sets, and therefore, it may be necessary to derive the health-based limit using a LOAEL. This occurs when statistically significant adverse effects are observed at the lowest dose used in a study. The Benchmark Dose (BMD) can be used in this situation.

                    

The BMD is a value, equivalent to a NOAEL, that is derived mathematically by fitting a curve to the dose-response data for the critical effect. The BMD10 is defined as the dose that corresponds to a 10% response rate, the minimum level of response typically detected by standard toxicology studies. The statistical lower bound on the BMD10 (BMDL10) is often used in risk assessments as a surrogate for a NOAEL. Caution should be taken when extrapolating well below the observable range.

5.3.5

            

                

## Application of Adjustment Factors — ADE/PDE Calculation

                

Interspecies, intraspecies, duration, severity, LOAEL conversion, PK adjustments | p42–47

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Section 5.3.5

                    

Health-based exposure limits, such as ADE values, are derived by dividing the NOAEL for the critical effect (after adjusting for body weight, e.g., 50 kg) by various adjustment factors (also termed assessment, safety, or uncertainty factors) to extrapolate to the "true" no effect level in the subpopulation of interest (e.g., elderly patients).

                    

These are multiplied together to form a Composite Adjustment Factor (AFC) that is used in the denominator of the general equation used to set health-based limits.

                    

                        **ADE Calculation Formula (ISPE Risk-MaPP):**  

                        ADE (mg/day) = (PoD × BW) / (AFC × MF × PK)
                    

                    

                        **PDE Calculation Formula (EMA):**  

                        PDE = (NOAEL × BW) / (F1 × F2 × F3 × F4 × F5)
                    

                    

**Key adjustment factors:**

                    

                        
- **AFA / F1 (Interspecies):** Extrapolates from animal NOAEL to human. Allometric scaling, values 2–12 depending on species.

                        
- **AFH / F2 (Intraspecies):** Accounts for variability within the human population (kinetics + dynamics). Default = 10.

                        
- **AFS / F3 (Study Duration):** Adjusts when study duration is shorter than exposure scenario. Typically 3 (Risk-MaPP) or 10 (EMA, studies <4 weeks).

                        
- **AFL / F5 (LOAEL-to-NOAEL):** Applied when only LOAEL is available. Default = 3.

                        
- **AFD (Database Completeness):** Missing DART studies. Default = 3.

                        
- **MF / F4 (Modifying Factor / Severity):** Residual uncertainties, severity of effect, slope of dose-response. Range: <1 to 10.

                        
- **PK (Pharmacokinetic):** Bioavailability correction for route-to-route extrapolation.

## Setting Occupational Exposure Limits (OEL)

                

Worker protection vs. patient protection — different targets, same toxicological data | p48–49

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — Section 5.4

                    

The primary purpose of this Chapter is to describe methods to set ADE values for patient protection; however, similar quantitative health-based assessments are performed to set OELs to protect workers handling these substances.

                    

The safe level of exposure for patients may be different from that established for workers. This is because the critical effect used to derive the limits may be different due to differences in the anticipated route of exposure, and even where this is the same, the choice of adjustment factors may differ. Workers are generally healthy individuals, whereas patients may be children or elderly, and by definition, have at least one active medical condition.

                    

Adjustments may be necessary to address route-to-route extrapolation, even if the same critical effect is used. For example, an OEL derived from the ADE may need to be adjusted for differences in bioavailability between the route used to identify the critical effect (e.g., oral) and the anticipated route of exposure (e.g., inhalation).

                    

If the same assumptions apply regarding the critical effect, susceptible subpopulations, and bioavailability (e.g., 100%), an OEL can be calculated directly from the ADE by dividing the dose allowed by the volume of air breathed by a worker, typically 10 m³, engaged in light work for 8 hours:

                    

OEL (µg/m³) = ADE (µg/day) / 10 m³

                    

Surface target values (e.g., wipe limits), which are used for worker protection, can also be derived by dividing the dose permitted in 8 hours at the OEL (i.e., OEL × 10 m³) by a standard surface area (e.g., 100 cm²).

                    

While it may be tempting to use wipe limits to assess the potential for cross-contamination, it is important to emphasize that they are developed for worker safety purposes. Any other use must be done within the context of a risk assessment for patient safety (cross-contamination) when considering specific plausible pathways of exposure to product.

                        OEL = 100 ÷ 10 = 10 µg/m³
                        

                        Wipe Limit = OEL × 10 m³ ÷ 100 cm² = ADE ÷ 100 cm²

## Documentation of the Health-Based Exposure Limit

The scientific rationale for the recommended health-based limit (e.g., ADE) should be summarized in a formal written document. The document should include a summary to facilitate review by stakeholders and the basis for the ADE should be clearly described.

### 6 Risk Analysis

                    

ICH Q9 defines risk analysis as the estimation of the risk associated with the identified hazards. As the hazard is inherent to the API and other process chemicals, the **exposure to the hazard** is the variable that needs to be analyzed to determine the relative risks.

                    

This Chapter describes risk analysis, which is closely related to exposure assessment, i.e., the process by which exposures are assessed, either qualitatively or quantitatively, to estimate the risk associated with the identified hazards.

                    

The risk analysis looks at the modes of exposure such as:

                    

                        
- **Mix-up**

                        
- **Retention**

                        
- **Mechanical transfer**

                        
- **Airborne transfer**

                    

                    

...for the product, as well as inhalation, dermal, ingestion, and mechanical transfer for workers, while considering the question *"What could go wrong?"*

                    

The identified hazard also should be analyzed in order to answer the questions:

                    

                        
- *"What is the likelihood (probability) it will go wrong?"*

                        
- *"What are the consequences (severity)?"*

                        
- *"Can the failure be detected?"*

### 6.1 Holistic Approach to Risk Analysis

                    

While the focus of ICH Q9 is understood, appropriate risk analysis should recognize that the production of medicines requires that a number of potentially conflicting requirements need to be met, which may impact each other, e.g.:

                    

                        
- **Cross-contamination** (focus on patient safety)

                        
- **Industrial Hygiene (IH)** (focus on worker safety)

                        
- Process safety

                        
- Production demands

                    

                    

Therefore, effective risk analysis requires a shared understanding to achieve the balance between these different requirements.

                    

#### Multidisciplinary Team

                    

A multidisciplinary approach is beneficial when drawing on expertise from several specialist areas and provides a holistic and balanced approach. This should include:

                    

                        
- Toxicologists

                        
- Industrial hygienists

                        
- Engineers

                        
- Quality managers

                        
- Users or operating personnel

                        
- Independent facilitators

                    

                    

#### GxP vs. Industrial Hygiene

                    

Although GxP and IH exposure assessments are linked, the application and interpretation of the criteria in the assessment process are different.

                    

#### High Hazard ≠ High Risk

                    

It should be noted that more hazardous compounds will not necessarily lead to higher potential for exposure. Some reasons that more hazardous compounds are not necessarily high risk of cross-contamination:

                    

                        
- Higher hazard compounds tend to be a **smaller percentage of the final product**

                        
- Processed in **more closed systems** than lower hazard products

                        
- Controls tend to be **more robust**

                    

                    

The exposure potential may not be the same for manufacture of the same compound and the same process in a **different location** as there are other variables such as worker behavior.

## 6.2 Tools for Risk Analysis —

                

Qualitative & Quantitative Methods, Air Sampling | p55–p57

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 6.2 Tools

                    

Fundamentally, risk analysis should incorporate both qualitative and quantitative processes to link the likelihood of occurrence with the severity of harm. The tools should be applied in a manner that is **commensurate with the risks**.

                    

In order to have a robust assessment, it is important to use **actual data** from the facility such as incident reports, cleaning and cleaning validation results, results from protocol studies, and regulatory action, to assist in categorizing the risk being assessed.

                    

The analysis will typically focus on a **defined part of the process**, e.g., charging a vessel or a particular unit operation such as compression. The entire process should be reviewed (including unexpected operation situations such as breach of containment, or a spill).

                    

#### 6.2.1 Qualitative Risk Analysis and Prioritization

                    

Qualitative risk analysis is a process that can be used to assess the risk of exposure and prioritize subsequent quantitative activities. Formats include, but are not limited to:

                    

                        
- Flowcharts

                        
- Check sheets

                        
- Process mapping

                        
- Risk ranking and filtering

                        
- Cause and effect diagrams

                    

                    

A robust qualitative risk analysis process should incorporate:

                    

                        
- Hazard characterization using ADE/OEL/TLV® data

                        
- Physical characteristics of the pharmaceutical compound

                        
- Definition of unit operations to be assessed including cleaning

                        
- Observation of operations and discussions with personnel

                        
- Review of similar existing unit operations, vendor input, benchmarking

                        
- Exposure profile including identification of plausible pathways

                        
- Checks that existing control measures are functioning properly

                    

                    

#### 6.2.2 Quantitative Risk Analysis

                    

Based on the results of the qualitative risk analysis, it may be necessary to perform a quantitative risk analysis involving the collection and evaluation of data. The quantitative analysis process provides an objective and empirical basis regarding risk potential.

                    

Statistical indices such as Process Capability Index (Cpk) or Process Performance Index (Ppk) can be used to evaluate cleaning residue data against upper limits. For cleaning data, which is typically **non-normally distributed**, the Process Performance Index (Ppk) is a preferred technique.

                    

#### 6.2.2.1 Air Sampling

                    

Workplace air sampling is normally conducted by industrial hygienists as part of an ongoing worker health protection monitoring program. Similar air sampling techniques may also be useful as part of an initial risk assessment for Quality Assurance purposes to help demonstrate that material is being adequately contained.

                    

There is **no regulatory requirement** to perform air sampling as part of GxP. Containment should routinely be proven through qualified and measurable physical parameters, such as pressure differentials and airflow volumes and direction.

## 6.3.1 Mix-up —

                

Most common cause of regulatory action / product recall | p55–p57

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

### 6.3.1 Mix-up

                    

Mix-up is frequently cited by regulators as one of the most common causes for **regulatory action/product recall**. For the purpose of this Guide, mix-up refers to the contamination at unsafe levels of one product with another.

                    

The highest risks from mix-up normally stem from **low-tech GxP failures** due to:

                    

                        
- Basic human error (e.g., failure to follow procedure)

                        
- System weakness (e.g., poor material labeling)

                    

                    

Obviously, there is an increased potential risk of cross-contamination if two or more products are being handled in the same area, even with appropriate batch to batch segregation.

                    

#### GxP Review Areas for Mix-up Prevention

                    

The risk analysis to identify potential cross-contamination routes should include a review of basic GxP to confirm that appropriate systems provide a high level of assurance that mix-up will not occur, such as:

                    

                        
- Identification and status labeling/controls of materials and products

                        
- Status labeling/controls of equipment and facilities

                        
- Receipt and controls of materials and products

                        
- Physical segregation, process flow, and security during material and product handling, storage, and staging

                    

                    

#### Typical Examples of Mix-up

                    

                        
- Inadequate line clearance between products

                        
- Accidental use of dirty equipment

                        
- Introduction of another product during sampling

                        
- Incorporation of the wrong starting material or excipient

                        
- Mislabeling of equipment and/or materials

                        
- Unintended transfer of materials from one vessel to another containing different product/materials

                    

                    

#### Human Error Related Factors

                    

                        
- Operator training and understanding of process requirements

                        
- Ergonomic requirements of activities against operator capabilities

                        
- Local environment (lights, noise, and heat)

                        
- Operator workload (too busy, bored)

                        
- Level of routine involved in an activity

                        
- Local culture, e.g., is procedural innovation discouraged?

                        
- Overly complex procedures

                        
- Ease of error, e.g., a function of design, can an error be made and how easily?

                    

                    

#### 6.3.1.1 Factors Affecting Exposure by Mix-up

                    

The presence of multiple processes within a common facility with shared or overlapping process flows may give rise to an increased risk of mix-up through increased potential for mistakes in materials management processes.

                    

Factors to be considered when assessing the risk of mix-up within the process include:

                    

                        
- Overlapping process flows and transit routes

                        
- Common dispensary areas

                        
- Common storage areas for change parts

## 6.3.2 Retention —

### 6.3.2 Retention

                    

Retention is defined as residual material on product contact surfaces after cleaning that can carryover from one product to another product in the same equipment, having been used in a sequential or campaign manner.

                    

Retention is the mode of cross-contamination directly addressed by the cleaning program which includes cleaning validation.

                    

Development and validation of cleaning procedures that consistently reduce residuals, both:

                    

                        
- **Intrinsic** (product/process materials)

                        
- **Non-intrinsic** (such as cleaning agents)

                    

                    

...on product contact equipment to acceptable levels is critical to the success of a cross-contamination control program.

                    

#### 6.3.2.1 Health-Based Acceptance Limits for Cleaning

                    

Basing the acceptance criteria on a health-based limit such as the ADE (or PDE) value has many advantages over traditional methods (e.g., 1/1000th of the low clinical dose).

                    

The most important is the fact that the ADE value is **toxicologically derived**.

                    

**Calculation of Health-Based Cleaning Limits:**

                    

*Swab Residue Level (mg/swab) =*

                    

                        (ADE × SB × TA × RF) / (MDD × SSA)
                    

                    

*Rinse Residue Level (mg/L) =*

                    

                        (ADE × SB × RA × RF) / (V × MDD × SSA)
                    

                    

Where: ADE = Acceptable Daily Exposure, MDD = Maximum Daily Dose, SB = Smallest batch size, SSA = Shared Surface Area, TA = Test Area, RA = Rinsed Area, RF = Recovery Factor, V = Volume of Rinse

                    

#### 6.3.2.3 Use of 1/1000th and 10 ppm

                    

The use of traditional acceptance limits is **not the preferred strategy**. Ideally, compound specific, scientifically derived, health-based values should be established whenever possible.

                    

                        
- 1/1000th method is often **overprotective** for most APIs → lowers apparent margin of safety

                        
- For highly hazardous drugs (e.g., antineoplastic), 1/1000th method may **not be sufficiently protective**

                        
- 10 ppm is arbitrary and not applicable to all compounds

                    

                    

The health-based limit should **not be seen as a limit** in the true sense, but as a reference point for determining the level of risk. Equipment must always be **visually clean**.

                    

#### 6.3.2.11 Factors Affecting Exposure by Retention

                    

Equipment-related factors:

                    

                        
- Large surface areas for potential product contact

                        
- Frequency of changeover and cleaning

                        
- Use of disposable equipment/product contact parts

                        
- Crevices or ledges where product can accumulate (occluded areas)

                        
- Rough or unpolished product contact surfaces

                        
- Drains

                        
- Material compatibility with preferred cleaning agents

                    

                    

Material-related factors increasing residue risk:

                    

                        
- Sticky or caked-on solids

                        
- Poor solubility or wettability with available cleaning materials

                        
- Materials that dry with hard surfaces

                        
- Highly viscous or shear thickening materials

                        
- Materials that react with common cleaning solutions to form solid or sticky gel-like materials

## 6.3.3 Mechanical Transfer —

                

Contaminated non-product contact surfaces, operator apparel, equipment routing | p65–p66

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

### 6.3.3 Mechanical Transfer

                    

Mechanical transfer includes all routes by which material can be transferred from contaminated non-product contact surfaces into or onto the product. This includes:

                    

                        
- Product contact surfaces contaminated by contact with contaminated surfaces (e.g., in cleaning areas)

                        
- Inadvertent or transient contact with other contaminated non-designated product contact areas

                        
- Direct contact of the product with operator apparel and gloves

                    

                    

The major issue with such routes is the **lack of control of contaminated items**.

                    

The factors which affect the risk of mechanical transfer are made up of those which influence the:

                    

                        
- Transfer of the material *to* the surface

                        
- Association of the surface with the product at risk

                        
- Release of the material *from* the contaminated surface to the product

                    

                    

#### 6.3.3.1 Factors Affecting Exposure by Mechanical Transfer

                    

The risk of mechanical transfer can occur with the colocation of a process with contaminated equipment or items, e.g., operators wearing contaminated clothing or contact between process equipment contaminated with different materials.

                    

**Factors include:**

                    

                        
- The use of common areas where segregation of processes and equipment is poor, e.g., **common manual cleaning areas** where dirty and clean equipment from different processes may be present at the same time

                        
- Use of common gowning regimes across areas where multiple products may be present simultaneously

                        
- Surface to surface contact, e.g., scraping, smearing, or wiping of process equipment

                        
- Use of common equipment, e.g., hand tools or change parts between different processes

                    

                    

The use of certain equipment may cause operators or mobile equipment to become contaminated and potentially transit to other process areas without undergoing decontamination procedures. Issues to be considered:

                    

                        
- The presence of **highly manual, uncontained/open processes**

                        
- The specification and location of decontamination procedures for users and equipment

                        
- Routing of equipment and users around facilities, especially where there is common routing of products

## 6.3.4 Airborne Transfer —

                

Aerosol generation, dustiness, CPT, physical barriers | p66–p68

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

### 6.3.4 Airborne Transfer

                    

The direct airborne transfer route assumes the generation of a stable aerosol, which moves to another area where it deposits in significant quantities on another exposed product.

                    

An aerosol is a suspension of fine solid or liquid particles in air. The operator has a greater risk of exposure than another product because of the inhalation of dust by the operator in relatively close proximity to the product compared to the **passive deposition** of dust into or onto another product distant from the emission source.

                    

Products typically are separated by **physical barriers, airlocks, and corridors**.

                    

                        

"Industrial hygiene monitoring data should not be used to quantitatively evaluate potential product cross-contamination due to these differences. However, high levels of airborne dust identified by monitoring may be qualitatively indicative of a poorly controlled process."

                    

                    

Chemical contaminants do not have the potential to proliferate as is the case with microbial contamination. In addition, there is generally **less potential for airborne contaminants to present a serious risk of cross-contamination** than from other routes, such as mix-up and retention.

                    

However, chemical contamination can increase due to **continued deposition from an extended time emission source** onto a continuously exposed at-risk product. Such deposition may also give rise to increased risk of subsequent mechanical transfer.

                    

#### Three Key Questions for Airborne Pathway Assessment

                    

                        
1. Can the contaminating process form a stable aerosol?

                        
2. Can the stable aerosol be emitted and move to a position where it may deposit on the at-risk product in significant quantities?

                        
3. Is the at-risk product sufficiently exposed to the aerosol for significant cross-contamination (i.e., above the ADE value)?

                    

                    

#### 6.3.4.1 Factors Affecting Exposure by Airborne Transfer

                    

Risk analysis should consider the following for any significant risk of cross-contamination:

                    

                        
- Open processing activities at both source and receiving points

                        
- Poorly sealed process equipment with **pressure driving forces** (positive pressure at source and/or negative pressure at receiving material positions)

                        
- The repeated transfer of material through breakable or poorly contained couplings

                        
- Relative locality of different process streams

                        
- Provision of rooms and other building features between source and receiving positions

                    

                    

#### Physical Properties for Stable Aerosol Formation

                    

Key issues include:

                    

                        
- Moisture content of solids

                        
- Liquid viscosity and surface tension

                        
- Particle size distribution

                        
- Particle shape

                        
- Particle density

                        
- Particle charge (related to relative humidity)

                    

                    

Such properties may be considered as a combined factor commonly known as dustiness — a measure of the propensity of the material to remain airborne for extended periods when in an aerosol.

                    

#### 6.3.4.2 Containment Performance Target (CPT)

                    

The Containment Performance Target (CPT) (expressed in µg/m³) is a target value that is used as the basis for plant design and selection of risk control strategies (i.e., containment systems) for protection of the workforce.

### 6.4 Probability of Occurrence

                    

A risk analysis designed to identify the probability of cross-contamination should consider all potential routes of cross-contamination under all operational conditions in a systematic and detailed manner. The analysis should be reviewed:

                    

                        
- At the design stage of any new or refurbished facility/equipment

                        
- At the introduction of new products or changes to any process or equipment

                        
- As part of any relevant change control

                        
- As part of a regular risk management review

                    

                    

The following are possible examples where the probability of failure or error may be enhanced, and these also should be considered as part of any review:

                    

                        
- Manual processes

                        
- Historically non-robust processes or equipment

                        
- Specific elements of a process (batch start up, batch changeovers, shift changeovers, post maintenance)

                        
- New, untrained, or changed personnel

### 6.4.1 Production Process Activities

                    

It should be noted that the correct application of GxP generally ensures that most processes are reasonably well contained and controlled with respect to cross-contamination. The production process needs to be fully reviewed to ensure that all potential exposures are identified and evaluated.

                    

#### 6.4.1.1 Operational Status: Set Up, Operation, Cleaning, Upset

                    

A system that controls product and worker exposure should perform at all times. Many processes are evaluated for containment performance during optimal operation, such as at Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT) and do not take account of the following conditions:

                    

                        
- Set up

                        
- Operation

                        
- Intervention for correction, heel recovery, end point determination, scrape down for yield/uniformity, sample recovery

                        
- Upset, such as equipment failure

                        
- Disassembly

                        
- Cleaning

                    

                    

Each process step should be evaluated to ensure that it does not provide a significant potential for exposure. The review of each of these items should ensure that the necessary actions can be performed and transfers of tools, materials, and components to perform the tasks can be done without significant or unacceptable exposure or carry over.

                    

#### 6.4.1.2 Process and Exposures: A Review of Potential Exposures Created within Processes

                    

A specific process will have a number of features, all of which may have a greater or lesser impact on the risk of cross-contamination by one or more of the routes. The major factors to consider include:

                    

                        
- The degree of open handling of the hazardous material (e.g., in bulk manual dispensing)

                        
- Energy in the form of velocity, pressure, and temperature inherent to the process, which may create transmissible aerosols, e.g.:
                            

                                
    - Milling

                                
    - Micronizing

                                
    - Blending

                                
    - Atomization

                                
    - Fluidization

                                
    - Agitated mixing

                                
    - Compression

                            

                        

                        
- Transfer: the repeated charging and discharging of unit processes through breakable couplings provides a possible route of release — the more transfers required, the greater the potential of risk

                        
- Worker behavior or compliance with SOPs: systems that are highly dependent on operator technique or allow wide scope for variations in operator performance are more susceptible to failure

                        
- Frequency: the more often a process is performed the greater the risk of material release or serial releases

                        
- Scale: the impact of scale may not be linear. The factors affected by scale include worker care (manually charging 100 kg requires a different approach to charging 100 g), and the volume of material available for release

                        
- Knowledge of the equipment used, operability, and performance

## Non-Processing Activities —

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

### 6.4.2 Non-Processing Activities

                    

#### 6.4.2.1 Waste Handling

                    

The risk analysis should cover all aspects of waste handling to ensure that items such as soiled clothing, disposable items (e.g., masks, gloves, and filters), and material/product waste streams are appropriately handled to avoid the potential for cross-contamination. Shared areas, e.g., common cleaning rooms, potentially containing simultaneously both dirty and clean equipment from different process streams should be carefully considered.

                    

#### 6.4.2.2 Planned and Unplanned Maintenance

                    

All planned maintenance activities should be reviewed in sufficient detail to identify potential routes for cross-contamination. Some planned maintenance may take place outside of normal operating hours and by personnel other than those involved with the batch manufacture.

                    

The planned maintenance review should consider the following aspects, as a minimum:

                    

                        
- Nature of the maintenance (e.g., air handling shutdown)

                        
- Frequency of the maintenance (e.g., daily, weekly, monthly, annual)

                        
- Status of production when the planned maintenance occurs (e.g., area clean or dirty)

                        
- The availability of approved procedures for planned maintenance

                        
- Necessary qualifications of personnel performing the maintenance activities

                        
- Appropriate training for those performing planned maintenance

                        
- The likelihood of powder spillage during maintenance (e.g., emptying local dust extraction and vacuum units)

                    

                    

It is more difficult to predict how unplanned maintenance may affect the probability of cross-contamination. A proactive review of potential unplanned events should be performed, to predict how to handle many of them in advance to help to avoid the risk of cross-contamination. This could lead to specific failure mode procedures for some major events (e.g., HVAC failure).

                    

#### 6.4.2.3 Changeovers

                    

The changeover between one product and the next is an important aspect to be reviewed, especially regarding the order of such changeover activities. The changeover activities should be appropriately documented as part of a procedure and clearly indicate the sequence of the changeover work.

                    

Changeover between products may require that equipment change parts have to be removed from the line for one product and be replaced with change parts for the second product. The handling, cleaning, transport, and storage of such change parts (e.g., tablet punches) should be carefully considered.

                    

#### 6.4.2.4 Cleaning

                    

Cleaning can in itself create a potential risk of cross-contamination if not performed properly. The cleaning procedures should be reviewed to ensure that they are sufficiently detailed for personnel to be able to follow them consistently and correctly.

                    

The cleaning processes which are developed should incorporate a risk-based approach to assist in the determination of critical controls and/or critical parameters. For example, a Design of Experiment (DoE) could be performed to determine what parameters have the greatest or least impact on the cleaning process.

                    

Automatic cleaning procedures such as CIP should, in theory, provide much more consistent cleaning than manual cleaning procedures. The CPPs associated with any CIP system (e.g., times, temperatures, and pressures) and how these are monitored, controlled, and alarmed should be understood. There also should be a clear understanding of what happens if these CPPs go outside of limits (e.g., low pressure), as well as the degree of safeguards against manual intervention (e.g., password control).

Original Text (English)

                    

### 6.4.3 Facility and Equipment Design

                    

The design of the facility and equipment should be reviewed when considering the potential risks of cross-contamination. The review should include consideration of risk elements such as:

                    

                        
- Surfaces and finishes (e.g., smoothness, accessibility to clean)

                        
- Air handling philosophy (e.g., filtration levels, fresh air or recirculation, pressure differential regimes, airflows, monitoring, recording, alarms, back up). The analysis also should review the need for once through air. If air can be recirculated without undue risk to workers and cross-contamination, this can have a large impact on the facility design as well as the operating costs. See Section 9.6 for more information.

                        
- Airlocks

                        
- Equipment layout (e.g., space, segregation)

                        
- Material and process flows including common areas for processes and personnel

                        
- Cleaning philosophy including provision of effective automated cleaning systems, such as CIP and Wash In Place (WIP)

                        
- Process equipment design (e.g., closed versus open systems, charging ports)

                    

                    

Actual exposure varies between individual process cycles due to operator performance differences, and the degree of this variability is often reflected in the method of exposure control. Closed systems have far lower variability than open systems, and open systems with engineering controls are better controlled than unprotected open processes.

                    

                        ADV TOPIC: The selection of the specific process equipment may have a major impact on the scope of exposure through careful design of the processing machine to minimize leaks. This may already be an inherent requirement of the equipment, e.g., with API reaction pressure vessels during reaction steps or fluid bed dryers during drying. As a result, process equipment or processes associated with them can be considered to be either "open" or "closed."
                    

                    

#### 6.4.3.1 Closed Process Definition

                    

A process step (or system) is considered closed where the product is not exposed to the immediate room environment (and vice versa). It is the manufacturer's responsibility to define and demonstrate closure for a process step.

                    

An example might include a pressurized vessel operation or a process enclosed in an effective "high containment" process equipment item, e.g., a sealed process system filled and discharged through split butterfly valves.

                    

#### 6.4.3.2 Open Process Definition

                    

Any process step that does not meet the definition of "closed process" is considered open. This might include open material handling during dispensing or where the process system is opened for a particular step, e.g., granulator bowl scraping or bowl removal from a fluid bed dryer for product recovery. It should be noted that the amount of time the process is open (whether it is brief or longer) should be considered during the risk assessment.

## Product Exposure Risk in Non-Product Contact Areas —

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

### 6.5 Product Exposure Risk in Non-Product Contact Areas

                    

In general, the risk of cross-contamination due to mechanical and/or airborne transfer is considered to be low relative to other possible pathways (e.g., retention or mix-up) although in these areas it may be critical for worker safety. If the risk assessment indicates that airborne contamination is a potential concern, then further analysis may be necessary using quantitative assessment tools based on the acceptance limits established reflecting the ADE of Product A and the batch size and maximum daily dose of Product B.

                    

#### 6.5.1 Non-Product Contact Area

                    

Figure 6.7🔍 illustrates the main classifications of non-product contact areas:

                    

                        
- Barriers: physically segregate the product contact surface from the surrounding product room area as well as from workers. Types of barriers can be closed processing equipment or enclosures such as a pressured vessel, isolator, or ventilated enclosures such as safety cabinets and fume cupboards.

                        
- Product room/suite: where one product is processed on a dedicated, batch, or campaign basis where the non-product contact surfaces include exterior surfaces of enclosed process equipment or barriers and interior finishes such as walls, floor, ceiling, and doors.

                        
- Supporting areas: adjacent to the product rooms/suites, where workers from other product rooms may be present.

                        
- GxP area: the common area where the worker is not in direct contact with the product as in the secondary packaging, shipping/receiving, and warehousing areas.

                        
- HVAC system: includes ducting, filters, Air Handling Units (AHUs), and control devices for a product room installed in the GxP area.

                    

                    

#### 6.5.2 Residue on Non-Product Contact Surfaces

                    

It is important that non-product contact surfaces are kept visually clean. Visual monitoring enables a wide area to be observed quickly. Swab sampling data may be available to permit assessment of potential cross-contamination of products from these non-product contact surfaces.

                    

Particularly where the exposure potential is high, swab testing against preset limits may be part of a more detailed review/action plan. These preset limits should take into consideration plausible, worst-case, assumptions of potential exposure pathways, the amount of Product A that could cross contaminate Product B, and the ADE value for Product A.

                    

Some companies may use wipe samples/limits to assess the potential dermal risk from systematic exposure of employees and this should not be confused with potential cross-contamination risks from non-product contact surfaces.

                    

#### 6.5.3 Plausibility

                    

Non-product contact parts represent elements within the process boundary which are not in product contact. Typical elements are the internal surfaces of an isolator or the surfaces of an engineering control device. These surfaces are far less critical than product contact surfaces and should be cleaned between products.

                    

Surfaces outside this zone are of even less significance, particularly where closed systems are used. Though the surface concentration taken over the whole surface area may seem a significant number, the risk of all or even a small fraction of this material being disengaged from the surface and entering the product is relatively insignificant.

                    

                        **Example calculation (Plausibility):** A room 5 m × 5 m × 3 m has a surface area of 110 m². Where the LOD is 5 µg/swab, where the swab is 25 cm² and the samples taken are below the level of detection; taking a very conservative approach, the total available on the surface is:  

                        110 m² × 10,000 cm²/m² × 5 µg/25 cm² = 220,000 µg or 220 milligrams.  

                        Supposing the potential contamination is to a batch with 100,000 maximum daily dose units and the ADE is 50 µg/day, the total that could be present and be within the limit is 50 µg × 100,000 = 5,000,000 µg or 5 grams. The margin of safety (5 g vs 220 mg) is significant.
                    

                    

                        **Second example (scoop transfer):** A stainless steel scoop with surface area 2,900 cm² at visual detection limit of 4 µg/cm²:  

                        2,900 cm² × 4 µg/cm² = 11,600 µg or 11.6 milligrams.  

                        With a safe carryover limit of 5 grams, the margin of safety = 5,000 mg / 11.6 mg ≈ 431. This represents low cross-contamination risk.
                    

                    

#### 6.5.4 Considerations for Exposure Assessment

                    

The mode of exposure for non-product contact areas is by either mechanical or airborne transfer. The following should be considered when conducting an exposure assessment:

                    

                        
- Open processes during operation, cleaning, upset, and maintenance

                        
- Retained or accumulated product inside the barrier or the product room/suite

                        
- Recirculated HVAC systems for the product room — is the air stream effectively protected from recirculated aerosols?

                        
- Retained and accumulated product in the ductwork, including airborne transfer due to pressure differentials

                        
- Leakage of product to areas above the ceiling or interstitial space or into the cavities of the building

                        
- Retained or accumulated product during maintenance or repair of the HVAC or utility systems

                        
- Transfer of product compound from the worker's clothing, hands, or feet

                        
- Transfer of product compound from dirty equipment transfer and their wheels

                        
- Transfer of product compound on the handling media

                        
- Manual cleaning of equipment and/or barrier

                        
- Emergency or upset situations in the pharmaceutical area

                        
- Shared HVAC systems serving several rooms or common areas where several different products are handled — should be evaluated in relation to each specific hazard

### 6.6 Detection

                    

An essential element in accurately assigning a risk level to a hazard arising from a compound is the degree of difficulty by which the compound can be detected. A hazard rating may be high, but if the method of detection is sufficient, linked to health-based limits and to appropriate control measures, then the subsequent risk arising from that hazard can be managed and low.

                    

The application of methodologies which possess the degree of selectivity and sensitivity necessary to detect a compound of interest is key to any detection-based control of risk from a hazard. Such controls, when optimally embedded into those points of the process which present the highest level of potential concern can effectively control the risk.

                    

In situations where the method of detection is visual only, it is important to understand the visual acuity of the staff and what level of residue is considered safe. If the safe level is below the visual acuity of the staff then the risk of failure not being detected may be considered high whereas if the safe level is well above (several orders of magnitude) the visual acuity of the staff the risk of failure not being detected may be considered low.

                    

The degree of variability in product contact equipment cleaning processes should be understood and considered as a primary factor in ongoing monitoring decisions. As an example, automated CIP processes present less risk from a variability perspective than manual cleaning methods, and may therefore justify less ongoing monitoring (once mechanical reliability is proven) than a manual process.

                    

#### 6.6.1 Developmental Controls Providing Process Knowledge

                    

During the course of pharmaceutical discovery and development activities, one focus is on the development of analytical methodology aimed at accurate identification and assessment of both drug substance and potential synthesis and degradation impurities. The drug substance itself and the potential preliminary toxicological profiles are established for each impurity as well as for the drug substance.

                    

Traditionally, once analytics have been established, efforts have turned to the optimization of test methods, an exercise which culminates in a "validation exercise."

                    

Process Analytical Technology (PAT) is increasingly being applied to processes for the purpose of acquiring information to facilitate process understanding and allowing risk control strategies to be established. Once established, these should be maintained and continually improved under the Pharmaceutical Quality System (ICH Q10).

                    

As such, the development, convergence, and subsequent integration of analytical technologies such as Near Infra-Red (NIR) Raman Spectroscopy and endpoint monitoring/control tools should be considered essential developmental activities. Complementary controls, such as barrier systems, also may be seen as suitable mechanisms to control a risk arising from any particular hazard.

                    

Subsequent scale up activities should embed continued refinement of PAT enablers. Such continual improvement initiatives should become institutional requirements within organizations, much akin to historical efforts of yield optimization and process train mapping.

                    

                        ADVANCED TOPIC: A potential challenge to the holistic approach presented may be encountered where traditional demarcation exists between research and commercial functions within the industry. This should not prevent existing methodologies being either discounted or replaced by new techniques or a technology developed by units supporting the progression of the compound throughout its life cycle.
                    

                    

#### 6.6.2 Compendia Comparison

                    

Prior to starting the development of complementary analytics, units concerned should assess the basis of current referee methodology utilized by regulatory agencies. The methods, both microbiologically and analytically derived, typically assist the agencies in the evaluation of a variety of sample collection analyses: to include challenging materials for the presence of compounds, such as penicillins and beta-lactams.

                    

The caveat for any internally developed analytical or microbial method is that it should formally demonstrate at least an equivalent level of sensitivity as the compendial (e.g., USP, EP, JP) or referee methodology. Reasons for developing an alternative methodology may include improved sensitivity, robustness, application to PAT technologies, or ease of use.

                    

#### 6.6.3 Adequacy of Controls

                    

Throughout the product life cycle, the containment technologies, equipment, and analytical testing which are utilized should continue to be evaluated for applicability, currency, reproducibility, and robustness. Without consistency of the controls which are employed, multiple variables will exist which will complicate root cause investigations should a detection technique identify a breach to the acceptable threshold of control which has been established.

## Risk Evaluation —

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

ICH Q9 places risk evaluation at the conclusion of risk assessment (risk identification / risk analysis / risk evaluation). Risk evaluation compares the identified and analyzed risk against given risk criteria. This step is designed to provide an estimation of risks to patients via product cross-contamination or to workers based on the hazard identification, dose-response assessment, and exposure assessment, along with consideration of the assumptions, limitations, and sources of uncertainty and variability in the analysis.

                    

All available information is evaluated in order to determine the robustness of the data sets used to assess the risk. Based on this evaluation, a conclusion can be made as to whether potential exposures are above or below acceptable levels.

                    

Incomplete knowledge about the hazard of a compound, the potential routes for mechanical and airborne transfer, and resulting variation in exposures brought about by design of process or equipment, are all sources of uncertainty whose potential impact should be considered in assessing both risk to patients by cross-contamination and risk to workers by direct exposure to the compound.

                    

If the data sets do not provide sufficient information, it may be determined during this step that additional data gathering is necessary. The output of the risk evaluation is either a quantitative estimate (e.g., distance between acceptance criteria and data gathered — true margin of safety) or a qualitative description (e.g., high, medium, a numerical probability, or low) of a range of risk.

                    

All aspects of risk assessment, not just the risk evaluation, should be evaluated and documented in a manner proportionate to the risk (the higher the risk, the more in-depth the evaluation and documentation process). The evaluation should be completely transparent so that the user fully understands the application and limitation(s) of the conclusions drawn. A fully transparent risk assessment can be used in the ongoing risk review process.

## Defining Acceptable Risk —

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

It is important for senior management to set guidelines on how to determine acceptable risk, and more importantly, unacceptable risk. Unacceptable risk requires that processing should cease until the risk is reduced to acceptable levels. Typically, there will be three action ranges:

                    

                        
- Acceptable risk: no action required / monitor only

                        
- Unacceptable risk: cease actions until risk is reduced to acceptable levels / monitor to confirm effectiveness of risk reduction

                        
- Reduce risk if possible: between acceptable risk and unacceptable risk, there is a range where risk reduction measures should be employed but actions can continue to be monitored to ensure risk is not increased to unacceptable

                    

                    

Risk can be categorized in qualitative terms such as high, medium or low risk, red, yellow, green, or in quantitative terms where the data is compared to acceptance criteria consisting of numerical limits.

                    

Caution should be exercised in the use of qualitative risk terms as these are indicative of the potential for risk and not a definitive risk outcome. All identified risks, whether qualitative or quantitative, high or low, should be reviewed periodically, based on the level of risk, to ensure that the risk profile has not changed.

## Risk Control —

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

ICH Q9 states that risk control is the process of decision making to reduce and/or accept risks. Risk acceptance and risk reduction should be considered as iterative steps and be subject to constant review, feedback, and communication. If the risk is still unacceptable, further risk reduction steps should be identified.

                    

A Risk Priority Number (RPN), from an FMEA is an example of a qualitative risk evaluation output and any action taken as a consequence is part of the risk control steps.

                    

It is important to consider control strategies that will address concerns in both IH (Industrial Hygiene) and GxP settings, and any control strategy used to maintain an acceptable level of risk for one setting should not add risk to the other.

                    

For example, a strategy that focuses on reducing exposure in the immediate workplace may have a negative, and perhaps unforeseen, impact on GxP issues. Worker exposure might be successfully reduced by the use of a totally closed system, close coupling of unit operations, or by the addition of redundancy in the form of gloveboxes. However, this may increase the risk of product retention and cross-contamination of subsequent products if the processing train is more difficult to clean.

                    

Once a strategy has been selected for one setting, a risk analysis of the other should be performed before accepting the control strategy. Similarly, before embarking on the implementation of risk reduction strategies, a qualitative risk assessment should be undertaken using risk ranking and FMEA tools or other appropriate tools or techniques.

                    

Once control strategies have been implemented, quantitative risk assessments should be made to demonstrate the performance capability of the system.

## Holistically Balanced Approach to Risk Control —

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

The fundamental objective for a risk management program is to ensure that exposure both of the patient through contamination of one product with another, and for a worker via direct contact/inhalation is maintained below the ADE value or other acceptable health-based limit, e.g., OEL for worker exposure and is visually clean, where appropriate.

                    

When working in multiproduct facilities, zero risk of cross-contamination of one product with another is neither realistic nor scientifically achievable. The degree to which such cross-contamination is controlled within the normal bounds of GxP should be commensurate with the risk identified to the patient, considering hazard, probability, and severity.

                    

Similar considerations should be applied within either multipurpose or dedicated facilities toward the potential exposure of workers to the compound concerned. In either case, the controls selected should be appropriate to the hazard as well as the nature and scale of the activity. These controls must ensure that safe levels are not exceeded and should be economically and technically feasible, and applied in both a holistic and balanced manner.

                    

In this context, holistic means that every aspect of product and process is considered, including formulation, equipment, and facility design and operation. All stakeholders should be considered when determining which risks exist and what measures are necessary to ensure they are adequately controlled. Each control measure implemented and the degree and way in which it acts as a control should be understood.

                    

Risk control measures should be determined by a multidisciplinary team and care should be taken to ensure that the risk to one set of stakeholders is not mitigated at the cost of exposing another stakeholder to an unacceptable risk. To achieve balance, understanding the individual aspects of control and the interactions between controls should be evaluated to meet multiple needs.

### 8.1.1 Operability (Function and Ergonomics)

                    

A holistic approach should take account of all the operational parameters involved when designing control strategies. Operability assessments should be conducted with the participation of workers and a full review of the operations required in setup, operation, intervention, disassembly, upset recovery, and cleaning.

                    

An ergonomic assessment should be conducted and should include evaluations, such as reach and force required to perform tasks. Ergonomics is the science of fitting the task to the person rather than the person to the task.

                    

### 8.1.2 Impact (Cost, Time, Disruption to Process)

                    

A cost benefit analysis should be undertaken to ensure that the benefits of a well-designed system are maximized. A lifetime cost analysis should be used to select the most appropriate solution. The time and disruption caused by the implementation should be taken into account in the evaluation.

                    

For example, a closed system may meet all the requirements for product and worker safety without dedication, but may have a very long time between campaigns due to the cleaning process. An evaluation on the impact may lead some manufacturers to dedicate some equipment for purely operational reasons.

                    

### 8.1.3 Minimize Over Engineering of Solutions

                    

One of the largest costs, and a significant impact on operability, is the over-design and over-specification of a solution. Complex engineering is much easier to achieve than a simple elegant, operational solution. The unnecessary complication of controls, features, etc., is common and can lead to increased product or worker exposure if tasks are too complicated.

                    

Workers may bypass the complex system and others, such as the cleaning personnel, may have an increased exposure potential. With proper use of risk management tools, the solutions should identify the areas requiring risk reduction, while indicating areas that are already within the acceptable risk range, and therefore, do not require further attention.

                    

Care should be taken in accepting vendor statements of performance such as *"Equipment X performs to Y OEL"*, as the statement is incomplete without additional data such as duration of task. The "keep it simple principle" should be applied.

## 9 Risk Reduction

            

ICH Q9 states that risk reduction is the implementation of control strategies to mitigate or avoid the risk. Risk reduction can be achieved by addressing the severity and/or probability of harm as well as provide improvements in the detection of the hazards.

            

Where the results of a risk assessment have identified the need for control strategies to reduce the risk of exposure to a safe level, a hierarchy for risk reduction should be applied to prioritize all significant risks. The hierarchy is listed in priority order and should be considered and applied in this order.

            

Each level of the hierarchy should be applied in a way that is appropriate to the activity and consistent with the risk assessment, and each should be implemented only after consideration of the preceding level. A holistic view of the product, process, and facilities may lead to a strategy that includes partial mitigation at several levels in the hierarchy before overall risk is reduced to acceptable levels.

#### 9.1.1 Elimination

            

Completely eliminates the risk. Since the hazard is a constant based on the compound, completely eliminating the risk is not practical.

            

Elimination of potential points of exposure (process steps and/or transfers) should be considered as a method to eliminate or reduce the risk. If an exposure point can be eliminated, then the risk associated with it is removed.

            

#### 9.1.2 Substitution

            

Substituting the hazardous material with a less hazardous material is also not practical since the hazardous material is the compound. Substitution of the process or formulation should be considered as a method to eliminate or reduce risk.

            

In the pharmaceutical industry, existing product registrations may limit the feasibility of substitution options. Addressing these issues early in development prior to fixing formulations or processes is recommended.

            

#### 9.1.3 Reduction

            

Limit material handling and reduce the potential for exposure through the use of engineering controls.

            

In this context, engineering controls can include the use of high integrity closed processing equipment, as well as additional containment devices such as extract-based systems and isolators. When employing these technologies, the primary concern — either worker protection or cross-contamination — should be made clear. When holistically applied, it is possible to address both aspects.

## 9.2 Risk Reduction: Routes of Exposure

            

Risk reduction strategies for each of the routes for cross-contamination are discussed.

            

#### 9.2.1 Mix-up

            

Procedures, protocols, physical controls, and redundant monitoring can help reduce mix-up. Methods that ensure detection of mix-ups are necessary, as it is unlikely that mix-up can be eliminated totally. Techniques to reduce the risk of mix-up include:

            

                
- Provision of redundant electronic verification of materials

                
- Color coding

                
- Bar coding and reading of materials, equipment, etc.

                
- Clear labeling of room status (in use/product present) as well as of used equipment (cleanliness, contaminant present etc.)

                
- Removal of unused API and other materials from plant local stores at the end of a campaign

                
- Using locked cages to store APIs with careful chain of custodian, control of the key

                
- Radio Frequency Identification (RFID)

                
- Separate redundant manual checks

                
- Selecting transfer and utility connect technologies that are unique to the process

                
- PAT and NIR verifications

                
- Label creation at point of application

                
- Physical separation of high risk products/materials

            

            

It is the manufacturer's responsibility to develop and demonstrate a well thought out and executed strategy for the prevention of mix-ups that takes full account of human fallibility.

#### 9.2.2 Retention

            

The development of a comprehensive cleaning program based on science, risk assessment, and statistical analysis will provide the greatest level of safety for the patient. Equipment should be designed for ease of cleaning and for the confirmation of the effectiveness of cleaning. Techniques to reduce the risk of retention include:

- Cleaning process design (avoid damaging highly polished product contact surfaces)

                
- Certified visual inspection program

                
- Overall cleaning program risk assessment

                
- PAT (Process Analytical Technology)

                
- On-line and at-line monitoring

                
- Process capability evaluations of swab sample data

                
- Use single use disposable product contact parts

#### 9.2.2.1 Cleaning Validation Program

            

A site's cleaning validation program is often the most visible and audited part of its overall cross-contamination control strategy. In order to be both effective and compliant, a cleaning process validation must:

            

                
- Be based on a science and risk-based understanding of the hazards

                
- Be executed using already qualified equipment systems (water or CIP)

                
- Be performed following Quality Unit preapproved protocols

                
- Include multiple test repetitions to demonstrate consistency

            

            

*Note: unlike manufacturing process validation, there is no current expectation to formally justify the number of validation runs — a three-run cleaning validation is still typical and generally acceptable. Science and process knowledge should always be the first consideration.*

            

#### 9.2.2.2 Continued Process Verification (CPV) for Cleaning

            

CPV plans for cleaning processes should include both aspects of acceptability: analytical measurement of residuals against a predetermined health-based safety limit and the visual observations of personnel inspecting cleaned equipment for any visible residue.

            

Analytical Criteria: where acceptance limits are well above visual detection and the cleaning process is robust, analytical sampling may be reduced as justified by risk assessments. Equally where the acceptance limits are well below visual detection — especially for manual cleaning processes — analytical sampling may be increased.

#### 9.2.3 Mechanical Transfer

            

Techniques to reduce the risk of mechanical transfer include:

            

                
- Providing a boundary so that mechanical transfer is minimized (see Section 9.5)

                
- Gown/glove change at the product boundary; use of material and personnel airlocks and decontamination procedures

                
- Workers should be gowned to protect the person, personal clothing, and the product from potential contamination

                
- Gloves should be worn at all times when in the product boundary; a second pair should be worn during critical operations such as connecting/disconnecting transfer systems and cleaning

                
- Removing critical gowning items at a defined point using a technique which avoids cross-contamination

                
- Providing flow routes that keep clean and dirty equipment, people, materials, separate

                
- Using airlocks for personnel, materials, or both to segregate work areas

                
- Wiping or cleaning of the exterior of material containers when removing from production area

                
- Physically separate rooms to separate products and processes

                
- Using "clean" construction to facilitate removal of potential contaminants from surfaces

                
- Telephones and similar devices requiring manual activation should be replaced by hands-free or touch pad devices

                
- Providing electronic means to pass information and data between the room/suite and the GxP area

            

            

#### 9.2.4 Airborne Transfer

            

Techniques to reduce the risk of airborne transfer include:

            

                
- Closed process systems

                
- Engineering controls to reduce the degree of openness of a process enclosure

                
- HVAC arrangement to flush critical zones with clean air and move airborne product away from room entry, operator space, or other potential vectors for cross-contamination

                
- Airlocks, single and double, with or without positive pressure cascades

                
- Gowning/decontamination of people and materials passing in and out

                
- Use of HVAC and pressure regimens to minimize airborne transfer to protect workers and product

                
- Use of appropriate filtration to capture airborne particulate and prevent recirculation

            

            

*Note: many of the techniques are similar to those listed to reduce mechanical transfer.*

            

Wipe samples taken outside the GxP area may show contamination due to failures of gowning procedures or even mechanical transfer on paperwork. The presence of drug substance at or near the limits for non-product contact is an indication of a failed system and careful assessment of all aspects of the hierarchy of risk control is essential to remediate.

## 9.3 The Role of Pharmaceutical R&D in Risk Reduction

            

                ICH Q8(R2): "The aim of pharmaceutical development is to design a quality product and its manufacturing process to consistently deliver the intended performance of the product… quality should be built in by design."
            

            

Opportunities to reduce or eliminate exposure should be considered as early as possible in the development of the product and the process.

            

The toxicological properties of a compound can be assessed early in the development process. An Occupational Exposure Band (OEB) is likely to have been assigned and it is possible to estimate an ADE from the OEB. This information may help influence formulation design at an early phase of development.

            

Priorities for a formulation scientist include: Bioavailability, Stability, Ability to manufacture. Physical form and particle size of the active drug substance together with the choice of excipients are considered.

            

#### Direct Compression / Granulation

            

Direct compression formulations can be employed to minimize cross-contamination and worker exposure by combining the active ingredient with suitable excipients. The formulator may consider selecting excipients that generate very little dust, such as silicified microcrystalline cellulose or mannitol. Processing steps can be significantly reduced — dispensing, blending, and compression — eliminating fluid bed drying and dry milling.

            

A well-designed granulation process should offer similar benefits. A granulating solvent can dampen the API and reduce the risk of aerosols. Single pot granulation systems allow granulation and drying in the same vessel, reducing transfer exposure potential.

            

#### Coating and Encapsulation

            

Coating the solid dosage form can significantly reduce the risk of cross-contamination. A film or sugar coating provides a significant barrier, protecting the active drug and reducing exposure risk in downstream operations such as packaging. It minimizes risks to downstream handlers — pharmacists, nurses, and patients.

            

Encapsulation allows powders to be filled directly into a shell. Capsules can be sealed, over-encapsulated, or film coated. Encapsulation can be a dusty process and close attention should be given to dedusting.

            

#### Liquid / Semi-solid Fill

            

Formulating the active substance in a liquid or semi-solid vehicle and filling into a capsule (hard or soft shell) is a particularly useful approach for high hazard compounds, as it removes any powder handling stages once the compound is solubilized.

            

Overall, early containment of an active drug substance via formulation — especially for more hazardous materials — may bring long-term handling advantages and risk reduction. There may also be cost benefit from reducing the need for specialized containment technology, dedicated equipment, or dedicated rooms.

            

Once formulations and processes are registered, it can be very costly and time consuming to make changes. Formulation scientists should pay close attention to opportunities to reduce risk throughout the entire manufacturing and supply chain, as early as possible in development.

## 9.4 Process and Related Technologies

            

Properly designed and operated processes, equipment, and other related technologies can reduce the risk of cross-contamination from mix-up, retention, mechanical transfer, and airborne transfer. During process and equipment design, the reduction of one set of risks (e.g., IH) should not negatively impact the risks to the other areas (e.g., GxP).

            

For most formulations, the only stage of the process in which the API is handled in its pure form is during dispensing, API sampling, and API addition to the first process; therefore, these operations require close attention. After blend uniformity or final mix, the risk increases because the acceptable limit at single dosage can be much lower than the ADE.

            

API addition in liquid or slurry form should result in far lower exposure potential than dry powder addition, unless there is a danger of aerosol formation on addition of liquid under pressure, or the solvent base is conducive to transdermal migration.

            

Integration of unit processes: systems that integrate a number of process steps, such as "one-pot" processors (granulator/dryers), reduce the need for transfers between unit operations.

            

Use of closed process: equipment vendors may claim a process is "closed," but the detail of such claims should be fully evaluated. Will there be a need for any process intervention?

            

                
- Manual scrape down

                
- Performing process corrections

                
- Changing contaminated or blocked parts such as mill screens

                
- Extract samples

                
- Determine process end points

                
- Perform cleaning

                
- Upset recovery

            

            

The impact of interventions on the potential for exposure should be evaluated. In-line measuring devices or automated devices to scrape down or clear blinded mill screens should be considered.

            

Selection of process equipment that reduces exposure potential: hammer mills can result in far higher exposure potential than cone mills. Charging a bin blender can provide lower risk through close coupling than charging a V blender.

            

Selection of process equipment to reduce retention: minimize surface area; eliminate ledges and crevices; reduce changeover frequency; use polished product contact surfaces.

            

#### 9.4.1 Redundancy

            

Redundancy is the provision of more than one element performing the function so that if one fails, the next takes up the required duty and detection devices alarm the failure of the primary system.

            

If a system is dependent on a single critical interface, it is likely to fail; when redundancy is introduced, the likelihood of failure diminishes. A robust system can be designed based on a series of non-optimal redundancies.

## 9.5 The Role of Facility Engineering in Risk Reduction

            

A properly designed and operated facility can reduce the risk of cross-contamination from mix-up, mechanical transfer, and airborne transfer. Proper flow of workers, product, equipment, materials, and wastes within a facility can have a significant impact on maintaining risk of cross-contamination at or below acceptable limits.

            

#### 9.5.1 Spatial Configuration

            

In planning a facility, the relationship of areas should be carefully considered to minimize the risk of cross-contamination. Individual ISPE Baseline® Guides address this subject specifically for the type of facility being reviewed, such as those considering API and Oral Solid Dosage (OSD).

            

#### 9.5.2 The Control Concept

            

Regardless of the hazard of a compound, it should be handled in such a way that the compound remains within the room or suite in which it is processed. The compound should not be detectable above acceptable limits outside the areas directly involved in processing the product.

            

#### 9.5.2.1 Hierarchy of Boundaries

            

There are a number of boundaries that contain the pharmaceutical compound (Figure 9.4🔍):

            

                
- Process Boundary: the process equipment and the interface through which it is charged, discharged, sampled, intervened, and recovered. Can vary from closed to open.

                
- Room or Suite Boundary: the room or suite in which one product is processed on a batch or campaign basis. May be product dedicated. Must contain the product at all times so that the common GxP corridor is never exposed to unsafe concentrations. May have material and personnel airlocks.

                
- GxP Boundary: the boundary between the outside world and the pharmaceutical environment — keeps outside contamination out and pharmaceutical materials in.

            

            

**Facility Zones (Figure 9.5🔍):**

            

                
1. Office, administrative, cafeteria, corridors — outside scope; product should never be in these areas; street clothes worn.

                
2. Warehousing for product-related goods (GxP areas). Raw API and intermediates should be segregated.

                
3. Controlled areas for gowning/material transfer — transitioning between outside and core GxP zones.

                
4. GxP corridors — flow routes; should not be product contaminated. Visible residues are a cause for concern.

                
5. Product rooms — single product campaign; process rooms are the final product boundary. Fully closed systems may allow more than one product per room.

                
6. Laboratories — restrict access; prevent contamination and worker exposure.

                
7. Technical space — utilities; adequate safeguards for dust collection, air handling filter changes.

- Single door to corridor: positive or negative pressure; positive pressure rooms place corridor at risk.

                
- Single stage airlock: significant improvement in containment; may be over or under engineered.

                
- Double stage airlocks: allows clean gowning and de-gowning in separate rooms; overcomes airborne and mechanical transfer.

## 9.5.3 Material and People Flows

            

The design of facilities to provide logical flow routes is complex. One common design is to segregate "clean" and "dirty" corridors. In this ideal scenario, only "clean" personnel, equipment, and product that is robustly contained and has acceptable surface concentrations can pass down the "clean" corridor, and only potentially contaminated personnel, equipment, and product pass down the "dirty" corridor.

            

Application of the product boundary decontamination concept for personnel, equipment, and product removes the need for segregated corridors.

            

Minimizing personnel and material flows is considered good practice, and for reasons outside the scope of this Guide, segregation of people and material movement can be required for safety and security reasons.

            

#### 9.5.3.1 Material and Personnel Air Locks (MAL / PAL)

            

Airlocks may have several purposes, including:

            

                
- Prevent mechanical transfer of product from the product boundary into the GxP corridor

                
- Provide a means to maintain pressure cascades

                
- Demonstrate entry into a controlled area

                
- Prevent contamination of the facility and product by external contaminants

                
- Provide an area for gowning/de-gowning

                
- Provide an area for operator to don controlled-use gowning to prevent mechanical transfer

            

            

A key element of any MAL or PAL is that there is a clean side and a dirty side with a physical barrier where the two sides meet.

            

A MAL requires three zones to minimize cross-contamination:

            

                
1. **Clean**

                
2. **Transit**

                
3. **Dirty**

            

            

Clean side personnel place material in the designated transit zone. Dirty side personnel collect materials without entering the transit zone. Decontamination takes place in the dirty zone. Personnel should not transit through a MAL because of the risk of mechanical transfer from contaminated clothing.

            

Airlocks are frequently operated with a positive differential pressure to adjacent rooms or corridors to prevent the transit of airborne contamination between these areas. Sticky mats should be used to further prevent mechanical transfer out of the airlocks.

## 9.5.6 Dedicated Principles

            

One route to prevent retention is to dedicate equipment; this can vary from the dedication of an entire process train to individual pieces of equipment that may be dedicated because of size and complexity.

### 9.6 HVAC and Environmental Control

                    

The key requirement of life science HVAC is to assure a suitable environment for the product and process with regard to temperature, humidity, and most importantly, airborne particulate, molecular, and viable cleanliness. This requirement is in addition to the usual industrial HVAC requirements of making workers comfortable and protecting them from hazardous airborne fumes, vapors, aerosols, and dust.

                    

Often, the need to control hazardous compounds complicates environmental control as cleanliness and containment may require conflicting pressurization schemes.

                    

As product potency increases, the need for control of airborne product/API also increases. The effectiveness of HVAC related contamination controls improve as proximity to the product increases. Three levels of increasing proximity are considered:

                    

                        
- **Room Level HVAC** — dilutes airborne contaminants by entrainment in air streams, extracted from the room. Susceptible to behavior, technique, and operational failures.

                        
- **Local Ventilation** — "near field" effect by placing exhaust and/or supply close to emission sources. Higher particulate concentration at source enables more efficient filtration or exhaust disposal.

                        
- **Equipment Level Ventilation** — e.g., isolator, RABS, vented balance enclosure, or closed equipment. Combines physical barriers with HVAC to control particulate buildup within the enclosure. Closing a process reduces the size of the critical environment, making the surrounding room a support area.

                    

                    

This triumvirate of control technologies can provide significant safety and product protection benefits, individually and in concert. They can safely handle highly hazardous materials with very low OELs.

                    

At HVAC equipment level, removal of undesirable airborne contaminants is achieved through exhaust (disposal) or depth filtration. Filters also serve as an enhanced boundary protecting against unintended backflow or migration of API dust through duct systems upon loss of HVAC. Terminal filters at air inlets and outlets of processing rooms are therefore common.

### 9.6.1 Once-Through versus Recirculation

                    

Recirculated HVAC systems offer energy savings and operational cost benefits over once-through systems. The air recirculated from manufacturing areas is often cleaner and much closer to the desired temperature and humidity conditions than fresh air.

                    

However, recirculation of air from powder handling spaces, especially in multiproduct concurrent manufacturing facilities, can convey airborne product particles to other areas and may maintain aerosols in suspension, depending on particle size and room air patterns.

                    

Regulations suggest that if air is to be recirculated, when potentially contaminated with airborne product particles, it should be adequately treated. The definition of adequate treatment is amenable to a risk-based approach, as described in Section 9.6.2.

### 9.6.2 Quantitating the Risk of Airborne Cross-Contamination

                    

*(Advanced Topic)*

                    

Control of cross-contamination is primarily a matter of understanding the mass of "product A" which might be carried into "product B". For contaminants spread through the air, the process involves:

                    

                        
- Understanding the potential maximum mass of airborne product that may be produced

                        
- Determining how much of this mass could be delivered to other spaces

                        
- Evaluating upset cases (e.g., spills) and potential failures of abatement equipment (failure sensitivity evaluation)

                    

                    

The method is similar to the approach used by Industrial Hygienists for worker safety: the actual quantity of product aerosolized during a working day is measured, and correction factors for PPE are applied.

                    

A quantitative evaluation provides:

                    

                        
- More robust understanding of contamination risks and appropriate controls

                        
- Verification measures through facility life

                        
- Data for investigations, event response planning, and critical management tasks

                        
- Assurance that resources are commensurate with risk — neither inadequate nor wasteful

                    

                    

### Contamination Sources and Potency

                    

The potency of the product and the potential maximum concentration of airborne product drive the need for more rigorous abatement. The highest particle concentration air streams — such as from dust collectors, especially when handling potent products — require the most stringent abatement and may not be candidates for recirculation.

                    

Results from Environmental Health and Safety studies are generally expressed in **µg/m³**, which couples well with the quantitative method outlined here. To determine appropriate filtration, fractional removal efficiencies across twelve particle size ranges from standards such as ANSI/ASHRAE 52.2, EN 1822, EN 779, or ISO 29463-1 are used.

                    

### Filter Efficiency Examples

                    

Table 9.2/9.3/9.4 (source tables) demonstrate mass reduction through filtration stages:

                    

A properly installed and maintained MERV 15 (~95% ASHRAE) filter can provide a 3 log reduction in contaminants. A MERV 11 (~50% ASHRAE) gives a 1.5 log (20:1) reduction.

                    

### Large Particles — Greater Cross-Contamination Risk

                    

While small particles (<10 µm) are of interest in worker safety (greater respirability), large particles (>10 µm) pose greater cross-contamination risk for two reasons:

                    

                        
1. In any heterogeneous particle dispersion, the greatest mass resides in large particles. A 10 µ particle is ~10,000 times heavier than a 0.5 µ particle and 1,000,000 times heavier than a 0.1 µ particle. At example specific gravity, it takes 15,000,000 of 0.5 µ particles to deliver 1 µg; only 2,000 of 10 µ particles are needed.

                        
2. Lower buoyancy of large particles (higher settling rate) makes them more likely to contaminate product by falling out of an airstream as velocity decreases in a production room.

                    

                    

This is good news for filtration: even moderate efficiency filters are quite effective at removing large particles.

                    

### Failure Analysis Considerations

                    

Analysis must always consider failure causes including:

                    

                        
1. Filter and/or frame leakage

                        
2. Spills and other upsets which cause an excess of airborne API

### 9.6.3 Directional Airflow and Pressure Regimens

                    

In order to control the migration of product aerosols from one room or area to another, it is important to manage the airflow between spaces. Two approaches are possible:

                    

**Approach 1 — High Mass Airflow Entrainment:** Entrain the aerosol in a large mass of air, typically moving at 0.2 m/s (40 fpm) or greater. Typically used in open downflow booths and similar devices, but not preferred for rooms due to large and rapid changes in airflow when doors are opened or closed (typically 400 m³/h (800 cfm)).

                    

**Approach 2 — Controlled Direction with Adequate Velocity (preferred):** Control the direction of airflow and assure adequate velocity to transport particles, typically in the range of 0.5 to 1.0 m/s (100 to 200 fpm). Two sub-approaches:

                    

                        
- Maintain a fixed offset between air entering and leaving via the HVAC system (fixed balance or airflow tracking)

                        
- Measure and control room pressurization, typically in steps of 10 to 15 Pa (0.4 to 0.6 in. W.G.), but no lower than 5 Pa (0.02 in. W.G.). High-pressure difference across doors results in high velocities at leak points, on the order of 3 to 3.5 m/s (600 to 700 fpm).

                    

                    

### Pressure Cascade Design Principles

                    

The pressure cascade and direction of airflow should be designed to be protective of product, personnel, and environment:

                    

                        
- To prevent environmental contaminants from entering: production area positive with respect to surrounding environment

                        
- However, a positive manufacturing space can spread product aerosols to other products and personnel; therefore, open product handling areas should be negative with respect to other GxP areas (e.g., the GxP corridor)

                    

                    

### Airlock Configurations

                    

Single door configurations between production room and corridor are challenged when the door is open. For greater protection, airlocks with interlocked doors are used. Three configurations (Figure 9.7🔍):

                    

                        
- **Simple Pressure Cascade:** Corridor (positive) → Production (negative). Effective for API containment.

                        
- **Sink-type Airlock:** Process room positive to airlock AND corridor positive to airlock. Airlock is the lowest pressure (sink).

                        
- **Bubble-type Airlock:** Airlock is positive with respect to both corridor and process room. Testing has shown *greater emission potential* with this configuration — generally not preferred for hazardous compounds.

### 9.7 Containment Related Technologies

                    

Containment related technologies are typically used to make a process step, or a process a closed system, keeping the product and the room environment separate. Employing containment related technologies can reduce the risk of cross-contamination by airborne or mechanical transfer routes.

                    

### 9.7.1 Selection of Appropriate Containment Systems

                    

The equipment selection process should result in individual equipment items or systems identified which give high confidence that the design target/performance expectation will be met. Success depends on an early understanding of the full range of activities, including normal operation, sampling, and cleaning, shared with the full team including supplier(s) and integrators.

                    

Planned and breakdown maintenance activities should be discussed at an early stage. Requirements for activities such as sampling should be questioned, as they may present significant difficulties in effective containment design and may present an exposure risk that outweighs other benefits.

                    

### Information Required for Successful Equipment Selection

                    

                        
- Detail of all activities to be undertaken including normal operation, sampling, and cleaning

                        
- Appreciation of the rate of movement of particles or the energy with which they may be released

                        
- Variety of containers in and out of the equipment

                        
- Way waste and contaminated equipment will be removed

                        
- Need for aids for heavy, awkward, or restricted space lifting

                        
- Operators' understanding of risks and how behavior affects containment performance — behavior has the greatest impact on local extract and enclosures and least impact on closed systems and isolators

                        
- Ergonomics of activities, especially repetitive actions, access requirements, working position, load characteristics, and operator stature range

                        
- Knowledge of containment or emission performance of equipment in similar applications

                        
- How containment performance may change with time due to ineffective cleaning and/or wear

                        
- Understanding of the problem by the design team

                        
- Potential for powder to ignite or support a dust cloud explosion — contained systems have smaller volumes and low air change rates creating risk of explosive concentrations; nitrogen inerting may be required

                        
- To dissipate electrostatic buildup, all metal parts should be grounded (earthed), including elements docked and insulated by plastic wear elements

## Personal Protective Equipment and Gowning

### 9.8 Personal Protective Equipment and Gowning

                    

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) refers to gowns, gloves, booties, hair covers, hoods, respiratory protection, face masks, etc.

                    

Respiratory Protective Equipment (RPE) covers devices designed only to protect the mouth and nose.

                    

The distinction is important because PPE, without RPE, is recommended for protecting the product from contamination and cross-contamination as well as for dermal protection for the operators at all times.

                    

Whenever RPE is used, it is important to consider its use, storage, maintenance, and decontamination, and whether or not it could lead to a risk of cross-contamination from mechanical transfer.

                    

### PPE is Normally Worn to:

                    

                        
- Help limit mechanical transfer of materials

                        
- Provide appropriate outer clothing to protect the product, e.g., made of non-shedding material

                        
- Provide a psychological and physical indication of transfer from the outside environment into a GxP facility where specific procedures, rules of conduct, and action apply

                        
- Provide dermal and chemical protection to the operator so that any drug exposure related effects are minimized

                    

                    

### Risk Acceptance Requirement (Section 10 cross-reference)

                    

Where risk reduction strategies (including PPE, containment, HVAC) are implemented, the manufacturer must demonstrate to all stakeholders that strategies are effective in maintaining cross-contamination and worker exposure at or below acceptable limits. This requires demonstration that:

                    

                        
- Procedures and detection devices are in place to minimize mix-up potential even in accident or error scenarios

                        
- Any residues on product contact and non-product contact surfaces fall within health-based limits, in support of cleaning processes

                        
- Flow of personnel, product, material, and HVAC are designed to minimize potential for mechanical transfer

                        
- Any product that may escape the process is maintained below levels that could impact cross-contamination and worker safety

### 11.1 Risk Ranking or Risk Matrix

                    

Qualitative risk assessments may be performed at manufacturing sites for the purpose of determining which unit operations pose the greatest risk or the greatest opportunity. A risk ranking or risk matrix provides a very simple high level objective process to consistently evaluate all the unit operations at a manufacturing site and rank the operations by risk.

                    

To apply the risk ranking, all the unit operations at a site are identified. To complete the ranking, the following parameters should be considered:

                    

                        
- Health Effects: ADE value, residue levels for cleaning, inactivation, etc.

                        
- Product Considerations: percent active in batch, batch size, product form (impact on API becoming airborne), maximum daily dosage

                        
- Operational Considerations: number of employees involved, frequency of the process, energy input, open or closed processing, surface area of product contact parts, batches per year, relative positions of source and at-risk materials, duration of process

                    

                    

For each parameter a numerical value, or high, medium, and low indicator is assigned, and for each unit operation, the scores are totaled. After all unit operations have been assessed, the risk ranking scores can be compared and activities prioritized.

                    

### Unit Operation Risk Ranking — Airborne Concentration Examples

                    

                        
- **High (H):** sampling, weighing and dispensing, sample testing (analytical), charging, milling, granulation, extruding (cleaning), compounding

                        
- **Medium (M):** mixing, blending, extruding, compression, capsule filling

                        
- **Low (L):** coating, charging, drilling, printing, packaging

                        
- **Negligible (N):** membrane coating, discharging cores, drying, pouching, gel dispensing, curing, assembling, inspection

### 11.3 Failure Mode and Effects Analysis

                    

Many pharmaceutical companies use FMEA techniques for risk ranking and have standard protocols for its use. It is used in Six Sigma evaluations and can be used for a wide range of risk assessments.

                    

FMEA was originally developed by the US Military in the 1940s and further developed by the automotive and aerospace industries. Where companies do not have a formally documented system, it is recommended that they use a recognized system such as in IEC 60812.

### 12 Risk Review

                    

ICH Q9 states risk management should be an ongoing part of the quality management process. A mechanism to review internal processes and external events should be implemented.

                    

Periodic reviews should be performed on all aspects of the risk management system to ensure that it is being applied effectively and that any potential improvements are identified and incorporated into the process.

### 13 Risk Communication

                    

ICH Q9 defines risk communication as the sharing of information about risk and risk management between the decision makers and others. Organizations should document, within their quality management systems, *who needs to communicate what, to whom, and at what frequency*, in support of a risk management process.

                    

Different levels of information will be appropriate to different stakeholders who may be internal or external to the organization. It is important to have an escalation process that allows management to be informed of areas that have risk that is unacceptable or near unacceptable.

                    

The holistic approach described in this Guide is predicated on the fact that all of those involved have received appropriate education or training and have ready access to supporting risk assessment and management documentation.

                    

Risk Communication can be enhanced by incorporating linkages within the change control, product quality review and CAPA quality systems.

                    

It is important that companies have a means to widely and effectively communicate to all parties that might be affected including company manufacturing sites, contract manufacturers and QA departments any identified amendments to ADEs (and OELs) or other items that may affect the risk assessment.

                    

In addition if a company chooses to have product-specific ADEs (where the following product is known) or ADEs for specific routes of exposure or dosage types (such as OSD or parenteral) rather than address these issues in the risk assessment, the company should have a robust process to manage these values and make clear to all that may depend on these values what the criteria used to determine the ADEs are and how they should be modified for other usage.

### Risk Assessment — Scenario 2

                

#### Establishment of ADE for Anti-neoplastic Agent C

                

Anti-neoplastic Agent C is an alkylating agent used in the treatment of numerous myeloproliferative and lymphoproliferative disorders and solid malignancies. It disrupts nucleic acid function by interfering with DNA replication and transcription of RNA. The recommended oral dosage ranges from 1 to 5 mg/kg/day for adults or 10 to 15 mg/kg IV every 7 to 10 days.

                

Anti-neoplastic Agent C is considered moderately toxic orally and produced adverse developmental effects in animals at dosages as low as **2.5 mg/kg/day**. It was shown to damage DNA in spermatozoa from adult male rats and increased the incidence of malignant tumors by several routes of administration. The compound has been shown to be a human carcinogen.

                

The critical endpoint for deriving a health-based value is **cancer**. Because it is a direct acting genotoxic material, the ADE was derived by extrapolating cancer risks from animal and human tumor data. An ADE of 1 μg/day was calculated corresponding to a **1 × 10⁻⁵ excess cancer risk**.

                

#### Risk Analysis — Plausible Pathways

                

**Mix-up:** Bar code reading control of all equipment and materials; separation of clean and dirty equipment by room; tagging of clean and dirty equipment.

                

**Retention on Product Contact Surfaces:** Swab monitoring of cleaning performance against health-based limits; visually clean inspection; routine monitoring shows consistent performance well below the health-based limit — except where the FMEA identified the blender issue.

                

**Mechanical Transfer:** Critical transfers are closed; all equipment and materials wiped down or bagged before leaving the process room; tack mats used at room boundaries.

                

**Airborne Transfer:** Processes occur in rooms protected by airlocks; corridor positive relative to process rooms; redundant HEPA filtration on exhausts.

- Cleanability assessments

                    
- Cleaning agent(s) selection

                    
- Design of cleaning processes

                    
- FMEAs for each cleaning process

                    
- Modification of cleaning processes based on FMEA results

                

                

Cleaning Risk Evaluation activities:

                

                    
- Collection of swab sample data on each cleaning procedure

                    
- Process capability evaluations of swab sample data

                    
- FMEAs of overall cleaning program to identify high risk procedures

                    
- Improvement/optimization of high risk procedures

The cleanability rank was a **2**, with a rank of 4 for the hardest to clean product. Current cleaning agents were found to be appropriate for solubilizing and suspending the product.

### Risk Evaluation — The Critical Finding

                

#### New Product Review

                

The new anti-neoplastic product was evaluated against the existing program for its compatibility with the cleaning processes and its ADE was assessed against the existing swab sampling database for the facility.

                

The FMEA analysis of cleaning showed a significant risk occurring in the V-blender which, due to its significant surface area, required a level of residue significantly below the historically achievable results.

                

#### Cleaning Risk Evaluation — Ppk Analysis

                

The swab data for all cleaning processes were found to have high Process Performance Indices (Ppk) with undetectable levels Defects per Million Opportunities (ppm) — with the exception of the blender.

                

The Ppk for the blender was 0.34 with the potential for Defects per Million Opportunities of 14,994. This evaluation indicated that the current cleaning process for the blender is not satisfactory to safely clean the new product.

                

In order to introduce the new product into the facility, the cleaning process for the blender would need to be improved or a new blender would have to be purchased.

                

#### Risk Control

                

A conclusion was made that the risks to patients with the existing manufacturing process was unacceptably high. This was due to the potential carryover of residuals of Anti-neoplastic Agent C to other products from the V-blender.

                

A Design of Experiment (DoE) was performed to improve the Blender Cleaning Process. Cleaning agent concentration, cleaning time, cleaning temperature, and rinse time were evaluated. The new data indicate that **the blender cannot be effectively cleaned to safe levels for the new product**. The introduction of a dedicated blender was not possible and so the proposal was abandoned and the anti-neoplastic will not be manufactured at this site.

                

#### Risk Communication

                

No further communication on the proposed introduction of Anti-neoplastic Agent C is required because the proposal was rejected.

## Appendix 3 — Definitions

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 16.2 Definitions of Terms

                    

As used in this Guide, derived from multiple sources: FDA, other ISPE documents, ICH, EFPIA Resource Document (Assessing Facilities Needs, 14 November 2005), EU EudraLex Volume 4, and various WHO documents.

                    

Acceptable Daily Exposure (ADE)

                    

A dose that is unlikely to cause an adverse effect if an individual is exposed, by any route, at or below this dose every day for a lifetime. By definition the ADE is intended to be protective of all subpopulations and by all routes of administration. Establishment of the ADE value is an important first step in a risk assessment and the inherent assumptions should be consistent with how it is applied within the product mix in a given facility.

                    

Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API)

                    

Any substance, or mixture of substances intended to be used in the manufacture of a drug (medicinal) product and that, when used in the production of a drug, becomes an active ingredient of the drug product. Such substances are intended to furnish pharmacological activity or other direct effect in the diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease, or to affect the structure and function of the body.

                    

Antineoplastic (see Cytostatic and Cytotoxic)

                    

Chemotherapeutic agent that down regulates growth or kills cancer cells. May be classified as cytotoxic or cytostatic.

                    

Aerosol

                    

Suspension of fine solid or liquid particles (e.g., dust, fiber, fog, fume, mist, smoke) in a gas (i.e., typically in air).

                    

Barrier

                    

Facility/room/area, process, equipment, or isolation devices (e.g., glovebox) designed to achieve API or product containment.

                    

Beta-lactam Antibiotics

                    

Penicillins, cephalosporins, monobactams, and carbapenems, other APIs containing beta-lactam rings, beta-lactamase inhibitors with beta-lactam rings (e.g., clavulanic acid, and sulbactam and tazobactam).

                    

Bulk

                    

Any product that has completed all processing stages up to, but not including, final packaging.

                    

Campaign

                    

The manufacture of more than one product in a facility, with strict adherence to accepted cleaning procedures between these products. The products may be run in the same equipment, but not at the same time.

                    

Carry Over

                    

The residue or accumulated product on product contact surfaces that is retained after cleaning and may be available to enter the next product processed with the same product contact surfaces (i.e. it is carried-over to the next product in production). See definition of Retention.

                    

Clean Zone

                    

An area with defined environmental control of particulate and microbial contamination, constructed and used in such a way as to reduce the introduction, generation, and retention of contaminants within the area.

                    

Closed Process

                    

A process step (or system) where the product is not exposed to the immediate room environment (and vice versa). It is the manufacturer's responsibility to define and prove closure for a process step.

                    

Contained Area

                    

Specific manufacturing space constructed and equipped with appropriate air handling and filtration and operated to prevent contamination of the external work environment.

                    

Containment

                    

State achieved by enclosures with separation between manufacturing environment and manufacturing operation. The action of confining a biological agent or other entity within a defined space.

                    

Contaminant

                    

Any particulate, molecular, non-particulate, or biological entity that can adversely affect the product or process.

                    

Contamination

                    

The undesired introduction of impurities of a chemical or microbiological nature, or of foreign matter, into or onto a raw material, intermediate, or API during production, sampling, packaging or repackaging, storage, or transport.

                    

Controlled Area

                    

Facility space with restricted access and operated to restrict introduction of potential contamination, consequences of accidental release of airborne contaminants.

                    

Cross-Contamination

                    

Contamination of a starting material, intermediate product or finished product with another starting material or product during production.

                    

Cytostatic (see Antineoplastic)

                    

Chemotherapeutic agent capable of impairing cell proliferation.

                    

Cytotoxic (see Antineoplastic)

                    

Chemotherapeutic agent intended to control or kill cancer cells, which may act as an indiscriminate poison preventing cell reproduction or growth and causing death of cancerous and potentially healthy cells.

                    

Decontamination

                    

Process that reduces, detoxifies, and/or eliminates contaminated substances to a predetermined acceptance level.

                    

Dedicated Area

                    

Separate area, which may comprise one or more rooms within a multipurpose facility with its own air locked personnel/material accesses, dedicated to a specific product or product range.

                    

Dedicated Building/Facility

                    

Physically separate or segregated building with its own personnel/material accesses and HVAC systems, dedicated to process steps associated with a specific product or product.

                    

Dedicated Change Part

                    

An equipment change part (e.g., sieve, filter, etc.) that is dedicated to a specific product or product range, and is identified accordingly.

                    

Dedicated Equipment

                    

Equipment that is dedicated to a specific product or product range, and is identified accordingly.

                    

Dedicated Suite

                    

One or more rooms within a multipurpose facility or dedicated area that is dedicated to a specific product or product range, and is identified accordingly.

                    

Detectability

                    

Ability to discover or determine the existence, presence, or fact of a hazard.

                    

Drug (a.k.a., API)

                    

Chemical compound that may be used on, or administered to, humans as an aid in the diagnosis, treatment, cure, mitigation, or prevention of disease or other abnormal conditions.

                    

Drug Product

                    

A finished dosage form, for example, tablet, capsule, solution, etc., that contains one or more APIs generally, but not necessarily, in association with inactive ingredients. The term also includes a finished dosage form which does not contain an API but is intended to be used as a placebo.

                    

Excipient (USP)

                    

Components of a finished medicinal drug product other than the API. They are included in the formulation to facilitate manufacture, enhance stability, control release of API from the product, assist in product identification, or enhance other product characteristics.

                    

Genotoxic

                    

Substances which damage or modify DNA.

                    

GxP Regulation

                    

The underlying international pharmaceutical requirements, such as those set forth in the US FD&C Act, US PHS Act, FDA regulations, EU Directives, Japanese regulations, or other applicable national legislation or regulations under which a company operates. These include, but are not limited to: Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP), Good Clinical Practice (GCP), Good Laboratory Practice (GLP), Good Distribution Practice (GDP), Good Quality Practice (GQP), Good Pharmacovigilance Practice, Medical Device Regulations, and PDMA regulations.

                    

Hazard

                    

Inherent pharmacologic and toxicologic property of a substance that adversely affects the health of people, environment, process or product.

                    

Hazardous Substance

                    

A substance which by reason of being explosive, flammable, toxic, poisonous, corrosive, oxidizing, irritant or otherwise harmful, is likely to cause injury.

                    

Health Hazard

                    

Classification of a chemical for which there is statistically significant scientific data that acute or chronic adverse health effects may occur in exposed persons. Examples include: cardiovascular toxins, carcinogens, toxic or highly toxic, developmental and reproductive toxins, immunotoxic agents, hepatotoxins, nephrotoxins, and neurotoxins.

                    

Highly Hazardous Drugs

                    

Genotoxic compounds that are known to be, or highly likely to be, carcinogenic to humans, compounds that can produce reproductive and/or developmental effects at low dosages, and compounds that can produce serious target organ toxicity or other significant adverse effects at low dosages.

                    

Holistic

                    

Considering the whole system or process and its interdependencies rather than addressing separate parts in isolation.

                    

Intermediate

                    

A material produced during steps of the processing of an API that undergoes further molecular change or purification before it becomes an API. Intermediates may or may not be isolated.

                    

Manufacturing Process

                    

All manufacturing and storage steps in the creation of the finished product from the weighing of components through the storing, packaging, and labeling of the finished product, including, but not limited to, the following: mixing, granulating, milling, molding, formulating, lyophilizing, tableting, encapsulating, coating, sterilizing, and filling.

                    

Mix-up

                    

The contamination, at unsafe levels, of one product with another via inadequate plant and process design or human error.

                    

Multiproduct Facility

                    

A facility that supports production of two or more products, either in a campaigned or concurrent manner.

                    

Mutagenic

                    

Capable of inducing inheritable effect either in vitro and/or in vivo at the genetic (i.e., DNA or chromosomal) level.

                    

No-Observed-Adverse-Effect Level (NOAEL)

                    

The dose in a study which produces no adverse effects.

                    

Occupational Exposure Band (OEB)

                    

A range of airborne concentrations, usually spanning an order of magnitude, corresponding to a category assigned to a compound based on its inherent toxicological and pharmacological properties. The OEB also corresponds to control strategies, known in advance, to control airborne levels within the range of concentrations associated with the band.

                    

Occupational Exposure Limit (OEL)

                    

A health-based airborne concentration for a conventional 8-hour workday and a 40-hour workweek, to which it is believed that nearly all workers may be repeatedly exposed, day after day, for a working lifetime without adverse effect.

                    

Open System/Process

                    

A system that fails to meet one or more of the requirements that set the criteria for a closed system.

                    

Permitted Daily Exposure (PDE)

                    

A substance specific dose that is unlikely to cause an adverse effect if an individual is exposed at or below this dose every day for a lifetime.

                    

Pharmaceutical Product

                    

Substance or combination of APIs and excipients, or preservatives which has a therapeutic, prophylactic, or diagnostic purpose, or is intended to modify physiological functions, and is presented in a dosage form suitable for administration to the patient.

                    

Physical Segregation

                    

The separation of materials, spaces, or operations by means of physical barriers to prevent their mixing or overlap.

                    

Plausible Pathways

                    

A reasonable route of potential exposure.

                    

Point-of-Departure (PoD)

                    

The dose in animals or humans which serves as the starting point for derivation of the ADE value. This is the dose associated with the critical effect, which is the most sensitive clinically significant adverse effect observed that is relevant to humans.

                    

Process Boundary

                    

The process equipment and the interface, through which it is charged, discharged, sampled, intervened, and recovered.

                    

Product Changeover

                    

The program by which a processing area is cleared of supplies and components used in the manufacture of a previous product and then readied for production of a new product. This often includes parts changeover and/or special cleaning to eliminate cross-contamination.

                    

Product Life Cycle

                    

All phases in the life of the product from the initial development through marketing until the product's discontinuation.

                    

Primary Containment

                    

The first level of containment, consisting of the inside portion of that container which comes into immediate contact on its inner surface with the material being contained.

                    

Quality Risk Management (QRM)

                    

A systematic process for the assessment, control, communication and review of risks to the quality of the drug product across the product life cycle.

                    

Quality System

                    

The sum of all aspects of a system that implements quality policy and ensures that quality objectives are met.

                    

Retention

                    

The residue, or accumulated product, on product contact surfaces that can carryover from one product to another in the same equipment used in a sequential or campaign manner.

                    

Risk

                    

Likelihood of an identified harmful consequence as a function of exposure to a hazard.

                    

Risk Metrics

                    

A collection of quality, compliance, and/or performance measurements which help management measure and control the risks associated with operating within a regulated industry. Also known as dashboards.

                    

Secondary Containment

                    

Level of containment that is external to, and separate from, primary containment.

                    

Self Contained Area

                    

Premises which provide complete and total separation of all aspects of an operation including personnel and equipment movement, with well established procedures, controls, and monitoring. This includes physical barriers as well as separate air handling systems, but does not necessarily imply two distinct and separate buildings.

                    

Sensitizer

                    

A compound that can cause immediate (IgE mediated) or delayed (cell-mediated) hypersensitivity reactions.

                    

Segregation

                    

The provision of facilities which, although dedicated to a particular use, can be within the same building as other operations. A fully segregated area within a common building is one where there is a continuous floor to roof wall. The wall should be sealed at all break points where common services breach the wall.

                    

Severity

                    

A measure of the seriousness of possible consequences of a hazard.

                    

Teratogenicity

                    

The occurrence of behavioral or structural malformations in a developing foetus when a substance is administered during pregnancy.

                    

Vaccine

                    

Antigens prepared from modified organisms that elicit immune response (antibody production) to protect a person or animal from a disease agent.

                    

Wipe Limit

                    

A surface limit applied to external equipment and facility surfaces for the purpose of evaluating worker exposures. Any use of wipe sample results should be done with caution, within the context of a risk assessment for cross-contamination based on the underlying ADE value.
