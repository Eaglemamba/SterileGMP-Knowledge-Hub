## Chapter 2 — User Requirements Specification (URS)

                

            

        

        
        

            

                
                

                    

### 2.1 Introduction

                    

The User Requirements Specification (URS) brings together system requirements from multidisciplinary sources to support system design, C&Q, operations, and maintenance. Within the C&Q process, it is a foundational document that identifies the product and process requirements for the system. These product quality related user requirements are based on product knowledge (CQAs), process knowledge (CPPs), regulatory requirements, and organization/site quality requirements. The product and process requirements in the URS are inputs to the C&Q process; they provide the science-based knowledge that forms the basis for applying QRM to C&Q.

                    

It is recommended that a URS is developed for each system that has the potential to impact product quality, or a direct impact system (as determined through system classification described in Chapter 3). A formal URS document is not always necessary. A statement of the URS may be contained in various forms, e.g., purchase request, functional specification, change request, or data sheet. For standard off-the-shelf or simple catalog systems, a statement of the URS may be a purchase order, vendor cut sheet, or catalog data. The system would be verified against this document.

## 2.2 Development of the User Requirements Specification

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 2.2 Development of the User Requirements Specification

                    

The URS defines the requirements that must be met for a system to be suitable for the intended purpose; it should not detail how those requirements are met. The URS is developed by a project team that includes SMEs (refer to Section 6.4 for an example approval matrix). It can be revised throughout the system lifecycle.

                    

### The URS document SHOULD include:

                    

                        
- Describe the intended purpose of the system

                        
- State the requirements in verifiable terms without describing how the requirements will be met

                        
- State the category and source of each requirement
                            

                                
    - Categories may include, for example:
                                    

                                        
        - Quality

                                        
        - Business

                                        
        - Health, Safety, and Environmental (HSE)

                                    

                                

                                
    - Sources may include, for example:
                                    

                                        
        - Product and process requirements derived from:
                                            

                                                
            - Product and process knowledge (CQAs, CPPs, CAs)

                                                
            - GMP regulatory requirements

                                                
            - Organization/site quality requirements

                                            

                                        

                                        
        - System Risk Assessment (includes CAs and associated CDEs)

                                        
        - Range and accuracy for any controlled parameters

                                        
        - National, local and site requirements, as applicable

                                        
        - Business, HSE, system owner, and SME requirements

                                        
        - Engineering specifications and industry standards (e.g., ASME, ASTM, ISO)

                                        
        - Project requirements document or project charter

                                        
        - Legacy system evaluations

                                    

                                

                            

                        

                    

                    

The URS should also include the following:

                    

                        
- Data integrity requirements

                        
- Data storage/display requirements

                        
- Alarm requirements, with identification of the critical alarms

                        
- System automation requirements
                            

                                
    - If there are any connections (e.g., data archiving) and interactions (e.g., alarm initiation) to a larger automation control system, then that system can have a separate URS. There needs to be clearly defined boundaries between the systems.

                            

                        

                    

                    

                        Note: System software development and testing is outside of the scope of this Guide. Refer to ISPE GAMP® 5: A Risk-Based Approach to Compliant GxP Computerized Systems.
                    

                    

The URS, which may be further detailed during early project phases, provides the foundation for the Basis of Design (BOD) and becomes a reference used to establish that a system is suitable for the intended purpose. To be suitable for the intended purpose, systems need to meet their URS.

                    

### The URS should NOT include the following information as requirements:

                    

                        
- How a requirement is to be met

                        
- Detailed design specifications

                        
- Sequence of operations

                        
- Generic statements (e.g., must comply with local codes, must meet all GxPs)

                        
- General references to government/national standards (e.g., nonspecific reference to ASME code, ISO standards)

                        
- Contractual terms and deliverables

                        
- Unverifiable parameters

                        
- Standard functionality of the equipment type

                    

                    

Refer to Chapter 16 (Appendix 2) for an example URS template. It is the responsibility of the organization/site to designate quality impacting elements of their URS in a manner that is most appropriate for their integrated C&Q process.

## 2.3 Approval of URS and Changes Post-Approval

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 2.3 Approval of User Requirements Specification and Changes Post-Approval

                    

The URS should be approved by the system owner, an appropriate SME, and the Quality Unit. However, if the Quality Unit has already approved the source documents from which the quality requirements within the URS were obtained, then the Quality Unit does not need to approve the URS. Refer to Section 6.4 for an example document approval matrix.

                    

The URS should be updated through the system lifecycle (from development through sustaining operations) until decommissioning of the system. After approval of the URS, changes or additions with the potential to impact product quality are evaluated against the System Risk Assessment (refer to Chapter 4) to confirm if risks are acceptable or to determine if additional risk controls are required. Changes to the URS are performed through change management (refer to Chapter 12).

## Chapter 3 — System Classification

                

            

        

        
        

            

                
                

                    

### 3.1 Introduction

                    

This Guide presents an approach for classifying systems into two categories depending on the system capability to impact product quality. System classification establishes whether a system is commissioned and qualified or only commissioned, as follows:

                    

                        
- Direct impact systems are commissioned and qualified

                        
- Not direct impact systems are commissioned

                    

                    

                        Note: In the previous edition of this Baseline® Guide, component classification could be carried out as a subset of system classification. **Component classification is no longer necessary** since the System Risk Assessment (as described in Chapter 4) identifies CDEs for the system in a more efficient manner.
                    

                    

For systems that are not direct impact, the scope of commissioning is determined by SMEs and is based on the business and HSE criticality of the system. For example, a chilled water system serving an administrative building (not direct impact) may be balanced only. Meanwhile, a chilled water system (not direct impact) serving direct impact process equipment may be balanced, with the balancing confirmed at the minimum and maximum design flow rates to ensure reliable system performance during operation.

## 3.2 System Boundaries

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 3.2 System Boundaries

                    

Projects are commonly divided up into systems to facilitate construction management, document collation, turnover, and C&Q. System boundaries are markings on design drawings to distinguish systems from one another; this includes all components and piping to logically define what is included and what is not included within the system. System boundaries should be defined and documented early on in the project (at the BOD phase) to enable development of the systems list and subsequent execution of System Risk Assessments. The systems are usually listed in the C&Q Plan (refer to Chapter 6). System boundaries should be defined such that the systems cover the entirety of the project scope; this is typically documented in the system Process and Instrumentation Diagram (P&ID) or Process Flow Diagram (PFD).

                    

The scope of supply from vendors is often used as the basis for defining system boundaries; however, these boundaries should be carefully reviewed as there may be benefits in defining the boundaries differently. For example, boundary considerations for a direct impact compressed air system are as follows:

                    

                        
- A Point of Use (POU) filter is typically outside the vendor scope of supply for a compressed air system but may or may not be included in the system boundaries.

                        
- The POU filter could be included within the boundaries for the compressed air distribution system. All compressed air testing and verification activities would be part of the compressed air system.

                        
- Alternatively, the POU filter could be included in the process system for which the air is being supplied to. All compressed air testing and verification activities for the POU filter would then be part of the process system.

                    

                    

During the early stages of a project, system boundaries may also be subdivided to facilitate development of C&Q documentation by different suppliers. For example, the URS for a compressed air system can include generation and distribution sections, each of which may have its own set of C&Q documentation.

                    

The optimum approach for the project should be evaluated and then defined in the C&Q Plan. In the compressed air system example discussed earlier, it may be simpler to provide a URS/C&Q package for the generation system from one supplier and a separate URS/C&Q package for the storage and distribution system that is built on-site by a separate supplier.

                    

                        Regardless of the C&Q documentation strategy, systems need to meet their URS.

## 4.1 Introduction

            

The System Risk Assessment is the application of QRM (Quality Risk Management) to examine the product quality risk controls for *direct impact systems*. This assessment identifies the critical design controls (CAs/CDEs) and procedural controls that are required to mitigate system risks to an acceptable level. It is important for the project team performing the System Risk Assessment to include technical SMEs that understand the science behind the process and the risks associated with the CQAs.

            

System Risk Assessments should be developed for direct impact systems (as determined through system classification described in Chapter 3). For standard off-the-shelf or simple catalog systems, some organizations may consider it unnecessary to perform the System Risk Assessment; a simple protocol or report (e.g., Installation and Operational Verification) could be used to provide the documented evidence that the installation and operation meet the system requirements.

            

Figure 4.1 shows the relationship between the activities and documents in the C&Q process for direct impact systems. C&Q inspection and testing activities can take place during fabrication and construction as well as during the commissioning/testing activities.

            

*Figure 4.1: Relational Diagram for C&Q Activities and Documents – System Risk Assessment*

            

After the System Risk Assessment is completed, the URS should be checked to ensure that the CAs/CDEs have been included; if they are not included, then the URS should be revised to add them. Procedural control elements should be added to the C&Q scope.

            

Acceptance and release of the system (described in more detail in Chapter 8) involves confirming that the controls identified in the System Risk Assessment are in place as follows:

            

                
- The design controls (CAs/CDEs) have been shown to operate as specified

                
- The procedural controls are in place

            

        

        
        

            

            

                

### 4.1.1 Rationale

            

The traditional approach to qualification did not directly utilize commissioning documentation; instead, it often relied on an additional set of documents, the Installation Qualification (IQ) and Operational Qualification (OQ) protocols. These protocols provided the documented evidence that the system was installed and operating according to its specification. The scope of the IQ/OQ was determined by various elements such as organization/site established guidelines, vendor specifications, user requirements, and regulatory and guidance documents.

            

This traditional approach to qualification often resulted in IQ/OQ activities **covering more scope than was necessary** and repeating successful tests already performed during engineering start-up and commissioning. Furthermore, commissioning activities were commonly not robust enough or well documented since engineering teams anticipated that additional or repeat testing would be performed during qualification activities.

            

This Guide proposes a more streamlined approach to C&Q. Engineering documentation (i.e., commissioning inspection and testing documentation) is used to provide the documented evidence that the system has been installed and operates to meet the specifications. The CQAs and related CPPs are documented for the system, and a System Risk Assessment is developed to define potential risks to the CQAs and identify design (CAs/CDEs) and procedural controls used to mitigate these risks. Qualification confirms the system is suitable for the intended purpose and that the risk controls (design and procedural controls) are in place and functioning as specified.

### 4.1.2 Purpose and Benefits

            

The System Risk Assessment has multiple purposes and is a means to:

            

                
- **Identify, assess, and record** the quality risks to CQAs and associated controls

                
- **Identify and document** the design and procedural controls that are required to control and manage the identified risks
                    

                        
    - The rationale for these controls can also be documented in the System Risk Assessment

                        
    - Testing of the design controls (CAs/CDEs) and verification of procedural controls provides the documentation used to support system qualification

                    

                

                
- **Provide guidance** for the scope of the DQ, i.e., the scope covers the design of the CAs/CDEs

                
- **Provide the information** that is required for the basis of acceptance and release

                
- **Provide a focus** for Quality Unit oversight

            

            

The System Risk Assessment establishes (if not available in other documents) and documents the CAs/CDEs of the system operation and design that can impact product quality. It identifies the design and procedural controls that would mitigate those risks to product quality to an acceptable level. By verifying these controls, the system is confirmed to match that risk profile.

## 4.2 System Risk Assessment Process

            

As a prerequisite, the CQAs and associated CPPs should be identified and documented in the URS.

            

This Guide presents the following approach to performing the System Risk Assessment:

            

                
- A **small team** develops an initial baseline draft that includes development of the process description and identification of design and procedural controls

                
- A **broader team** reviews this initial baseline draft, making any necessary revisions to ensure the information is correct and no risks or controls have been omitted, and assigns the system risk profile

            

            

As a general guide, the initial session by the small team and the review session by the broader team should not exceed more than a few hours each if the preparation work is complete and teams with the correct level of expertise are used.

### 4.2.3 Using Completed Assessments as a Starting Point for New System Baseline Drafts

            

The experience and documentation gained from completing System Risk Assessments can be applied to future systems. In addition, many systems used for pharmaceutical manufacturing and packaging are standard systems that have been in use for a reasonable period of time, have built-in control strategies, and are developed by experienced vendors with established engineering quality systems. Therefore, any new systems that are **identical to or similar to existing systems** can utilize the completed System Risk Assessments as a starting point.

            

The small team that develops the baseline draft (as described in Section 4.2.1) should assess the new system against the completed System Risk Assessment for the existing system that is identical/similar. The SMEs should determine if the system under review has different functionality and/or components. If the SMEs determine that these differences may introduce additional or different risks, then the baseline draft needs to be updated to include the risks and associated risk controls.

            

The remainder of the System Risk Assessment process would proceed as described in Section 4.2.2. The broader team reviews the baseline draft and determines the risk profile for the new system.

            

The use of completed assessments as a starting point for new system baseline drafts can **improve the efficiency and consistency** of the process.

## 4.3 Timing of System Risk Assessments

            

### 4.3.1 Development of the System Risk Assessment

            

The System Risk Assessment can be developed after completion of concept design and prior to the start of detailed engineering. It can be updated later as required. The System Risk Assessment can be completed when the Issued for Construction (IFC) design drawings and specifications are available, whether for new or modified systems. These drawings and specifications represent what will be built including any incorporated changes made through the DR/DQ process. Therefore, the System Risk Assessment can be confirmed and approved at this stage.

            

Note: Depending on the level of product and process knowledge, some of the CDEs associated with CAs may be known while the balance of the CDEs will be identified during the engineering design lifecycle (and can be documented as part of the DQ). All CDEs should be listed in the System Risk Assessment.

            

The System Risk Assessment defines the system risk profile, i.e., what it will be after the risk controls have been verified to be in place and operating as specified. Until the verification is completed, it is considered a provisional risk assessment.

            

For novel systems, starting the System Risk Assessment earlier in the design process can be beneficial since this allows for any identified risks to be controlled by design. Most risk control is achieved through system design; a procedural control is not considered as robust.

            

For standard or catalog systems where the potential risks are well understood and design controls are built in where possible, the System Risk Assessment could be performed at any time before the final DR of the system. Alternatively, an Installation and Operational Verification document could be used. DQ can also confirm that the system risk controls are adequate.

### 4.3.2 Lifecycle of the System Risk Assessment

            

Depending on the project size, timing, and complexity, additional product/process or system knowledge may become available after completion of the System Risk Assessment. If this additional knowledge impacts the system risk profile, the System Risk Assessment needs to be reviewed and updated.

            

**System Risk Assessments are not subject to time-based periodic review;** it is more efficient to reassess the risk profile in the event of a deviation related to the system performance and determine if an update is required. The System Risk Assessment can also be used as a reference document for assessing potential changes to a system.

            

For any changes to the system, the URS and System Risk Assessment should both be reviewed as part of the change management process (refer to Chapter 12). If the proposed change introduces additional risks or impacts existing risk controls, the System Risk Assessment (and associated URS) needs to be reviewed and updated.

## Introduction —

                

DR and DQ: purposes, scope, and the critical distinction

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.1 Introduction (p.35)

                    

This chapter provides guidance for performing and documenting Design Reviews (DRs) and Design Qualification (DQ). This Guide emphasizes the importance of DRs leading to DQ as a continual process throughout all phases in the design of new or modified systems.

                    

DRs provide assurance that the design deliverables are consistent with the URS and the mitigation control strategies developed during the System Risk Assessment. When properly performed, DRs reduce the need for costly design-related change orders.

                    

DQ is performed only for **direct impact systems** (as determined through system classification described in Chapter 3). DQ involves more formal documentation, with Quality Unit approval, to show traceability of the CAs and relevant CDEs in the design to the CPPs and CQAs.

                    

                        DR and DQ are not intended to be separate activities, but rather **separate documentation** in which DQ is focused on CAs/CDEs and involves the Quality Unit as an approver. There should be minimal duplication of work; the final report from DR is a key input into the DQ process.
                    

                

                
                

                    

### Commentary

                    

                        

## Design Process —

                

Three phases: Concept → BOD → Detailed Design; GEP and risk-based approach

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.2 Design Process (p.35–36)

                    

The design process can be divided into several phases to ensure the design deliverables are sufficiently developed, the risks are identified and mitigated, and the project (as defined) meets financial objectives. The phases of the design process are:

                    

                        
1. **Concept**

                        
2. **Basis of Design (BOD)**

                        
3. **Detailed Design**

                    

                    

The concept and BOD phases define the Scope of Work (SOW) for the project.

                    

In the **concept phase**, several approaches (scope options) to meeting the URS may be evaluated. These alternatives should be evaluated for their impact on the business objective, project cost, and risk to quality. The project stakeholders should select the preferred scope option; the project then progresses to the BOD phase.

                    

In the **BOD phase**, additional engineering work provides a more developed SOW. The BOD design package contains Issued for Design (IFD) documents; it is the basis for the detailed design phase which delivers Issued for Construction (IFC) documents.

                    

### 5.2.1 Importance of Good Engineering Practice

                    

During the design process, it is important to follow GEP to support the integrity of related data and documentation. DR activities include evaluating deliverables against engineering standards and business, operations, and quality requirements as well as identifying gaps and proposing required corrective actions. Documenting these activities using Good Documentation Practice concepts (as described in Chapter 13) supports the overall effort to ensure the system is suitable for the intended purpose.

                    

### 5.2.2 Science and Risk-Based Approach to Design Reviews

                    

A quality risk assessment of the manufacturing process (including the environmental conditions supporting the process and/or critical systems) is a significant activity outside of the C&Q scope that can influence the early phase of design. Design stage System Risk Assessment typically uses knowledge from process risk assessments and other process knowledge to evaluate system design with respect to process control failure modes. These assessments are an integral part of the science and risk-based C&Q approach.

                    

Design stage quality risk reviews are an **iterative process** that is linked to the design development of the process system. The review of the System Risk Assessments and how the results affect the proposed design should be included in the scope of DRs.

## Design Review —

                

Objectives, SME roles, process, and outcome including Figure 5.1

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.3.1 Objectives of Design Review (p.36)

                    

DRs are defined in ASTM E2500-13 [5] as follows:

                    

                        "Planned and systematic reviews of specifications, design, and design development and continuous improvement changes performed as appropriate throughout the life-cycle of the manufacturing system. Design reviews evaluate deliverables against standards and requirements, identify problems, and propose required corrective actions."
                    

                    

DRs include both **quality critical** and **non-critical** aspects of the system design.

                    

The documented results of the DR should be the basis for any design modifications/additions performed to correct any deficiencies in the design under review.

                    

### 5.3.2 Role of Subject Matter Experts and Quality Unit

                    

Throughout the design process, a cross-functional project team (refer to Section 6.4 for example approval matrix) reviews the design of the system to ensure that the product and process user requirements, GMP, and regulatory requirements are included. Thus, it is important that this team includes technical SMEs that have the appropriate experience and correct knowledge of the process, process equipment, control strategies, and facilities to ensure that the design under review will meet the requirements of the URS, function efficiently, and consistently produce quality product.

                    

If the system being reviewed is direct impact, then the team should also include a Quality Unit representative to ensure that all quality requirements are met. This can help to facilitate the review and approval of the DQ.

                    

### 5.3.3 Design Review Process (p.36–37)

                    

Prior to the DR, design documents should be circulated to the DR project team for their review to ensure a comprehensive review process. It is the responsibility of the project manager and the design leads to ensure that the appropriate participants are part of the DR. Senior management from the Quality Unit, operations, and production are responsible for ensuring the proper representatives with the appropriate experience are present during the DR.

                    

The project team should define the method for conducting DR according to the **system type, size, and risk**. This method should be documented and approved in applicable plans (e.g., Project Execution Plan, C&Q Plan).

                    

The effort, formality, and documentation of DRs should follow the ICH Q9 [3] principles of being **commensurate with the level of risk**. DR documentation may take the form of engineering meeting minutes or notes. For highly critical novel systems, DRs may be more focused and detailed.

                    

For off-the-shelf equipment, DR should consider the complexity and/or risk to system or facility operation and to quality. Vendor documents are typically used to conduct these reviews and, if necessary, a technical vendor representative could attend the DR. The focus should be on the CPPs controlled by the equipment and the CAs/CDEs required for the product.

                    

DRs focus on both **engineering design** and **quality aspects** of the design.

                    

The *engineering design focus* is the technical aspects of the system; the objective is to verify the operability, maintainability, and safety aspects of the system. Technical SMEs, system owners, and any other stakeholders need to be present. It is optional for the Quality Unit to attend these reviews that focus on the technical aspects; however, their presence is beneficial and can simplify the DQ step.

                    

The *quality focus* of the DRs is on compliance elements and the CAs/CDEs of the system design that affect product quality; the objective is to verify that all regulatory requirements are addressed by the design. It is **strongly recommended** the Quality Unit attends these reviews with quality focus, and that these reviews are documented.

                    

### 5.3.4 Design Review Outcome (p.37)

                    

The DR project team should have appropriate methods for recording and distributing the results of the DRs and managing any consequent changes. The results should confirm the continuing suitability of the design and confirm that the design is suitable for the intended purpose.

                    

The team is responsible for communicating to their management any issues affecting design.

The figure illustrates a sequential process flow: starting from design deliverables, proceeding through the DR meeting (with SME and QA participation), generating action items / open issues, resolving deficiencies, and concluding with a documented DR report that feeds into the DQ process.

                    

*Note (from Figure 5.1):* The Traceability Matrix is a document format which compares the product and process requirements for the system with the CAs/CDEs.

5.4

            

                

## Design Qualification —

                

Objectives, prerequisites, DQ process, and final DQ report

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.4.1 Objectives of Design Qualification (p.38)

                    

DQ provides documented verification that the design of a new or modified direct impact system will result in a system that is suitable for the intended purpose. The objectives of DQ are to:

                    

                        
- Verify the design will meet the quality user requirements as listed in the URS

                        
- Verify the design will adequately control risks to product quality and patient safety, as identified during the System Risk Assessment

                        
- Verify the CAs/CDEs necessary to implement these requirements and risk controls

                    

                    

### 5.4.2 Prerequisites

                    

The prerequisites for DQ include:

                    

                        
- Approved URS

                        
- Functional requirements (for automated systems)

                        
- System Risk Assessments with identification of design (CAs/CDEs) and procedural controls that will be used to mitigate the unacceptable risks

                        
- Completed DRs, with verification that all open design issues that affect CAs/CDEs, CPPs, and CQAs are closed and included in the final design

                    

                    

It is the responsibility of the project manager to ensure that all prerequisites have been completed and are made available to the participants prior to the DQ for their review. The DQ should be scheduled to allow adequate time for preliminary review of the material to be covered.

                    

### 5.4.3 Design Qualification Process (p.38–39)

                    

DQ is the documented process for the review and approval of the final system design to verify the following:

                    

                        
- All process and product requirements in the system URS have been incorporated into the design

                        
- Quality and regulatory requirements in the system URS have been incorporated into the design

                        
- Any changes to the design approach for the CAs and any associated CDEs have been reviewed, as appropriate

                    

                    

The DR documentation should be reviewed to confirm the Quality Unit was included in the DR, as defined in the project plan, and has provided the appropriate input to the design.

                    

The approach for performing DQ should be defined in planning documents. For small projects, there may be one DQ performed after all the design is completed. For larger projects, the design may be broken into several areas or functions, with the DQ performed when each design is completed.

                    

A formal protocol document to check requirements against the design is **not a regulatory requirement**. However, the following information should be defined: plan for conducting the DQ, scope of the DQ, and Quality Unit approval of the DQ. This information could be documented for individual systems or for specified integrated systems.

                    

A Traceability Matrix can be used to document traceability of the CAs and any associated CDEs, and CPPs to the CQAs. The CA and CDE acceptance criteria can also be documented in the Traceability Matrix.

                    

### 5.4.4 Final Design Qualification Report (p.39)

                    

The final DQ report should include or refer to the following, as applicable:

                    

                        
- Any needed risk reviews to document the incorporation of design control elements to support quality product manufacturing

                        
- Identification of each CDE (critical aspect of the physical and functional design) that serves to control identified potential process failures

                        
- Description of each CA and any supporting CDEs in terms that are verifiable through inspections or testing (acceptance criteria)

                        
- The CDEs that serve to meet a product or process user requirement in terms that are verifiable (mainly through performance testing)

                    

                    

A final DQ report should be issued stating the design is suitable for the intended purpose; the report should be **approved by representatives (from the appropriate level) in Engineering, Manufacturing and the Quality Unit**.

                    

**Figure 5.2** provides an overview of the DQ process: beginning with DQ prerequisites (approved URS, completed SRA, completed DRs), proceeding through the DQ review of CAs/CDEs against URS requirements, using the Traceability Matrix, and concluding with the final DQ report bearing tri-party approval.

### 6.1.1 Purpose and Benefits

                    

A well-conceived and executed C&Q Plan can greatly facilitate a timely and cost-effective qualification effort. It is essential to have excellent communication, planning, and coordination between the Quality Unit, vendors, operations, automation, engineering, construction, commissioning, and qualification personnel, to name a few, to deliver a project on time, within budget, and meeting all expectations. A C&Q plan facilitates this communication.

                    

An important secondary goal of the C&Q Plan is to foster an **interdisciplinary team approach** to C&Q. Well-planned project logistics are crucial to the success of every project. A well-coordinated effort leads to a high quality, effective, and efficient C&Q project.

This schedule should cover all systems (utilizing defined system boundaries as discussed in Section 3.2) and phases of the projects lifecycle from construction to Process Validation/PPQ completion. This schedule should be updated and issued regularly. These updates should not drive a C&Q Plan revision, but the C&Q Plan should identify how the schedule updates will be captured and communicated.

                    

An integrated schedule, using a critical path method, can increase the awareness of dependent activities and provide a clear understanding of the critical path. Including all project team members in the schedule development will engage the team members and foster better planning. All team members should comprehend the schedule format. Ordering long delivery time items early can help timelines, but consideration should be made to ensure the process requirements for those items are understood and included in the requisition. Understanding when to engage support for C&Q helps to support the success of the project.

                    

A well-planned effort can ensure that the user, business, regulatory, and patient expectations are met. All efforts to ensure the system is suitable for the intended purpose (e.g., start-up, balancing, trial runs, establishing maintenance programs) should be included in the planning phase since this is where the formality of the documentation can be established. These expectations include:

                    

                        
- Systems are delivered on time and within budget

                        
- Systems meet approved user requirements and are suitable for the intended purpose

                        
- Stakeholder needs, including speed to market, are met

                        
- Products produced from the systems meet defined quality standards

### 6.2.3 Elements to be Addressed

                    

This section is intended to be a discussion of the elements that need to be addressed as part of C&Q planning, not the documents where they are captured.

                    

#### 6.2.3.1 Scope of Testing

                    

System classification (as described in Chapter 3) identifies which systems require qualification. The System Risk Assessment (as described in Chapter 4) identifies which elements in those systems require qualification. The URS (as described in Chapter 2) and System Risk Assessment establish the scope of testing for qualification. The equipment specifications establish the scope of testing for commissioning.

Engineering firms, vendors, contractors, quality, engineering, and owners provide the documentation necessary for design, construction, commissioning, qualification, operation, and technical support of the systems. ### 6.3.3 Documentation

An alternative is to provide only high-level descriptions in the C&Q Plan and leave the detailed descriptions to execution documents. The selected approach should increase the level of efficiency and effectiveness. Commissioning encompasses testing from FAT, testing during construction, SAT, installation testing, and much of the start-up activities of a project lifecycle including those that will be used to assess the qualification of a direct impact system.

                    

Testing during commissioning should be used to support qualification. If commissioning documentation will be used to support qualification, it is critical to involve the leader of the qualification effort and the Quality Unit in the planning and coordination of commissioning activities and deliverables. A periodic commissioning planning and status meeting should be established.

- **Installation:** Documentation supporting installation is successfully completed. This may include FAT and SAT documentation as well as site commissioning for systems built at the site.

The plan should address the steps to follow when test results do not meet the expected results or acceptance criteria (**discrepancy**). There should be a predefined methodology for how discrepancies should be handled (see Section 7.4).

## Introduction

                

Purpose and safety scope of C&Q testing

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

This chapter provides guidance on C&Q testing and inspection activities and associated documentation for recording the results of the testing/inspection. The goal of testing and inspection is to confirm that the system has been installed and operates to meet the requirements.

                    

Although this chapter provides some general guidance on the safety aspects (e.g., pre-start-up checks), the specific requirements for safety aspects and any local requirements (e.g., signage, access control, equipment protection, Material Safety Data Sheets) are outside the scope of this Guide. It is the responsibility of the system designer to produce a system that is compliant with any related local regulations.

                

                

                    

                    

                        

#### 📌

                        

                        

                    

                    

                        

## Approach and Scope

                

From design to release — static, dynamic, integrated system testing

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

C&Q testing and documentation starts as early as design and ends with the acceptance and release of the system to the end-user. The process includes planning, document review, and static and dynamic testing of the installation, function, and performance, including testing of any defined worst-case scenarios.

                    

The requirements and stage of the testing/inspection should be predefined in the planning phase to avoid duplication of testing. Testing should only be repeated if necessary. For example, retesting may be necessary if there are changes that may impact a test result, e.g., differing utilities or disconnecting equipment and control systems after FAT to facilitate shipping.

                    

For systems where the automation is an integral part of the system (typical for manufacturing equipment, e.g., WFI storage and distribution system with a dedicated control panel), the entire system is tested as an integrated system comprising the mechanical, electrical, and associated automation.

                    

The scope of testing varies depending on the project scope, but for a new process area would include facilities, utilities, manufacturing equipment and their associated automation or process control systems.

                    

Testing activities and rigor will depend on the type of system (e.g., manufacturing, automated, utilities), potential to impact product quality, and on the project (e.g., green field, brown field, relocation, stick-built, modular, off-the-shelf system).

## Roles and Responsibilities

                

The four C&Q team roles — coordinator, user, technical expert, quality unit

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

The planning, execution, and closeout of testing should be performed by a multidisciplinary team (which includes vendors, engineering, and users) with appropriate Quality Unit oversight. Refer to Section 6.4 (Example Approval Matrix) for an example of the roles and responsibilities for the review and approval of deliverables.

                    

The following roles are typically included in a C&Q team:

                    

                        
- Testing Coordinator

                        
- Production user (system user)

                        
- Technical experts (engineering, automation, validation, or other SME)

                        
- Quality Unit representative

                    

                    

The **testing coordinator** represents the project manager in this scope of work. In small projects it is possible that the testing coordinator and the system owner is the same person, whereas for large projects it may be reasonable to designate the role to another individual. **Production users** represent the system owners who will operate the system. In large construction projects, this group may be the engineering representatives that designed the system, due to their knowledge of the planned operation and requirements. Production users are responsible for ensuring the system operates as expected and that documentation (such as operational procedures and batch records) are developed so that they are approved for acceptance and release.

                    

**Technical experts** are part of or support the testing team, as appropriate. Where testing activities are performed by vendors or third parties, the responsibilities between both parties need to be defined and documented.

## Turnover Packages (TOPs)

                

Purpose, typical contents (15 items), and format requirements

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

The purpose of the Turnover Package (TOP) is to provide complete documentation on system components, equipment, and setup. This documentation should also include as-built documentation (agreed upon data, drawings, software, etc.) turned over to the end-user. The TOP may be used throughout the system lifecycle, including ongoing maintenance, system change management, and retirement.

                    

TOPs should be reviewed to ensure that they are complete and represent the installed system. Once completed, the TOP should be accepted and approved. Updates to the TOP should be included in the change management of the system where appropriate. Where documentation from the TOP is used to support operations or represent the basis for qualification, they should be stored in a suitable location that provides adequate control and access.

                    

#### Contents of the TOP

                    

The TOP contents are usually determined by factors such as organization/site procedures, end-user preferences, GEP, system type, and contracts with the vendors. Information included in a TOP should ensure controlled operation, maintenance, and change. A complex novel system would, therefore, require more types of information than a simple off-the-shelf pump. Due to the variability of systems, vendors, and documentation, the TOP contents need to be flexible.

                    

A typical TOP may include the following lifecycle related documents:

                    

                        
- Vendor-provided SOPs

                        
- Spare parts lists

                        
- Maintenance requirements

                        
- Maintenance records for work conducted prior to turnover

                        
- As-built drawings

                        
- Material of construction certificates

                        
- Construction test and inspection records (e.g., pressure tests)

                        
- Loop calibration documentation

                        
- Material safety data sheets

                        
- System start-up and shutdown information

                        
- Warranties

                        
- Records of adjustable settings

                        
- Any specific documentation required by local regulations

                    

                    

The TOP contents should be clearly set out and agreed upon by all parties prior to placement of orders. The delivery of the documentation requirements should not present a risk to the project at a later stage, from either content or timing aspects.

                    

#### TOP Formats

                    

System vendors typically have their own format for TOPs, but the owner organization should clearly define a standardized format that aligns with their document control processes. This can help to ensure consistency between the various TOPs that are submitted. The owner organization can propose a standard format or accept the vendor format if it meets internal requirements. The format might be system-based. The format of TOPs should ensure that they are user friendly with tabulated sections. TOPs should be obtained for all operating systems. To facilitate storage, electronic versions of these documents are often required by the owner organization.

## 7.3.2.2 Factory Acceptance Test (FAT): Installation Tests

                

p56 — Earliest verification at vendor site; shipping agreements; FAT vs SAT scope

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — English

                    

### 7.3.2.2 Factory Acceptance Test: Installation Tests

                    

The FAT is inspection/testing of the system at the **vendor (system manufacturer) site**. The objective of the FAT is to provide testing and documentation at the **earliest possible time**, to verify that the vendor has fulfilled contractual responsibilities and the system meets the requirements. This allows for quicker and more efficient remedy of any failings and avoids the delays to schedule that would result from discovering problems later, on-site.

                    

After completing the FAT, the owner representative agrees with the vendor whether the system is ready to be shipped or on **pre-conditions for shipping**. Agreements are documented and approved by the vendor and owner.

                    

Where a system needs to be disassembled for shipping, the process should be documented. Details should include how the system will be disassembled, any marking or labeling of components, and how it will be reassembled and tested on-site. This information provides the rationale for:

                    

                        
- Testing that **can be accepted from the FAT**

                        
- Testing that **will be repeated as part of the SAT**

                    

                

                

                    

                    

                        

## 7.3.2.3 Receipt Inspection & 7.3.2.4 Site Acceptance Test

                

p56–p57 — Delivery documentation; tagging; SAT at owner site; turnover conditions

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text — English

                    

### 7.3.2.3 Receipt Inspection

                    

Receipt Inspection (RI) provides documentation that delivered components, materials, or systems conform to the requirements, e.g., manufacturer, model, serial number as applicable with the required supporting documentation. This is typically done **immediately upon delivery** of the component to the owner site.

                    

RI also provides documentation that all components have been received in good condition and are **tagged correctly**, where applicable.

                    

Any items that do **not pass RI** are separated for issue resolution. Items that pass RI are stored under suitable conditions for installation.

                    

### 7.3.2.4 Site Acceptance Test: Installation Tests

                    

The SAT is the testing of the system at the **owner organization site**. The objective of the SAT is to provide testing and documentation of the installed system, to verify that the vendor has fulfilled contractual responsibilities and that the system meets the requirements **prior to contractually accepting the system**.

                    

The SAT test plan includes a **review of the FAT punch list** (items carried over from the FAT that did not meet the acceptance criteria/expected results) and the plan to resolve open items. The SAT also includes reconfirming FAT test results where required due to system disassembly and reassembly.

                    

After completing SAT testing, the owner representative agrees with the vendor whether the system is ready to be turned over to the owner or on pre-conditions for system turnover. Agreements are documented and approved by the vendor and owner. **Acceptance of a system with open discrepancies** can be made with the approval of the Testing Coordinator and System Owner.

### 7.3.2.5 Installation Verification

                    

Installation verification includes verifying quality requirements related to installation (**CDEs as defined through the System Risk Assessment**). Example activities include:

                    

                        
- Verification that the product, ingredient and/or cleaning material contacting parts meet the specifications (**materials of construction** and finishes, as applicable)

                        
- Installation of critical instruments

                        
- Calibration of critical instruments (calibration could also be verified at the start of the functional testing, prior to the commencement of testing)

                    

                    

### 7.3.3 System Start-Up

                    

System start-up is a **GEP approach** to ensure that the system is energized and started up in a safe manner. This is also where adjustments to the system, settings to regulate the system, or any balancing needed are made. A prerequisite to system start-up is that **the system is mechanically complete without open critical punch list items for safety**. System start-up may be conducted both at the vendor facility and after installed on-site.

                    

The vendor operation or start-up manual should be reviewed prior to energizing the system. For systems that include vendor-provided control systems, **training should be completed prior to energizing the system**. Training of system operators, engineers, and maintenance personnel should be conducted in association with system vendors and properly documented.

                    

System start-up is a **four-step process**, consisting of:

                    

                        
1. Verification that the system is complete with all valve switches in the correct position. For stick-built systems and modular systems, ensure that the integrated system (including automation) has been reviewed before it is energized.

                        
2. Pre-start-up **safety review**.

                        
3. Supervised **energizing of the system utilities**.

                        
4. Pre-functional check of the system, with focus on **safety functions**.

                    

                    

Note that Steps 1 and 2 above need to be completed before Step 3. Step 3 needs to be completed prior to Step 4.

                    

Any issues identified during system start-up activities should be verified to not impact future testing and addressed during the following testing steps.

Original Text — English

                    

### 7.3.4 Operational/Functional Testing

                    

Operational/functional testing verifies that the system functionality matches what has been approved in the specifications (including those identified as CAs) and is suitably documented in a test plan/record for the **full desired range of system operations in the target environment**.

                    

The sequence of functionalities being tested should follow a planned approach. Functional tests should be executed after completion of all installation tests that can impact the functional tests. Testing begins after system start-up and calibrations defined in the respective plan have been completed.

                    

Testing may be conducted within a single report/test plan or separated into FAT, SAT, commissioning, and qualification phases. Installation and operational testing may be combined in a single document.

                    

The scope of functional testing includes verifying system functionality including mechanical, electrical, automation, and HSE related aspects. Documents, drawings, and operational procedures may be drafted and redlined, and discrepancy lists may be established.

                    

Typically, the final step of functional testing is a **full operational testing** — a **full load test** of the system to encompass **worst case conditions**. This test may need to be deferred if it is not possible to provide the relevant conditions. Final tuning of control loops may need to be delayed until system balancing has been performed (e.g., air or water balancing) or until enough system loads are available.

                    

Functional testing activities include, for example:

                    

                        
- Testing integrated automation controls and equipment for the whole system — both **normal and alarm/failure modes**

                        
- Testing analog control loops at a range of values representing the intended system use — encompassing the **anticipated range of normal operation, including upper and lower operating limits** (worst case conditions) without inducing process failure

                        
- Confirming the system can handle **predictable simultaneous use scenarios** for utility systems

                        
- Determining and recording manual component settings (e.g., valve positions, limit switch/screw positions, control switch settings)

                    

                    

#### 7.3.4.1 Factory Acceptance Test and Site Acceptance Test: Operational Tests

                    

After installation testing and system start-up, the FAT and SAT test documents may include execution of **operational testing**.

                    

#### 7.3.4.2 Qualification Operational Tests

                    

Operational tests that demonstrate the ability of the system to meet the **CAs/CDE functionalities** are considered part of the **qualification of the system** and require Quality Unit oversight. Complex or novel systems may require formal system integrated operational testing to document **PQ/PPQ operational criteria/control strategy**.

Original Text — English

                    

### 8 Acceptance and Release

                    

### 8.1 Introduction

                    

This chapter provides guidance on the acceptance and release phase which is where **all testing is formally reviewed and accepted**. The conclusion of the acceptance and release phase is that the system can be released for:

                    

                        
- Manufacturing operations

                        
- The next stage of qualification (e.g., for a WFI system)

                        
- Use in the manufacture of PPQ or commercial batches

                    

                    

**Acceptance and release represents the initiation of the "qualified state"** and the beginning of QA-managed **Quality Change Control** (see Chapter 12), continuous performance monitoring, and periodic review. The project team transitions system ownership to operations/engineering.

                    

The acceptance and release phase should occur as a **cooperative review** between the appropriate SMEs, including a representative from the department that will accept care, custody, and control of the system. The SMEs should review the results from an overall technical perspective.

                    

If the system includes **CAs/CDEs (direct impact)**, then the Quality Unit needs to be part of the acceptance and release review and approval, focusing on CAs/CDEs. Acceptance of a system with open discrepancies should be a Quality Unit decision. Typically, all discrepancies have to be addressed; however, **non-product impacting discrepancies may remain open** with an appropriate approved action plan.

                    

The acceptance and release phase is *not intended* to be a catchall for closeout of all system or project documentation.

### 8.3 Requirements to Complete the Acceptance and Release Phase

                    

In order to complete the acceptance and release phase, the following items are required to be addressed: **completion of test documentation**, **closure of discrepancies and changes**, and **implementation of quality system elements**.

                    

#### 8.3.1 Completion of Testing Documentation

                    

All of the planned testing needs to be successfully executed and the documentation completed. At least one SME review should be documented within the executed tests.

                    

The documentation for completion of testing should be approved by the team of SMEs and include the following information **at a minimum**:

                    

                        
- Testing description (component verification, calibration verification, etc.)

                        
- Testing expected results and if met or not met

                        
- Testing acceptance criteria and if met or not met

                        
- Reference to test protocol and execution data (e.g., vendor documents, commissioning)

                        
- Summary and conclusion

                    

                    

#### 8.3.2 Closure of Discrepancies

                    

Discrepancies should be categorized by potential impact.

                    

                        
- Discrepancies that **do not impact product quality may remain open**. The report should contain an indication of any open discrepancies along with potential manufacturing planning implications. These discrepancies should be assigned to a responsible individual with defined responsibility for resolution within a specific timeline.

                        
- Discrepancies that **could impact product quality need to be closed**.

                        
- All discrepancies should be listed with status (closed or open) within the summary report.

                    

                    

#### 8.3.3 Closure of Engineering Changes

                    

All changes should be handled in accordance with change management (as described in Chapter 12). The system changes that have occurred during the testing phase should be closed as part of the acceptance and release phase, unless there is agreement with the Quality Unit for these open changes to be carried forward. All engineering changes should be listed with status (closed or open) within the summary report.

                    

#### 8.3.4 Implementation of Quality System Elements

                    

For systems that contain CAs/CDEs, the implementation of quality systems is required prior to releasing a system for manufacturing operations. These aspects are referred to as quality system elements and may include (as applicable), but are not limited to:

                    

                        
- Availability of **SOPs** for operation of the system

                        
- **Calibration/maintenance system** and associated procedures

                        
- Mechanism for tracking system use and maintenance (e.g., **logbook**)

                        
- **Vendor documentation** (e.g., maintenance requirements, spare parts)

                        
- Operator **training** within the training quality system

                        
- Completion of any other organization requirements to add the system into the operating facility

                    

                    

A statement that all required quality system elements are in place should be included in the summary report for systems with CAs/CDEs. If a system is released with outstanding items (requires Quality Unit approval), a quality system should be in place to track these items to closure with an appropriate level of Quality Unit oversight.

### 8.4 Summary Report Organization

                    

The acceptance and release process is completed through a summary report. The contents and complexity of the report may vary, but it needs to include SME review and approval of: closure of testing documentation, discrepancies, engineering changes, implementation of quality system elements (as required), and documented formal release of the system.

                    

The summary report can be accomplished in several different ways depending on the scale and complexity of the project. A separate document for the summary report is not always necessary.

                    

#### 8.4.1 Example Option #1: Commissioning Summary Report – Systems with No CAs/CDEs

                    

For systems with **no CAs/CDEs** (not direct impact), the acceptance and release can be included within the Commissioning Summary Report, which includes:

                    

                        
- Completion of testing documentation

                        
- Closure of punch list items and discrepancies

                        
- Closure of engineering changes

                        
- Confirmation of documentation in place to ensure the system can operate and be maintained properly

                        
- Clear statement of formal release of the system

                    

                    

#### 8.4.2 Example Option #2: Qualification Summary Report – Systems with CAs/CDEs

                    

For systems with **CAs/CDEs**, where all of the requirements are ready at about the same time, the acceptance and release can be included within a single C&Q Summary Report, which includes:

                    

                        
- Completion of testing documentation

                        
- Closure of discrepancies

                        
- Closure of engineering changes

                        
- Quality System Elements implementation (or reference to change control record)

                        
- Clear statement of formal release of the system (or reference to change control record)

                    

                    

#### 8.4.3 Example Option #3: Formal Release with Use of Change Control – Existing Facility

                    

When new systems or changes to existing systems are implemented through QA-managed Quality Change Control, some or all of the requirements may be contained within the change control. For **existing facilities**, it is common to verify the implementation of quality system elements and document the formal release of the system in the change control rather than in the Qualification Summary Report. The use of the change control allows for **closure of the testing phase in a timely manner**.

                    

#### 8.4.4 Example Option #4: Acceptance and Release Report – Large Projects with Interconnected Systems

                    

For **large projects with interconnected systems**, multiple reports may be used to complete the acceptance and release phase. An overall acceptance and release report would reference the completed summary reports for each of the interconnected systems, document completion of all quality system elements, and include the **formal release statement**.

                    

One strategy may be to release per **processing area** (e.g., cell culture) after all testing is completed and closed out with separate summary reports.

                    

**Note:** The grouping strategy may complicate future changes. The grouping would also require presenting information from all systems during regulatory audits even when only a single system is requested. The advantages are that qualification would more closely reflect the grouping created by the process risk assessment as well as the batch record structure and eliminate potential gaps with the integration of systems.

For other types of systems, organizations have traditionally used an annual detailed review of the change history, maintenance, and operational records to provide this confirmation. - For a critical utilities system (such as HVAC, clean compressed air, pharmaceutical water and steam systems), routine monitoring includes:

    - Regular reporting of system performance trends, preventative maintenance and calibration metrics

If routine monitoring is performed, then **no additional review is considered necessary**. The monitoring confirms system performance and the drawing verification confirms that documentation is up-to-date.

                        

                        
- For other systems (e.g., manufacturing systems), the relative potential risk to product quality for different system types is evaluated and the frequency of review is defined based on the risk.

                    

                    

This proposed approach is a triage process that looks for any unusual performance signals at a high level. If none are observed, then the review is complete. If there are signals observed, then a deeper review is conducted that may result in detailed investigation and testing.

## Chapter 10: Vendor Assessment for C&Q Documentation Purposes

                

10.1 Introduction & Purpose/Benefits & Principles — Pages 79–80

            

        

        

            

                

                    

🌎 Original Text — English

                    

### 10.1 Introduction

                    

This chapter describes vendor assessments and controls for work where vendors follow their own procedures to perform testing and produce documentation that may be used to support system qualification. When vendor tests are used to directly support qualification where appropriate, the result is reduced on-site testing and reduced cost and time in the schedule without impacting quality. In addition, the SMEs on the project team can contribute to improved product quality by focusing their attention on other higher risk quality critical activities.

                    

Vendor assessments (for C&Q documentation purposes) do not include the vendor selection and/or commercial assessment, though the results may impact those assessments. Vendor assessments for C&Q documentation purposes may be integrated into this broader vendor selection and qualification process or comprise a standalone activity for C&Q planning and execution purposes.

                    

Regulatory expectations for vendor assessments are provided in Chapter 15 (Appendix 1). For detailed information on assessing vendors of automated systems, refer to ISPE GAMP® 5.

                    

**Note:** It is not necessary to perform vendor assessments, as described in this Guide, for vendors that provide qualification services (sometimes referred to as secondary services) when those vendors are contracted to follow the regulated owner organization procedures and practices or applicable specialist standards, such as the US National Environmental Balancing Bureau (NEBB).

                    

### 10.1.1 Purpose and Benefits

                    

The objective of a vendor assessment is to establish confidence in the vendor and the vendor quality systems to perform testing, manage changes, and provide documentation that is accurate, complete, and unambiguous, with results matching what was expected. Vendor verification documentation assessments are highly recommended when documents may be used to support qualification. Purchaser and vendor cooperation is key to successfully achieving this goal.

                    

Vendor assessments can minimize business/project risk associated with vendor performance, including project cost and schedule risk, by performing testing and completing documentation deliverables required to support verification of CAs (qualification) right the first time.

                    

The use of vendor documentation can prevent repeating work that was appropriately performed and documented and can help to achieve GMP compliance at a reduced cost and in less time. Vendors are the experts in their particular systems and the use of vendor testing and documentation should be encouraged. As an example, consider verification of product contact materials. Accepting a certificate of conformity from a quality vendor can be an acceptable, low-risk approach for material verification; checking 100% of the related heat certificates would therefore be unnecessary.

                    

### 10.1.2 Principles/Concepts

                    

It is good engineering practice to assess vendor's capability for testing and documenting the systems they provide for conformance to requirements and specifications, based on the criticality of the system. The decision and justification to use vendor documentation to support the verification of CAs and associated CDEs should be based on the intended purpose of the manufacturing system and the suitability of the vendor quality systems. The decision and rationale should be documented in the C&Q Plan and approved by SMEs and the Quality Unit.

                    

The Quality Unit and Engineering/Technical SME should agree on the type (e.g., checklist, test plan, execution report) and quality (Good Documentation Practices) of the deliverables. The Quality Unit should review the vendor assessment and confirm the extent to which the vendor's documentation package may be used to support qualification and verify suitability for the intended purpose. This review and confirmation should be conducted and documented as early as possible.

                    

Quality Unit oversight in the use of vendor verification documentation is a risk-based decision, with consideration of risk to product quality. For example, there is less product quality risk in documenting pipe boroscoping or passivation activities as compared to an SAT for a sterile filling isolator.

## Chapter 11: Engineering Quality Process

                

11.1 Introduction & Rationale — Pages 83–84

            

        

        

            

                

                    

🌎 Original Text — English

                    

### 11.1 Introduction

                    

When the C&Q process is considered as a part of an organization-wide PQS, the result is an achievement of both GMP and GEP concepts. A separate quality system is not required for C&Q; instead, it can be part of the overall PQS or a subset of the PQS. For the purposes of this Guide, the term Engineering Quality Process is used to describe the process within the PQS that covers C&Q. This Guide recognizes that this function is referred to differently across organizations which have their own structure and assignment of roles and responsibilities. This chapter intends to focus on the concepts that should be addressed for this function.

                    

The overall organization PQS program provides the quality practices for processes in order to consistently deliver quality products. The Engineering Quality Process, as a subset process of the PQS, addresses the quality practices for the C&Q process and is intended to support the overall PQS program.

                    

Both the PQS and Engineering Quality Process need to emphasize **quality** (i.e., the product or service meeting the specified requirements) versus **compliance** (i.e., meeting current procedures or organization requirements). Good quality practices and methods through the PQS produce robust reliable systems and ensures compliance. The Engineering Quality Process needs to be established as part of the PQS; the Engineering Quality Process can then be used for developing sustainable, consistent, cost-effective, and practical engineering processes for project and operational lifecycle management.

                    

### 11.1.1 Rationale

                    

Current regulatory authority and ICH guidances emphasize a focus on building quality into the product and not by inspection. ICH Q10 augments the defined GMPs by describing specific quality system elements and management responsibilities that should be in place to achieve three main objectives; it states:

                    

                        "Implementation of the Q10 model should result in achievement of three main objectives which complement or enhance regional GMP requirements:  

                          ▶ Achieve Product Realisation [Section 1.5.1]  

                          ▶ Establish and Maintain a State of Control [Section 1.5.2]  

                          ▶ Facilitate Continual Improvement [Section 1.5.3]"
                    

                    

The FDA 2006 Guidance for Industry: Quality Systems Approach to Pharmaceutical CGMP Regulations states the following as the goal of the guidance:

                    

                        "This guidance describes a comprehensive quality systems model, which, if implemented, will allow manufacturers to support and sustain robust, modern quality systems that are consistent with CGMP regulations."
                    

                    

In alignment with ICH Q8, the FDA guidance states:

                    

                        "The overarching philosophy articulated in both the CGMP regulations and in robust modern quality systems is: **Quality should be built into the product, and testing alone cannot be relied on to ensure product quality.**"
                    

                    

Another useful resource in understanding Engineering Quality Process as a philosophy is the work of Dr. W. Edwards Deming. Deming believed in improving the process and in doing so using process measures to guide improvement efforts. In his book, Deming states:

                    

                        "Conformance to specifications may be achieved in several ways:  

                        1. By careful inspection, sorting the bad from the good. Dependence on inspection is hazardous and costly.  

                        2. By work on the production process to shrink variation about the nominal value."
                    

                    

The approach presented in this Guide embraces regulatory guidance and Deming's principle that demonstration of conformance to specifications can be achieved by developing a process, which addresses causes and concerns for variability and reduces reliance on inspection. Thus, the focus of the Quality Unit should be to ensure adherence to a process that identifies CAs/CDEs and confirms that they have been satisfactorily tested.

The Engineering Quality Process should include aspects that support the C&Q effort, such as maintenance and calibration, change management program, critical utilities, and the procedures for the C&Q process.

                    

The approach described in this section is based on the six-system inspection model described in the FDA 2006 Guidance, whereby an organization's overall quality system provides the foundation for the five manufacturing systems that function within it. The six systems are as follows:

                    

                        
- Quality system

                        
- Production system

- Laboratory control system

                        
- Materials system

Product and process development/knowledge may be added to this list or may be included as part of the production system.

                    

As part of the overall PQS, the Engineering Quality Process plays a significant role in providing the practices and controls within the **facilities and equipment system**.

                    

The application of an Engineering Quality Process also supports other requirements, such as for HSE and efficiency. Understanding this broader context and embracing Engineering Quality Process as a philosophy, rather than simply a collection of tools and templates, supports the design of a sound C&Q program that delivers systems suitable for the intended purpose using cost-effective solutions.

                    

An Engineering Quality Process can help to transform the way in which organizations currently operate and to reinforce the efficient implementation of a science and risk-based approach to facility start-up. The Engineering Quality Process has additional objectives, including to:

                    

                        
- Provide a lean framework to foster innovation, encourage (rather than obstruct) continual improvement, and facilitate the effective, cost-efficient, and focused application of time and resources

                        
- Provide the framework for linking the CAs to the CDEs and the mechanism to capture the traceability

- Acknowledge the importance of personnel development by facilitating an SME-led environment which accredits or trains those individuals that are responsible for executing these practices

- Implementing the Engineering Quality Process by senior management, which requires engineering and Quality Unit sponsorship

                        
- Implementing a proposed C&Q process (transforming current practice to a desired practice) with stakeholders engaged

                        
- Identifying stakeholders

                        
- Defining roles and responsibilities

                        
- Identifying where the organization or site is in terms of implementation of the new C&Q process and the pathway for implementation

                        
- Establishing what the stakeholders can contribute to the proposed process

                        
- Measuring and maintaining progress, suitability, and effectiveness

                        
- Informing and getting input from stakeholders on the proposed C&Q process

                    

                    

When properly applied, the Engineering Quality Process achieves the following:

                    

                        
- Delivers systems that are suitable for the intended purpose

                        
- Provides a systematic engineering structure to define, develop, and implement efficient streamlined engineering processes

                        
- Enables the assurance of quality and compliance with corporate policies, external regulations, and industry standards

                        
- Reflects the scope and extent of the C&Q program, and provides the associated tools and templates

                        
- Incorporates technical expertise and best practices

                    

                    

Once the Engineering Quality Process is established, the relevant internal and external engineering organizations should be appropriately trained on the Engineering Quality Process program and associated tools (including GEP).

                    

In addition, the Engineering Quality Process should be sufficiently flexible in its operation to obtain the benefits of standardized work practices by providing a toolbox of templates and forms without imposing unnecessary duplication where information is available in alternative formats and of an acceptable quality. The Engineering Quality Process should both facilitate and encourage the use of knowledge generated in a previous stage/step which is relevant to the C&Q activity currently being undertaken.

## 11.3 Deliverables — Document Hierarchy

                

Policies → Standards → Procedures → Executable Documents — Pages 86–87

            

        

        

            

                

                    

🌎 Original Text — English

                    

The main deliverable from a quality system is to ensure that systems are operating as intended as captured in documentation. A document hierarchy within the Engineering Quality Process helps to identify where the requirements/expectations are derived. Figure 11.1 illustrates a typical document hierarchy for a quality system.

                    

**Figure 11.1: Typical Document Hierarchy for Quality System**

                    

On the top of the hierarchy are **policies/directives**, which are the high-level documents that capture the expectations of the organization. The expectations for C&Q may be that the systems used to manufacture products have been qualified to be suitable for the intended purpose. These expectations may be part of an overall validation policy or detailed in an independent policy.

                    

The next level is **standards**, which identify the minimum deliverables that are required for implementation of the program. The intent is to avoid different parts of the organization interpreting the intent of the policies/directives differently. For C&Q, the deliverable may be a Qualification Summary Report. Standards should:

                    

                        
- Reference any specific relevant regulatory authority guidance

                        
- Be used to implement a harmonized approach throughout the organization — this should be an organization-wide model with a process for sites to learn from each other and practice a wider continual improvement path

                        
- Limit special conditions — the standard should not make allowances for individual site preferences or commitments, but should include a process to grant exceptions after proper review and approval

                    

                    

The next level is **procedures**, which may establish steps to be followed for the implementation of standards. Procedures may be corporate or site-specific documents. Procedures may be used for multiple sites but should avoid the term "global" since not all sites in the organization may follow them. Examples of expected procedures are for:

                    

                        
- URS

                        
- System Risk Assessment (identification of CAs/CDEs)

                        
- DR/DQ

                        
- Protocols/Plans

                        
- Engineering Change Management

                        
- Reports

                    

                    

The bottom level of the hierarchy is **executable documents**, which are the plans, test scripts, reports, etc. that document compliance to the established requirements.

                    

In summary, the Engineering Quality Process should:

                    

                        
- Define how oversight by the Quality Unit on the program or project will be accomplished

                        
- Define approvers of documentation — the Quality Unit does not need to approve every document

                        
- Establish the linkage to the QRM process for C&Q (System Risk Assessment as described in Chapter 4)

                        
- Address integrated C&Q efforts — e.g., integrate the C&Q process so that one set of testing and documentation can satisfy C&Q requirements

                        
- Address what can be done as part of start-up and how much shakedown is done before documentation starts

📖 Original Text (English)

          

### 12.1 Introduction

          

Change management processes should be established and applied throughout the lifecycle of manufacturing systems. The risk-based application of SME-led change management is one of the biggest challenges in the implementation of QRM-based C&Q as it applies the principles of ICH Q9. This Guide presents an approach which involves two modes of change management:

          

            
- Engineering Change Management (ECM)

            
- Quality Change Control (QCC)

          

          

Project change management that covers the elements of project scope, cost, and schedule is outside the scope of this Guide.

          

A system is considered to be changed when it is modified, altered, added to, removed, or improved in a way that makes its functions, physical features, or performance different from what they were prior to the change.

          

Replacing an existing part of a system with an identical one (like-for-like) may not be considered as a change. Organizations should define and follow a robust assessment process for classifying changes as like-for-like.

          

#### 12.1.1 Purpose and Benefits

          

Change management is a documented program that:

          

            
- Defines what is subject to change

            
- Determines the risk for product impact caused by the change

            
- Establishes a process for initiation, review, and approval of the request for change

            
- Defines when the Quality Unit should be involved

            
- Establishes a process for evaluation of the actual impact of the change

          

          

Change management is needed for the following reasons:

          

            
- Ensures control of the impact on the current state of the system

            
- Provides a basis for peer (SME) review and approval

            
- Is a regulatory requirement (e.g., 21 CFR 211.100 and EU GMP Volume 4 §5.23)

            
- Is a good business practice

          

          

Change management processes should not be (or perceived to be) an obstruction to fostering innovation and continual improvement. Efficient use of change management adds value by delivering technically sound solutions in an efficient manner.

ECM is a key component of the Engineering Quality Process. It provides the primary control mechanism and is fundamental in supporting the execution of regulatory/quality change control for CQAs. Appropriate SMEs, process owners, and the Quality Unit (where CAs/CDEs are impacted) should be involved in the evaluation, review, and approval.

          

There are three primary areas of application for ECM:

          

            
- Provide lifecycle management for systems supporting both GMP and non-GMP regulated processes during operation

            
- Provide the primary change management mechanism during a project phase including work intended to support C&Q (e.g., during DR/DQ, fabrication, FAT/SAT, and verification)

The review and approval method for the ECM system should enable effective and rapid change management that is sufficiently flexible to adapt to:

          

            
- The stage of the project and related regulatory impact

            
- The impact on manufacturing, maintenance, and system lifecycle

During the operational phase of a system, ECM should continue to be applied to **non-critical aspects** of the system. Each change should be evaluated on whether CAs/CDEs are affected.

## 12.5 Engineering Change Management Process — ECM

        

Pages 94–95

      

    

    

      

        
        

          

📖 Original Text (English)

          

ECM is defined as a process by which qualified representative(s) reviews proposed changes for its impact, approves or denies the requests, and manages and tracks their implementation. ECM is a good engineering practice to effectively manage project execution.

          

An ECM process may contain the following attributes:

          

            
- Recorded name of the originator and date

            
- Description of the change, including the affected system or area and intended purpose

            
- Assessment of the potential impact of the change on:
              

                
    - Quality requirements

                
    - Project schedule

                
    - Project budget (relative to the latest approved budget)

                
    - System scope, design, or performance requirements (including safety, operability, reliability, construction, commissioning, operations, and maintenance)

                
    - Other systems

                
    - Engineering documentation

                
    - Qualification documents (including system classification)

              

            

            
- Notification of the proposed change to stakeholders

            
- Record of the approval or denial of the proposed change

            
- Tracking through completion

          

          

#### ECM Application by Project Phase:

          

**Project Conceptual Phase:** There needs to be a clear definition of which documents are controlled by QA and where QA is only involved on request. Key product and process knowledge documents (such as the URS, pilot scale process reports, System Risk Assessment, and VMP) provide the basis for basic design and the requirements for CQAs and CPPs. Changes to these key documents should be addressed using ECM with Quality Unit approval.

          

**Design Phase:** Change management should be applied in alignment with the principles of GEP, with approval by SMEs. The mechanics of proposing, approving, implementing, and tracking changes should be simple since the design phase is very fluid. At the completion of the design phase, DR/DQ is completed. At this point, ECM is applied to GEP changes, and ECM with Quality Unit approval is applied to changes in CAs/CDEs.

          

**Construction, C&Q Phase:** For large projects, the site and project are primarily under the control of a contract organization. Changes should be implemented using the contract organization's change management policy with input by the owner. During construction, many changes are driven by the reality of the installed environment versus the design intent. Typical changes include:

          

            
- *Field inspections and testing performance* — Controlled by ECM; may require retesting post-change

            
- *Departures from specification*:
              

                
    - Readily corrected (e.g., pipe labels): Correct, reinspect, and document

                
    - Corrected later (or impacting other inspections/tests): Use discrepancy/punch list or change management

                
    - Cannot correct: Needs evaluation by the technical reviewer

              

            

            
- *Deviations* — Only occur during performance testing when a product/process user requirement is not met. Steps:
              

                
    - Documentation and evaluation by Quality and Manufacturing Units

                
    - Determination of an assignable cause

                
    - Corrective action: definition, approval, implementation, test, confirmation, and documentation

              

            

          

          

Correction of deviations should be addressed in the following preferred order:

          

            
1. Design change

            
2. Installation change

            
3. Operational change

          

          

Non-correctable deviations may require an SOP or other procedural change (procedural control) to compensate for the shortcoming.

## 13.3 Document Execution —

        

13.3.1 Test Execution Basics & 13.3.2 Good Test Execution Documentation | Pages 99–102

      

    

    

      

        
        

          

📖 Original Text (English)

          

### 13.3.1 Test Execution Basics

          

#### 13.3.1.1 Executor Qualification and Signature Log

          

Individuals who enter information in a test document (i.e., executors) must possess the education, training, experience, or any combination thereof to perform their work and record their printed name, signature, and initials in a signature log. An executor **cannot review or approve his/her own work**.

          

#### 13.3.1.2 Contemporaneous Records

          

Documents should be completed as work is performed. Results should be recorded **at the time of activity**. Backdating is not permitted.

          

The steps within a test case should be executed in order unless specified otherwise. Provide an explanation for any step or task performed out of sequence.

          

If a test script takes more than one calendar day to execute, indicate where testing stopped and initial and date this entry at the appropriate test step.

          

#### 13.3.1.3 Transcribed Data

          

It is permissible to transcribe data from one form to another (e.g., printed tabular summary of manual entries or manually transcribing single entries from computer generated printouts). Transcribed data should be **signed and dated**. The date of the actual data should be included. Transcribed data should be **checked by a second person**. If data is transcribed, the raw data should be included — labeled, initialed and dated.

          

#### 13.3.1.4 Printouts

          

Test steps should indicate when printouts are to be used. Printouts should be attached to the test plan/report appendix or contained in the appropriate reference file.

          

Where the output is from a thermal printer (e.g., for a particle counter), a photocopy should be used, signed and dated as a true copy by the individual responsible for the test. A footnote should be annotated in the test document to reference the location of the original.

          

#### 13.3.1.5 Supporting Documentation

          

Attach or reference supporting documentation as necessary. Original data sheets and checklists should be available for review until the transcribed document has been accepted and approved. Refer to organization requirements or relevant regional regulations regarding any specific documentation requirements prior to destroying supporting documentation for GMP records/documents.

          

#### 13.3.1.6 Proper Handling of Documents

          

It is understood and accepted that testing documents will experience some wear and tear typical of field executed documentation. However, care should be taken to protect documents from spills, excessive folding, or other physical damage.

          

#### 13.3.1.7 Document Changes

          

Changes to approved documents require approval by representatives of the **original approving groups**.

          

### 13.3.2 Good Test Execution Documentation

          

The typical requirements for recording a GEP document should include:

          

            
- Use permanent, non-fading, non-smearing ink.
              

                
    - Blue or black ink is preferred.

                
    - If colors are used for redline drawings, the definition of color meaning must be clear.

                
    - GEP field documentation recorded in pencil: a photocopy should be used, signed and dated as a true copy.

              

            

            
- Ditto marks (") or arrows (↓) to indicate duplicate entries are **not permitted**.

            
- Writing over an existing entry is **not permitted** (see Section 13.5 for corrections).

            
- No scribbling on the record. When adding notes, use unique alpha/numeric identifiers to trace each entry to the corresponding footnote.

            
- If a signature log is maintained, initials are acceptable in lieu of signatures everywhere *except for document approvals*.

            
- Initial and date any handwritten changes or comments. If the reason for the change is not clear, provide an explanatory note.

            
- Initial and date each page with an entry at least once.

            
- Date format needs to be unambiguous. **DD-MMM-YYYY is preferred** (e.g., 30-MAY-2019).

            
- Time format: use 24-hour clock (e.g., 1425) or am/pm (e.g., 2:25 pm).

            
- For significant figures:
              

                
    - Use a zero before a decimal point if there is no other digit.

                
    - Record all figures as shown on the measuring instrument or readout.

                
    - Ensure calculations indicate the correct unit of measure and use the correct number of significant digits.

                
    - For final reported data, round to the number of places that agrees with the stated specification or limit.

              

            

            
- For quantitative results, record the number. Examples of **unacceptable entries**:
              

                
    - "Pass", "Fail", "As Expected", "Same", "OK", "Yes", Checkmark "✓"

              

            

            
- Record the observed system response from the test step execution in the results column. Use unambiguous terminology; it is not necessary to record verbatim the expected results.

            
- Line out, initial, and date all blank spaces. Be careful to distinguish "N/A" (Not Applicable) and "N/AV" (Not Available). "N/A" should only be used prior to approval to execute.

            
- Where entire sections, columns, rows, or whole pages are not utilized, cross out with a single diagonal line; initial and date.

            
- Data superseded by a subsequent test or inspection may be deleted. The final record should be annotated to indicate if a repair, engineering change, or correction has been made.

            
- Test reports should include a summary of the results including a conclusion that the system is or is not suitable for the intended purpose.

            
- The completed record should be able to be **copied/scanned legibly**.

### 13.7 Storage, Retrieval, and Retention of Documents

          

Methods of document storage should be established, as dictated by organization/site policies and procedures and in a manner that allows documents to be located and retrieved within a reasonable time period, as required. The methods should include access control and tracking. The storage area should be secure.

          

Copies and original documents should be labeled as such. Executed documents can be scanned after final approval and archived as electronic records in a validated document management system. Once archiving is completed, the hard copy can be destroyed. Retention of records should follow organization/site specific records management policy. Check the specific requirements of authorities for the regions that the site will supply before destroying GMP records/documents.

          

### 13.8 Disaster Recovery

          

There should be a system in place to handle the reconstruction of records. All measures should be taken to recover the original document. For a record that is lost or destroyed and not recoverable, a copy of the original record is acceptable as the official copy, which should be approved by Management.

          

            **Note:** Storage and management of documents during the execution of a project should be defined to minimize risk of loss and/or damage.

### 14.1 Introduction

                    

This chapter describes strategies how organizations can effectively transition to the science and risk-based C&Q process described in this Guide. Implementing change successfully often requires high-level sponsorship due to the numerous stakeholders impacted.

                    

The key to a successful transition is to recognize that there are multiple ways that the regulatory expectations can be met; adopting the science and risk-based approach does **not invalidate current work or existing documentation**. It should be noted that any process used for C&Q is expected to be science and risk-based.

                    

                        Qualification documents represent a picture of the system at a point in time; this information changes with time and there are quality systems in place to manage this change (including associated revision of the qualification and engineering record documents), such as change management, maintenance, calibration, and periodic review. Therefore, existing documentation can remain acceptable to provide the documented evidence that the system is suitable for the intended purpose.

### 14.2 Preparing for Implementation

                    

The suggested steps for implementation preparation are described in more detail as follows:

                    

                        
1. 
                            **Define the organization approach:**  

                            This Guide describes a suggested approach for organizations to adopt, but it may need to be tailored to fit the specific structure of the organization.
                        

                        
2. 
                            **Evaluate the organizational maturity:**  

                            It is useful to perform a documented assessment of organizational maturity, considering the quality systems and GEP, to determine the degree and pace of organizational transition. The assessment should consider all relevant aspects (e.g., experience, personnel resources, organizational culture). Examples of criteria for relevant departments include:
                            

                                
    - *Research and Development/Process Development:* Does this department have robust documentation and clear communication channels to provide data to enable the process?

                                
    - *Quality:* Is the Quality Unit open to an alternative approach to C&Q and to the different documentation deliverables?

                                
    - *Engineering:* Is the engineering department ready to deliver documents per Good Documentation Practice? Do they have adequate change management systems in place? Is there adequate document storage?

                                
    - *System Owners:* Are the system owners adequately trained to perform the necessary reviews?

                            

                        

                        
3. 
                            **Develop an implementation plan:**  

                            A critical requirement for successful implementation is to have a well-defined implementation strategy with support from senior management. The use of initial small-scale proof of concept projects can help to identify any program aspects that require augmentation for a specific organization.
                        

                        
4. 
                            **Develop supporting quality systems and associated procedures:**  

                            The procedures defining the engineering, quality, and documentation standards, as covered in this Guide, should be developed.
                        

                        
5. 
                            **Train staff on the approach:**  

                            An important aspect for implementation is to provide overview training and ensure there is a good understanding of the key concepts. Considerations should also be made for how to present the approach to regulatory inspectors.
                        

                        
6. 
                            **Train staff on the procedures:**  

                            The relevant staff should be sufficiently trained on the new procedures and have a good understanding of the overall program and its goals.

### 14.3 Approaches for Implementation

                    

For new projects or facilities, the use of a **champion** for the new approach is recommended. This individual helps with the transition and implementation of the new approach and should have a good understanding of the process concepts/goals.

                    

While implementation is more straightforward for new systems (for which there are no existing procedures/documentation), organizations also need to determine how the approach can be applied to a change on an existing qualified system. The most cost-effective way for implementation of the risk-based approach is to **supplement the legacy approach as gaps are identified or changes to the system are made**, thus maximizing the use of existing documentation.

                    

A best practice approach would be to first assess if the system requires qualification — does the system have the potential to impact product quality? (see Chapter 3 for system classification criteria)

                    

                        
- If it does **not** have the potential to impact product quality, then recategorize the system and commission the changes.

                        
- If it **has** the potential to impact product quality, then it requires qualification:
                            

                                
    - Review the existing documentation to determine if it meets current expectations.

                                
    - If it does not, then qualify the system using the new approach, and remediate any gaps identified.

### 15.14 Chapter 14: Implementation of Science and Risk-Based C&Q Process

                    

Regulatory guidance highlights the importance of product quality risk management and risk assessment to determine the scope and extent of qualification as described in the following regulatory text:

                    

                        "A quality risk management approach should be applied throughout the lifecycle of a medicinal product. As part of a quality risk management system, decisions on the scope and extent of qualification and validation should be based on a justified and documented risk assessment of the facilities, equipment, utilities and processes." [EU GMP Annex 15]
                    

                    

The entire framework of Chapter 14's implementation strategy is underpinned by this single principle: the scope and extent of C&Q activities should be proportionate to risk, determined by a justified and documented risk assessment. This applies equally to the decision of how to implement the new approach — whether for new systems, existing systems with minor changes, or existing systems requiring major remediation.

### Implementation Summary: The ISPE Vol.5 Complete Lifecycle

                    

This section represents the culmination of the ISPE Baseline Guide Vol.5 Commissioning and Qualification (2nd Edition). The Guide has described a complete, science and risk-based framework for C&Q that:

                    

                        
- Starts with understanding intended use (URS, Chapter 2)

                        
- Classifies systems by GMP impact (Chapter 3)

                        
- Uses risk assessment to scope qualification activities (Chapter 4)

                        
- Reviews and qualifies design (Chapter 5)

                        
- Plans C&Q activities systematically (Chapter 6)

                        
- Executes testing based on risk-identified CDEs (Chapter 7)

                        
- Formally accepts and releases qualified systems (Chapter 8)

                        
- Maintains qualified state through periodic review (Chapter 9)

                        
- Leverages vendor data appropriately (Chapter 10)

                        
- Ensures engineering quality throughout (Chapter 11)

                        
- Manages changes systematically (Chapter 12)

                        
- Documents everything per GDocP (Chapter 13)

                        
- Implements strategically with legacy consideration (Chapter 14)

                    

                    

All of this is underpinned by regulatory expectations from FDA, EU GMP Annex 15, and ICH Q8/Q9/Q10, as documented in Appendix 1 (Chapter 15).
