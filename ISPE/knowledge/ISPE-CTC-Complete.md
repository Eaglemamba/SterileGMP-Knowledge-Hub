## Chapter 1 — Introduction |

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

                    

A **Controlled Temperature Chamber** is defined as a system, unit, equipment, or room in which the environmental conditions (usually temperature) of a chamber are controlled/maintained/regulated to meet specific user requirements.

                    

The ISPE Good Practice Guide: Controlled Temperature Chamber Mapping and Monitoring expands on the ISPE Concept Paper on Controlled Temperature Chamber Mapping to include topics such as:

                    

                        
- Commissioning

                        
- Testing strategies

                        
- Acceptance criteria

                        
- Qualification approaches

                        
- System monitoring

                        
- Operational issues

                        
- Periodic review

                    

                    

                        "Types of Controlled Temperature Chambers considered by this guide range from purchased Commercial Off the Shelf items, such as freezers and incubators, walk-in cold rooms and walk-in freezers, to custom built units, such as warehouses."
                    

                    

This Guide provides industry good practice for the temperature mapping of controlled temperature chambers, development of test acceptance criteria and a risk based approach to practices for periodic review of system performance.

## 1.1 Purpose and Objectives |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

With the increasing complexity of global distribution for medicines requiring controlled temperatures and the recently introduced regulations, the ISPE Good Practice Guide was developed by an industry team in order to provide guidance on good practices for:

                    

                        
- Definition of requirements (producing a user requirements document)

                        
- Design

                        
- Purchasing

                        
- Commissioning (including temperature mapping)

                        
- Qualification

                        
- Maintenance of controlled temperature chambers, warehouses, and refrigerated storage areas

                    

                    

These systems are used to store raw material, work in progress, or finished product, and operate under current Good Manufacturing Practice.

                    

The approach described is consistent with that described in the ISPE Good Practice Guide: Cold Chain Management.

## 1.2 Scope |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

This Guide provides guidance on Controlled Temperature Chambers operating under Current Good Manufacturing Practices (CGMPs). Types of Controlled Temperature Chambers considered by this guide include:

                    

                        
- Purchased "commercial off the shelf" items (such as freezers, refrigerators, and incubators)

                        
- Warm rooms designed to maintain consistent temperatures

                        
- Ovens

                        
- Custom built units, such as controlled room temperature environments (e.g., warehouses, walk-in cold rooms and freezers)

                    

                    

This Guide does **not** include systems designed for heating/cooling of material such as blast freezers, although some of the concepts described may be useful when commissioning/qualifying these systems.

## Freezers /

            

-20°C to -196°C storage — design, defrost cycles, and GMP monitoring requirements

        

    

    

        

            
            

                

Original Text (ISPE 2016, pp.16–18)

                

### 4.1 Freezers

                

Freezers are used for short to long term preservation of pharmaceutical and biopharmaceutical materials, including: Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients (APIs), process intermediates, stability samples, laboratory materials, and cell banks. Temperature uniformity is maintained within a qualified range to ensure product quality is preserved. Freezers with set points ranging from -20°C to -80°C (-4°F to -112°F) are frequently used.

                

Operation can be controlled via a temperature controller that maintains the locally configured temperature set point and also provides a local alarm. Manual defrost chambers are usually equipped with the general freezer refrigeration components of: compressor, receiver, condenser, evaporator, tubing, insulation, and structural support.

                

The refrigerant compressors are usually hermetically sealed for integrity. The evaporator is typically a tube and fin design. The chamber control monitors interior temperature using a permanently installed thermocouple or Resistance Temperature Detector (RTD) — referred to as the **control probe**. Typically, the control probe is calibrated at the factory based on the temperature at the geometric center of the chamber. The evaporator is usually located within the upper portion of the chamber interior and chamber temperature uniformity is obtained through natural air circulation.

                

For units with automatic defrost, the control actuates an automatic defrost cycle at a predetermined frequency (typically 6 to 24 hours) to minimize ice accumulation. The defrost cycle is terminated by: elapsed time, evaporator coil temperature set point, or a combination of time and temperature. The frequency of defrost is adjustable to allow for conditions of use (frequency of door openings, length of time for door openings, and exterior air humidity level).

                

**For GMP applications these systems should be monitored by an independent system.** It can be difficult to calibrate the system control sensor, and the use of an independent system allows routine calibration, and the flexibility to place the sensor in a location that measures the "worst case" temperature. This system is used as the quality "system of record" to provide the data used to demonstrate that materials are being stored at the specified conditions.

                

### 4.1.1 Conditioning / Blast Freezers

                

Facilities may process large quantities of bulk product that needs to be frozen from ambient conditions to a specified temperature, e.g., -70°C, prior to transfer into -70°C storage locations. Dedicated conditioning freezers are needed to perform this function, as storage freezers are not capable of handling the large volume of product and significant temperature disparity without potentially compromising the temperature of stored material. This type of equipment is outside the scope of this guide.

                

### 4.1.2 Liquid Nitrogen Freezers

                

Used for storage of material below -120°C (-184°F), e.g., cell banks. If materials are immersed into liquid nitrogen, they eventually assume the temperature of the liquid (-196°C). Materials stored in the vapor above the liquid become warmer as they are moved away from the liquid (up to -120°C). The temperature of the vapor can be changed by varying the amount of liquid nitrogen in the chamber.

## Refrigerators /

            

2–8°C storage — forced air vs. convection, wire shelving, auto-defrost

        

    

    

        

            

                

Original Text (ISPE 2016, p.17)

                

### 4.2 Refrigerators

                

Refrigerators are typically used to store material and vaccines within a qualified range of 2°C to 8°C (36°F to 46°F). The capability of the equipment to achieve temperature uniformity should be considered when assigning the chamber set point.

                

Operation is typically controlled via a temperature controller that is used to maintain the locally configured temperature set point, and may also provide a local alarm. Refrigerators may be equipped with fans which cool via forced air circulation and utilize a hermetically sealed compressor and refrigeration system. Alternatively, refrigerators may use a heat exchanger located in the storage space, relying on convection to maintain an even temperature.

                

**Refrigerators should have wire shelving to improve air circulation.**

                

Refrigerators will usually automatically defrost the evaporator to maintain system performance. Evaporator defrost occurs by re-directing the hot refrigerant gas into the evaporator coil for a short time to melt any ice/frost accumulation.

## Cold Rooms /

            

Walk-in cold rooms — PLC control, redundant condensing, multi-sensor configurations

        

    

    

        

            

                

Original Text (ISPE 2016, pp.17–18)

                

### 4.3 Cold Rooms

                

Cold rooms are typically used for storage of material within a qualified range of 2°C to 8°C (36°F to 46°F). Cold rooms should be equipped with a refrigeration compressor/condensing unit. The air-cooled condensing unit is usually located outside, e.g., on the roof of the building. Cold rooms may be provided with redundant condensing systems, each capable of handling the total system load.

                

Walk-in cold rooms may be prefabricated, metal-clad structures, furnished and installed as self-contained units. These may include all essential refrigeration equipment, controls, air circulation, and other equipment necessary to reach the specified environmental conditions.

                

Cold room control systems usually consist of a local **Programmable Logic Controller (PLC)** to monitor and control the temperature within the cold room. Within the main chamber, the temperature may be measured by temperature sensors placed in different locations. One sensor should be routed to the main controller for controlling interior temperature. The second sensor should be connected to a monitoring system for temperature trending and alarm; for larger units there may be multiple monitoring sensors.

### 4.4 Stability Chambers, Incubators / Incubator Shakers

                

Stability chambers are chambers that are designed to maintain a stable environment for incubating materials or holding samples. These may include cultures for microorganisms or cell culture and hybridization in production or Quality Control laboratories. The incubator may accommodate cell culture vessels from small tissue culture flasks to roller bottles and small spinner flasks.

                

A local controller should control system operation for process parameters such as temperature, humidity, and CO₂. Incubator shakers can also control shaker speed (Revolutions Per Minute (RPM) of the drive).

## Scope of Commissioning /

            

Standard catalogue systems vs. site-built systems — documentation expectations

        

    

    

        

            

                

Original Text (ISPE 2016, pp.19–20)

                

### 5.1 Scope of the Commissioning

                

The commissioning process should be a well-planned, documented, and managed engineering approach to the start-up and turnover of both the system and the equipment to the End User. Commissioning should result in a safe and functional environment that meets established design requirements and stakeholder expectations (see also the ISPE Baseline® Guide: Commissioning and Qualification). The complexity of commissioning can vary, depending on the type of system.

                

If a risk-based approach is being used, the commissioning documentation may be used to provide evidence of the installation and operation. The commissioning documentation needs to be of an acceptable standard. Typical expectations for the commissioning documentation are provided (see Section 6).

                

### 5.1.1 Standard "Catalogue" Systems

                

Standard "Catalogue" Systems are comprised of mechanical components assembled and/or produced by a manufacturer for general industry applications. There is a limited standard menu of options with respect to hardware and/or software. A manufacturer normally provides a standard operations manual that applies to the operation and maintenance of the system. Standard "Catalogue" Systems are usually set to work following the manufacturer's instructions. Examples include freestanding freezers and refrigerators.

                

Commissioning is usually simpler for standard catalogue equipment, as the control system is an integrated part of the standard catalogue system, with data on all the components contained within the supplier documentation. A typical commissioning approach could follow the steps listed below:

                

                    
- Recording of system data

                    
- Verification of correct installation (installation verification)

                    
- Confirmation of satisfactory operation / temperature mapping (operational verification)

                

                

Generally, Standard "Catalogue" Systems have been set up in a factory by the manufacturer; typically, the scope of work does not include mapping — this is usually added to the vendor's requirements.

                

### 5.1.2 Site (Custom) Built Systems

                

Site (Custom) Built Systems normally require additional verification to ensure that operation is as specified, usually with more adjustment (set up and tuning) of the refrigeration and associated control systems, in order to ensure that performance meets the user requirements and design specifications.

## Commissioning Strategy /

            

Commissioning Plan objectives, roles, and acceptance criteria definition

        

    

    

        

            

                

Original Text (ISPE 2016, pp.20–22)

                

### 5.2.1 Execution Process

                

The testing strategy and the acceptance criteria should be documented in a Commissioning Plan prior to test execution. The objective is to:

                

                    
- Show that the work will meet Owner and regulatory agency expectations

                    
- Provide clear and early definition of deliverables and handover requirements

                    
- Define roles and responsibilities

                    
- Define the Engineering Change Management System

                    
- Define the approach to be used for training of the maintenance department and End Users

                    
- Ensure consistency of approach and testing across the project

                    
- Ensure full transparency on progress metrics (test execution and productivity)

                    
- Scope the testing required

                    
- Provide review and approval of test method statements

                    
- Provide progress reporting

                    
- Provide reporting and handling of test discrepancies

                    
- Define the location and marking (identification) of mapping points

                    
- Define the acceptance criteria / expected results

                    
- Witnessing requirements and notification procedures

                    
- Handling/retention of raw data

                    
- Format and content of the commissioning report

                    
- Defined responsibilities for operating and maintaining a facility during commissioning

                    
- Identify who is responsible for maintaining the operating log from start-up to handover

                    
- Define how handover is to be managed

                

                

The Commissioning Plan should also define how safety is considered. When executing the plan, Commissioning and Qualification may be performed in two distinct phases, or the phases can be combined.

                

*Note: acceptance criteria are "non-negotiable" test results; expected results may be acceptable across a broader range, or are results taken for reference purposes.*

                

### 5.2.2 Roles and Coordination

                

Typical roles and responsibilities of parties involved include:

                

                    
- **Commissioning Lead** — responsible for coordinating and managing commissioning activities and allocating resources; writes and/or executes the activity; responsible for accuracy of content

                    
- **System Owner** — reviews content; by signing the Plan and Test Report, agrees to the content; has decision making authority and is accountable for compliance to the URS; during construction, determines acceptance of the "as built" system at mechanical completion

                    
- **Commissioning Team Member** — involved in execution, documentation, and expertise (site activities: verification, safety analysis, protocol execution, punch items, maintenance training; office activities: document preparation, red-line updating, archiving)

                    
- **Engineering/Technical Services and Validation Group** — on distribution list; may review as SMEs but do not take direct action on commissioning documents

## Commissioning at the Design Phase /

            

Design Review, FMEA risk assessment, URS verification, utility interactions

        

    

    

        

            

                

Original Text (ISPE 2016, pp.22–23)

                

### 5.3.1 The Design Review

                

From the commissioning perspective, the Design Review is intended for the commissioning team to become familiar with the project and to verify that the design has adequate provision for the tests required, as defined in the equipment specification and URS.

                

Requirements in the URS should be clarified with attendees. Key design documents should then be reviewed. Issues relevant to continuous operation of the Chamber are critical aspects to consider and include:

                

                    
- Chamber loading scenarios

                    
- Loading and unloading of products

                    
- External/adjacent environmental conditions

                

                

Typical documents for verification include:

                

                    
- Layout and HVAC zoning layout

                    
- Room environmental parameter schedule

                    
- Piping and Instrumentation Diagram (P&ID)

                    
- Functional specifications

                

                

During the Design Review phase, documents relevant to the areas surrounding the chamber and to the utilities serving it (e.g., hot water, chilled/glycol water, electricity) should be included in the review in order to properly understand the interaction between the Controlled Temperature Chamber and the rest of the facility.

                

The Commissioning Team should define the list and sequence of at-site activities and verifications needed, and complete the Commissioning Plan. A tailored approach is usually required as the sequence and activities are dependent upon the specific site conditions, utilities available, operating conditions and type of Controlled Temperature Chamber.

                

In this phase, the Commissioning Team should be given opportunity to provide suggestions to the designer, in order to obtain a design that will facilitate Commissioning Tests and activities. Suggestions may include availability of access to regulation points, provision of testing points in an appropriate location, or access ports to the room for installation of cabling, if required during temperature mapping.

                

### 5.3.2 Design Risk Assessment

                

A risk assessment may be used to help critique and develop a robust design. A Failure Mode Effects Analysis (FMEA) risk assessment can be used to identify possible failures that could lead to the loss of the required performance, identified by review of design and vendor documentation.

                

Risks should be evaluated as to their probability and severity, in order to determine the impact to process variation and effect.

                

Unacceptable risks should be mitigated following the preferred hierarchy of:

                

                    
- Elimination by design

                    
- Elimination by automated control

                    
- Elimination by procedural controls

                

                

The risk management requirements identified should be designated as critical and include all components, functions, and features of the design that serve to control risks. These functions and features should be tested during commissioning.

## Commissioning During Installation /

            

Physical inspection, safety checks, setting to work, functional testing, red-line drawings, FAT/SAT

        

    

    

        

            

                

Original Text (ISPE 2016, pp.23–26)

                

### 5.4 Commissioning Activities during the Installation Phase

                

Commissioning activities may be divided into four broad categories:

                

                    
1. **Inspection of the physical installation** — field verification of installation drawings and documentation/test verification (e.g., pipework pressure and evacuation tests) to ensure compliance with design specifications

                    
2. **Pre-operational safety checks**

                    
3. **Setting to Work** — setting a static system into motion ("shake down") and regulation and adjustment

                    
4. **Functional testing** — regulation, adjustment, and functional testing may be iterative activities within fine tuning of the system

                

                

### 5.4.1 Inspection of the Physical Installation

                

The Commissioning Team scope should include confirmation that:

                

                    
- The equipment and materials are checked to ensure compliance with design data sheet and specifications

                    
- Installation has been completed in accordance with the design specifications and manufacturer instructions

                    
- As-built documentation is available and correct

                

                

Usually, 100% of the installation is verified (packaged units such as the compressor would be considered as a single system component).

                

It is usually a duty of the Commissioning Team to maintain a **"red line"** copy of the construction record documents. Comments and changes should be marked on the document following a defined procedure, to be turned over to the responsible party for updating the record documents to be provided to the System Owner.

                

Some of these activities may be performed at the vendor's factory and documented within specific **Factory Acceptance Test (FAT)** protocols/reports. Completion of this stage should verify that the installation meets specifications.

                

### 5.4.2 Pre-operational Safety Checks

                

This phase is intended to eliminate as many risks as possible in regard to the safety of personnel during commissioning and also during operation. The "Lock Out/Tag Out" procedure can determine that utilities (chilled water/gas/steam and electrical power) are provided to the system once the following are available and documented:

                

                    
- Conformity of electrical system and correct settings (e.g., overloads/fuse ratings)

                    
- Evidence of pressure test of utilities lines

                    
- Presence of pressure/temperature safety release devices (with right settings)

                

                

### 5.4.3 Setting to Work

                

This is the initial startup and tuning of the system. This may include:

                

                    
- Energizing the system

                    
- Filling with fluids

                    
- Check motor drives (mechanically, electrically) and direction of rotation checks

                    
- Internal layout of the Chamber verification and as-built dimension measurements

                    
- Chamber and doors tightness verification

                    
- Input/Output (I/O) check of the control system

                    
- Alarms check

                    
- Calibration of the instruments

                    
- Balancing and fine tuning

                

                

### 5.4.4 Functional Testing

                

Checks performed in previous phases should have been executed successfully as a prerequisite to functional tests. The system should be tested to confirm that the design performance is achieved within the acceptance ranges specified in all working modes.

                

Where feasible, the worst case conditions for the surrounding environment should be simulated during testing. Where the area being tested has duty/standby conditioning units, these should both be tested to ensure they will perform adequately. Studies of trends of the transition phase between the stop of the main system and the full operation of the standby system should be included in testing.

                

The operation and influence of the surrounding environment on defrosting systems should be verified and documented during the Commissioning and Qualification Phase.

                

### 5.4.5 Engineering Change Management

                

During Design — cumulative changes should not influence the ability of the design to meet user requirements. During Construction and Commissioning — changes proposed should be agreed with by the engineer of record. If a test fails and can be repeated after adjustments, it is acceptable to annotate the test sheet with the adjustments made and repeat the test; where changes are required, an engineering change control should be used to gain approval prior to execution and repeating the tests.

                

Once Qualification is complete, change should be managed through the site GMP change control system.

## Handover, System Manual & Safety /

            

Punch list management, handover certificate, job safety analysis

        

    

    

        

            

                

Original Text (ISPE 2016, pp.26–27)

                

### 5.5 Handover, the System Manual, and Training

                

Handover usually represents turnover of the system to the user, with handover of operation and maintenance. Commissioning and Qualification activities are usually completed. There should be a documented record confirming that the functionality and operation of the system is considered acceptable and Commissioning can be considered complete. This may be the Commissioning or Qualification Summary Report, or a separate handover certificate, depending on site practices.

                

A **punch list (snag list)** is typically used to manage completion of the system to meet the specifications. Major items on this list would prohibit handover, other items that can be resolved following handover are generally accepted. Typical categories used for a punch list include:

                

                    
1. Items that affect performance — to be addressed prior to handover

                    
2. Items that affect safe operation of the equipment — to be addressed prior to handover

                    
3. Items that block next activities/verifications — to be addressed prior to handover

                    
4. Minor items that do not impact operation — to be addressed after handover

                    
5. Desirable changes/improvements — to be addressed after handover through the change control system

                

                

Following handover, responsibility for operation and maintenance of the system should be transferred to End Users and the maintenance team. Appropriate operation and maintenance procedures should be established.

                

### 5.6 Safety

                

A job safety analysis covering all the phases of commissioning should be prepared by the personnel who will execute the site activities. The analysis should describe the potential risks, their probability, and should identify protection devices. Operators or technical personnel should be informed about the outcome of this analysis when receiving permission to execute commissioning activities. When judged appropriate, the testing area or room should have signs informing personnel of risks or forbidding access.

## Outcomes |

                

What temperature mapping is designed to confirm — Source pp. 28

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 6.1 Outcomes

                    

Testing is intended to:

                    

                        
1. Confirm that the space that will be used for storage remains within defined limits with respect to temperature uniformity and stability over time across usable space.

                        
2. Determine locations representing operating temperature extremes to establish the strategy for monitoring and alarming the space used for storage and/or processing.

                    

                    

The operational temperature limits should be defined, together with any allowable excursions (time periods, acceptable limits).

                    

                        

#### Why Two Outcomes, Not One?

                        

Outcome 1 answers: "Does this chamber work uniformly?" Outcome 2 answers: "Where do I put my monitoring sensors permanently?" Both are required — you cannot claim compliance without addressing both.

## Risk Assessment |

                

Factors to evaluate before developing the test strategy — Source pp. 28–29

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 6.2 Risk Assessment

                    

Prior to developing the test strategy, evaluate potential sources of excursions taking into account the following factors:

                    

*(Note: a company may use Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) modeling to evaluate impact of these factors but still needs some level of confirmation of the results through actual testing.)*

                    

                        
1. **Purpose of the facility:** storage, process cooling, transportation preconditioning

                        
2. **Size:** speed and magnitude of changes impacted by the footprint as well as the height, with attention to potential stratification with heights over 15 ft (457 cm)

                        
3. **Layout/configuration:** walls and obstructions, fixed racks, temporary racks, pallets on floors, duct supply and return locations

                        
4. **Location of facility:** geographic with respect to external climate exposures, physical location within temperature-controlled facility, windows, doors, traffic and access frequency

                        
5. **Operating characteristics of the HVAC system:** defrost cycles, redundant systems, number of units supporting storage space and how they interact with each other

                    

                    

                        

#### CFD Modeling — Supplement, Not Substitute

                        

CFD can help visualize airflow patterns and predict temperature gradients before physical testing. However, it cannot account for all real-world variables (door seals, product loading behavior, actual defrost timing). ISPE is explicit: actual testing is always required.

### 6.2.1 Risk-based Approach

                    

With the advent of ASTM-E2500 "Standard Guide for Specification, Design, and Verification of Pharmaceutical and Biopharmaceutical Manufacturing Systems and Equipment," an organization may use a risk-based approach, with the equipment installation and operation being verified. For further information, see:

                    

                        
- The ISPE Good Practice Guide: Cold Chain Management

                        
- The ISPE Guide: Science and Risk-Based Approach for the Delivery of Facilities, Systems and Equipment

                    

                    

There should be a clear definition of the acceptance criteria; typically, this is provided in the URS or equivalent document.

                    

Temperature mapping is used to confirm that the system is operating to meet specifications — i.e., the temperature range within the unit is acceptable.

                    

It is standard practice in the pharmaceutical industry to provide monitoring of critical parameters using an independent system. Advantages include:

                    

                        
- Calibration of the monitoring sensors can be carried out without impacting the system operation

                        
- The independent system becomes the quality "system of record"

                        
- The data can be used to provide continuous verification of acceptable system performance

                    

                    

Mapping results may be used to define the number and location of monitoring sensors for a warehouse, cold room, or chamber.

## When to Perform Temperature Mapping |

                

Timing within commissioning and relationship to supporting systems — Source pp. 30

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 6.3 When to Perform Temperature Mapping

                    

Temperature mapping should start as early as possible during commissioning. This allows:

                    

                        
- Early detection and resolution of any performance issues

                        
- Identification of potential operational constraints to discuss with End Users, to determine if the constraints are acceptable or require resolution

                    

                    

Where there are systems that support operation of a system (e.g., the warehouse, HVAC system, or a condenser water cooling system), these systems should be:

                    

                        
- Identified and commissioned or in a known (and monitored) state prior to temperature mapping

                        
- This helps ensure complete and representative testing, and facilitates investigation in the event of performance problems

                    

                    

                        

#### Acceptable Exception — Chilled Water System

                        

It can be acceptable to temperature map an area served by an HVAC system where a chilled water system has not been fully balanced, IF the chilled water system is known to provide an adequate supply of chilled water at a constant temperature. The BMS/BAS could provide supporting data that the chilled water supply temperature remains within operating limits and that the HVAC cooling control valve does not fully open (indicating adequate supply).

- Equipment receipt and/or installation verification records

                        
- Cable tests (continuity, insulation)

                        
- Grounding (earth) tests

                        
- Motor insulation "megger" and direction of rotation tests

- Control loop wiring checks

                        
- Piping leak (hydrostatic) and evacuation tests

                        
- Pipework cleaning/flushing records

                        
- Record of cleaning for ductwork

                        
- Ductwork and Air Handling Unit (AHU) leakage tests

                        
- Operation and Maintenance manuals

                        
- As-built drawings/specifications

                    

                    

The test requirements will depend on the type of system, and how critical performance is to the product and/or business. Project specifications should define documentation and test requirements.

## Factors Influencing the Mapping Strategy |

                

Comprehensive factor list including load state, probe types, and sampling rate — Source pp. 30–32

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 6.4 Factors Influencing the Mapping Strategy

                    

                        
- **Purpose:** storage, processing, transportation, conditioning

                        
- **Type of facility:** general warehouse, CRT warehouse, cold storage, freezer, refrigerator, incubator, stability chamber

                        
- **Size of facility:** span (air change rates and flow patterns), height (potential stratification)

                        
- **Number of conditioning units controlling environment**

                        
- **Layout/configuration:** open space versus walls, racks, obstructions; fixed vs. variable racks; duct supply/return positions; variable obstructions (moveable pallets, racks)

                        
- **Location:** geographic impact of external climate conditions; physical relationship within facility (conditioned space, windows, doors, exposed walls, high traffic areas, frequency and duration of access)

                        
- **Contents of facility:** motors, coils, lights, people; exothermic/endothermic reactive substances; hot material being cooled within facility

                        
- **HVAC operating characteristics:** defrost cycles, redundancies, interactions between evaporators, Fan Coil Units (FCUs), Direct Expansion (DX) coils; failure modes; hysteresis and time lags

                        
- **Empty versus Full state:**
                            

                                
    - Testing using a simulation of fully loaded conditions should be performed

                                
    - Testing with a partial load can be considered unnecessary (system tested at operating extremes)

                                
    - Note: typically, chambers are tested empty

                            

                        

                        
- **Probe types:** accuracy, reliability, ease of use and calibration — all impact measurement uncertainty

                        
- **Buffered probes:**
                            

                                
    - May be used for monitoring if performance relationship to stored product is known

                                
    - Should NOT be used as controlling probes

                                
    - Should NOT be used for mapping (when goal is to see actual room temperatures)

                            

                        

                        
- **Sample rate:** at least once every 5 minutes; may need to be more frequent if temperature spikes anticipated (defrost, door openings)

                        
- **Operational requirements:** how long doors open, how many people, traffic patterns, equipment

6.4.1

            

                

## Selection of Mapping Equipment |

                

Accuracy requirements, measurement uncertainty, and humidity mapping — Source pp. 31–32

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 6.4.1 Selection of Mapping Equipment

                    

Selection of the equipment to be used for mapping should consider the required accuracy and repeatability related to the predefined acceptance criteria.

                    

**Example:** A unit with acceptance criteria of 2°C to 8°C (USP standard storage conditions). If the mapping system has an accuracy of ±0.5°C, this provides results (allowing for rounding) to meet the acceptance criteria of 2°C to 8°C.

                    

The user requirements may have a tighter design specification, e.g., expected results of 3–7°C.

                    

**Important:** Some low-cost mapping dataloggers have accuracy specifications of ±2°C. Use of these devices would not be appropriate for this application unless the acceptance criteria or expected results were revised to allow for the potential error.

                    

Temperature sensors with a rapid response time are typically used for temperature mapping, to provide accurate information on system performance.

                    

**Humidity mapping considerations:**

                    

                        
- If humidity has been defined as a critical quality attribute, humidity sensors should be included

                        
- If there are no significant sources of humidity in the warehouse, it may be assumed that absolute humidity will equalize — a small number of sensors may suffice (one at low level, one at high level for stratification)

                        
- If there are significant sources of moisture, a greater number of humidity sensors will be required — number and location developed using similar concepts as temperature mapping

                        
- Where RH is the criteria, levels should be checked based on temperature variations using measured RH at the local temperature

                        
- Duplex sensors (T+RH combined) can simplify data analysis

                    

                    

Mapping sensor locations should be documented to allow a test to be repeated if necessary. The proposed load test strategy should be reviewed and approved by the Quality Unit and the System Owner.

### 6.4.2 Determining Number and Locations for Mapping Sensors

                    

The diagram in Figure 6.1 shows:

                    

                        
- **Small chambers (<70 cu ft / <2 m³):** minimum suggested sensor locations as illustrated in the guide's left diagram

                        
- **Medium chambers (<700 cu ft / <20 m³):** minimum sensor locations as illustrated in the guide's right diagram

                    

                    

The inner box represents the working area (where product is stored); circles represent the sensor locations.

                    

The sensor locations used for mapping should be defined based on:

                    

                        
- Where product will be stored

                        
- The potential major influences on the internal conditions during use

                        
- The number and location of heating/cooling units

                        
- For conditioning units: sensors should be in the load

                        
- Defrost cycles should be considered when outlining test procedures

                    

                    

                        

#### Critical Note on Rationale Requirement

                        

General guidance should not be used without a supporting rationale. For example, "there should be a sensor every 4 m" — if the area being mapped has no factor that could cause a change in temperature, multiple sensors to monitor the same effects would be redundant. The rationale describing the location and number of sensors must be reviewed and approved by the Quality Unit and the area/System Owner.

                    

                    

**Additional considerations for sensor number and locations:**

                    

                        
- Increase mapping sensor locations as necessary to monitor areas where changes in conditions are likely (e.g., near conditioned air discharge)

                        
- Where multiple HVAC outlets operate consistently, the area may be treated as a zone, with the mapping sensor layout developed for those zones

                        
- An area may be divided into zones that perform similarly — mapping a representative zone may provide adequate data to represent the whole area

                        
- Where appropriate, there should be a mapping sensor adjacent to the system control sensor

                    

                    

**For systems with external walls:** Data should be available on external conditions from another calibrated reference source (e.g., BMS/BAS) — not from the mapping system itself. This allows the designer to evaluate data in the context of design temperature ranges.

                    

**For conditioned rooms or standard catalogue systems:** There should be data on facility/room conditions at the time of testing outside the store, to ensure that any open-door tests are representative of normal operating conditions.

## Chapter 6 Summary & Key Takeaways |

                

Consolidated learning from all sub-sections

            

        

        

            

                A mapping study that only tests ideal conditions gives you a false sense of security — the risk lives in the extremes you haven't tested.  

            

            

                

                    

### Core Principles Summary

                    

                        
- **Two outcomes required:** Uniformity confirmation AND identification of monitoring locations — neither alone is sufficient.

                        
- **Risk assessment first:** The five factors (purpose, size, layout, location, HVAC) must be evaluated before designing the test strategy.

                        
- **CFD supplements, never replaces:** Computational modeling is a useful planning tool, but actual sensor data is always required by ISPE.

                        
- **QA pre-approval is mandatory:** The mapping protocol must be approved before execution — not after.

                        
- **Equipment accuracy matters:** Sensor accuracy must be appropriate for the acceptance criteria. Low-cost ±2°C loggers are often unsuitable for pharmaceutical applications.

                        
- **Buffered probes ≠ mapping probes:** Use unbuffered sensors during mapping to capture actual air temperatures, including short-duration excursions.

                        
- **Sample at least every 5 minutes:** Increase frequency when defrost cycles or frequent door openings are expected.

                        
- **Test the extremes:** Empty + full-load; summer worst-case; door-open scenarios — not just steady-state, not just empty.

                        
- **Scientific rationale is non-negotiable:** Every sensor location requires a documented reason, reviewed and approved by QA.

                        
- **Seasonal variation is a risk:** A winter test result does not guarantee summer performance — especially in Taiwan's climate.

### 10.1 Challenging the Area with Operational Activities

                

The area being mapped should be challenged with specific known operational activities during the thermal mapping process. These activities should simulate normal processes and functions that would occur for routine operations, such as opening interior doors, opening exterior doors (Receiving Dock Doors, Shipping Dock Doors), running of equipment (e.g., thermo-formers, stretch wrappers, forklifts, conveyors) etc. where there is a potential impact to the conditions.

                

By performing these functions and operating equipment during the execution of the mapping studies, cause and effect relationships can be identified for the thermal profile.

                

Operational activities to simulate during mapping include:

                

                    
- Opening interior and exterior doors (dock doors, personnel doors)

                    
- Running warehouse equipment: forklifts, conveyors, stretch wrappers

                    
- Simulating loading and unloading cycles

                    
- Normal personnel traffic through the space

                

                

                    

#### Note on Cause and Effect

                    

The primary purpose of operational challenges is to capture the *actual* thermal profile experienced during real production — not an idealized empty-room baseline. A mapping study that omits these activities may underestimate temperature variability and produce specifications that cannot be met during routine operation.

### 10.2 Open Door Recovery Test

                

An example of a typical operational activity is to examine recovery time for the internal chamber temperature after opening the access door and simulating loading/unloading. It is recommended to determine the time that the chamber door may remain fully open before the monitoring sensors are no longer within the acceptable range.

                

This test can also help to determine how quickly the monitoring sensor recovers and returns to the normal operating temperature range.

                

#### Recommended Test Conditions

                

                    
- Use a **minimum (simulated) load or no load** — worst case with no thermal mass

                    
- Locate temperature sensors as recommended for thermal mapping, with **special attention to sensors nearest the door**

                    
- Set data acquisition rate to **1-minute intervals** due to uncertainty in test duration

                

                

#### Acceptance Criteria

                

Generally, there are no specific acceptance criteria for this test unless a recovery time is specified in the unit manufacturer's specifications. The key output is a data-supported understanding of the chamber's tolerance for door-open events.

                

                    

#### Special Case: Walk-in with Vinyl Curtains

                    

For larger walk-in CTCs fitted with vinyl curtains, the units may be able to maintain internal conditions indefinitely, as the impact of passing through curtains is short-term and negligible. Vinyl curtains should be well-fitted, rest on the unit walls, and sit just clear of the floor.

### 10.3 Power Outage / Thermal Degradation Test

                

Another type of test that may be helpful from a business continuity, disaster recovery, and operational perspective is a simulated Power Outage Test. This type of test would measure the amount of time the Controlled Temperature Chamber remained within the desired temperature range once electrical power was turned off.

                

**Note:** although it is expected the Controlled Temperature Chamber will have redundant and backup power supplies, the results from this test would provide data to define how quickly materials need to be moved to another location if the backup power systems failed. Doors to the storage chamber should remain closed during this test.

                

#### Recommended Test Setup

                

                    
- Use a minimum (simulated) load or no load in the CTC (worst case)

                    
- Perform as commissioning activity referenced in OQ before the storage unit is put into use, if possible

                    
- Locate temperature sensors as recommended for thermal mapping

                    
- Data acquisition rate: **1-minute intervals**

                

                

#### Additional Operational Value

                

Once power is restored, the amount of time required to return to the appropriate required temperature can be of value for disaster recovery planning — specifically, the elapsed time from when power is restored until the *last* temperature sensor returns to the acceptable range.

                

**Note:** This test was previously included in the USP, but has subsequently been removed.

### Calibration and Calibration Strategies

                

There are benefits to be derived from a considered calibration strategy:

                

                    
- **Refrigeration system instruments:** Many can only be calibrated when the system is emptied for major maintenance — therefore, they are calibrated following manufacturer recommendations

                    
- **Control sensor:** Can be verified at a single point, as the control functions are usually based on whether the system is above or below the operating set point

                    
- **Monitoring sensors:** Should be calibrated across the operating range, as they provide the data to support quality determinations in support of product quality

                

                

### Access Control

                

Areas where product is stored should have a system to prevent unauthorized access. This system can be part of a site keycard system, or use a lock and key with procedural controls which identify who holds, and is entitled to use, the key.

                

### Dehumidification

                

Dehumidification should be considered for a cold room or freezer. The life of the equipment can be extended where there are fewer defrost cycles required (reduced expansion/contraction). Dehumidification can also reduce the risk of ice forming on a freezer floor, which presents a safety hazard.

                

For large distribution facilities it may be worth considering the layout, and providing access to a walk-in freezer through a cold room, in order to reduce moisture in the freezer.

                

### Equipment Durability

                

When specifying a new facility, it is worthwhile defining the system design life — the expected operating lifespan without significant maintenance. High quality refrigeration systems can have an expected life of 12 to 15 years. Standby systems are usually installed for an installation holding material of a significant commercial value.

### 11.1 Active Shipping Containers: Unit Load Devices

                

Currently there are two types of Unit Load Devices (ULD) systems suitable for the shipping of temperature sensitive product, designed for use in aircraft. They are powered to either cool or heat the air within the container.

                

#### System 1 (TCCU) Key Features

                

                    
- Maintains 5°C (41°F) for **72 hours** at 30°C (86°F) ambient

                    
- Handles brief ambient excursions from -40°C to +60°C

                    
- GPS/GSM module for real-time location and temperature transmission

                    
- Two modes: recharge mode (connected to AC) and transport mode (24VDC battery)

                    
- Interior designed for 40×48 inch pallet, up to 500 kg product

                

                

#### System 2 Key Features

                

                    
- Maintains 5°C for **30 hours** at -10°C to +30°C ambient

                    
- NiMH batteries; three cooling compressors with heaters

                    
- Supports US and EURO pallet sizes, up to 953 kg

                    
- LED alarm indicators: system on (green), charge (blue), alarm (red), alert (yellow)

                    
- Alert triggers: battery <30%, inside temp out of spec, ambient temp out of spec, compressor failure

                

                

#### 11.1.3 Qualification Approaches

                

Two possible approaches for active shipping containers:

                

                    
- **Unit Qualification:** Map each individual unit; confirm satisfactory operation; qualify service supplier for maintenance and calibration quality systems

                    
- **Supplier Assessment:** (1) Qualify the manufacturer (design + factory testing); (2) Qualify service supplier (maintenance + calibration quality systems). Usually paired with an independent temperature logger per shipment.

                

                

Regardless of approach, loading and unloading methods must be qualified and procedurally defined. When unloading, shipment should be placed into quarantine until data logger confirms conditions were maintained. If out-of-conditions, a Non-Conformance must be raised and the implications investigated.

### 11.2 Temperature Controlled Trailers

                

Temperature controlled over-the-road trailers can be used to move significant amounts of temperature sensitive pharmaceutical product. Trailers have a different set of environmental and usage profiles when compared to in situ custom-built CTCs:

                

                    
- Loading and unloading operations

                    
- Defrost cycles

                    
- Loading patterns

                

                

Moisture, as a degradation element, is much more prevalent in trailers due to the operational profile — continuous running for several days, followed by periods of downtime when the trailer is empty. Rigorous inspection of each trailer should be performed prior to loading.

                

#### 11.3 Trailer Design

                

Product loaded into the trailer should be equilibrated to the appropriate temperature before loading. During transit, as heat or cold penetrates the walls, roof, and floor, circulating airflows capture the environmental heat or cold and condition it to the desired temperature in the HVAC unit on the front of the trailer.

                

Depending on the geographical area, manufacturers build and test trailers to meet standards of one of two organizations:

                

                    
- **North America:** American Trucking Association (ATA) Technology & Maintenance Council (TMC)

                    
- **Europe:** ATP Agreement on International Carriage of Perishable Foodstuffs

                

                

#### TMC / ATP Classification Table

                
                    
| TMC (North America) | ATP (Europe) | Application |
| --- | --- | --- |
                    
                        
| C65 | FRA | Controlled Room Temperature |
                        
| C35 | FRB | Refrigerated (2–8°C) |
                        
| F | FRC | Frozen (-20°C) |
                        
| DF | — | Deep Frozen (-25°C or below) |
                    
                

                

#### 11.4 Insulation Degradation

                

Polyurethane foam insulation gradually loses its blowing agent gas (CO₂ and other gases) over time, which is replaced with air and water vapor. This transition occurs slowly over years and is normal. The temperature control unit is sized to assure a useful life of 15 years with timely maintenance.

                

#### 11.5 Trailer Validation Approach

                

The validation exercise is focused on loading patterns and assuring that proper airflow can be achieved under conditions of heat and cold. Loading practices that block airflow impede heat addition/removal and result in product temperatures that may not be controlled within the required range. A written protocol should document temperatures nearest the product during seasonal extremes.

### 11.6 Trailer Loading Patterns

                

Loading patterns in a well maintained truck are the key determinant in assuring even product temperature within the trailer. Key requirements:

                

                    
- Product should be loaded on **pallets that allow free airflow** the length of the trailer

                    
- Product should **not be in contact with the walls** of the trailer

                    
- Double stacking of refrigerated goods should be carefully considered, as airflow distribution in the rear of the trailer may not be sufficient to maintain uniform temperature under extreme conditions

                    
- Product should be **equilibrated to the appropriate temperature** before loading

                

                

#### 11.6.1 Inspectional Criteria

                

The Quality Agreement between the carrier and the company should define the shared responsibilities of inspection. The following items should be inspected for each use:

                

                    
- Air distribution chute intact

                    
- Air supply, air returns and floor drains clear of debris

                    
- Repairs to interior and exterior surfaces (damage from forklifts etc.)

                    
- Door seals intact

                    
- General cleanliness (visual inspection)

## Chapter 12: Maintenance |

            

        

    

    

        

            

                ISPE Source Text
                

### Chapter 12 Maintenance — Overview

                

The supplier of the refrigeration equipment should provide general recommendations for the maintenance of the equipment. These recommendations may not recognize the high value of the stored goods in this application. The potential impact on the maintenance program should be assessed.

                

#### 12.1 Cleaning of the Coils

                

The coils (unit cooler and air cooled condenser) should be cleaned regularly and checked for any damage to ensure that performance levels are maintained. Damaged or distorted fins should be straightened with a suitable comb. This inspection should also ensure that there is no corrosion of the fins.

                

Regular inspection should also confirm that the defrost cycle is effective (where applicable), especially if the cycle is adjusted to account for seasonal variation.

                

**General Cleaning:** Dry cleaning is preferred, in order to minimize risk of contamination. Regular inspection is recommended to ensure that there is no unseen microbial/fungicidal growth.

                

#### 12.2 Check the Condensate Pipework

                

The condensate pipework should be checked to ensure that it is not damaged and that it has the correct fall and flows freely. Trace heating or insulation also should be checked. Periodic verification during operation is also recommended; this should ensure that the pipe supports are adequate and have not loosened.

                

#### 12.3 Functional Testing of Unit Coolers / Defrost Elements

                

There is usually no feedback from the solenoid valve on the unit coolers, so the only way of checking their correct operation is to carry out, periodically, a functional check to verify the performance of each unit cooler.

                

#### 12.4 Leak Testing

                

                    
- Periodically check pipe runs and equipment for leaks using electronic test devices

                    
- Check refrigerant fluid level and pressure against system specification — early detection may help avoid system failure

                    
- Keep a record of refrigerant used for the system (may be mandatory depending on region)

                    
- Equipment rooms may be fitted with leakage detectors/alarms — these should be calibrated and checked regularly

                    
- End Users should be aware of local regulatory requirements

### 12.5 Thermal Scanning

                

The panels, doors, door seals, and sealing of utility penetrations can degrade over time (foamed panels may be better in this respect than panels filled with mineral wool). A thermal scan of the installation will allow any degradation to be seen and appropriate action taken.

                

As a minimum, the penetrations should be visually inspected to ensure that they are sound with no leakage/ingress of condensation. For conduit penetrations, the internal sealing also should be checked to ensure that condensation is not leaking inside the conduit.

                

### 12.6 System Alarms

                

The entire alarm system should be periodically tested by direct or simulated temperature change against the specified alarm set points and proper functionality of audible and visual alarms at local and/or remote alarm stations (e.g., emails, phone calls, or text based alerts) should be confirmed and documented.

                

Regardless of the root cause, product losses from cold storage-related failures can be prevented with the proper design and operation of the alarm system, and proper End User response to an alarm event. Periodic testing of the alarm system should help to ensure that it will work when needed.

                

The system maintenance procedures can be used to provide a confirmation that the system performance remains satisfactory, supporting a **reduced requirement for remapping** where appropriate. The data from the monitoring system should be reviewed periodically, to look for any negative performance trends.

### PM Procedures by Equipment Type

                

#### CRT Storage Area (Warehouse)

                

                    
1. Inspect building envelope — no visible degradation of materials of construction or seals/curtains that would impact ingress of unconditioned air

                    
2. Review system alarm history since last maintenance (temperature monitoring system, HVAC temperature/humidity, airflow volume sensors) — look for negative performance trends

                    
3. Visually check HVAC system balance — verify balancing dampers remain in their as-commissioned/marked settings

                    
4. Check and clean heating/cooling coils if necessary

                    
5. Check control system records — confirm control valves are not operating at 100% (indicating performance limits or utility system issues)

                

                

#### Walk-in Refrigerators / Freezers

                

                    
1. Inspect system envelope — no visible degradation, door/window seals, door curtains, or air leakage paths (moisture or ice buildup)

                    
2. Review system alarm history (temperature monitoring + refrigeration system alarms) — note observations in maintenance record

                    
3. Check refrigeration system set point being maintained (e.g., compressor suction pressure)

                    
4. Review maintenance history to confirm:
                        

                            
    - Coils have been cleaned if needed

                            
    - Fan operation has been checked

                            
    - Operation of unit cooler control valves and thermal expansion valves checked

                            
    - Defrost settings and operation verified (if applicable)

                        

                    

                

                

#### Standalone Refrigerators and Freezers

                

Same four-point checklist as walk-in units (envelope inspection, alarm history review, set point verification, maintenance history review for coils / fans / valves / defrost).

                

#### Performance Review by SME (Alternative to Full PM)

                

Where a system has multiple monitoring locations (control sensor + independent temperature monitor at hot/cold spots), the preventive maintenance is not required to confirm satisfactory unit operation in every cycle. Instead:

                

A review of current system performance data against the as-Qualified data by an SME can be used to confirm consistent system performance. This review should assess the thermal performance of unit insulation and the conditioning system, allowing the SME to determine if there has been a significant change in performance that may require maintenance activities, or if the system is still performing acceptably.

## Section Summary & Key Takeaways |

            

        

    

    

        

            

                

### Key Messages from Chapters 10–12

                

                    

#### Chapter 10 Core Takeaways

                    

                        
- **Operational realism:** Mapping studies must include real operational activities — door openings, equipment operation, loading simulation — to be valid

                        
- **Open door recovery:** Test under empty (worst-case) conditions; 1-minute data acquisition; outputs inform SOP time limits

                        
- **Power outage:** Empty-chamber degradation data = the clock that governs your emergency response window

                        
- **Calibration differentiation:** Monitoring sensors need full-range multi-point calibration; control sensors only need single-point verification

                        
- **Access control:** Unauthorized access is a GMP deficiency, not just a security issue

                    

                

                

                    

#### Chapter 11 Core Takeaways

                    

                        
- **ULD qualification:** Two approaches — individual unit mapping or supplier assessment system. Either way, an independent in-shipment logger is essential

                        
- **Trailer as a CTC:** Trailers are a mobile subclass of CTCs, with additional challenges from moisture, loading patterns, and road vibration

                        
- **TMC/ATP standards:** Industry standards provide a "family" qualification basis — an F-rated trailer study covers DF as well

                        
- **Airflow is everything:** Loading patterns that block airflow are the leading cause of temperature non-uniformity in trailers

                        
- **Pre-shipment inspection:** Every trailer, every time — document it per the Quality Agreement

                    

                

                

                    

#### Chapter 12 Core Takeaways

                    

                        
- **Manufacturer ≠ GMP:** Equipment supplier maintenance recommendations do not account for high-value pharmaceutical storage — you must set your own risk-based standards

                        
- **Dry clean coils:** Moisture introduced during wet cleaning creates microbial growth risk in cold environments

                        
- **Alarm testing is non-negotiable:** Periodic testing of the full alarm pathway (local + remote) is a GMP requirement, not optional

                        
- **Good maintenance can reduce re-mapping:** Consistent PM + SME trend review can justify extended intervals between full thermal mapping studies

                        
- **Major repairs trigger re-qualification:** Compressor replacement, unit cooler change, controller replacement — all require re-assessment

## BMS Integration Checklist /

  

  

    

      

### BMS Integration Requirements & Technical Specifications

      

        

#### What BMS Integration Means in Practice

        

The Building Management System (BMS) — sometimes called SCADA, EMS, or EMCS — is the central nervous system of a pharmaceutical facility. For CTCs, BMS integration means:

        

          
- Continuous temperature data transmitted from local sensor to BMS server

          
- BMS generates time-stamped electronic records (21 CFR Part 11 compliant)

          
- Remote alarm annunciation: phone/pager/email notification to on-call staff

          
- Centralized alarm management: acknowledge, escalate, close with reason codes

          
- Data export in validated format (CSV, PDF reports, API) for GMP batch records

          
- Audit trail: who changed what alarm setpoint and when

        

      

      

        

#### IQ Phase — BMS Connection Verification

        

          
- BMS communication protocol confirmed (Modbus RTU / BACnet / OPC-UA / 4–20 mA)

          
- Physical wiring / network connection from CTC panel to BMS gateway verified

          
- BMS tag names match URS and P&ID nomenclature

          
- CTC sensor signals visible in BMS engineering workstation

          
- Data historian (e.g., OSIsoft PI, Wonderware) configured for CTC tags

          
- Alarm setpoints entered in BMS per approved alarm setpoint table

          
- BMS server backup and disaster recovery procedures in place

          
- 21 CFR Part 11 assessment of BMS software version completed

          
- BMS user access levels configured and documented

          
- Network firewall rules documented (cybersecurity review)

        

      

      

        

#### OQ Phase — BMS Functional Testing

        

          
- Simulate temperature above action limit: confirm BMS alarm generated within defined time

          
- Confirm alarm transmitted to on-call pager/phone within SLA (e.g., <5 min)

          
- Confirm audible alarm at CTC panel (6.14) AND remote BMS notification both triggered

          
- Acknowledge alarm at BMS: confirm local annunciation silenced, BMS record updated

          
- Simulate BMS network outage: confirm CTC local logger continues recording

          
- Restore BMS connection: confirm data gap identified and backfilled or documented

          
- Test alarm escalation: if unacknowledged for X minutes, escalate to supervisor

          
- Test data export: generate CSV / PDF temperature report for one week of data

          
- Verify electronic audit trail captures setpoint change with user ID and timestamp

          
- Test automatic log-off (URS 6.7) in BMS interface

        

      

      

        

#### PQ Phase — BMS Continuous Performance

        

          
- Confirm BMS records uninterrupted data throughout full PQ duration (14–30 days typical)

          
- Verify no unexplained data gaps in historian

          
- Confirm all PQ temperature exceedances (if any) were detected and alarmed by BMS

          
- Verify BMS report format matches GMP batch record requirements

          
- Confirm periodic BMS backup executed during PQ without data loss

## Supplier Documentation Deliverables /

  

  

    

      

### Required Documentation from Equipment Supplier

      

        

#### Why Documentation Requirements Belong in the URS

        

Including documentation requirements in the URS — not just in a separate procurement contract — means they become acceptance criteria for IQ. If the supplier fails to deliver a document, IQ cannot be closed. This gives the qualification team commercial leverage and a clear audit trail for the decision to accept or reject the equipment.

      

      

        

#### Qualification Documentation

        

          
- Factory Acceptance Test (FAT) protocol and completed report

          
- Site Acceptance Test (SAT) protocol and completed report

          
- IQ protocol template (supplier-provided, site-approved)

          
- OQ protocol template (functional test scripts, pass/fail criteria)

          
- PQ protocol template (temperature mapping methodology, acceptance criteria)

          
- Completed FAT/SAT records with witnessed signatures

          
- Deviation log from FAT/SAT with disposition of each deviation

        

      

      

        

#### Calibration Documentation

        

          
- Calibration certificates for all temperature sensors (NIST-traceable)

          
- Calibration certificates for pressure instruments

          
- Calibration procedure and frequency recommendations

          
- Measurement uncertainty analysis for each sensor type

          
- Calibration point data (actual vs. reference at min 3 points)

        

      

      

        

#### Technical Documentation

        

          
- As-built drawings (mechanical, electrical, P&ID)

          
- Equipment installation drawings with dimensional clearances

          
- Electrical schematics and panel wiring diagrams

          
- Control system architecture diagram (controller, BMS interface, HMI)

          
- Component data sheets (compressors, sensors, valves, controllers)

          
- Bill of materials (BOM) with part numbers and manufacturer details

          
- Spare parts list with recommended on-hand inventory

          
- Material of construction certificates (CoC) for wetted surfaces

        

      

      

        

#### Operational Documentation

        

          
- Operation and Maintenance (O&M) manual

          
- Preventive maintenance (PM) schedule with task descriptions

          
- Alarm list with recommended setpoints and delay times

          
- Troubleshooting guide for common fault conditions

          
- Operator training materials (slides, video, or hands-on checklist)

          
- Service contact list and SLA (response time, escalation)

          
- Recommended spare parts list with supplier part numbers

        

      

      

        

#### Compliance Documentation

## CDMO Fleet Management Considerations / CDMO

  

  

    

      

### Managing Multiple CTCs as a Fleet

      

        

#### The Fleet Management Challenge

        

A typical pharma CDMO (aseptic fill-finish) operates 5–20+ controlled temperature chambers simultaneously: walk-in cold rooms (2–8°C), freezers (–20°C, –80°C), incubators (25°C/60%RH, 40°C/75%RH). Each requires its own URS, qualification records, calibration schedule, alarm response SOP, and PM plan. Managing this as independent silos is unsustainable at scale.

        

Fleet management treats similar CTCs as a standardized program, reducing documentation redundancy while maintaining compliance integrity.

      

      

        

#### Strategies for Fleet Qualification

        

          
- **Master Validation Plan (MVP) for CTCs**: One MVP covers all CTCs, defining the common qualification strategy, acceptance criteria template, and periodic re-qualification schedule.

          
- **Generic Protocol Templates**: One IQ/OQ template covers all cold rooms of the same model. Site-specific data (serial number, location, BMS tag) filled in per unit.

          
- **Lead Unit / Matrix Approach**: Full IQ+OQ+PQ on the most challenging unit; reduced protocol for identical units (reference lead unit data).

          
- **Centralized Calibration Schedule**: CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System) tracks all CTC calibration due dates fleet-wide.

          
- **Common SOP with Unit-Specific Appendix**: One SOP for alarm response; Appendix A lists alarm setpoints for each CTC by room number.

        

      

      

        

#### Re-qualification Triggers (Fleet-Wide)

        

Any of the following events triggers a re-qualification assessment for the affected CTC(s):

        

          
- Compressor replacement or major refrigeration repair

          
- Significant change in loading pattern (new product type, new storage layout)

          
- Temperature exceedance event (OOS — Out of Specification temperature)

          
- Building renovation affecting HVAC or electrical supply to CTC

          
- Periodic re-qualification due date (typically every 1–3 years per site policy)

          
- Alarm setpoint change (requires change control + at minimum OQ re-test)

          
- BMS system upgrade (requires IT validation + re-qualification of affected functions)

## Appendix 2 — Number and Location of Mapping Sensors

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Introduction & Purpose

          

This appendix provides a **sample document** defining the number and location of temperature mapping sensors, together with the load test rationale. It uses a Client's Warehouse as the worked example, but the principles apply to any controlled temperature chamber (CTC).

          

The mapping study serves two purposes:

          

            
- Characterise the spatial temperature distribution within the chamber under simulated normal use

            
- Identify the **maximum and minimum temperature locations**, which then govern where permanent monitoring sensors are installed

          

          

Where the stored product has no critical humidity requirements, humidity need not be mapped or monitored — decisions about scope should be driven by product CQAs, not by convention.

## Appendix 2 — Heat Gain & Loss Analysis

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Identifying Heat Sources Before Sensor Placement

          

Before selecting sensor locations, the engineer must analyse all external and internal heat gains and losses. This analysis directly determines which zones are likely to show temperature extremes.

          

**External Heat Gains and Losses**

          

            
- North wall and ceiling — primary conductive heat exchange paths with the outdoor environment

            
- Other perimeter walls — typically surrounded by air-conditioned spaces, so influence is smaller

            
- Loading dock doors — during normal operation, doors open for loading/unloading; vinyl curtains with inflatable truck seals mitigate but do not eliminate warm air ingress

          

          

**Internal Heat Gains and Losses**

          

            
- **Lighting:** low-energy fluorescent lamps left on during the working day — small but constant load

            
- **Product:** supplied at storage temperature — negligible thermal load

            
- **Equipment:** stacking forklift — small, intermittent load

            
- **Personnel:** approximately two people — small transient load

            
- **Air conditioning outlets:** single system with equally spaced duct outlets in aisles

## Appendix 2 — Sensor Placement Rationale

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Sensor Location Selection Principles

          

The Guide references the French Standard **NF X15-140 (October 2002)** — "Measurement of air moisture – Climatic and thermostatic chambers – Characterization and verification" — as guidance for determining sensor locations.

          

The chamber is first divided into **zones**, each served by one air conditioning vent. Key placement rules:

          

            
- A sensor at the **lowest** and **highest** product storage points in each zone

            
- A sensor at **each corner** of the room on the nearest racking/product position

            
- One sensor in the **centre** of the space at mid-level

            
- Additional sensors near **external walls and doors** — where heat exchange is greatest

          

          

The symmetrical nature of central zones allowed a **reduced** sensor count in those areas compared to the end zones (which have door and end-wall influences). Sensor reduction must be scientifically justified, not assumed.

          

For permanent **monitoring system** sensor placement, the mapping data is used to identify the locations of maximum and minimum temperature — the monitoring sensors go there, providing the earliest warning of an out-of-limit event.

## Appendix 2 — Load Testing & Seasonal Variation

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Load Test Strategy

          

The warehouse must be mapped under two distinct load scenarios. In both cases, **minimum thermal mass** is used so that temperature changes propagate quickly and worst-case conditions are observable:

          

            
- **"Empty" condition:** No product in chamber. No airflow obstruction; lowest average air velocity throughout the space. This reveals the equipment's fundamental temperature distribution.

            
- **"Full" condition (simulated):** The front 1.5 zones are filled with *empty boxes* simulating the largest stored product case. Empty boxes provide minimum thermal mass but maximum airflow interruption — showing the worst-case impact of racking on temperature distribution.

          

          

The rationale for focusing on front zones: the warehouse is symmetrical, so understanding the door-adjacent zone under load is sufficient to characterise the most at-risk area.

          

### Seasonal Variation Coverage

          

The initial mapping should be conducted in **summer** (worst-case heat load). The mapping is not considered complete until seasonal variation exceeding the **90% design conditions** has been experienced. Local meteorological data provides the 90%/95% summer and winter boundary conditions used in design.

          

Data from the summer campaign may justify a **reduced sensor array** for the subsequent winter mapping, based on demonstrated symmetry and stable performance.

## Appendix 3 — Measurement Uncertainty: Overview & Concepts

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Why Measurement Uncertainty Matters

          

The maximum and minimum temperature locations are identified during mapping. However, the data recorded is not the "true" temperature — it is a **measurement** subject to uncertainty from multiple sources:

          

            
- Sensor calibration uncertainty

            
- Data logger/input module uncertainty

            
- Sensor placement effects (thermal contact, convective environment)

          

          

For monitoring sensor alarm limits, the Guide distinguishes two scenarios:

          

            
1. **Monitoring sensor located at the max/min spot:** Use only the sensor's own measurement uncertainty (COD/MU) to set alarm limits

            
2. **Monitoring sensor NOT at the max/min spot:** Add an *offset* representing the temperature difference between the monitoring location and the true max/min, in addition to the COD/MU

          

          

In both cases, the measurement uncertainty of the *mapping sensors* used to determine the offset must also be included in the calculation.

## Appendix 3 — Guard-Band Formula & Decision Limits

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### The Guard-Band Formula

          

The ISPE Guide provides explicit formulae for calculating decision limits (DL) from the Proven Acceptable Range (PAR) and measurement uncertainty:

          

For the LOW limit:
DL_low ≥ PAR_low + [GB – Usr]

For the HIGH limit:
DL_high ≤ PAR_high – [GB – Usr]

Where:
  DL     = Decision Limit (alarm trigger point)
  PAR    = Proven Acceptable Range
  GB     = Guard Band = k × u_system
  k      = coverage factor (minimum 2.00, ≥97.7% confidence)
  u_sys  = combined measurement uncertainty of the system
  Usr    = Required Uncertainty of Limits (usually = 0)

          

When **Usr = 0** (no allowable excursion from PAR), the formula simplifies to:

          

DL_high = PAR_high – GB
DL_low  = PAR_low  + GB
GB = k × u_system (k ≥ 2)

          

When a sensor is located at a position that is offset from the max/min temperature location, the offset is incorporated:

          

GB_with_offset = offset + k × √(u_T1² + u_T2²)

          

where u_T1 and u_T2 are the uncertainties of the two sensors involved (the mapping sensor at the extreme and the monitoring sensor).

## Appendix 5 — Science & Risk-Based Development of Testing

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Purpose and Framework

          

Appendix 5 describes a structured **risk assessment process** to determine the risks to product quality from CTC equipment and define associated risk controls. The output of the risk assessment drives the scope and depth of qualification testing — including how many sensors, which scenarios, and how long to test.

          

**Key assumptions in this approach:**

          

            
- The *output* of the system = the Product

            
- The *inputs* to the system are Qualified (meet specification)

- Focus is on **product quality (patient safety)**

            
- Maintenance and calibration quality systems are assumed to be in place and effective (to avoid FMEA becoming unmanageably detailed)

          

          

**Information used for the assessment:**

          

            
- Critical Quality Attributes (CQAs)

            
- Critical Process Parameters (CPPs)

            
- Potential risks to product quality

            
- Source of each risk

            
- Risk controls (design or procedural)

            
- Means of detection if controls fail

## Appendix 5 — Defining Scope & Risk Assessment Process

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Risk Assessment Process Structure

          

The ISPE Guide recommends a two-stage process for efficiency:

          

            
1. **Initial draft** by a small number of Subject Matter Experts (SMEs) — generates the system description, control strategy, and preliminary risk list

            
2. **Review and scoring** by a larger cross-functional team including Quality/Validation, Operations, and Engineering representatives, led by an experienced facilitator

          

          

Pre-defined action levels:

          

            
- **Low risk:** Accepted as-is

            
- **Medium risk:** Can be accepted by team, or additional controls may be added

            
- **High risk:** Must have additional controls added to reduce to Medium or Low before acceptance

          

          

Scoring uses the three-dimensional model:

          

            
- **Severity (S)** — impact of failure on product CQA

            
- **Occurrence (O)** — likelihood the failure will happen

            
- **Detection (D)** — ability of existing controls to detect the failure

          

          

The risk assessment report must contain: system description and control strategy; CQAs; CPPs; Critical Aspects (CAs) where applicable; methodology; scope; results summary; mitigation plans; communication to senior management where required; and conclusions.

## Appendix 5 — CDMO Multi-Product Storage Risk Analysis

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Risk Factors Unique to CDMO Cold Storage

          

A Contract Development and Manufacturing Organization (CDMO) faces unique CTC risk factors compared to a single-product manufacturer:

          

            
- **Multiple clients, multiple PAR ranges:** The same cold room may need to satisfy different temperature acceptance criteria for different clients simultaneously (e.g., 2–8°C for Client A at ±1°C, 2–8°C for Client B at ±0.5°C)

            
- **Variable loading patterns:** Different clients have different batch sizes, different pallet configurations, and different schedules. The "full" condition may represent a mixture of product types with varying thermal mass

            
- **Change control complexity:** Any change to one client's storage arrangement may affect temperature distribution for other clients — triggering a re-evaluation of mapping data validity

            
- **Audit frequency:** Each client may conduct their own supplier audit, potentially with different expectations for mapping depth and frequency

            
- **Regulatory diversity:** Clients may have products approved in different jurisdictions (FDA, EMA, PMDA), each with different interpretations of mapping requirements

## Summary — Key Takeaways from Appendices 2, 3 & 5

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Appendix 2 — Sensor Placement

          

            
- Sensor placement must be **scientifically justified** based on heat source analysis, zone structure, and chamber geometry — not arbitrary

            
- Key zones: corners, highest/lowest product points, near doors, near evaporator coils, near external walls

            
- Two load scenarios (empty + simulated full) reveal different failure modes

            
- Seasonal coverage (summer + winter) is required for complete mapping

            
- Mapping results drive permanent monitoring sensor locations

          

          

### Appendix 3 — Measurement Uncertainty

          

            
- All temperature measurements carry uncertainty; ignoring it leads to non-achievable acceptance criteria

            
- Guard-band = k × u_system (k ≥ 2). Tightens DL toward the PAR center

            
- When monitoring sensor is not at the max/min location, add offset to the guard-band calculation

            
- Larger offsets narrow the DL — best engineering solution is relocating the sensor to the extreme point

            
- Two sensors (one at high, one at low extreme) eliminate offset and maximize DL width (Example C(d))

          

          

### Appendix 5 — Risk-Based Testing

          

            
- ICH Q9 applies: test scope and frequency proportional to risk level

            
- Three dimensions: Severity × Occurrence → interim risk; then Detection → final classification

            
- High-risk CTCs (sensitive products, old equipment, no redundancy) require more sensors, more scenarios, more frequent requalification

            
- CDMO environments add complexity: multiple PAR ranges, variable loading, multiple audit expectations

            
- Risk assessment is a living document — revisit after changes, deviations, and requalifications

Guard-banding is the practice of tightening acceptance criteria to account for Measurement Uncertainty (MU). If USP requires 2–8°C for Cold storage, and MU = ±0.3°C, then the guard-banded acceptance criteria become: **2.3°C ≤ T ≤ 7.7°C**.

## Top 10 Must-Know Principles |

      

The essential knowledge framework for CTC mapping and monitoring — applicable from first qualification to lifecycle management

    

  

  

    

      

        

### Principles 1–5

        

          

#### 1. URS Drives Everything

          

Every qualification decision — acceptance criteria, sensor placement, alarm setpoints, test conditions — must trace back to the User Requirements Specification. If a requirement is not in the URS, it cannot be a qualification failure. If it is in the URS, it must be tested. The URS is the bridge between business/product needs and engineering/QA execution.

          

        

        

          

#### 2. Worst-Case Location Is Non-Negotiable

          

Mapping studies must identify the worst-case locations (hottest and coldest points) under all relevant conditions: empty chamber, loaded chamber, door-open conditions, and seasonal extremes. The monitoring sensor must be placed at the worst-case location(s) identified in mapping — not at the most convenient or accessible point.

          

        

        

          

#### 3. Measurement Uncertainty Must Be Accounted For

          

Every temperature measurement has uncertainty. MU must be calculated for the complete measurement chain (sensor + cable + data acquisition + calibration) and applied to acceptance criteria via guard-banding. Ignoring MU means your qualification data may not actually demonstrate that the product temperature was within specification.

          

        

        

          

#### 4. Alert ≠ Alarm — Both Are Required

          

Alert levels are early warning signals that allow corrective action before product is at risk. Alarm levels indicate the temperature has reached a point where product quality may be compromised. Setting only one level (as many companies do) is insufficient. The gap between alert and alarm must be scientifically justified based on MU, chamber response time, and product stability data.

          

        

        

          

#### 5. FAT + SAT + IQ + OQ + PQ Is the Minimum

          

A complete qualification lifecycle requires all five stages. FAT validates the equipment meets specification at the vendor site. SAT confirms it still meets specification after shipping and installation. IQ verifies correct installation. OQ verifies correct operation (empty, worst-case). PQ verifies performance under actual or simulated operating conditions (loaded). Skipping any stage creates qualification gaps that may be cited during inspections.

          

        

      

      

        

### Principles 6–10

        

          

#### 6. Load Configuration Changes Invalidate Mapping

          

Temperature distribution in a loaded chamber is fundamentally different from an empty chamber. PQ mapping must be conducted with a representative product load. Significant changes to load configuration (e.g. changing from 50% to 100% capacity, changing packaging format, adding new shelving) should trigger an assessment of whether requalification is required.

          

        

        

          

#### 7. Monitoring System Data Must Be 21 CFR Part 11 / Annex 11 Compliant

          

All electronic temperature monitoring data used for GMP decisions (product release, deviation investigation, batch records) must meet data integrity requirements: audit trails, access controls, backup procedures, and system validation. This applies to BMS, data loggers, and any standalone monitoring software. Non-compliant data may be considered unreliable during FDA or MHRA inspections.

          

        

        

          

#### 8. Excursions Require MKT Assessment Before Product Disposition

          

Every temperature excursion must be investigated, documented, and assessed for product impact before making a release or rejection decision. MKT calculation is the scientific basis for excursion impact assessment — it accounts for the non-linear relationship between temperature and degradation rate. A short excursion to a moderately elevated temperature may not require rejection if MKT remains within specification.

          

        

        

          

#### 9. Periodic Review Maintains Qualification State

          

Qualification is not a one-time event — it is a lifecycle programme. Annual (or frequency-based) periodic reviews must verify: no unreported changes, calibration compliance, alarm response performance, trend analysis of temperature deviations, and continuing fitness for purpose. The result of a periodic review should be a formal documented conclusion: "qualification status maintained" or "requalification required."

          

        

        

          

#### 10. Risk-Based Approach Is Not an Excuse to Do Less

          

The guide's risk-based framework allows scientific justification for qualification scope decisions — fewer sensors, shorter study duration, reduced testing frequency for low-risk chambers. However, this flexibility requires documented risk assessment (FMEA or equivalent) with explicit rationale. "Risk-based" without documented risk assessment is simply "doing less" — which will not survive inspection scrutiny.

## CDMO Cold Storage Complete Qualification Checklist | CDMO

      

A practical lifecycle checklist covering all stages from URS through periodic review — aligned with ISPE CTC Guide 2016

    

  

  

    

      

        

#### How to Use This Checklist |

        

This checklist covers the complete lifecycle of a CTC qualification programme at a CDMO. Use it for: (1) new equipment qualification planning, (2) inspection readiness gap assessment, (3) periodic review completeness check, (4) change control impact assessment. Items marked Critical are routinely checked by regulatory inspectors.

      

      

        

#### 

        

      

    

    
    

      

#### Phase 1: User Requirements Specification (URS) & Qualification Planning | URS

    

    

      
- Critical URS developed and approved by QA, Engineering, Operations, and Product Management before equipment procurement

      
- Critical URS defines: temperature range, allowable excursions, storage capacity, alarm setpoint requirements, power backup (UPS), security access, data integrity requirements

      
- High URS references applicable standards: USP <659>, EU GDP 2013, 21 CFR Part 211, product-specific storage requirements

      
- High FMEA or equivalent risk assessment performed to identify Critical Aspects and highest-risk failure modes

      
- High Qualification Plan (QP) approved defining scope, schedule, responsibilities, and acceptance criteria methodology

      
- Standard Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) created linking URS requirements to IQ/OQ/PQ test cases

      
- Standard Measurement Uncertainty (MU) budget established for mapping sensors and monitoring sensors

      
- Standard Guard-banding methodology documented and approved (showing how MU tightens acceptance criteria)

    

    
    

      

#### Phase 2: Factory Acceptance Test (FAT) & Site Acceptance Test (SAT) |

    

    

      
- Critical FAT protocol approved and executed at vendor site before equipment shipment

      
- Critical FAT verifies: temperature performance within specification, alarm functionality, controller operation, documentation package (manuals, calibration certificates, drawings)

      
- High FAT includes temperature uniformity test (empty chamber) to confirm factory performance matches URS

      
- High All FAT deviations documented and resolved (or accepted with risk assessment) before shipment approval

      
- High SAT protocol executed after installation at CDMO site, before IQ initiation

      
- Standard SAT confirms equipment arrived undamaged and performs as per FAT results in the CDMO environment

      
- Standard SAT includes initial alarm test and basic temperature uniformity spot check

    

    
    

      

#### Phase 3: Installation Qualification (IQ) |

    

    

      
- Critical IQ Protocol approved before execution; includes all test cases traced to URS requirements

      
- Critical Equipment identity verified: serial numbers, model numbers, specifications match URS and purchase order

      
- Critical Calibration certificates verified for all installed instruments (temperature sensors, RH sensors, pressure gauges)

      
- High Installation drawings (P&IDs, electrical diagrams) match as-built configuration

      
- High Utility connections verified (electrical supply, drainage, network for BMS)

      
- High Safety systems verified: over-temperature protection, low-temperature alarm, door alarm, power failure alarm

      
- High UPS / backup power system tested and confirmed operational

      
- Standard Spare parts list documented; critical spares procured and available

      
- Standard SOPs for operation, cleaning, and emergency response referenced in IQ

      
- Standard IQ Report completed and approved by QA before OQ initiation

    

    
    

      

#### Phase 4: Operational Qualification (OQ) — Mapping Studies |

    

    

      
- Critical OQ Protocol approved; mapping sensor number and placement justified (minimum per chamber size and type)

      
- Critical Mapping sensors calibrated to traceable standard within 12 months; calibration certificates current

      
- Critical Empty chamber mapping completed at setpoint temperature — minimum study duration met (typically ≥72 hours; ≥ 3× thermal equilibrium time)

      
- Critical Worst-case conditions tested: maximum door-opening frequency, maximum ambient temperature (summer conditions), minimum ambient temperature (winter conditions) where applicable

      
- Critical Guard-banded acceptance criteria met: all mapped locations within (setpoint ± tolerance − MU) throughout study

      
- High Worst-case locations identified from empty chamber mapping data (hottest and coldest points)

      
- High Alarm and alert setpoints tested and confirmed functional at correct thresholds

      
- High Power failure simulation: CTC maintains temperature within spec for minimum required duration on backup power

      
- High Data logger performance verified: sampling interval, memory capacity, alarm transmission tested

      
- Standard Statistical analysis completed: min, max, mean, standard deviation for each sensor location reported

      
- Standard OQ Report completed and approved by QA before PQ initiation

    

    
    

      

#### Phase 5: Performance Qualification (PQ) — Loaded Conditions |

    

    

      
- Critical PQ Protocol approved; representative product load configuration documented and justified

      
- Critical Loaded chamber mapping completed with product load ≥ minimum expected operating capacity

      
- Critical Worst-case load scenario tested (maximum product mass, densest packing, worst air circulation configuration)

      
- Critical All acceptance criteria (guard-banded) met under loaded conditions

      
- High Worst-case locations confirmed or updated based on loaded mapping results

      
- High Monitoring sensor placement confirmed/adjusted based on PQ worst-case location data

      
- High Product temperature response time tested: how long does product take to warm up if door is left open or power fails

      
- Standard PQ Report completed and approved by QA; qualification status formally declared

      
- Standard Post-qualification SOPs updated: storage procedures, loading limits, alarm response, excursion management

    

    
    

      

#### Phase 6: Ongoing Monitoring & Continuous Qualification State |

    

    

      
- Critical Continuous temperature monitoring system operational and recording at specified interval (typically ≤5 min)

      
- Critical Monitoring sensors calibrated on schedule (typically every 6–12 months); calibration SOP followed

      
- Critical All temperature excursions documented in deviation system; investigated and closed within SOP timeline

      
- Critical MKT calculated for all excursions; product impact assessment documented before release or rejection decision

      
- High Alarm response SOP in place: defined escalation, response time, corrective action steps

      
- High BMS/monitoring system subject to periodic access review (who can acknowledge alarms, modify setpoints)

      
- High Backup/contingency plan documented: what to do when CTC fails (alternative storage, emergency contacts, product transfer SOP)

      
- Standard Temperature trend reports generated and reviewed quarterly (or per SOP frequency)

      
- Standard Calibration drift analysis performed at each calibration cycle; out-of-tolerance instruments trigger retrospective review

    

    
    

      

#### Phase 7: Periodic Review & Requalification Triggers |

    

    

      
- Critical Annual (or defined-frequency) periodic review completed and documented

      
- Critical Periodic review covers: deviation history, calibration compliance, maintenance records, change history, trend analysis

      
- Critical Formal conclusion documented: "qualification status maintained" or "requalification required"

      
- High Requalification triggered by: major repair, relocation, refrigerant change, control system replacement, persistent alarm trends, or failed periodic review

      
- High Change control programme covers all modifications to CTC system (equipment, SOPs, alarm setpoints, load configuration)

      
- High All changes assessed for impact on qualification status before implementation

      
- Standard SME (Subject Matter Expert) review obtained for all qualification-impacting changes

      
- Standard Requalification scope risk-assessed: full or partial remapping based on nature and extent of change

### Question 1 — Mapping Methodology & Worst-Case Design |

      

**Scenario:** Your CDMO operates a 200 m² cold room (2–8°C) that stores biopharmaceutical drug products and biological starting materials. The room has been in use for 3 years with a monitoring system but has never been formally mapped. An EU GDP inspector is scheduled in 6 months. You are the Validation Manager tasked with designing and executing the first comprehensive temperature mapping study.

      

      

        
1. 
          **Sensor Planning:** Using ISPE CTC guidance, determine the minimum number of mapping sensors required for a 200 m² walk-in cold room. Where would you place them, and how would you justify your placement rationale? What are the mandatory locations that must always be included?
            
        

        
2. 
          **Worst-Case Conditions:** List all worst-case test conditions that must be included in your OQ mapping protocol. For each condition, explain what makes it a worst case and how you would simulate it in the 200 m² cold room.
            
        

        
3. 
          **Measurement Uncertainty:** Your mapping data logger has a specified accuracy of ±0.3°C. Describe how you would calculate the total Measurement Uncertainty (MU) for the mapping system, and how you would apply guard-banding to the 2–8°C acceptance criteria. Show the mathematical relationship between MU and guard-banded limits.
            
        

        
4. 
          **Inspection Readiness:** The EU GDP inspector specifically asks: "Show me your temperature mapping data and explain how you identified the worst-case monitoring sensor location." Walk through the complete chain of evidence you would present, from the mapping protocol to the monitoring sensor placement justification.
            
        

      

      

        

#### Model Answer Framework |

        

          
- **Sensors:** ISPE recommends ≥1 sensor per 10 m³ volume (or minimum per chamber type). For 200 m² × ~3m height ≈ 600 m³: minimum ~15–20 mapping sensors. Mandatory locations: near evaporator, near door(s), corners at 3 heights (top/middle/bottom), centre of room, near air curtain return. Total grid typically 3D with horizontal and vertical coverage.

          
- **Worst-case conditions:** (1) Empty chamber at setpoint; (2) Maximum door-opening frequency (simulated operational use); (3) Maximum ambient temperature (summer — highest expected external temperature); (4) Minimum ambient temperature (winter — risk of freezing near walls); (5) Power failure simulation; (6) Loaded chamber at maximum product density (PQ stage).

          
- **MU calculation:** Total MU = √(u_sensor² + u_calibration² + u_resolution² + u_drift²). If sensor accuracy = ±0.3°C, apply expanded uncertainty U = k × MU (k=2 for 95% confidence). Guard-banded limits = [2 + U] to [8 − U] = [2.3°C to 7.7°C] if U = 0.3°C.

          
- **Evidence chain:** Approved mapping protocol → raw data from all sensor locations → worst-case analysis report identifying hottest/coldest locations → monitoring sensor placement justification document → current calibration certificate for monitoring sensor → ongoing monitoring SOP referencing worst-case location.

        

      

    

    
    

      

### Question 2 — Temperature Excursion Investigation & MKT Assessment | MKT

- 02:30 — 9.1°C (first alarm reading)

### Question 3 — Guide Integration & Lifecycle Strategy for a New CDMO CTC Programme | CDMO CTC

      

**Scenario:** You have just been appointed as the Head of Validation at a growing aseptic fill-finish CDMO. ### Bonus Question — Regulatory Intelligence |

      

      

        
1. What is the difference between a "Cold" storage condition and a "Controlled Cold Temperature (CCT)" condition per the ISPE CTC Guide? In what business scenario is CCT more relevant than Cold?  

        
2. Why must monitoring sensors be placed at the worst-case location from mapping, rather than near the temperature controller sensor? What is the risk if they are in the same location?  

        
3. An FDA investigator asks you to explain why your OQ mapping acceptance criteria are ±0.5°C tighter than the USP <659> specification limits. What is your answer?  

        
4. What triggers a mandatory requalification of a CTC according to the ISPE CTC Guide? List at least 5 triggering events.  

      

      

        

#### Short Answer Keys |

        

          
- **Q4.1:** Cold = ≤8°C (refrigerator: 2–8°C); CCT = maintained at 2–8°C but allows 0–15°C excursions if MKT ≤8°C. CCT is more relevant for distribution scenarios (loading docks, shipping legs) where brief excursions are unavoidable but product integrity can still be demonstrated via MKT.

          
- **Q4.2:** The controller sensor is placed for equipment control accuracy (near the evaporator or return air), not to represent the worst product temperature. If the monitoring sensor is co-located with the controller, it will show the best-case temperature rather than the temperature the product actually experiences. This could mask excursions at the worst-case location where product is stored.

          
- **Q4.3:** The tighter criteria reflect application of Measurement Uncertainty (MU) guard-banding per ISO/IEC Guide 98-3 (GUM). Our mapping sensors have a combined MU of ±0.5°C. To ensure that any measurement within our acceptance window guarantees the true product temperature is within USP limits, we apply guard-banding: if MU = ±0.5°C, acceptance criteria = [2 + 0.5] to [8 − 0.5] = 2.5°C to 7.5°C.

          
- **Q4.4:** Requalification triggers: (1) Major equipment repair (e.g. compressor replacement); (2) Relocation of the CTC; (3) Significant change to load configuration or storage capacity; (4) Replacement of control or monitoring system; (5) Recurring or unresolved temperature excursions; (6) Failed periodic review; (7) Significant facility modification affecting ambient temperature or air circulation; (8) Change of product type with different temperature sensitivity stored in the chamber.
