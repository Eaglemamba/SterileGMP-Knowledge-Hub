## Background |

                

Why effective sampling matters — and what goes wrong when it fails

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    Original Text (English)
                    

Effective sampling is of paramount importance to the success of any pharmaceutical critical utility system. Unfortunately, there is very little regulatory guidance with regards to sampling. Extracting a representative sample from a utility system is an involved and complicated process and error may be introduced due to:

                    

                        
- Environmental conditions

                        
- Sample valve design

                        
- Sample point location

                        
- Technique

                        
- Container type

                        
- Sample handling

                        
- Sample storage and transport

                        
- Other factors

                    

                    

The impact of these factors is widespread and occurs throughout industry. Errors due to sampling are so common that the **1993 US FDA "Guide to the Inspection of High Purity Water Systems"** specifically addresses sampling errors in recommendations to their field inspectors by stating that:

                    

                        "It is expected that [Water For Injection systems] be essentially sterile. Since sampling frequently is performed in non-sterile areas and is not truly aseptic, occasional low level [bacteria] counts due to sampling errors may occur."
                        — US FDA, Guide to the Inspection of High Purity Water Systems, 1993
                    

                    

Improper sampling may be responsible for generating **Out Of Specification (OOS) data** and prompting investigations when a system is actually producing and delivering acceptable quality. Conversely, improper sampling may also create a more dangerous scenario where the quality is actually OOS but the data incorrectly indicates that the quality is acceptable.

                    

In either case, there could be negative impact on company image, cost, productivity, ethics, and regulatory liability, resulting from improper sampling. Identifying the elements of proper sampling and eliminating the underlying reasons for these negative consequences formed the primary driving force behind this ISPE Good Practice Guide.

## What is a Sample? |

                

Defining the intent and inherent limitation of sampling

            

        

        

            

                

                    Original Text (English)
                    

Simply stated, the intent of sampling is to take a small but **representative portion** of a much larger stream, where the sample collected accurately represents the content of the larger stream.

                    

The sample collected should not be altered or changed in any way because the sampling process, but this is an almost impossibly difficult proposition as all sampled utilities come into contact with air, containers, etc. during the sampling process.

## Why Do We Sample? |

                

The five purposes of pharmaceutical utility sampling programs

            

        

        

            

                

                    Original Text (English)
                    

It is most desirable to monitor quality parameters using **in-line, on-line, or at-line monitors**, which minimize and, in some cases, eliminate, sample handling issues completely. However, when the quality attribute of concern cannot be analyzed with an instrument or where an instrument is not present, a sample has to be removed from a stream and evaluated **off-line**. In most cases, these attributes are microbiological or endotoxin in nature.

                    

Sampling generally occurs for one or more of the following reasons:

                    

                        
- **Regulatory requirements** — compendial requirements for CQAs

                        
- **Quality control purposes** — ensure utility meets established quality parameters; samples collected exactly as the utility is used in manufacturing

                        
- **Process control purposes** — confirm a particular process is operating under a state of control; frequency differs from QC sampling

                        
- **Technical, investigational or diagnostic purposes** — triggered by Alert Level excursions; as-needed basis

                        
- **Test method validation purposes** — periodic duplicate sampling to document consistency

                    

                    

                        **Note:** There is no purpose classified as "sampling for informational purposes only." Every sample point should have a specific purpose. The authors recommend abandoning this designation as being too vague and non-specific.
                    

                    

When developing a sampling program, a **risk-based approach** should be utilized to establish or adjust sampling frequencies based on data collected over time. Alert (2σ) and Action (3σ) Levels are established based on actual operating conditions.

## How Often Do We Need to Sample? |

                

Risk-based frequency determination for different utility grades

            

        

        

            

                

                    Original Text (English)
                    

Sampling frequency depends on the system and the grade of the critical utility.

                    

For **Water For Injection (WFI)**: it is an expectation that water samples will be taken from each Point Of Use (POU) and sample location in the distribution system at least **once per week during validation**. Industry has continued to utilize this same sampling frequency beyond the completion of the Performance Qualification, although it is not mandated.

                    

By contrast, **purified water** has no such preconceived sampling requirements, so the end user is not mandated to follow any specific schedule during or following validation.

                    

Pharmaceutical steam, compressed air, and process gases have different frequencies during validation and ongoing operation.

                    

When no sampling frequency is specified, a **risk-based approach** may be utilized in conjunction with the purpose of sampling to establish initial sampling frequencies. As data is generated and an operating baseline is established, the collected data should be reviewed to determine if the operating limits, Alert and Action Levels, and/or the sampling frequency may be adjusted (increased or decreased) based on data generated on system operation.

## Are All Quality Attributes Uniformly Distributed? |

                

The most critical paradigm shift in pharmaceutical utility sampling

            

        

        

            
            

                

### Paradigm Shift Alert |

                

The answer is a resounding NO — and this is one of the most important paradigm shifts that the reader should understand.

                

            

            

                

                    Original Text (English)
                    

The answer is a resounding **no**, and this is one of the most important paradigm shifts that the reader should understand when defining and evaluating a sampling program.

                    

In pharmaceutical water systems:

                    

                        
- If a quality attribute is a **chemical constituent** (e.g., nitrates, conductivity, dissolved metals, TOC) then the constituent is considered **uniformly or homogeneously distributed** (Second Law of Thermodynamics).

                        
- However, if the quality attribute is **biological in nature** (e.g., bacteria, endotoxin), then the quality attribute will **not be uniformly distributed**. Non-uniform or heterogeneously distributed attributes are much more challenging to sample accurately as these attributes may vary from point to point in a system.

                    

                    

A significant portion of this Guide is devoted to providing guidance on the most effective methods for accurate sample collection involving these **heterogeneously distributed quality attributes**.

                    

In pharmaceutical steam systems, compressed air, and process gas systems, attributes may or may not be uniformly distributed and sampling programs must take this into account.

### 2.1 Introduction

        

Water is one of the most widely used materials in the pharmaceutical industry and is commonly used as a:

        

          
- Product or Ingredient

          
- Cleaning agent

          
- Sterilizing media

          
- Analytical agent

          
- Formulation aid

        

        

When water is used for pharmaceutical purposes it needs to be tested to ensure that it meets monograph requirements or other specifications for applicable CQAs (Critical Quality Attributes). When water is extracted for sampling purposes at any point in a system, proper sampling is the key to ensuring that the collected sample accurately represents the quality of the water at that particular point in the system.

        

When water is sampled in the same way as it is used during manufacturing, sampling is considered the best indicator available of the quality of water going into a product.

        

          

#### Key Principle: Sample as You Use

          

The closer a sampling method mirrors actual manufacturing use, the more representative the result. This principle drives every decision in this guide — from valve selection to flushing volume to sample container choice.

        

        

### Water Types in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing

        

All major pharmacopoeias define at minimum two compendial bulk grades:

        

          
- **Purified Water (PW)** — for non-parenteral products, equipment cleaning, and general manufacturing

          
- **Water For Injection (WFI)** — for parenteral products, ophthalmic preparations, and final equipment rinse in aseptic processing

        

        

The European Pharmacopoeia additionally defines **Highly Purified Water (HPW)** — meeting WFI chemical specifications but produced by a non-distillation process (e.g., double-pass RO + UF).

        

All pharmacopoeias (USP, EP, JP, Chinese Pharmacopoeia) require monitoring of the following CQAs:

        

          
- Conductivity — ionic purity indicator

          
- TOC (Total Organic Carbon) — organic contamination indicator

          
- Endotoxin — WFI and HPW only; LAL or rFC method

          
- Bioburden — not a monograph requirement, but universally tested

## Homogeneously Distributed Impurities |

      

    

  

  

    

    

      

        

### 2.1.1.1 Homogeneously Distributed Impurities

        

Chemical impurities, small particulates, and other soluble impurities are typically expected to be homogeneously distributed throughout a distribution system because of the solubility and nature of these impurities, and because of chemical equilibrium processes.

        

Since the impurity levels at any one location in a distribution system should be identical to the impurity levels at every other location within the distribution system, sampling for homogeneously distributed impurities **may not be required at every POU** and may be performed at a limited number of locations, provided that there is no likelihood of downstream sources of contamination.

        

Homogeneously distributed impurities are candidates for continuous monitoring using on-line, in-line, or at-line instruments if the impurity levels at the instrumentation locations have been validated through sampling and laboratory analysis to be predictive of the levels at all POUs.

        

Sampling and testing of these impurities is typically done at the **return of a distribution loop**, but may be done in other locations. Impurities falling into the homogeneously distributed category include:

        

          
- Ionic impurities (detected as electrical conductivity or resistivity)

          
- Organic impurities (detected as TOC)

          
- Nitrates (EP requirement for PW and WFI)

          
- Heavy metals (EP requirement for PW — not required if conductivity meets WFI limits)

        

        

          "Localized sources of chemical impurities may be introduced to a system downstream of a particular point... such as from hoses, gaskets, or a POU heat exchanger may contaminate the water as it exits the system at one particular location."
        

        

Sub-loops, parallel loops, or other isolated portions may not be accurately represented by on-line instrumentation in the main distribution system. In these cases, additional instruments or manual POU testing may be required.

        

### 2.1.1.2 Heterogeneously Distributed Impurities

        

Bacteria and endotoxin are attributes that are **not uniformly distributed** throughout a water system. The majority of bacteria present in the system (99.9 to 99.99%) reside in biofilm — attached to system surfaces, not free-floating.

        

It is the small fraction (0.01 to 0.1%) of free floating or planktonic bacteria that contribute to the bacteria and endotoxin levels detected by sampling.

        

Biofilm development occurs more rapidly in areas of low or no flow. Examples of low-flow areas that allow bacteria to attach include:

        

          
- Within any non-flowing side connections of any length, including dead legs

          
- On the upstream/wet side of valve sealing surfaces

          
- On the water-stopping sealing surfaces within the valve

          
- On the downstream side of valve surfaces (which may remain wet or moist)

          
- On fittings and gaskets used to connect a hose or other connector

          
- Within the hose or connector to the actual location of water use

          
- Within any additional external device: flow meters, pressure regulators, heat exchangers, check valves, hoses, fittings, adapters

        

        

Because of variable local flow conditions, bacteria and endotoxin populations may vary widely at different locations. Therefore, **sampling is required of each POU location** to confirm the presence or absence of bacteria. Sampling only the supply and return is insufficient to characterize bacterial populations throughout the system.

## Purpose of Sampling |

      

    

  

  

    

      

        

### 2.1.2 Purpose of Sampling

        

The purpose of sampling should be identified and documented for each potential sample location within a system as early as possible during the design process. This process should justify and document the requirement for sampling and identify the reason for sampling at a particular location.

        

The purpose of any potential sampling location should fall into one or more of the following categories:

        

          
1. Regulatory requirements

          
2. Consensus standards

          
3. Process control purposes

          
4. Quality control purposes

          
5. Technical, investigational, or diagnostic purposes

        

        

The justification for a potential sampling location should also consider:

        

          
- The impurities of interest at the potential location

          
- The type of valve and other accessories best suited for the location

          
- Whether sampling should be performed at an established frequency or on an event-based frequency (i.e., reaching an alert or action level)

          
- Accessibility to staff and safety considerations

          
- Any special considerations for sample handling after collection

        

        

### 2.1.2.1 Regulatory Requirements

        

All pharmacopoeias define two bulk grades of water for pharmaceutical purposes: **Purified Water (PW)** and **Water For Injection (WFI)**. *Highly Purified Water (HPW)* is an additional grade defined by the European Pharmacopoeia.

        

The general consensus across pharmacopoeias is that the following impurities should be monitored:

        

          
- Conductivity

          
- TOC

          
- Endotoxin (for WFI only)

        

        

Some pharmacopoeias also require testing for heavy metals and nitrates. While bacteria testing is not a monograph requirement, all pharmaceutical companies sample and test for bacteria.

        

The pharmaceutical industry references the **1993 US FDA "Guide to the Inspection of High Purity Water Systems"** which stipulates the following maximum bacteria action limits:

        

          
- PW: ≤ 100 cfu/mL

          
- WFI: ≤ 10 cfu/100 mL

        

        

The pharmaceutical industry has universally adopted these limits. Thus, water systems are routinely sampled and tested for: Conductivity, TOC, Endotoxin (WFI only), and Bacteria.

        

### 2.1.2.2 Consensus Standards

        

Consensus standards such as ISO, ASTM, AAMI, and CLSI have application-specific standards for water purity. These may apply where the use of water within the facility is consistent with those standards. Water quality specifications from these standards may be found in Appendix 1 of the guide.

        

### 2.1.2.3 Process Control Purposes

        

Monitoring a water purification system for process control involves monitoring the performance and success of the various treatment processes for specific attributes of concern and includes following the quantitative trends of those attributes.

        

Process control indicating trigger values, such as Alert Levels and Action Levels, are typically used to assist in identifying when the process is deviating from its normal operating capabilities.

        

        

          

#### Alert Levels

          

Alert Levels should be set at levels near the top edge of normal data trends — typically at **two standard deviations (2σ)** from normal operating levels, based on analysis of historic performance data.

          

An occasional Alert Level excursion barely above the limit could be a normal expectation within the upper reaches of normal operating trends. However, frequent excursions should be considered out of trend. Responses include notification of quality, manufacturing, and maintenance personnel, along with closer attention to routine data.

        

        

          

#### Action Levels

          

Action Levels are typically set at **three standard deviations (3σ)** from normal operating levels. They should be:

          

            
- Above normal data trends and indicative of routine process controls beginning to fail

            
- Far enough from the specification so that remedial action can restore control before a specification excursion occurs

          

          

Resampling and retesting should be one of the first corrective actions. Where high values are confirmed, appropriate remediation activities should be instituted and the cause(s) determined to preclude recurrence.

## 2.1.2.4–2.1.2.5: Quality Control & Investigative Sampling |

      

    

  

  

    

      

        

### 2.1.2.4 Quality Control Purposes

        

When monitoring for quality control of the water, the intent is to:

        

          
1. Exactly duplicate the quality of water that is being used for manufacturing by collecting samples at routine intervals

          
2. Collect water samples at the beginning of a period when the water is being drawn from the system for manufacturing use

        

        

Exact duplication of the preparatory procedures utilized by manufacturing should be used during sampling, and should include use of the same:

        

          
- Outlet sanitization practice (if applicable)

          
- Hose and gasket

          
- Outlet flush procedure (if any)

          
- Attached devices such as heat exchangers, reducers, adapters, check valves, and flow meters

        

        

The purpose of including all of these potential sources of contamination in the flow path for sampling is to attempt to duplicate the water quality delivered to the manufacturing process, including any heterogeneously distributed contaminants from associated biofilms on or within these components.

        

Hard-piped or otherwise permanent connections between the water system and manufacturing equipment, as well as automatic valves, create challenges for obtaining samples that duplicate the way water is used by manufacturing. One engineering solution may be to utilize a three-way divert valve that may be installed on the manufacturing equipment.

        

While POU valves with a built-in upstream sample port may be used when sampling for process control purposes, they are typically **not used when sampling for quality control purposes**. The only exceptions are:

        

          
- The conduit is routinely sanitized with a validated procedure prior to water use for manufacturing (hot water, steam, or chemicals), so the conduit provides no added contamination

          
- There is no feasible engineering solution that allows sampling exactly as used during manufacturing

          
- The relevant attributes at that particular POU are not microbial (only homogeneously distributed chemical impurities, e.g., for a washer whose cycle ends with a hot drying step)

        

        

### 2.1.2.5 Sampling for Technical, Investigative, or Diagnostic Reasons

        

Sampling performed for technical, investigative, or diagnostic reasons may include:

        

          
- Gathering data to better understand or pinpoint the root cause of a process deviation or specification excursion

          
- To determine the extent of contamination within a system

          
- To spot-check the long-term efficacy of a particular remedial action (e.g., from a CAPA response)

        

        

Additional sampling points installed for technical, investigative, or troubleshooting purposes may not be routinely sampled. Data from technical, investigative, or diagnostic sampling can also be used to establish a baseline for non-routinely measured water attributes which may be valuable in future investigations. Samplings of this nature are typically done only occasionally and only when needed for long-term profiling or as part of an investigation.

        

          

#### Alert, Action, and Out Of Specification (OOS) Levels

          

Sampling could be performed from the same sample location for both process control and quality control purposes. Different Alert and Action Levels could be established; however, process control levels may typically be established at tighter values than those for quality control.

          

For an excursion above a specification limit, an investigation should be performed to determine, correct, and prevent the cause. The particular investigation would be an OOS investigation and the impact of this water on the product should be assessed.

## 2.2 Determining Sampling Locations |

      

    

  

  

    

      

        

### 2.2 Determining Sampling Locations

        

Locating sample points to ensure samples are taken from suitable process locations before and after unit operations, at points of use and elsewhere, should assure a successful water system monitoring program.

        

A Risk Analysis (RA) may be performed to help determine sample locations. The RA can provide a rationale for the sampling points in a water system and can help to define the intended purpose of each sampling location, based on established CQAs and Critical Process Parameters (CPPs).

        

Sample valve locations should be addressed not only on a P&ID but should also be addressed during equipment and piping installation. Components, hardware, or other items that interfere with correct sampling techniques should be avoided through careful planning and appropriate field execution. Considerations should include the disposition of waste from flushing, location of sample ports relative to other equipment, local environmental issues, and other concerns. **Design in consultation with QA, QC, and Microbiology Subject Matter Experts (SMEs) is desirable.**

        

          "Sample valves located in return loop piping that are inaccessible to samplers, sample valves blocked by other equipment or with otherwise limited access... do not accomplish the goal of ensuring that the samples are representative. Improper location may put a sampler's safety at risk or may yield data with a high degree of variability, and may result in an increase in alert limit, action limit, and OOS findings."
        

        

Sampling locations should include all appropriate sections of the system including:

        

          
- Source water

          
- Pretreatment unit operations

          
- Primary and final treatment

          
- Storage

          
- Distribution systems (main loops and sub loops)

          
- POU and any special POU treatments

## 2.2.1–2.2.3: Source Water, Pretreatment & Primary Treatment |

      

    

  

  

    

      

        

### 2.2.1 Source Water

        

Source water feeding any compendial pharmaceutical water system should meet local drinking water standards such as those specified by the **WHO**, the **US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)**, drinking water regulations of the EU or Japan, or other competent authorities.

        

Source water that does not meet potable or drinking water standards should be brought into compliance with recognized drinking water regulations prior to being used to feed a pharmaceutical water system.

        

Chlorination (or chloramination) of municipal water has become common in many areas of the world for microbiological control. If residual chlorine levels are inadequate for microbial control, rechlorination may be required and should be defined in a SOP and monitored on a regular basis through an appropriately located sample port.

        

Complete chemical analysis of source water that confirms it meets the appropriate potable or drinking water requirements may be performed on a periodic basis to trend incoming contaminant levels and to make system adjustments if required. If a source water storage tank is employed, additional testing after storage is advisable before water enters the water purification system.

        

### 2.2.2 Pretreatment Unit Operations

        

Sampling locations should be included **before and after each unit operation** for diagnostic purposes so that monitoring can be performed as required. Sampling before and after individual unit operations helps in establishing a unit operation's performance.

        

The following unit operations are examples of monitoring locations that may be considered:

        

          
- Before and after media filtration (multi-media, cartridge, etc.)

          
- Before and after softeners

          
- Before and after Activated Carbon Filters (microbial monitoring is additionally critical here because activated carbon beds tend to promote microbial growth)

          
- Before and after Ultraviolet units

          
- Before and after chlorination or dechlorination units

          
- Before and after Reverse Osmosis (RO), Continuous Electro Deionization (CEDI), or ion exchange units

          
- Before and after any other unit operations within the pretreatment system boundary

        

        

### 2.2.3 Primary and Final Treatment

        

Primary and final treatment for compendial or non-compendial water may include several different treatment technologies. Examples include:

        

          
- Reverse osmosis

          
- Continuous electro deionization (CEDI)

          
- Ion exchange deionization

          
- Microfiltration / Ultrafiltration / Nanofiltration

          
- Distillation (still for WFI)

          
- Ultraviolet treatment

          
- Heat exchangers

        

        

Sampling locations should be provided before and after each treatment unit operation as appropriate. Attributes to be evaluated are based on equipment performance expectations and may include: Conductivity, TOC, Chlorine, Ammonia, Hardness, Silica, Silt Density Index (SDI), Microbial, and Endotoxin.

        

Final treatment unit output water should be monitored using **in-line, on-line or at-line analyzers** where possible. Conductivity and TOC analyzers are usually used for these purposes. Where multiple final treatment units are present in a facility, there should be a sample point before and after each.

## 2.2.4–2.2.6: Storage Tanks, Distribution Systems & Points of Use |

      

    

  

  

    

      

        

### 2.2.4 Storage Tanks

        

Sampling locations directly on a storage tank may provide valuable information if any microbial buildup (biofilm) is occurring in the tank and also allows testing of the stored water quality. A sample valve installed for direct sampling from the storage tank may be required for validation or for diagnostic purposes and may or may not be used for routine sampling.

        

Sampling valves located at storage tanks should be easily accessible and should minimize the risk of contamination of the sample and also the storage tank itself during sampling.

        

Tank water quality can also be assessed **indirectly** through monitoring of:

        

          
- The primary generation supply (incoming water to storage)

          
- The loop return (water returning to storage)

          
- The tank outlet which feeds the pumps and subsequently the use points

        

        

These samples indicate the water quality prior to entry to the storage tank, after returning from the loop, and at the storage tank outlet, providing an indirect indication of the impact the storage tank is having on water quality.

        

### 2.2.5 Distribution Systems (main loops and sub loops)

        

Quality control sampling locations in the main distribution system should include **all POUs**, with sampling occurring in a manner identical to how the water is used by manufacturing. Process control sampling locations should be located before the first and after the last POU and at other specified locations.

        

Sampling the loop return after the last POU ensures process control as this location may be the worst case location for water quality in the entire distribution system. Water systems with multiple return loops should have sampling locations in each of the return loops. For one-way (non-recirculating) branch systems, all POUs should be sampled.

        

When temperature changes are required, the distribution system should be designed such that the pharmaceutical water is maintained at a higher pressure than heating/cooling media such as in a heat exchanger to prevent leakage from the non-sanitary utility to the sanitary side of the heat exchanger.

        

Each sub-loop returning to the main loop return should have a **sampling point before the sub loop connects to the main loop return**.

        

In the distribution loop of a pharmaceutical water system, all use point valves and sample valves should be of a sanitary design. Sample points in a laboratory system may not be sanitary and may contain dead legs such as may be present in a gooseneck faucet.

        

### 2.2.6 Points of Use

        

A Point Of Use (POU) is defined as the location where the water delivered from the water distribution system is actually used. POUs may be located on the main loop or in sub loops.

        

When sampling for quality control purposes, the POU is typically **not the valve on the distribution system itself** from which the water is taken. Instead, it is the **end of the delivery system** that transfers the water from the distribution system to the process (e.g., sinks, autoclaves, washers, process tanks). Sampling at POUs should include all components and surfaces along this water delivery pathway (e.g., heat exchangers, hoses, fittings) that the water needs to travel through to reach the process.

        

When the outlet of a POU cannot be sampled, the next most representative sampling location should be identified. Rationale for the selection should be provided in the risk assessment. Validation and/or routine monitoring data should conclude that the sample location is representative of the POU.

        

Every POU in a water distribution system that is not active should be **locked out or tagged out of service** and should be appropriately maintained to prevent contamination of the POU or the distribution system.

### Key Takeaways: Sections 2.1–2.2

        

          
- Water is used in 5 roles; sampling must mirror the actual use condition

          
- Chemical (homogeneous) impurities can be monitored with validated online instruments at the loop return; microbiological (heterogeneous) attributes require per-POU sampling

          
- 99.9%+ of bacteria reside in biofilm; planktonic counts are lagging indicators

          
- Five purposes of sampling: regulatory, consensus, process control, quality control, investigative — each requires different location and frequency logic

          
- Alert Levels = 2σ; Action Levels = 3σ — both must be below specification to allow time for remediation

          
- QC sampling must include all downstream components (hoses, heat exchangers) to be representative

          
- Loop return = worst-case for PC; most-remote POU = worst-case for microbial risk

          
- Each sub-loop must have its own sample point before rejoining the main return

          
- Inactive POUs must be locked/tagged out; pre-use sampling required before restarting

## Developing Sampling Plans |

      

Introduction — life cycle approach, documentation requirements

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

The frequencies and durations of sampling plans have historically coincided with the phases of the validation program for the water system. Using this rationale, the final phase of validation forms the basis for ongoing sampling frequencies with the goal of ensuring that the system is maintained in a validated state.

        

However, the use of risk analysis tools coupled with periodic data review may be used to alter the frequency of sampling when the data provides overwhelming support for a change (increase or decrease) in sampling frequency. Regular data review should be documented as part of the planning process.

        

### A sampling plan should define and document:

        

          
- Potential sampling points and the purpose for each

          
- Sample locations intended for continuous, regular, routine, periodic, or as-needed monitoring

          
- Attributes to be tested at each sampling location

          
- The frequency at which samples are required for any given attribute

          
- The purpose and use of the resulting data for those attributes

          
- When sample valves and use point valves may be used as the same valve

        

        

Sampling plans are required for both new and renovated systems and may involve sampling locations throughout the water purification and distribution systems, including source water.

        

In specific circumstances, adequate scientific and statistical rationale may be required to not sample at an installed sampling point.

        

Sample locations installed for technical, investigative, or diagnostic purposes may not be included in a routine sampling plan. The purpose and use of these sample locations should be defined, and they should be used on an as-needed basis, be suitably maintained, and not pose a contamination risk to the system.

        

**Note:** Designating sampling locations "For Information Only (FIO)" or "for future use" should be avoided.

## Background Philosophy |

      

Life cycle approach to water system sampling — from design through PSE

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

Sample plan development should be based on the expected sampling required during the life cycle of the system.

        

### A typical life cycle scenario for water sampling activities may include:

        

          
- Conceptual and detailed design

          
- Construction and installation

          
- Commissioning

          
- Performance Qualification (including worst case test and special scenario testing)

          
- Ongoing testing during production

          
- System changes and modifications (major and minor)

          
- Periodic System Evaluation (PSE)

        

        

Sampling guidance is provided in Tables 2.2, 2.3, and 2.4 regarding specific sampling locations, testing frequencies, and the type of monitoring or testing for various activities including commissioning and qualification/verification.

        

Automated electronic monitoring of conductivity and TOC can provide a comprehensive database from which chemical water quality trends may be observed over time. When analyzed, this information may allow for minimization or elimination of manual chemical testing of the water during routine operations.

        

However, during the Performance Qualification, confirmation that the on-line or in-line readings are representative of water quality used should be verified if this data is going to be used for product release purposes.

        

Water may be delivered at several different temperatures for use at a single POU. The water sample collected from this point should be collected at the worst case temperature for microbiological control. For example, if water is delivered at both 65°C and 20°C from the same POU, the sample should be collected at 20°C.

## Regulatory Requirements Related to Monitoring Frequency |

      

USP, EP, JP, ICH Q7, FDA — comparative regulatory landscape

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

No well-defined regulatory requirements exist regarding routine sampling frequency and duration. Various pharmacopoeias provide specifications for microbiological and chemical contaminants for the various grades of water, but do not define the frequency or duration of sampling.

        

### General expectations from compendia and consensus standards:

        

          **USP <1231>** — "Water systems should be monitored at a frequency that is sufficient to ensure that the system is in control, and continues to produce water of acceptable quality"; and "The sampling plan should take into consideration the desired attributes of the water being sampled."
        

        

          **EP GMP Vol. 4 Annex 1** — "Water sources, water treatment equipment and treated water should be monitored regularly for chemical and biological contamination and, as appropriate, for endotoxins."
        

        

          **JP16 General Information G8 (Section 4.2)** — "Sampling frequency should be established based on the data from validation studies on the system."
        

        

          **ICH Q7 Section 4.20** — "All utilities that could impact on quality (e.g., steam, gases, compressed air) should be qualified and appropriately monitored and action should be taken when limits are exceeded."
        

        

          **1993 US FDA Guide to Inspection of High Purity Water Systems** — "The results of the Commissioning & Qualification are designed to demonstrate that all of the process steps and components within a Purified Water or Water For Injection system are capable of consistently providing water meeting the necessary water quality requirements."
        

        

The FDA guide provides the most specific guidelines for sampling frequency during system validation, but does not stipulate frequency beyond validation and recognizes that more than one approach may be acceptable.

        

**Summary:** Routine sampling should always be adequate for the intended purpose. Frequency and duration decisions should be based on risk assessment with documented rationales.

## Commissioning and Pre-validation |

      

GEP-based testing to establish baseline before formal PQ

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

Water testing of the pretreatment, generation and distribution systems for PW and WFI systems during commissioning is an exercise in Good Engineering Practice (GEP). ## Source Water |

      

Potable water requirements, responsibility boundaries, risk assessment

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

USP <1231> states: "To ensure adherence to certain minimal chemical and microbiological quality standards, water used in the production of drug substances or as source or feed water for the preparation of the various types of purified waters must meet the requirements of the National Primary Drinking Water Regulations (NPDWR) (40 CFR 141) issued by the U.S. EPA, or the drinking water regulations of the European Union or Japan, or the WHO drinking water guidelines."

        

Various nomenclature may be in use across the globe for drinking water including: potable water, tap water, city water, and feed water, however, the quality intent remains consistently the same.

        

WHO Technical Report Series No. 929, Annex 3 recommends daily sampling of incoming potable water during the early validation phase to verify its quality, but this may not be required in all areas of the world.

        

If there is little or no knowledge of the feed water quality at the system inlet, it is recommended to take daily samples for analysis. If the potable water quality is already well known and historical data shows good, stable water quality, a rationale can be made to perform less source water testing during the PQ period.

        

Typically, the potable/drinking water supplier is responsible for the potable water quality up to the property boundary; thereafter the responsibility is transferred to the property owner/site operator. Pharmaceutical product manufacturers are ultimately responsible for end product quality.

        

An impact assessment should be performed on potable water depending on the attributes, the treatment technology employed, and the end use of the water.

## Pretreatment Sampling |

      

Unit-operation specific sampling — filters, softeners, carbon beds, RO, CEDI

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

Pretreatment represents designated treatment steps typically prior to primary/final treatment. When used as feed water for WFI or Pure Steam, pretreated water does not necessarily need to comply with PW requirements to ensure WFI/PS quality. Note: the Chinese Pharmacopoeia requires that supply water to a WFI still must comply with PW specification. USP, EP, and JP do not require PW quality as feed for production of WFI.

        

In order to ensure that the pretreatment fulfills its purpose, sampling is carried out specifically for each purification step:

        

          
- **Sand/mixed media filter:** SDI measured before and after. Worst case: just before automatic back flush.

          
- **Water softener:** Hardness measured before and after. Worst case: immediately before regeneration.

          
- **Carbon filter:** Chlorine concentration before and after. Worst case: immediately before back flush.

          
- **RO system:** Conductivity before and after the RO unit.

          
- **CEDI:** Conductivity before and after the CEDI.

        

        

For all purification steps, microbiological testing may be required, including before and after the RO if applicable. Acceptable microbiological levels should be defined for each purification step. There should at least be an Action Level with consideration for adding an Alert Level if appropriate.

        

If, during risk assessment, the pretreatment is determined to be a no-impact system, samples should be taken for record purposes during PQ. If determined to be indirect impact, a rationale should be developed for the sampling frequency during PQ of the pretreatment.

Original Text (English)

        

POU sampling plans should rotate through all use points on the system, with the expectation that samples are collected on a daily basis from various use points, and that all use points are sampled on a routine basis based on risk analysis. System representative locations (e.g., supply and/or return) may be sampled on a daily basis for process control purposes and to aid in the investigation of OOS results from individual use points.

        

During the PSE of a WFI (or PW) system, sampling frequency may be evaluated. Increases or decreases in sampling frequency should be based on historical data and should only occur when data indicates that more or less frequent sampling is justified based on performance of the use point or system.

        

Sample frequency should not be arbitrarily adjusted without adequate scientific and statistical justification. A decrease in sampling frequency may result in increased regulatory scrutiny.

        

Decreased sampling frequency also puts more product at risk during investigations because of longer periods between acceptable test results surrounding a deviant test result. In short, careful consideration should be given before decreasing sampling frequency relative to the increased number of product batches that will be put at risk.

## Sampling for Performance Qualification/Verification |

      

PQ framework introduction — new vs. modified systems

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

The PQ/Verification sampling plan covers three scenarios:

        

          
1. Typical (nominal) use conditions

          
2. Worst case use conditions (system limits)

          
3. Special scenarios

        

        

The goal is to document that the stated water quality is delivered from the water system under all of the above conditions and that the water complies with the requirements for the grade of pharmaceutical water type specified.

        

The period for the PQ depends on whether it applies to a completely new system or to modifications made to an existing system.

        

### PQ Sampling Phases — FDA Three-Phase Approach:

        

**Phase 1 — Initial Sampling:** Most intensive, typically 2–4 weeks (10–20 consecutive working days). All sample and use points in the distribution system tested daily. Worst case: first day after weekend if no water consumption occurred.

        

**Phase 2 — Intermediary Sampling:** Less intensive, 10–20 working days. Reduced sampling frequency. Demonstrates repeatability and consistent quality using SOPs. May provide additional worst case data since use points are not as frequently active.

        

**Phase 3 — Extended Sampling:** Not less than the remaining balance of one year (minus Phase 1 and Phase 2 time). Captures seasonal variations in potable water feed, development of the system's natural flora, and any other annual variations. Typically reduced frequency. Forms the basis for the routine monitoring and testing program once validation is complete.

      

      

        

        

          

## Purpose of PQ Sampling | PQ

      

Documenting water quality delivery under all operating conditions

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

The purpose of PQ/verification is to document that the stated water quality is delivered from the water system during:

        

          
1. Typical (nominal) use conditions

          
2. Worst case use conditions (system limits)

          
3. Special scenarios

        

        

...and that the water complies with the requirements for the grade of pharmaceutical water type specified.

        

The period for the PQ depends on whether it applies to a completely new system or to modifications made to an existing system. When minor changes are made to a qualified system, reduced sampling requirements may be considered.

        

The PQ sampling protocol should describe the purpose, scope, and strategy (including justification). The sampling plan and the expected data from in-line/on-line measuring equipment should be described in the protocol. The prerequisites and the acceptance criteria for the performance testing should also be stated in the protocol.

        

All stakeholders involved in the PQ (production, engineering, quality control, and quality assurance) should have an opportunity to review and comment on the protocol. Communication and information sharing between the different participants before, during, and after the PQ are essential for efficient and successful qualification.

## Worst Case Use Conditions |

      

System limit testing — stagnation, maximum flow, sanitization boundaries, maintenance events

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

A worst case scenario tests the limits of the system and is defined as the most extreme situations during the operation of the water system.

        

### Examples of worst case conditions:

        

          
- **No water consumption** (e.g., during a weekend or shutdown): Can result in a rise in the level of bioburden and conductivity. The first day of the week after no weekend consumption may represent a worst case scenario for sampling.

          
- **Maximum use of water:** Confirms the system or unit operation is capable of delivering suitable water quality during a period of maximum delivery.

          
- **Just before sanitization:** If routine sanitization occurs, sampling just before the start of sanitization captures the maximum time of accumulation of bioburden.

          
- **Just after sanitization:** Confirms that sanitization has been effective and that chemical sanitant residuals are within acceptable limits.

          
- **After a maintenance procedure:** After a periodic event such as derouging and/or passivation, confirming water quality restoration.

## Special Scenarios |

      

Power failure, emergency maintenance, seasonal qualification — pre-validation to minimize future disruptions

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

Special scenarios are situations which may occur during normal use either as unplanned or as planned scenarios. Often these special scenarios occur at inconvenient times, and it would be advantageous to validate these scenarios to avoid delays when they occur.

        

Validation of special scenarios can be part of the ordinary PQ of the pharmaceutical water system, or it can be performed as a specific PQ for only this purpose.

        

The validation of a special scenario may need to be performed in a worst case scenario and be documented by water quality sampling data. The outcome and conclusion of the validation, based on the data, can decrease time spent on the procedure during daily operation, when going from the special scenario to normal condition, due to less sampling, sanitization, etc.

        

### Examples of special scenarios:

        

          
- **Power failure:** To document that the pharmaceutical water quality is unchanged after restarting after a defined period of time without any sanitization. A prerequisite for this test is no breach of integrity of the water system. If water retains quality for three hours after power failure, then up to a three-hour power failure can be followed by normal operation without additional sanitization (provided system integrity is not otherwise compromised).

          
- **Emergency or unplanned maintenance procedures:** Including unusual or unexpected component failure, system damage from internal or external action. Validation of all emergency or unplanned maintenance procedures is not practical.

        

        

Acceptance criteria should be set up properly for the special scenario. A special scenario test may be performed for a longer period than the period used as the limit, to incorporate a safety factor (e.g., testing four hours to validate a three-hour limit).

## Risk-Based Approach to C&Q/Verification |

      

CQA definition, impact classification, non-critical equipment testing strategy

    

  

  

    

      

        

Original Text (English)

        

A risk-based approach to C&Q/verification may be utilized to develop the monitoring and testing strategy. Risk analyses or impact assessments may be utilized to determine what equipment or functions of the water system are considered critical.

        

There may be unit operations or process steps within the generation or distribution system that require testing during initial commissioning as well as during ongoing routine operation (e.g., UV treatment, pumps, heat exchangers) that may not be considered critical.

        

If determined to be non-critical, such equipment operation (e.g., multimedia filters, softeners) may be tested only during commissioning as part of the C&Q/Verification process. Testing relating to the continued operation of a unit process may become part of a routine monitoring program based upon the impact to the system and the finished quality of the water.

        

Frequency of routine testing could be adjusted based on the consistency of equipment performance. These tests assure proper maintenance and operation of the water system (e.g., regeneration of resin beds, sanitization of equipment).

        

A **Critical Quality Attribute (CQA)** is a test defined as critical, based on the need to meet a specific compendia specification or other defined non-compendial requirements. For example, conductivity may be a CQA of water when measured at the outlet of a PW generation system.

        

A risk-based approach may also be utilized for determining the sampling approach to qualify changes made to an existing validated water system.

## Risk-Based Approach to PQ Sampling

        

      

    

    

      

        
        

          

### Original Guidance Text

          

The PQ sampling plan should be developed using a risk-based approach. The risk assessment should consider the following factors when determining the scope and frequency of sampling:

          

            
- The type and grade of water required (e.g., Purified Water, Water for Injection)

            
- The intended use of the water and the associated product risk

            
- The design and complexity of the water system (purification and distribution)

            
- The historical performance of similar systems

            
- The number and criticality of Points of Use (POUs)

            
- Existing commissioning data and knowledge

            
- Regulatory expectations applicable to the specific market

          

          

The outcome of the risk assessment should be documented and should provide justification for:

          

            
- The selection of sampling locations

            
- The attributes to be tested at each location

            
- The testing frequency at each location during each phase of the PQ

            
- The duration of each PQ phase

          

          

            "The total period required for the actual PQ should be based on a documented risk evaluation with rationales for the decisions, but for not less than one year."
              
*— ISPE GPG Sampling (2016), referencing FDA 1993 High Purity Water Guide*
          

          

Risk analysis tools (such as FMEA, risk ranking and filtering, or fault tree analysis) may be used to support the determination of sampling scope and frequency. The output of such tools provides documented justification that can be referenced during regulatory inspections.

          

Where commissioning data is available and demonstrates acceptable performance, it may be possible to reduce the duration of the initial PQ phase, provided that a documented risk rationale supports this decision. Conversely, systems with limited commissioning data or complex designs should plan for longer initial phases and higher sampling frequencies.

          

It may be possible to spend more time and sampling effort during commissioning and pre-validation in order to gain improved process knowledge and thereby execute initial and intermediary sampling each at the lower end of the recommended time frame.

## Performance Qualification Sampling — The Four-Phase Framework

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### The Four PQ Phases

          

The PQ sampling program for pharmaceutical water systems is organized into four distinct phases, each with a specific purpose and typical duration:

          

            
              
                
                  
                  
                  
                  
                
| Phase | Name | Typical Duration | Primary Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
              
              
                
                  
                  
                  
                  
                
| 0 | Commissioning | Prior to formal PQ | Confirm installation, baseline data |
                
                  
                  
                  
                  
                
| 1 | Initial Phase | 2–4 weeks (typical) | Intensive sampling, establish normal operation range |
                
                  
                  
                  
                  
                
| 2 | Intermediary Phase | 4–8 weeks (typical) | Confirm system performance, begin frequency reduction |
                
                  
                  
                  
                  
                
| 3 | Extended Phase | Remainder to 12+ months | Long-term performance; basis for routine monitoring |
              
            

          

          

The frequency of testing generally decreases from phase to phase as the system demonstrates consistent performance. However, testing is never completely eliminated — the extended phase establishes the minimum routine monitoring frequency.

          

Sampling during the commissioning phase provides baseline data that can be used to:

          

            
- Confirm that equipment has been installed and is operating as designed

            
- Identify any initial system anomalies before formal qualification begins

            
- Reduce the duration of the initial qualification phase (if data is acceptable)

Different pharmacopoeias have different chemical attribute requirements for PW and WFI. For example:

            

              
- USP PW: Conductivity + TOC suffice; no individual chemical ion limits

- JP PW: Refers to JP-specific monograph

            

            

The applicable pharmacopoeia for the target market determines which chemical tests are mandatory. This note prevents over-testing for markets where simplified compendial tests are accepted.

## Performance Qualification Reporting & Annual Review

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Reporting Requirements

          

**Intermediary Phase Summary Report:** At the conclusion of the PQ intermediary sampling, a summary report should be prepared that includes recommendations for the PQ extended sampling plan (sampling sites, parameters and frequencies).

          

**Final PQ Report:** At the conclusion of the PQ sampling (all portions) the final report, including any recommendations, should be made available to the participants of the PQ as well as to current and future users of the pharmaceutical water system, as this information may provide useful input regarding their daily use of the water and the associated SOPs, instructions, etc.

          

**Annual Periodic System Evaluation (PSE):** At least annually, as part of the PSE, data that has been generated from the sampling of a water system should be reviewed. This review affords the opportunity to:

          

            
- See seasonal trends

            
- Make changes to the sampling frequency (i.e., daily to weekly or weekly to monthly) based on the accumulated data

            
- Adjust Alert Levels and/or Action Levels previously established

          

          

            "The 1993 US FDA Guide to the Inspection of High Purity Water Systems describes the suggested sampling frequencies and durations; however, the total period required for the actual PQ should be based on a documented risk evaluation with rationales for the decisions, but for not less than one year."
          

          

**POU Sampling Expectations:** POU sampling is expected; however, some POUs cannot be sampled for practical reasons or physical limitations. In cases where POU sampling is not possible, a representative sample point may be substituted, but rationales for any substitute sampling sites should be established before the start of the PQ.

          

**Initial POU Valve Sampling:** When POU valves that may be inaccessible for sampling once permanently connected are initially installed, these POU valves may be included in the initial sampling and compared with data from the substitute sampling points for confirmation of suitability.

## Sample Valve Design |

                

            

        

        

            
            

                

                    

2.4 Original Text (ISPE GPG 2016)

                    

### General Design Principles

                    

Good sample valve design should not allow contamination of the sample itself and should take into account both the internal function and external operation of the valve. Design considerations should include the method of connection, the length of the flow path, water retention within the valve body upstream of the seat/seal, and the amount of water held within the exit path after the valve has been closed.

                    

This water downstream of the valve is either trapped or held by capillary properties of water. Valve outlet configuration should allow for drainage while not obstructing the sampling process. Good sample valve outlet fitting design should not be so constructed as to facilitate water holdup in the valve resulting in microbial growth.

                    

Any hardware added after the sample valve often poses a significant risk of contaminating samples by introducing extrinsic contaminants that are not representative of the actual water produced and delivered by the system.

                    

Sample valves should also be suitable for use in the selected system such that they should be appropriate for the pressure, temperature, and flow of the system. Sample valves should also be selected based on the location within the system.

                    

                        "Sample valves designed to sanitary requirements should be employed when feasible and appropriate. Sanitary diaphragm valves are a well-accepted standard within the pharmaceutical industry although other designs have been successfully employed."
                    

                    

Ball valves and other non-sanitary valves should be avoided and only implemented when design constraints (high pressure, the requirement to meter flow rate, or other constraints) necessitate their use. In these cases, additional microbial control consideration may be required.

### Valve Outlet & Connection Configurations

                    

The most common connection for sample valves in the sanitary portion of the system is via sanitary clamp, regardless of whether used for chemical or microbial sampling, although some may be welded or occasionally mounted via other specialty fitting.

                    

Valve configurations vary widely and are available with alternate outlet connections ranging from hose barb, to sanitary clamp, to plain end. Common configurations include:

                    

                        
- 1/2" clamp inlet with 1/2" barb outlet

                        
- 1/2" clamp inlet with 1/4" barb outlet

                        
- 3/4" to 2" clamp inlet with 1/2" or 1/4" barb outlet

                        
- 1/2" clamp inlet with barbed outlet with separate or onboard port for sanitant injection

Cooling the water or condensing the steam reduces safety concerns and simplifies sampling but adds an inherent risk of microbial contamination.

## Sampling Techniques |

                

            

        

        

            
            

                

                    

2.5.1–2.5.2 Representative Samples & Containers

                    

### 2.5.1 Obtaining Representative Samples

                    

The goal of any sampling program is to obtain samples that accurately reflect the contents of the larger system and/or to ensure water of suitable quality is used for manufacturing or other designated purposes. The vast majority of chemical constituents are uniformly distributed and relatively easy to sample with a minimal amount of rinsing and flushing.

                    

Sampling technique, including flushing, that is representative of use should be performed, as bacterial and endotoxin contaminants will not be uniformly distributed because bacteria primarily adhere to surfaces in a system.

                    

                        "The 1993 US FDA Guide to Inspection of High Purity Water Systems states that WFI should essentially be sterile and that the allowance of bacteria counts of 10 cfu per 100 ml are to account for sampling error."
                    

                    

### 2.5.2 Use of Appropriate Sample Containers

                    

When taking samples, efforts should be taken to ensure that sampling containers do not introduce measurable amounts of the contaminant being monitored to the sample. This implies that several different sample containers may be required to sample any given location.

                    

                        
- **TOC:** certified low-TOC containers — many containers will introduce extractables that increase TOC values

                        
- **Bacteria:** pre-sterilized containers

                        
- **Endotoxin:** depyrogenated or pyrogen-free containers (glass preferred, heat-treated ≥250°C for ≥30 min)

                        
- **Conductivity:** clean plastic containers (glass leaches sodium and silicate ions)

                    

                    

Sample containers should allow samples to be collected in an aseptic manner and minimize splashing and aeration upon collection. They should provide an adequate sealing mechanism and provide a means to record pertinent sample information.

2.5.3–2.5.9 Hoses, Training & Full Sampling Technique

                    

### 2.5.3 When Hoses Are Involved

                    

When the sample port is located in a tough to reach spot, hoses may be employed. However, hoses provide an opportunity to introduce contamination. If a hose is not disconnected and allowed to fully drain and dry between uses, bacteria may grow inside the hose that would introduce contamination to the sample. Hoses used for sampling should be installed immediately before samples are taken and then removed immediately afterward or within a qualified period.

                    

### 2.5.4 Training

                    

Personnel taking samples should be trained on proper sampling technique. The vast majority of variations in results can be traced back to variations in how samples are taken. Ideally, one person would be designated to take all samples and their technique would be regularly reviewed. Without rigorous training and continual review, variations in water quality data may reflect variations in sampling technique rather than actual variations in water quality.

                    

### 2.5.7 Sample Point Preparation Before Taking Samples

                    

Samplers should understand that preparation of the sample point for one type of analysis may introduce extrinsic contamination for a subsequent sample. Sample point preparation may involve flushing, spraying, disinfecting, or other treatments. Spraying and disinfecting a sample point with isopropyl alcohol (IPA) prior to taking a sample for bacterial testing could increase the value of a sample taken subsequently for TOC measurement.

                    

**Sampling order:** Samples for bacteria and endotoxins should be taken last in the sequence; samples for chemical constituents, metals, and TOC should be taken first.

                    

### 2.5.8 Sampling Technique — 10-Step Procedure

                    

When sampling for process control or diagnostic purposes:

                    

                        
1. Locate a proper size receptacle or drain to receive the water being flushed from the valve. The receptacle must be large enough.

                        
2. Slowly open the valve until it is fully open (or meets 8 FPS).

                        
3. Allow water to flow at full force for at least 30 seconds, then slowly close the valve to reduce flow to a comfortable rate.

                        
4. Collect samples for any required non-microbial constituents (conductivity, TOC) in appropriate containers.

                        
5. If spraying with IPA for bacteria/endotoxin, slowly close the sample valve.

                        
6. Spray the outlet of the sample valve with disinfectant (IPA).

                        
7. Wait 30 seconds.

                        
8. Repeat steps 2 to 3 and collect bacteria and endotoxin samples in appropriate containers.

                        
9. Close sample valve and post-treat if appropriate to remove or displace retained water in the outlet flow path.

                        
10. Ensure samples are labeled and handled/processed properly.

2.6

            

                

## Handling of Samples |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

2.6 Original Text — Sample Handling

                    

### Offline Analysis Contamination Risks

                    

If sample analysis is being performed by an in-line or on-line instrument or analyzer, then the sample fluid never leaves the closed confines of the water system en route to analysis. Where justifiable, having in-line or on-line instrumentation that can measure a homogeneously distributed attribute is generally preferable to collecting a sample and transporting it to an off-line analyzer in a remote laboratory.

                    

Sampling for off-line analysis in the laboratory can affect the measured quality of a given attribute, exposing it to external contamination by any of several routes:

                    

                        
- Through manual sampling procedures that could be poorly performed due to negligence, lack of training or inadequate procedural detail

                        
- From transfer between the closed confines of the water system and the sample container

                        
- Through contact of the inside of the sample container with the outside surface of a sample port

                        
- During its transport between the sample location and the laboratory

                        
- During storage prior to analysis

                        
- Through sample container opening and re-closure in the laboratory

                        
- During transport and/or restorage for repeat or investigative retest

                    

                    

### Transport & Temperature Control

                    

The transport of the samples to the laboratory for testing should be executed so that sampling container contents spillage or coolant seepage into samples does not occur. This may involve protective bagging of sample containers and/or the application of additional barriers (such as protective tape) to protect the sealing surfaces from leakage or seepage during transport.

                    

Depending on the attributes of concern and its intended testing destination, temperature control may also be a consideration. This may involve the use of insulated containers, as well as the use of coolant materials such as **waterless cold packs**. The use of water and ice should be avoided since sloshing and seepage of this melting coolant into inadequately sealed sampling containers could occur. Similarly, the use of ultra-cold coolants should be avoided since the freezing of the sampled water in the container is usually contraindicated for most types of samples.

                    

The duration and temperature of sample storage is a hotly debated issue, especially for attributes that could quantitatively change during that storage period.

                    

### 2.6.1 Conductivity

                    

Container cleanliness and atmospheric CO₂ are the two greatest risks. Sample containers should be filled as much as practical to minimize air entrapment in the headspace. As a best practice, samples for conductivity testing should **not be refrigerated** during storage while waiting testing, because it increases the time required to run the Stage 2 conductivity test. Sample testing should occur as soon as possible.

                    

### 2.6.2 Total Organic Carbon (TOC)

                    

A significant impact on TOC during sample storage can be from organic residues leaching from the container. For TOC samples only, if certified low TOC containers are used, the samples should be suitable for up to **2 to 3 days** at room temperature or when refrigerated without suffering any substantial increase or decrease in TOC level. If reused sample containers are employed, testing within a few hours after collection is advised.

                    

### 2.6.3 Microbial Count

                    

The concern is that either microbial proliferation or microbial loss due to adsorption or starvation could occur if the elapsed time after sample collection is more than a few hours. Generally, **refrigeration is recommended if testing cannot commence within about 2 hours** of sample collection. Refer to USP <1231> or other relevant compendia for guidance regarding refrigerated hold times.

                    

### 2.6.4 Bacterial Endotoxin

                    

Because at least a portion of the endotoxin in a sample could be of a particulate nature, the same guidelines as for microbial testing should be followed for endotoxin test samples.

### 2.6.6 Avoiding Extrinsic Contamination Through Closure Design

                    

When a sample is collected, the ease and cleanliness of opening and closing the sample container should be considered. This activity occurs multiple times between sampling and eventual analysis in the laboratory. The process of opening and closing the container during sampling has specific precautions:

                    

                        
- Placement of fingers near, or on, the sealing surfaces of the closure should be avoided to prevent a source of extrinsic sample contamination, even when the hands are gloved and alcohol sanitized

                        
- Fingers should be able to grasp and open the closure at a reasonable distance from any sealing surface

                        
- Sealing surfaces of the container should not contact the potentially contaminated external surfaces of the container or closure during opening and closing

                    

                    

### 2.6.7 Chain of Custody

                    

Maintaining a chain of custody provides documented proof of where the samples are and have been and under what conditions, at what times, and handled by whom. This may be a significant consideration when there is an excursion in the value of one or more attributes. The sample's history could have a bearing on how to interpret the test results.

                    

Chain of custody information needs to be proactively collected on every sample in order to have a retrospective interpretation. Chain of custody should also be considered for samples being sent to contract laboratories or sister sites within the same company for testing.

                    

If the test results for samples become included in any legal disputes, chain of custody should be able to verify that the samples are legitimate and have maintained their integrity. The user may not know this in advance.

## Parametric (Real Time) Release |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

2.7 Original Text — Parametric Release

                    

### Concept and Regulatory Basis

                    

Releasing products in real time based on monitoring and control of manufacturing in process parameters critical to finished product quality has been a long-term goal for pharmaceutical water systems. This approach employs Process Analytical Technology (PAT) and Statistical Process Control (SPC) and should be properly validated.

                    

Generally, these parameters should be continuously monitored, so that deviations from acceptable ranges can be detected and process adjustments made to maintain the acceptable finished product quality. Speed and timeliness of these analyses is crucial, which is why real time measurements are needed for real time product release.

                    

The traditional and untimely alternative for release of pharmaceutical waters is to collect water samples and send them to a lab for testing prior to release, all by human mediated procedures.

                    

### 2.7.1 Chemical Attributes — Real-Time Capability

                    

Since 1996, with the changes made with USP 23rd Edition, 5th Supplement, USP has facilitated the use of:

                    

                        
- **On-line monitoring of TOC** (USP <643>)

                        
- **In-line monitoring of Conductivity** (USP <645>)

                    

                    

The only provision is that the data from these on-line, in-line, and at-line instruments should be representative of those attributes as measured at the POU. Chemical attributes are **homogeneously distributed** hence the values measured at remote locations in a simple water distribution system usually are identical to the values measured at the POUs, except for the unavoidable issues associated with sample contamination during collection and off-line testing.

                    

However, if opportunities for non-equivalence exist, due to complex system designs (with sub or parallel loops, heat exchangers, etc.), and potentially TOC contributing hardware, then additional sampling points may be required.

                    

### 2.7.2 Microbiological Attributes — Limitations

                    

Microbiological attributes of pharmaceutical water are **not homogeneously distributed**. Although microbiological quality is not a USP monograph requirement, suggested action levels are furnished in USP <1231> which reflect the original guidance established by the FDA for their inspectors in 1993.

                    

Microbial counts in the water being used from POUs could vary significantly in the distribution system, as planktonic or free floating microorganisms originate from biofilms and biofilms are localized in the piping or hardware between the water distribution system and the POU. This localized phenomenon can cause rapid on-line microbial testing to be an inaccurate representation of the microbial quality of the water that is being used for manufacturing.

                    

Similar concerns exist with bacterial endotoxin, as it is produced by the Gram-negative microorganisms that can inhabit the water distribution system, the water purification system, and may be present in the feed water.

### 2.7.3 Grab Sampling vs. On-Line/At-Line Monitoring

                    

Because of the potential for localized, outlet specific contamination, grab samples for microbial and endotoxin testing have been unequivocally accepted from POUs as reflective of the quality of the water being used at the POU. However, manual sampling and conventional testing approaches can lead to days of delay in data availability and potential for artificially influenced good or bad data.

                    

If rapid on-line or at-line technologies were installed for microbial and endotoxin testing at a single point in the distribution system, the resulting data may be valuable for **process control purposes, but could not be used for product release purposes**. POU testing is required for release.

                    

### 2.7.4 Rapid Microbial Detection Techniques

                    

Rapid techniques can be discriminated as being either destructive (microbial cell is killed during analysis — cannot be speciated) or non-destructive (cells not killed — identification possible by culturing).

                    

Rapid microbial detection techniques can also be discriminated by whether or not there is a need to amplify the number of original CFUs. The quickest techniques which can generate data in perhaps **1 to 2 hours** usually involve destructive techniques. Many of the slower techniques are non-destructive but take longer (**1 to 3 days**) for data availability.

                    

### 2.7.5 Rapid Endotoxin Detection Techniques

                    

The detection of the miniscule levels of endotoxin in WFI can currently only be accomplished by the molecular amplification process involved with the bioassay employing Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL) reagents. Analogs and chemical modifications of these reagents have allowed endotoxin to be quantitated photometrically in as little as **15 minutes**. The LAL technology has been commercialized as a rapid portable version that can be easily employed at-line, making it **a near real time at-line test**, rivaling the test result turnaround time of some on-line TOC instruments.

                    

### 2.7.6 Future of Real Time Release for Pharmaceutical Water

                    

The localized formation of biofilm between the distribution loop outlets and the POUs has consigned the definitive microbial testing of the water used by manufacturing to be from that POU where the water is delivered to a process or piece of equipment.

                    

From an on-line instrument perspective, and with the rapid advances occurring with instrumentation, real time release of the water for microbial attributes may become a more accepted practice in the relatively near future. For WFI systems where endotoxin levels are also an important attribute, the use of the portable at-line endotoxin test kit essentially adds endotoxin to the list of attributes that can be assured as passing in the finished water with an essentially real time test.

                    

A 1 to 3 day delay in result availability is not suitable for a parametric or real time release approach, but currently emerging technologies may make real time release a reality.

#### Pure Steam — Key Characteristics

                    

Pure steam may also be referred to as *clean steam, pyrogen-free steam, WFI steam,* or *USP purified water steam*. Regardless of the term used, when condensed, the steam condensate must meet requirements for **USP/EP WFI**.

                    

Pure steam is predominantly used for sterilization. Steam used for sterilization in autoclaves for international manufacturing should also meet the requirements of **BS EN 285:2015** for non-condensable gases, degrees of superheat, and dryness.

                    

                        
- No additives; limited generated superheat (except when generated pressure is significantly higher than use pressure).

                        
- Condensate must meet USP/EP WFI requirements, have no buffer, and have a relatively low pH compared to plant steam.

#### Feed Water Pretreatment Requirements

                    

Feed water requires proper pretreatment, as all generators are susceptible to scaling and corrosion. Pretreatment includes:

                    

                        
- Minimizing or preventing scale formation to minimize or prevent corrosion.

                        
- Removal of objectionable volatiles, such as ammonia, that are not removed by distillation/steam generating processes and would carry over into the clean steam.

                        
- Utilization of the facility's high-purity water system (e.g., PW, WFI). Note: steam generators may not require feed water to meet PW or WFI requirements. This practice, however, ignores the ability of the pure steam generator to remove impurities.

Materials and Design Practices

                            

Pure steam distribution systems have additional requirements for contact materials due to the aggressive nature of pure steam. Corrosion-resistant 316L stainless steel tubing or solid drawn pipe is typically utilized in new systems. Legacy systems may be constructed of 304/304L stainless steel.

                            

#### Surface Finish Guidelines

                            

Surface finishes for pure steam distribution systems are *not as strict* as for pure water distribution systems because of the high operating temperatures and self-sanitizing nature of pure steam. Considerations include:

                            

                                
- Mill finish or mechanically polished pipe may be used; typical mechanical polishes of **35 to 20 Ra** are utilized for clean steam generators and distribution systems.

                                
- Internal surfaces, including orbital and mechanical welds, may be passivated to improve corrosion resistance.

                                
- Electropolishing may be utilized but may not be necessary because of the high operating temperatures and an increase in the formation of rouge.

                            

                            

#### General Design Practices

                            

                                
- Allowance for thermal expansion and the ability for condensate to drain effectively.

                                
- Sloping of piping between **1/8 inch/foot (10 mm/m) to 1/4 inch/foot (20 mm/m)** as outlined in ASME BPE-2014, but good engineering practices and risk analysis tools may be used to determine the appropriate slope.

                                
- Sloping of piping in the direction of steam flow and to a low point condensate trap for adequate condensate removal.

                            

                            

*Note: Sloping of distribution piping in the direction **opposite** of flow is not recommended because condensate may be easily reintroduced into the pure steam.*

                            

                                Reference: ISPE Baseline Guide: Water and Steam Systems (Second Edition), Chapter 10; and ASME BPE-2014 (Figures SD-4.2.2-1 and SD-4.2.2-2).

3.3.1.1 Non-condensable Gases (NCG)

                            

If non-condensable gas removal is required, these gases may be removed in the pretreatment to a pure steam generation system either by applying heat or using a degassifier because these gases pass directly through a pure steam generator and into the pure steam.

                            

Including a degassifier at the beginning of a pure steam distribution system may be considered. The degassifier can be as simple as a tee with the vent leg pointing upward and terminating with a balanced pressure air vent that opens to expel NCGs from the pure steam.

                            

Non-condensable gases should be sampled with a sample valve suitable for its intended purposes. Sampling for chemical and endotoxin purposes would use a **different** sample valve.

                            

Proper sampling for non-condensable gases is described in BS EN 285:2015, Chapter 22.1, and uses the equation:

                            

                                

#### NCG Concentration Formula

                                

**Cn = (Vb / Vc) × 100%**

                                

                                    
- Cn = concentration of non-condensable gases (%)

                                    
- Vb = volume of water displaced from the burette (ml)

                                    
- Vc = volume of water collected in the graduated cylinder (ml)

                                

                                

*Limit: Cn ≤ 3.5% per BS EN 285:2015*

                            

                            

---

                            

3.3.1.2 Dryness

                            

Dryness refers to the level of steam saturation and is the **ratio of vapor mass to the mass of the steam mixture**. Dryness is dimensionless; a value of 1.0 represents ideal dry saturated steam.

                            

Dry saturated steam may have excess heat introduced, which creates superheated steam. The amount of superheating should be minimized for efficient steam sterilization, since superheated steam is not saturated and will create uneven temperature distribution at the surface being sterilized.

                            

Dryness may be adversely impacted by system design issues:

                            

                                
- Incorrectly or inadequately sloped lines

                                
- Inadequate condensate removal (insufficient or incorrectly located traps)

                                
- Insufficient pipe insulation causing excess condensation

                                
- Incorrect point-of-use (POU) design

                            

                            

**Specification:** Dryness should be greater than 0.95 for metal loads and 0.90 for other loads per BS EN 285:2015, Chapter 13.3.3.

                            

For dryness sampling, the sample should be taken from the **center of the steam distribution pipe**.

                            

*Note: this sample does not take into account the moisture present as a film on the pipe wall or the condensate on the bottom of the pipe. It is, therefore, not an accurate value, but represents only an approximation. However, this sampling technique has become the most commonly used method.*

                            

---

                            

3.3.1.3 Superheating

                            

Superheating must be below 25°C when the pure steam is expanded to atmospheric pressure per BS EN 285:2015, Chapter 13.3.4. Samples should be taken from the center of the steam pipe.

                            

**Practical simplification:** If the pressure is below 2 barG (~30 psi) and the dryness value is below 1.0, the steam cannot get superheated and will always pass the superheat requirement for pure steam.

                            

For higher pressures (>2 barG), the formula is:

                            

                                

#### Superheat Check Formula (P > 2 barG)

                                

**D × H + (1 – D) × h < 2724.53 kJ/kg**

                                

                                    
- D = measured dryness

                                    
- H = enthalpy of steam at measured steam pressure at POU (kJ/kg)

                                    
- h = enthalpy of water at measured steam pressure at POU (kJ/kg)

                                    
- Limit: 2724.53 kJ/kg (= atmospheric enthalpy 2673.8 + 2.029 × 25°K)

                                

                            

                            

---

                            

3.3.1.4 Conductivity, TOC, and Endotoxins

                            

Condensate collected from pure steam sample points should comply with the specifications for **WFI grade water**. Such sample points should be placed at:

                            

                                
- The outlet of the pure steam generator

                                
- POUs or as close to POUs as possible

                            

                            

There may be one sampling cooler at each sampling point, or coolers can be transported from sample point to sample point, depending on the design of the system.

                            

A simple method for sample collection is to have a valve with a sanitary connection at each drainage point, allowing connection of a steam trap and condensate cooler without closing the system down. Design considerations for sample valves are identical to those for water systems (Chapter 2 of this Guide).

## Sampling Plans (Frequency and Duration) /

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Overview — Sampling Plan Framework

                    

                        "Pure Steam is prepared from suitably pretreated source water analogously to either the pretreatment used for Purified Water or Water for Injection." — USP
                    

                    

Pure steam that is used in aseptic applications, as well as applications where the steam or condensate directly contacts products, excipients, container closures, or equipment/components, should be appropriately sampled.

                    

Recommended sampling frequencies should be determined based on the **state of the system**:

                    

                        
- Restoration from scheduled/unscheduled shutdown

                        
- Maintenance

                        
- Validation

                        
- Normal routine operation

                    

                    

Physical, chemical, and biological elements (i.e., endotoxin) should be monitored. There may be different sampling frequencies employed within these categories.

                    

Risk analysis tools may be used to develop a sampling rationale that takes into account:

                    

                        
- The criticality of the POUs on the system

                        
- The frequency of use of each POU

                        
- The accessibility for sampling

                        
- Historical performance

Definitions

                            

                                "Commissioning: a well-planned, documented, and managed engineering approach to the start-up and turnover of facilities, systems, and equipment to the end-user that results in a safe functional environment that meets established design requirements and stakeholder expectations." — ISPE GPG: Approaches to C&Q of Pharmaceutical Water and Steam Systems
                            

                            

**Qualification** is intended to provide documented evidence with oversight by quality assurance personnel that a pure steam system will consistently produce the appropriate quality of steam to end users in accordance with regulatory expectations.

                        

                        

                            

                            

                                

#### Commissioning vs. Qualification

                                

                                

                                

                            

                        

                    

                

            

            
            

                

                

                

                    

                        

                            

Commissioning Sampling

                            

Sampling and monitoring during commissioning is considered **GEP**. Commissioning is where the intended operating parameters of the equipment are identified and tested.

                            

                                
- Chemical testing with condensate meeting applicable WFI monograph requirements (EP, USP <1231>, JP16).

                                
- Duration varies depending on complexity and size of the system.

                                
- Once the entire system is operating, each unit operation should be tested **at least once** during commissioning.

                                
- If any changes are made to defined CPPs during commissioning, the commissioning phase should be repeated.

                            

                            

---

                            

3.4.2.1 Qualification — Three-Phase PQ

                            

Qualification typically consists of IQ, OQ, and PQ. PQ is organized into three phases:

                            

                                
1. Initial sampling

                                
2. Intermediary sampling

                                
3. Extended sampling

                            

                            

#### Phase 1: Initial Sampling

                            

                                
- Demonstrates the system consistently operates within predetermined ranges and delivers required steam quality.

                                
- Sampling points: immediately after the PSG, at POUs, and at worst-case locations (most distant from PSG or lowest points in the system).

                                
- Physical attribute testing (NCG, superheat, dryness) should be performed while equipment (e.g., autoclaves) is operating.

                                
- Duration: **minimum 3 consecutive working days**, up to 10 working days.

                                
- Pure steam should **NOT** be used for production purposes during initial sampling.

                                
- Formal provisional release requires QM review and summary report.

                                
- For larger systems with multiple branches: sample ends of each individual pipe branch.

                            

                            

#### Phase 2: Intermediary Sampling

                            

                                
- Same duration as initial phase; occurs immediately after initial phase completion.

                                
- Sampling frequency nearly identical to initial phase, except PSG outlet need not be sampled daily (reduced frequency — e.g., start and end of intermediary phase).

                                
- QM assessment required before uninterrupted progression to extended phase.

                                
- Pure steam produced during intermediary phase **may be provisionally released** for manufacturing, provided initial phase was assessed as successful by QM.

                            

                            

#### Summary Interim Reports

                            

Required after both initial and intermediary phases. Reports:

                            

                                
- Post-initial: justifies provisional system release for manufacturing.

                                
- Post-intermediary: justifies full release for manufacturing purposes.

                            

                            

#### Phase 3: Extended Sampling

                            

                                
- Demonstrates the system over a longer period (typically balance of the year).

                                
- PQ is considered **complete when performance data for a full year** (inclusive of all phases) are obtained.

                                
- PSG outlet: sampled **weekly**.

                                
- Other locations: rotating basis so that each location is sampled every **two weeks**.

                            

                            

A final report documenting the overall qualification process (all phases) with a clear conclusion statement must be reviewed and authorized by all relevant functions including QM.

Routine Monitoring Considerations

                            

Following successful qualification, routine ongoing monitoring should involve sampling and testing at frequencies supported by a documented risk analysis process. Considerations include:

                            

                                
- The use of the system, including processes where steam is used (e.g., autoclaves)

                                
- Design and implementation of POUs (frequency of port use, cool points, dead legs, line slopes, types of valves, location of port)

                                
- Material of construction of piping

                                
- Sampling technique and sample container utilized

                                
- Design of feed water and steam distribution systems (age, system use, feed water quality, steam trap placement)

                                
- Routine maintenance (rouge inspections, passivation)

                                
- Qualification results

                            

                            

Physical clean steam quality attributes (dryness, NCG, superheat) should be monitored at established regular intervals but **may have different frequencies** from chemical and biological aspects.

                            

Quality attributes (TOC, conductivity, bacterial endotoxins) should be measured in condensate and tested according to WFI specifications. Items "Appearance" and "Nitrates" should be included for EP compliance. Testing for total aerobic count is **not necessary** due to the lethal properties of steam.

## Chapter 3 Part 1 — Summary & Key Takeaways /

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Key Concepts Covered

                    

                        
- **3.1** Pure steam (PSG) is mandatory for direct product contact sterilization, humidification in critical areas, and SIP of high-purity water systems. Condensate must meet USP/EP WFI specifications.

                        
- **3.2** Feed water pretreatment is critical — especially deaeration to control NCG. Distribution systems use 316L SS with minimum 10 mm/m slope toward condensate traps.

                        
- **3.2.1** Two regulatory layers: USP <1231> / EP for condensate chemical quality; BS EN 285:2015 for physical steam quality (NCG ≤3.5%, dryness ≥0.95/0.90, superheat ≤25°C).

                        
- **3.3** Different CQAs require different sampling equipment and locations. NCG and dryness share the same pipe-center sample point; chemical/biological attributes use condensate coolers at POUs.

                        
- **3.3.1.1** NCG is the primary cause of steam penetration failure. Removed by heat deaeration before PSG or inline balanced-pressure air vents at high points.

                        
- **3.3.1.2** Dryness measured by Pitot tube from pipe center — an approximation but the industry-accepted method. System design defects (slope, traps, insulation) are the main causes of low dryness.

                        
- **3.3.1.3** Superheat ≤25°C is automatically met when operating pressure <2 barG; enthalpy calculation required for higher pressures.

                        
- **3.4** Three-phase PQ: Initial (≥3 days, no production) → Intermediary (same duration, provisional release) → Extended (balance of year, weekly PSG / biweekly POU). Full year of data = completed PQ.

                        
- **3.4.3** Routine monitoring: weekly at PSG; monthly at distant points; biannual at all POUs; annual physical quality at autoclave POUs. Trending with Alert/Action levels required.

                        
- **3.4.4** After extended shutdown: mandatory chemical and endotoxin testing at all points before resumption of production.

Original Text (English)

          

### 3.5 Sample Valve Design

          

This is covered in Section 2.4 of this Guide.

          

For pure steam sampling applications, sample valves must be compatible with the high temperature and pressure conditions of pure steam systems. Key considerations include:

          

            
- Valve body and seat materials must withstand saturated steam at 3–4 bar (approximately 135–145°C) without leaching contaminants into the condensate sample.

            
- Diaphragm valves are the preferred design for pure steam sample points due to their sanitary construction and ability to be cleaned and sterilized in place.

            
- All wetted surfaces should be 316L stainless steel with electropolished finish, free of dead legs and crevices.

            
- The sample valve should be located downstream of the condensate trap where applicable, to avoid sampling mixed condensate and vapor.

            
- Valve materials (including seals and gaskets) must not contribute TOC, endotoxin, metals, or particulate to the condensate sample.

            
- Where sample valves are located at manufacturing-grade POU connections, the design standard should match that of the distribution system.

          

          

            "Sample valves should be of a sanitary design, although laboratory points may not need to be as hygienically designed as those supplying pure steam to manufacturing."
              
*— ISPE Sampling Guide, Section 3.6.4.1*

## Pure Steam Sampling Techniques |

        

Condensate collection, equipment requirements, sample containers, temperature

      

    

    

      
      

      

        

          

3.6.1 Obtaining Representative Samples

          

The goal of the sampling program is to obtain samples that accurately reflect the contents of the steam system. When sampling a liquid, contaminant distribution should be accounted for as part of the sample evaluation.

          

Pure steam cannot be evaluated in its vapor state; therefore, pure steam needs to be condensed in order to be evaluated. Based on compendial requirements (USP, EP, JP, etc.), condensed pure steam should be evaluated using the same criteria used to evaluate WFI. The techniques used are similar to those for water; however, there are two issues that can affect pure steam condensate sampling:

          

            
1. The equipment used to condense the pure steam and collect samples

            
2. The final temperature of the condensate

          

          

#### 3.6.1.1 Equipment Considerations

          

When condensing pure steam for sampling, the equipment should not contribute contaminants to the sample. The equipment should be designed to allow for complete cleaning and drainage. It should also be possible to sterilize the equipment and for it to be depyrogenated to ensure that no endotoxins are added to the sample.

          

In addition, the equipment typically requires a cooling source (such as chilled water) to facilitate condensation. The amount of chilled water required may be significant, necessitating larger than expected equipment sizes and/or cooling media flow rates, because of the significant amount of latent heat that needs to be removed to condense the pure steam.

          

Sampling heat exchangers can be sterilized in place, using the pure steam from the system. This can help to simplify the sampling process, but may result in a larger cooling load.

          

Heat exchangers used for pure steam condensation may need to meet the requirements of heat exchangers used for WFI sampling in order to mitigate the risk of undetectable cooling water contamination of the condensate sample. Requirements may include double tube sheet design and routine maintenance.

          

The size of this equipment will depend on the:

          

            
1. Pure steam pressure (and resulting temperature)

            
2. Sample temperature desired ("delta T")

            
3. Temperature and flow of cooling media available

          

          

#### 3.6.1.2 Final Temperature of the Condensate

          

The condensate temperature for steam sampling should be **< 50°C (122°F)** and preferably near ambient temperature. It is recommended that sample conditioning condensers, coolers, and associated fittings and valves be constructed of 316 stainless steel and passivated.

          

Any special sampling equipment required that can make steam contact during collection (e.g., POU gaskets, sanitary fittings, hose barbs, steam condensing equipment) should be pyrogen free or must have been depyrogenated.

          

            "Note: bioburden is not required of steam sampling per the USP/NF. However, establishing that the pure steam line has no thermophilic bioburden should be an evaluation done prior to or during the PQ."

### 3.6.1 (cont.) Sample Containers & Types |

      

        

          

Sample Container Requirements

          

For most high purity and pure steam samples, three to four groups of samples are taken:

          

#### 1. TOC

          

TOC samples should be taken in TOC collection vials (glass). Plastic containers can introduce TOC to the sample results. The TOC vials typically hold a 40 ml sample and should be filled completely before the closure is applied.

          

#### 2. Conductivity and pH

          

Typically, glass (borosilicate), sterile polypropylene plastic, or inert fluoropolymer containers are the most common container material. Glass containers may introduce trace residues that will change both the conductivity and pH. Plastics tend to avoid the ionic leachables, but most are somewhat permeable to atmospheric CO₂, which will alter the conductivity of the sample. Containers should be able to hold at least 300 ml of sample to allow for Conductivity Stage 1, Stage 2, and Stage 3 testing.

          

#### 3. Bioburden

          

Bioburden samples should be taken in sterile containers, typically an inert plastic such as polypropylene or a suitable fluoropolymer.

          

#### 4. Endotoxin

          

Endotoxin sampling should be done using pyrogen free containers of either glass or inert plastic. If considering plastic, the type of plastic container is important to minimize the potential for endotoxin adsorption issues that could result in false negative results. Collecting approximately 10 to 20 ml in sterile pyrogen free containers.

          

            "Efforts should be made to ensure that sampling containers do not introduce measurable contaminants. This may necessitate using several types of sample containers to sample a given POU."

Original Text (English)

          

### 3.6.2 Training Sample Collectors

          

Operators should be properly trained and should have demonstrated their ability to collect samples following the approved sample collection SOP. There should be a **particular emphasis on safety training** when sampling pure steam, its condensate, or other hot liquid. Pressurized pure steam, often at pressures of 30 to 45 psig (135°C to 145°C) and its condensed water (at temperatures near 82°C) can cause severe burns and potentially death.

          

During their training samplers should have observed a trained sampler who demonstrates the collection procedure for each type of collection, as well as the proper handling of each type of collected sample prior to hand off to the testing function. When collecting samples during training, there should be a duplicate set of samples taken by the trainer.

          

Based on industry accepted best practice, trainees may be required to demonstrate that they can collect a minimum of three distinct sets of samples, one set per day for each type of collection and each unique location.

          

### 3.6.3 Preparation for Sample Collection

          

Materials should be prepared before beginning the actual sampling process. Examples of typical items that may be staged for use include:

          

            
- Rolling railed cart, stainless or cleanable plastic

            
- Plastic/wire baskets and TOC racks

            
- Sample containers (all types, labeled)

            
- Labels, waterproof markers and indelible ink pens

            
- Laminated sampling plan

            
- Heat resistant gloves

          

          

#### 3.6.3.1 Sample Point Preparation

          

Sample point preparation for sampling pure steam may or may not include flushing, spraying, disinfecting, or other treatments, based on the temperature, and assuming proper design. If particulate contamination from the local environment may be a problem, physical cleaning of the sample outlet may be indicated.

          

#### 3.6.3.4 Duplicate Samples

          

An important but often times overlooked part of any quality control program is to perform a predetermined percentage of all analytical tests in duplicate. Duplicate samples should be taken from the same sample location, but in two separate sample containers. Both containers should be labeled with the same sample identification, but can be distinguished by adding "-1" and "-2" after the sample number.

          

#### 3.6.3.5 Blanks

          

A blank containing no sample should be submitted to the laboratory for each type of container used during sampling. The blank collection container should be from the same lot as the collection container used for that date.

## 3.6.4 Sampling Procedures — General & Labeling |

        

GMP gowning, flush requirements, sample labeling, TOC/Conductivity/Bioburden/Endotoxin collection steps

      

    

    

      

        

          

3.6.4.1 General Considerations

          

For routine operation of critical systems that have completed PQs, there should be detailed sampling procedures approved for use. It is a good practice to keep pure steam sampling procedures separated from those used to collect pure water samples.

          

When systems are new and undergoing PQ or have been modified and as part of a requalification PQ with a new sampling plan, it is acceptable to use the draft procedures.

          

            **Safety:** Care should be taken to protect the sampler from scalding. Proper heat resistant gloves should be present in the collection kit and worn to protect the sampler from accidental burning.
          

          

If the POU is located in a dirty area, clean the sample port with **3% hydrogen peroxide** or **70% sterile IPA** prior to flushing the sample point. The cleaning procedure must be qualified prior to routine use and must account for the fact that the use of sterile IPA will impact TOC results.

          

Samplers should wear powder free gloves before taking samples or handling sample containers.

          

Samplers should open the sample valve and flush the POU by running steam/condensed water to waste for the qualified flush time interval.

          

#### 3.6.4.2 Sample Labeling

          

Samplers should label all sample containers with:

          

            
1. Sample identification — POU (include system ID and building)

            
2. Type of sample (e.g., condensed steam) and indicate if a duplicate sample

            
3. Date and time of sampling

            
4. Analysis required

            
5. Sampler's name

          

          

#### 3.6.4.3 Sampling Procedure for TOC, Conductivity/pH, Bacteria, and Endotoxin

          

            
1. Flush the POU for the qualified flush time interval and at the qualified flush flow rate.

            
2. Wear clean powder free gloves.

            
3. Remove and set aside the sample container closure, then manually open the valve on the condenser.

            
4. To prevent contamination, do not let anything except the sample touch the inside of the container or closure.

            
5. Fill each container completely, except for conductivity/pH samples where approximately 1 inch of air space should be left at the top to allow for adequate agitation prior to analysis.

            
6. Securely close the sample containers to prevent leakage or contamination.

          

          

*If there is any question whether a sample has become contaminated, it should be discarded and a new sample taken.*

3.6.4.4 Non-Condensable Gases (NCG) — Henriksen Method

          

It is recommended that a sequence of tests should be performed to determine the variability in the level of non-condensable gases. The procedure is as follows:

          

            
1. Fill the 2 L container and burette with cold, deaerated water. Invert the burette with great care to ensure no air is trapped in the burette.

            
2. Remove the steam sampling pipe from the 2 L container and open the needle valve to purge all air from the pipe. Return sampling pipe to container, allowing it to bubble into the funnel. Add deaerated water to the 2 L container until water flows out through the overflow pipe.

            
3. Place the graduated cylinder to collect overflow. Adjust the needle valve to allow a continuous flow of steam into the funnel, ensuring that the steam enters the funnel in a way that any non-condensable gases will be collected in the burette.

            
4. Close the sampling valve.

            
5. Start the sterilization cycle and when steam is flowing to the sterilizer, open the sampling valve, allowing a continuous sample of steam into the funnel at a rate sufficient to cause a small amount of steam hammer.

            
6. Allow the steam sample to condense in the funnel. Any non-condensable gases will collect in the top of the burette. Collect the overflow in the graduated cylinder and observe the temperature of the water in the 2 L container. When the temperature in the container is between 70°C to 75°C, close the needle valve.

            
7. Record the volume of water displaced in the burette and the volume of water collected in the graduated cylinder.

            
8. Calculate using the equation below.

          

          

            

#### NCG Calculation Formula

            

```

Cn (%) = (Vb / Vc) × 100

Where:
  Cn = concentration of NCG (%)
  Vb = volume of non-water displaced from burette (mL)
  Vc = volume of water collected in graduated cylinder (mL)

Acceptance criterion: Cn ≤ 3.5% (V/V)
            
```

### 3.6.4.5 Dryness Fraction |

      

        

          

Dryness — Dewar Flask Method

          

Steam should be tested for, and should pass the non-condensable gases test, prior to being tested for dryness. This test assumes the use of a large steam sterilizer operating in the textile cycle.

          

            
1. Assemble the apparatus as shown in BS EN 285:2015 Figure 3.7.

            
2. Weigh the assembled Dewar flask and record the mass as **Me**.

            
3. After several sterilization cycles, select the textile cycle with a temperature of 134°C.

            
4. Remove the stopper assembly and place 650 ml of water into the Dewar flask, with the water at a temperature at or below 27°C. Weigh the new assembly and record the mass as **Ms**.

            
5. Place a standard test pack (details in BS EN 285:2015) in the sterilizer.

            
6. Introduce a temperature sensor into the Dewar flask and note the temperature of the fluid in the flask, record this value as **T1**.

            
7. When the sterilizer steam valve first opens, attach the rubber tube to the pitot tube ensuring free drainage of condensate into the Dewar flask. Record the steam temperature as **T3**.

            
8. When the water in the Dewar flask reaches approximately 80°C, disconnect the rubber tube, agitate the flask to thoroughly mix the contents and record the temperature as **T2**.

            
9. Weigh the Dewar flask again, record this mass as **Mf**.

          

          

            

#### Dryness Fraction Formula

            

```

D = [(T2 – T1) × (Cpw × (Ms – Me) + A)] / [L × (Mf – Ms)]
    – [(T3 – T2) × Cpw] / L

Where:
  D    = dryness fraction
  T1   = initial water temperature in flask (°C)
  T2   = final mixed temperature in flask (°C)
  T3   = steam temperature (°C)
  Ms   = mass of flask + 650 mL water (g)
  Me   = mass of empty flask assembly (g)
  Mf   = mass of flask after test (g)
  L    = latent heat of steam at T3 (kJ/kg)
  Cpw  = specific heat of water = 4.18 kJ/kg/°K
  A    = effective heat capacity of apparatus = 0.24 kJ/°K

Acceptance:
  D ≥ 0.95 for metal loads
  D ≥ 0.90 for other load types
            
```

### 3.6.4.6 Superheat |

      

        

          

Superheat Measurement

          

Superheat refers to the condition where steam temperature exceeds the saturation temperature corresponding to the steam pressure. Superheated steam behaves more like a gas than a condensing vapor.

          

The procedure per BS EN 285:2015 involves:

          

            
1. Assemble the apparatus as indicated in Figures 3.8 and 3.9 of BS EN 285.

            
2. Run a sterilization cycle with an empty chamber.

            
3. At the completion of this cycle, immediately place a full textile load and run another sterilization cycle. At the completion of the sterilization cycle, check the temperature recordings and confirm that the temperature did not differ by more than 3°C from that measured in the steam pipe during the dryness test.

          

          

            "Superheated steam does not condense as readily as saturated steam, reducing the rate of heat transfer to the load. This impairs the sterilization process."
          

          

Common causes of superheat:

          

            
- Pressure drops through steam traps or pressure reducing valves (PRVs)

            
- Excessive pipeline heat loss in long, uninsulated steam mains

            
- Throttling of steam through partially open valves

          

          

Acceptance criterion: Steam temperature should not exceed saturation temperature by more than 25°C, and temperature difference between steam pipe and chamber should not exceed 3°C compared to the dryness test measurement.

## Sample Handling |

        

Condensate handling, time to analysis, temperature effects, endotoxin considerations

      

    

    

      

        

          

3.7 Sample Handling

          

Clear instructions should be provided to personnel collecting and handling pure steam condensate samples. A sample holding time limit should be established prior to the PQ to confirm the maximum time between collection and testing.

          

Samples should be transferred to the laboratory and placed on test within **an hour of collection**, to help increase the accuracy of the test results. If multiple samples are being collected and the collection process takes longer than an hour, the collector should divide up the sampling process, deliver samples to the testing laboratory, and then return to sampling.

          

**Refrigerating samples will help to inhibit microbial growth, but gas absorption may create inaccurate test results.**

          

            "A sample holding time limit should be established prior to the PQ to confirm the maximum time between collection and testing."
          

          

Key handling requirements for pure steam condensate samples:

          

            
- Maintain sample integrity from point of collection to laboratory

            
- Minimize exposure to atmosphere (CO₂ absorption affects conductivity)

            
- Do not allow condensate to cool below ambient where phase separation may occur

            
- Endotoxin samples: keep at room temperature, test within validated hold time

            
- Bioburden samples: refrigerate at 2–8°C if not tested within 1 hour, but consider CO₂ absorption impact on other tests

            
- Chemical (conductivity, TOC, pH) samples: test as soon as possible, within qualified hold time

## Other Factors Influencing Sampling Strategies |

        

Geographic variation, source water types (WFI / PW / potable), PSG design, mist elimination

      

    

    

      

        

          

3.8 Other Factors

          

Sampling strategies may be influenced by other factors such as:

          

            
- Geographic location of the plant

            
- Incoming water quality

            
- Pretreatment equipment

            
- PSG (Pure Steam Generator) design

          

          

The geographic location of the plant may lead to variation in incoming water quality as well as variations in the type of chemical treatment in use (e.g., chlorine, chloramine, ozone, etc.). In some instances, ammonia may be present as a byproduct of the chloramine process. Ammonia and carbon dioxide are particularly troublesome since they will pass through a pure steam generator and will influence the non-condensable gas content as well as the conductivity.

          

#### 3.8.1 Source Water

          

#### 3.8.1.1 Feed Water from WFI Systems

          

When the feed water to a PSG already has very low or undetectable levels of endotoxin, and is otherwise chemically pure, no additional PSG feed water pretreatment system monitoring is required. When properly operated, the chemical attributes of the PSG feed water are essentially the same as those of the steam it generates.

          

#### 3.8.1.2 Feed from Purified Water Systems

          

With PW, there is no requirement that it is free from bacterial endotoxins; therefore, without specific microbial and endotoxin control precautions, excessive levels of endotoxin may be present.

          

Since PSGs have a finite impurity removal capability (including endotoxin), the endotoxin levels in the PW feeding the PSG should be monitored to ensure that the removal capability is not overwhelmed. Assuming a minimum 3-log endotoxin removal capability and proper equipment operation, the endotoxin level in the feed water would have to be **100 EU/mL or greater** to be problematic.

          

#### 3.8.1.3 Potable Water

          

Although there is no compendial requirement that this water first meets PW or WFI specifications, it should be suitable feed water for the steam generator in terms of its chemical content. The source of the potable water supply could have a dramatic impact on its starting purity, and therefore, on the required pretreatment.

          

#### 3.8.2 Steam Generator Mist Elimination Capability

          

The USP monograph for pure steam specifically states that the pure steam generation process uses a process that "prevents source water entrainment." Well designed and well operated systems using WFI as steam generator feed water essentially have no need for mist elimination since the feed water quality is already suitable for steam.

          

A standalone PSG feed water system that is either not well designed or not properly maintained carries a **high risk of contaminant carry over** into the PS. These systems can be significantly more susceptible to the variable quality of the incoming potable water and could also experience endotoxin and TOC generated in situ by the pretreatment system.

          

The purification system generating the water feeding a PSG typically needs the same monitoring and maintenance attention as pretreatment systems feeding distillation units that generate WFI. It is commonly accepted that the phase change and mist elimination processes of distillation will purify that feed water by at least three orders of magnitude (≥ 1000-fold or ≥ 99.9% impurity reduction).

#### Phase 1: Installation Qualification (IQ)

          

            
- Verify material certifications: valve bodies (316L SS + EP), gaskets (PTFE/EPDM, USP Class VI), heat exchanger (double tube sheet)

- NCG test: ≤ 3.5% — perform at each PSG + major autoclave fed by the system

- Establish flush time for each sampling POU

- Sample all POU per approved sampling plan (minimum 3 consecutive days × 3 weeks per ISPE guidance)

- Confirm all results meet WFI specifications

          

          

#### Phase 4: Ongoing Monitoring

          

            
- Routine: conductivity and TOC at each POU (frequency per risk assessment)

            
- Periodic: endotoxin, pH, NCG (quarterly or annually)

            
- Trigger-based: full requalification after major maintenance, PSG replacement, or feed water source change

## Introduction |

        

Types of gases, regulatory context, and critical quality attributes

      

    

    

      
      

        

          

Original Text (ISPE GPG 2016)

          

This chapter contains information and best practices gathered from several major pharmaceutical organizations listing their current practices for assuring the quality of gases used in their operations. Testing may be performed by the pharmaceutical manufacturer or by a certified independent laboratory.

          

The quality of product contact compressed gases should be assured through adequate design, appropriate controls (e.g., change control or change management procedures), and routine maintenance of the system. If adequate controls are in place, critical compressed air and other process gas systems typically require analytical testing on a periodic basis to confirm that the system is operating under a state of control.

          

Routine release testing (e.g., identification testing) of compressed air or process gases furnished by a qualified supplier may be required for release of incoming raw materials. If it is determined that analytical testing of a distribution piping system is necessary, the specification limits for the testing should be suitable for the intended use of the gas.

### 4.1.1 Types of Process Gases Covered

### 4.1.2 Regulatory Requirements and Consensus Standards

          

No well-defined regulatory requirements exist regarding the frequency, extent, or method of routine sampling for compressed air or other process gases. None of the pharmacopeia monographs specifies limits for particles or total viable counts (bacteria), but some consensus standards and US FDA guidance documents provide additional information and should be consulted as part of the risk analysis process.

          

Some monographs stipulate that the moisture content of compressed air and other process gases should be below -46°C (-50°F) (equivalent to 67 ppm for compressed air).

          

            **US FDA (2004) — Aseptic Processing Guidance:**  

            "A compressed gas should be of appropriate purity (e.g., free from oil) and its microbiological and particle quality after filtration should be equal to or better than that of the air in the environment into which the gas is introduced. Compressed gases such as air, nitrogen, and carbon dioxide are often used in cleanrooms and are frequently employed in purging or overlaying."

### 4.1.3 Critical Quality Attributes of Compressed Air and Process Gases

          

A sensible and logical method to determine the requirements for compressed air or process gases is to review the role played by the gas in the actual process in which it is used. Examples of applications include:

          

            
- Displacement or conveyance of product from one location to another

            
- As a feed source to fermentation processes

            
- Actuating pneumatic valves in classified and controlled areas, with resulting exhaust gases venting into processing spaces

            
- Occupying head space in lyophilization vials, solution vials, ampoules, etc.

            
- Occupying head space or acting as a blanket in a bioreactor

            
- Drying of product in fluidized beds

            
- Drying of product contact equipment surfaces

            
- As a carrier in spray tablet coatings

            
- As a carrier to suspend an API during micronizing or milling steps

            
- As a blanket to provide an inert atmosphere within API vessels

            
- As a blanket or to pressurize WFI storage tanks

          

          

The CQAs most generally defined are:

          

            
- Moisture content (dew point)

            
- Total hydrocarbons (oil aerosol + oil vapor)

            
- Particles (nonviable)

            
- Viable particles (bacteria)

## Sampling Locations |

        

Where to sample in compressed air and process gas systems

      

    

    

      

        

          

Original Text (ISPE GPG 2016)

          

Sample points may be installed for varied reasons in a compressed air or process gas system, including:

          

            
- Quality control purposes

            
- Process control or diagnostic purposes

          

          

Sample points intended for quality control purposes should be determined based on the CQA being monitored and on a suitably developed sampling plan.

          

Sample points intended for process control or diagnostic purposes should:

          

            
- Be used as needed to ensure that processes are under control

            
- Be regularly maintained

            
- Not introduce a risk of contaminating the system

            
- Not be included in the sampling plan

          

          

The determination of sample point location should be initiated during system design. Risk analysis tools may be utilized to define the sampling strategies. Expectations for sampling points include:

          

            
- Sampling points should be easily accessible

            
- Avoid locations common to other clean utilities (e.g., high purity water or pure steam) to prevent cross contamination

            
- Clearly and uniquely identified on P&ID drawings and tagged in the field

            
- Test results should be trended and reviewed annually, with sampling frequencies adjusted accordingly

          

          

**Key principle:** Typically, a sampling location should reflect the quality of a gas at the POU. However, for a gas line fitted with a POU sterile filter, the sampling point should be located *before* the sterile filter to avoid product contamination risk from a sample point downstream of the filter.

### 4.2.1 Sampling Locations for Pharmaceutical Compressed Air Systems

          

Pharmaceutical compressed air is used for many purposes. Air that comes in direct contact with product and/or product contact surfaces can affect product quality.

          

**Process control sample point locations:**

          

            
- After the compressor, dryer, and coalescing filter on the central system

            
- Before and after any in-line filters on the central system and prior to the distribution system

          

          

**Quality control sample locations:**

          

            
- Following any central treatment process intended to control the levels of an identified CQA

            
- The central supply header of the distribution system

            
- The most distant POU from the central system on each floor

            
- Prior to a POU final filter if the POU cannot be sampled

          

          

CQAs for the compressed air system typically involve sampling for:

          

            
- Moisture content — *following* drying process and at farthest distribution line end

            
- Total hydrocarbons — after final coalescing filter, at end of distribution lines, at critical POUs

            
- Non-Viable & Viable Particles — after central final filter, at most distant POU, at critical POUs

### 4.2.2 Sampling Locations for Cylinder and Liquefied Gas Systems

          

Since there are fewer steps in cylinder-based systems, fewer sample points are required as the finished product is supplied by a qualified supplier. Depending on the CQAs established, the following sample ports would be utilized for quality control purposes:

          

            
- Moisture (if required) — by dew point indicator following final filter, prior to furthest final filter, or at critical POUs

            
- Nonviable and viable particles (if required) — prior to the furthest final filter or at critical POUs

          

          

In many cases, there is no POU filter and the actual POU would be used as the sampling location.

          

### 4.2.3 On Site Generation and Bulk Storage Systems

          

On site generation and bulk storage systems vary significantly depending on the process gas involved, but should follow the general rules outlined for compressed air systems. Sample locations should be carefully reviewed and installed for both process control and quality control purposes in a similar fashion to those outlined for compressed air systems.

## Sampling Plan: Tests, Frequency, and Duration |

        

Risk-based framework for routine monitoring and qualification

      

    

    

      
      

        

          

Original Text (ISPE GPG 2016)

          

### 4.3.1 General Considerations

          

Compressed air or other process gases should be monitored at a frequency that would assure that gas does not adversely affect the environment or the product. Routine monitoring frequency should be similar to the frequencies established for the cleanroom, but these frequencies can be adjusted using the risk analysis tools once a base of historical data has been generated.

          

### 4.3.2 Risk Analysis for Sampling

          

Risk analysis of process gas systems should be performed upon initial system design and construction, or following significant system modifications. This risk analysis should identify sampling points that may present a risk to product, environment, or the integrity of the gas system.

          

Risk analysis principles should include the inherent risk of both viable and nonviable particulate level requirements that may already be controlled through adequate system design and utilization of critical POU filtration and maintenance.

          

Compressed air and process gas systems typically are designed, controlled, and maintained in a dry state (i.e., very low dew point), which inherently reduces the risk of microbial growth.

- **Requalification during major or minor changes**

## Sample Valve Design |

        

Design requirements for gas sampling valves in pharmaceutical systems

      

    

    

      

        

          

Original Text (ISPE GPG 2016)

          

Sample valves located within a central compressed air or process gas treatment system may be **ball valves** when installed prior to the central system final filter and prior to distribution.

## Sampling Techniques for Compressed Air and Process Gases |

        

Methods for microbiological, particulate, moisture, and hydrocarbon testing

      

    

    

      
      

        

          

Original Text — 4.5.1 Total Particles, Non-viable and Viable

          

The selection of a sampling method is dependent on the concentration and size range of the impurity/contaminant being monitored. Other evaluation criteria include sampling times, air volumes, and allowable outlet pressure restrictions.

          

Any gauges, adaptors, fittings, or tubing used to connect the sampling equipment to the sampling outlet should be clean and particle free, and should not introduce contamination. The preferred connection should be:

          

            
- As short as practical

            
- Constructed of stainless steel or other suitable material

            
- Without sharp bends (approximately 6 inch radius if bends are present)

            
- Non-restrictive

          

          

**Softer metals such as brass or copper can produce particles when joined and should not be utilized.**

          

Methods include: laser particle counting with a high-pressure diffuser, condensation nucleus counting coupled with scanning mobility particle sizing, differential mobility analysis, and membrane capture as covered in ISO 8573-4:2001.

          

Important notes:

          

            
- Flow meters, control valves, pressure gauges, quick connect fittings and polymeric tubing can shed particles — specify non-shedding materials

            
- When closed, valves capture particles; when opened they release them — use high purity diaphragm or bellows valve for flow regulation

            
- Seal material must not be rubber or other polymeric materials, with exception of Teflon

            
- Valves and regulators should be fully opened to minimize particle shedding

          

          

**Tubing materials for minimizing particle contamination (in order of preference):** stainless steel, conductive polymer, polyester, vinyl (if plasticizer does not interfere), polyethylene, copper, glass, Teflon, and aluminum.

### 4.5.2 Solid Particles — Viable Bacteria

          

Viable particles or microbial particles may be monitored using several different techniques:

          

            
- **Slit-to-agar (STA) technique**: Fixed rate air flow, fixed motor carriage rotates an agar plate at constant speed (typically 30 minutes per revolution). Plate media surface positioned at defined distance from air outlet slit.

            
- **Impaction technique**: Air forced perpendicular to the agar surface through a diffuser of fixed holes to monitor bacterial levels.

            
- **Hydrophilic membrane technique**: Fixed volume of air/gas passed through membrane where microorganisms are captured. Conventional laboratory enumeration techniques used to determine bacterial counts.

            
- **Real time monitoring**: Rapidly evolving technique; gas stream passed through analyzer with direct microorganism detection. Progress is being made rapidly due to huge demand for on-line, real time microbiological monitoring.

### 4.5.3 Water Vapor (Moisture / Dew Point)

          

Moisture content is typically monitored using on-line instrumentation providing real time information. For systems where the level of moisture is considered critical, the instrumentation and alarms associated with monitoring should be commissioned and qualified.

          

            "Moisture in process gases is usually measured as either dewpoint or pressure dewpoint. Methods include condensation devices (Cooled Mirror Dewpoint, Electrolytic, and Impedance Hygrometers, and Polymer Film Relative Humidity Sensors), instrumentation devices (Chilled Mirror, Electrolytic, and Impedance Hygrometers, Polymer Film RH Sensors)." [ISPE Process Gases Guide]
          

          

            "When using a hygrometer, the temperature and line pressure should be accounted for during dewpoint measurements. The instruments may be used in line for continuous monitoring or may be used to periodically monitor critical POUs. The type used should be determined by the degree of accuracy required for downstream processes, maintenance and calibration schedules, costs, and equipment reliability."
          

          

**ISO 8573-3:1999 Annex C guidance on dew point sampling:**

          

            
- Flush sample lines and hygrometers with dry gas, or by evacuating to low pressure before measurement

            
- Bake assemblies if possible to drive off stray residual water

            
- Select impermeable materials (steel and metals are practically impermeable; PTFE/Teflon is slightly permeable but satisfactory above -20°C; PVC, nylon, and rubber are not satisfactory)

            
- Polished or electropolished stainless steel is recommended for best results

            
- Sample tubing should be as short as possible; minimize connections and dead legs

          

          

When using chemical reaction detector tubes manually: break tube tips and insert immediately into sampling device; read color change immediately upon removal; readings in mg/m³, then convert to ppm for comparison to specifications.

### 4.5.4 Total Hydrocarbon: Oil Aerosol and Oil Vapor

          

Total hydrocarbon includes both **oil aerosols** (oils that can form an aerosol remaining suspended in air until impinging a surface) and **oil vapors** (also called total volatile hydrocarbons). Both are typically associated with oils introduced as part of a compressed air system; oils are typically not present in cylinder-based systems.

          

The total hydrocarbon content within compressed air systems is typically addressed through design or engineering controls by installing **oil-free compressors**, which eliminate the vast majority of hydrocarbon and related issues. Removal of oil aerosols is performed through coalescing filters in the distribution system.

          

Where design or engineering controls are in place, oil aerosols are not typically expected to be present, and **annual or less frequent testing or monitoring is required**.

          

**Methods for sampling oil aerosols:**

          

            
- Pre-weighed filter membrane(s) — analysis by gravimetric or infrared techniques

            
- Detector tubes

          

          

**Warning:** If oil liquid is observed, the system must be immediately shut down and the cause of failure determined — oil liquid is intolerable in pharmaceutical manufacturing.

          

**For oil vapors:**

          

            
- ISO 8573-1:2010 considers oil vapor measurement optional if purity limit is >1.0 mg/m³

            
- Glass or stainless steel cylinders: suitable for lower molecular weight hydrocarbons (C1–C10)

            
- Charcoal tubes or cylinders packed with charcoal: suitable for higher molecular weight vapors (C6+)

            
- ISO 8573-1:2010 defines oil as mixture of hydrocarbons with ≥6 carbon atoms (C6+)

## Chapter 4 Key Messages |

        

Critical takeaways for CDMO aseptic filling professionals

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Key Takeaways

          

            
1. **Gas quality = environmental quality:** Any compressed gas or process gas in direct contact with sterile products or their environment must meet the same microbiological and particulate standards as the cleanroom. This is a regulatory expectation from both FDA (2004) and EU GMP Annex 1 (2022).

            
2. **Risk analysis drives everything:** Sampling frequency, test scope, and sampling locations must all be justified by risk analysis tied to the specific use of the gas in the process.

            
3. **Two types of sample points:** QC sample points (included in sampling plan, require alert/action limits, trigger deviations) and process control sample points (diagnostic only, not in sampling plan) must be clearly distinguished on P&IDs and in SOPs.

            
4. **POU sampling rule:** For gas lines with POU sterile filters, sample *before* the filter — not after — to protect the sterile barrier while still assessing gas quality delivered to that barrier.

            
5. **Monitoring frequency by CQA:** Dew point = continuous; NVP/VP = quarterly; oil content = annual. These frequencies can be increased or decreased based on risk analysis and historical data.

            
6. **Sample connection matters:** The tubing, fittings, and valves used to connect sampling instruments can be contamination sources. No brass or copper; prefer electropolished stainless steel.

## Appendix 2 — Examples of Water System Sampling Point Locations

        

Pages 98–101 · POU configurations · Loop geometry · System boundary designations

      

    

    

      

        

          

Original Content — Appendix 2

          

            Note: These figures are found in Appendix 3 of the ISPE Good Practice Guide: Approaches to Commissioning and Qualification of Pharmaceutical Water and Steam Systems (Second Edition). They have been modified and reproduced in this Sampling Guide. System Boundary designations apply.
          

          

### Figure Descriptions — Five POU Configuration Types

          

#### Figure 6.1: POU Piping Direct Connect to Process Equipment

          

            

Configuration: Direct Hard-Pipe to Process Equipment

            

**System boundary:** From the distribution loop POU valve through a short direct-connection pipe directly into the process equipment inlet (e.g., bioreactor, formulation vessel).

            

**Sampling point placement:** Sample valve positioned immediately downstream of the POU valve, before the process equipment connection. This captures loop water quality at point of delivery, representative of what enters the equipment.

            

**Key characteristic:** Simplest geometry — minimal dead-leg risk if properly drained. Sample valve should be accessible and installed as close as possible to the POU valve on the distribution side.

          

          

#### Figure 6.2: POU Hard-Piped to Process Equipment (Extended Run)

          

            

Configuration: Hard-Piped Extended Connection

            

**System boundary:** Distribution loop POU valve → extended hard piping run → process equipment. The piping between POU valve and equipment represents a potential accumulation zone.

            

**Sampling point placement:** Sample valve at the POU valve on the loop side AND consideration of sampling at the equipment inlet where the extended run joins the equipment. The extended piping section falls within the system boundary and must be qualified.

            

**Key characteristic:** Risk of stagnant water in the connecting pipe between uses. Flush volume should be established during qualification. The sampling plan must account for the flush period before collecting a representative sample.

          

          

#### Figure 6.3: POU Piping with Integral Heat Exchanger

          

            

Configuration: POU with Inline Heat Exchanger (Cooler)

            

**System boundary:** Distribution loop POU valve → heat exchanger (cooling to ambient or process temperature) → process equipment connection.

            

**Sampling point placement:** Sample valve on both the inlet side (hot loop side) and the outlet side (post-heat exchanger, cooled water side) to detect any heat exchanger fouling or ingress. The post-exchanger sample is especially important for detecting microbial activity at lower temperatures.

            

**Key characteristic:** Heat exchangers introduce a microbial risk zone. Hot loop water is microbiologically controlled by temperature; once cooled, stagnant areas can support biofilm. Sampling frequency downstream of the HX should be justified in the risk assessment.

          

          

#### Figure 6.4: POU Piping with Hose Connection

          

            

Configuration: Hose-Connected POU (Mobile/Flexible)

            

**System boundary:** Distribution loop POU valve → flexible hose → process equipment. The hose is a detachable element and represents a significant hygienic risk zone.

            

**Sampling point placement:** Sample valve at the POU valve (loop side). For critical applications, an additional sample port at the hose outlet nozzle may be required during requalification or investigation.

            

**Key characteristic:** Hoses must be controlled under a separate SOP: defined draining, drying, storage, and re-connection procedures. Hoses are typically covered by a periodic re-qualification program (not continuous monitoring) and are identified as a separate system sub-boundary.

          

          

#### Figure 6.5: Water Distribution System Sub-Loop

          

            

Configuration: Main Loop with a Sub-Loop Branch

            

**System boundary:** Main distribution loop → sub-loop branch (may serve a separate production area or building) → return to main loop or dedicated tank.

            

**Sampling point placement:** Sample points required at: (a) the supply take-off from main loop, (b) within the sub-loop at representative POUs, and (c) the return connection back to main loop or tank. This ensures the sub-loop is independently characterized and cannot re-contaminate the main loop.

            

**Key characteristic:** Sub-loops with lower flow rates or intermittent use carry higher biofilm risk. The sub-loop must have its own documented flushing and sanitization protocol. The return valve from sub-loop to main loop is a critical control point.

          

          

### General Sampling Point Principles from Appendix 2

          

            
- All POU sample valves should be of hygienic design (zero dead-leg or minimal dead-leg per ASME BPE standards)

            
- Sample valves should allow complete flushing of the valve itself before the sample is collected

            
- System boundary demarcations determine which components fall under the qualified water system versus process equipment scope

            
- Sampling locations should be documented on the P&ID with unique identification numbers corresponding to the sampling plan

            
- The sampling frequency at each location is determined by the risk assessment (Chapter 2 of this Guide) and the qualification phase (Initial/Intermediary/Extended)

## Appendix 3 — Factors Influencing Pure Steam Generator Performance

        

Pages 102–105 · Source water · Mist elimination · Non-condensable gases · Blow down · Chloramine · Additives

      

    

    

      

        

          

Original Content — Appendix 3

          

            The purification system generating the water feeding a PSG typically needs the same monitoring and maintenance attention as pretreatment systems feeding distillation units that generate WFI grade water. This monitoring can help to optimize control of the pretreatment unit operations and steam generation processes, and alert the user to take remedial action to avoid Action Limits and specification failures in the steam.
          

          

The process of generating steam from water is reasonably efficient at separating pure water vapor from chemical contaminants. However, the separation is not absolute due to potential entrainment of mist from the incoming water (and its chemical contaminants) and carryover of contaminants into the pure steam.

          

            

#### Key Principle: 3-Log Reduction

            

It is commonly accepted that the phase change and mist elimination processes of distillation will purify feed water by at least three orders of magnitude (≥ 1000-fold or ≥ 99.9% impurity reduction), allowing no more than 0.1% of the feed water impurities to remain in the distillate or steam (see ISPE Baseline® Guide: Water and Steam Systems, Second Edition [7]).

          

          

### 7.1 Source Water

          

#### 7.1.1 WFI as PSG Feed Water

          

Organizations may choose to feed their PSGs with WFI from a POU valve on the WFI loop. When feed water has very low or undetectable endotoxin levels and is chemically pure, no additional PSG feed water pretreatment monitoring is required. Chemical attributes of the WFI feed water will be very similar to those of the steam it generates.

          

#### 7.1.2 Purified Water (PW) as PSG Feed Water

          

Many organizations feed PSGs from a PW distribution loop. Because PW has no endotoxin requirement, without specific microbial and endotoxin control precautions, excessive endotoxin levels may be present in the feed water.

          

With an assumed 3-log endotoxin removal capability and proper operation, the endotoxin level in the feed water would have to exceed 100 EU/mL to challenge PSG removal capability. Endotoxin levels this high are typically not seen in a well-controlled PW system. However, the actual log reduction capability of a PSG depends on its design (especially mist elimination presence).

          

#### 7.1.3 Potable Water as PSG Feed Water

          

The USP monograph for PS requires preparation from a starting material meeting drinking/potable water requirements. While there is no compendial requirement that PSG feed water meets PW or WFI quality, water quality should meet the PSG manufacturer's specifications. Water quality attributes such as conductivity, TOC, endotoxin, ammonia, and others should be monitored to ensure they do not exceed PSG purification capabilities.

          

Based on initial cost and maintenance economics, it may be more practical to include additional capacity in PW/WFI systems to support the PSG rather than to build a dedicated potable water pretreatment system.

          

### 7.2 Steam Generator Mist Elimination Capability

          

The USP monograph for pure steam specifically states that the generation process "prevents source water entrainment." PSGs without mist elimination will have poorer log reductions of feed water contaminants and this should be considered when devising a sampling plan.

          

            
- Well-designed systems using WFI feed water: low risk — no need for mist elimination since feed is already pure. No need to monitor feed water for conductivity, TOC, or endotoxin if WFI complies with USP.

            
- PW feed water: mist elimination becomes more important since endotoxin levels may be higher.

            
- Standalone PSG pretreatment (not well-designed or maintained): highest risk — most rigorous monitoring required regardless of mist elimination presence.

          

          

### 7.3 Non-condensable Gas (NCG) Removal

          

Without a dissolved gas removal process, non-condensable gases pass through the PSG and into the distributed steam. In low-usage systems, NCGs can accumulate. NCG presence reduces the efficacy of steam sterilization processes. BS EN 285:2015 and HTM 2010 specify NCG requirements for steam used in sterilizers.

          

Hot WFI feed water contains very little dissolved gas — few NCG problems. Ambient temperature feed water contains dissolved CO₂, O₂, and N₂. If highly deionized ambient feed water is used, CO₂ will be absent but N₂ and O₂ may be present (especially with ozone-based TOC/microbial control).

          

NCG removal methods: vacuum degasification or high-efficiency deionizers (CO₂ removal only).

          

### 7.4 Blow Down Adjustment

          

Monitoring of the blow down discharge coupled with periodic testing of conductivity, TOC, and endotoxin in the feed water may be appropriate to establish a blow down rate aligned with the manufacturer's recommendations and guidelines.

          

### 7.5 Potable Water Chloramine Use

          

Chloramine use in potable water disinfection is gaining popularity over chlorine (avoids THMs and HAAs). However, when chloramines are removed by pretreatment, residual ammonia may remain. Ammonia passes through most pretreatment processes and through a PSG, causing PS to fail its conductivity specifications.

          

Processes designed to remove chlorine are usually not as effective at removing chloramine. Changes from chlorine to chloramine disinfection by a municipality without notification may cause equipment damage and non-compliant WFI or PS — potentially impacting product quality and patient safety. Both ammonia and chlorine should be monitored after a dechlorination step when chloramine is in use.

          

### 7.6 Anti-scaling Steam Additives

          

The use of steam additives to minimize corrosion in PSG and distribution piping is strictly forbidden. Plant steam additives are intended to carry over into the steam. If used in PSG feed water, they would cause PS to fail its quality attributes. Plant steam condensate is never returned to PSG feed.

          

### 7.7 Monitoring Locations and Frequency

          

There are no pretreatment system monitoring requirements stipulated by regulatory authorities. Quality attributes should be periodically monitored at:

          

            
- Incoming source water

            
- Between pretreatment unit operations

            
- Feed water to the steam generator

          

          

The frequency should be appropriate for each system and informed by risk assessment and historical data.

Guide Architecture Overview

          

            

#### Purpose of This Guide

            

This ISPE Good Practice Guide provides practical guidance on sampling programs for pharmaceutical water systems (PW, WFI), pure steam (PS), and process gases (compressed air, nitrogen, CO₂, etc.). It bridges the gap between regulatory requirements and day-to-day QA/QC operational practice in pharmaceutical and biopharmaceutical manufacturing facilities including CDMOs.
