6	 C&Q Planning
6.1	
Introduction
C&Q is a planned, documented, and managed engineering activity that starts with the establishment of the URS 
and continues to start-up and acceptance and release of systems. A well-planned program is essential and leads to 
an efficient, “right the first time”, and cost-effective C&Q effort; the result is the delivery of systems that operate in a 
safe manner, meet established design requirements and stakeholder expectations, and are suitable for the intended 
purpose. Planning provides the ties between process understanding and system design; it focuses qualification 
efforts to enhance the likelihood that the system will deliver quality products while maintaining a controlled operating 
state. It also acts as a means for communication between members of the multidisciplinary project team.
This chapter focuses on the formal C&Q Plan. There are other plans that can be generated, such as overall project 
plans or cleanliness plans, which have great value but are not covered in this chapter. Refer to Chapter 21 (Appendix 
7) for a listing of supporting plans and general concepts for each.
Regulatory expectations for C&Q planning are described in Chapter 15 (Appendix 1).
6.1.1	
Purpose and Benefits
A well-conceived and executed C&Q Plan can greatly facilitate a timely and cost-effective qualification effort. It is 
essential to have excellent communication, planning, and coordination between the Quality Unit, vendors, operations, 
automation, engineering, construction, commissioning, and qualification personnel, to name a few, to deliver a project 
on time, within budget, and meeting all expectations. A C&Q plan facilitates this communication.
An important secondary goal of the C&Q Plan is to foster an interdisciplinary team approach to C&Q. Well-planned 
project logistics are crucial to the success of every project. A well-coordinated effort leads to a high quality, effective, 
and efficient C&Q project.
A single, integrated, overall schedule should be developed with input from the entire project team. This schedule 
should cover all systems (utilizing defined system boundaries as discussed in Section 3.2) and phases of the projects 
lifecycle from construction to Process Validation/PPQ completion. This schedule should be updated and issued 
regularly. These updates should not drive a C&Q Plan revision, but the C&Q Plan should identify how the schedule 
updates will be captured and communicated.
An integrated schedule, using a critical path method, can increase the awareness of dependent activities and provide 
a clear understanding of the critical path. Including all project team members in the schedule development will 
engage the team members and foster better planning. All team members should comprehend the schedule format. 
Ordering long delivery time items early can help timelines, but consideration should be made to ensure the process 
requirements for those items are understood and included in the requisition. Understanding when to engage support 
for C&Q helps to support the success of the project.
A well-planned effort can ensure that the user, business, regulatory, and patient expectations are met. All efforts to 
ensure the system is suitable for the intended purpose (e.g., start-up, balancing, trial runs, establishing maintenance 
programs) should be included in the planning phase since this is where the formality of the documentation can be 
established. These expectations include:
•
Systems are delivered on time and within budget
•
Systems meet approved user requirements and are suitable for the intended purpose
•
Stakeholder needs, including speed to market, are met

6.2	
C&Q Planning Approach
The amount of planning, documentation, reporting, and level of detail of the C&Q Plan should be based on 
expectations of the organization/site, size and complexity of the system or project, and the potential impact on 
product quality and patient safety.
A C&Q Plan should not repeat details that are in other documents but needs to capture the organization of those 
documents in order to facilitate an efficient project execution.
A separate C&Q Plan is not always necessary. Aspects of C&Q planning can be included with other documents, such 
as a validation plan or change control. For example, the simple change of adding a small system would not need 
a separate C&Q Plan; the requirements could be incorporated into a change control plan. More complex systems 
would benefit from a separate C&Q Plan, for example, the installation of a filling line with terminal sterilization and 
secondary packaging or the doubling in capacity of a manufacturing site including additional utilities to support the 
expansion.
6.2.1	
Timing of C&Q Plan
In an overall scheme, the creation of the C&Q Plan falls in the process map as shown in Figure 1.1 (Chapter 1).
It is important to note that C&Q planning begins prior to the creation of any formal written plans. C&Q planning starts 
during the planning and design phase of a project and continues through project implementation. A documented 
strategy may be developed as a precursor to a C&Q Plan, to help align team members with the process and 
personnel who will be responsible for C&Q.
A C&Q Plan should begin early in a project in the concept or early basic engineering phase.
The C&Q Plan needs to be approved before the respective testing documents (i.e., test instructions) are 
approved, and such test instructions need to be approved before test execution starts to support qualification. 
This is recommended for commissioning plans and test instructions. The C&Q Plan should address how existing 
test documentation and test results from commissioning (including FAT, SAT, etc.) are referenced in qualification 
documents, as appropriate.
6.2.2	
Lifecycle of C&Q Plan
The C&Q Plan is not finalized until after the risk management process has reached a point to capture the impact of 
product quality decisions or of establishing the CDEs.
C&Q Plans are expected to be kept up-to-date as changes to the project are made. Revisions of the C&Q Plan may 
be necessary as new information is learned or as modifications to the design are made, e.g., changes in scope 
(additions or removals), timing, or identification of additional CAs/CDEs.
Approval is recommended for these early plans and for subsequent changes made by the revision process; this 
ensures agreement at various stages of the C&Q approach. Changes to the C&Q Plan should be reviewed and 
approved by the original approvers (or their designate) and a revision history should be maintained of significant 
changes to the Plan.
It should be noted that revisions may not always be necessary. Minor departures from the C&Q Plan may be 
documented in the Qualification Summary Report with appropriate justification and rationale, as deemed appropriate 
by the project team.

At the end of a project, a written document capturing the completion and closure of the project should be created. 
See Chapter 8 for more details regarding acceptance and release.
Changes to the C&Q Plan should be handled under change management (as discussed in Chapter 12) to ensure that 
as changes are implemented, the impact to C&Q efforts is addressed.
6.2.3	
Elements to be Addressed
This section is intended to be a discussion of the elements that need to be addressed as part of C&Q planning, not 
the documents where they are captured.
6.2.3.1	 Scope of Testing
System classification (as described in Chapter 3) identifies which systems require qualification. The System Risk 
Assessment (as described in Chapter 4) identifies which elements in those systems require qualification. The 
URS (as described in Chapter 2) and System Risk Assessment establish the scope of testing for qualification. The 
equipment specifications establish the scope of testing for commissioning.
The testing approach is based on the most efficient method to test the system and should be captured in the C&Q 
Plan. If systems are tested separately, the C&Q Plan should include a final test verifying that all systems are able to 
work together prior to acceptance and release. If PPQ is included in a separate plan, the linkage to the execution of 
that critical phase should be captured.
6.2.3.2	 Identification of Document Source
Engineering firms, vendors, contractors, quality, engineering, and owners provide the documentation necessary for 
design, construction, commissioning, qualification, operation, and technical support of the systems. It is important to 
identify the documentation source early in the process so the impact on the testing of the system is understood and 
thus the quality of the documentation understood. This understanding will allow for utilization of the documentation to 
support qualification.
For example, the decision should be made during planning if any FAT, construction, SAT, or commissioning testing 
results and documentation will be used to satisfy the requirements to declare the system qualified or suitable for the 
intended purpose. This decision is based on vendor assessments for C&Q documentation purposes (as described in 
Chapter 10) and the criticality of the testing. It is often desirable to minimize the amount of retesting performed.
The C&Q Plan may also address the expectations of the documentation. It should be noted that pristine 
documentation, i.e., documentation that has minimal errors or corrections, is not essential but the documentation 
needs to be easy to follow and understand. Refer to Chapter 13 for guidance on Good Documentation Practice.
6.2.3.3	 Coordination with Overall Project Execution Plan
Another important aspect is working with the project manager to ensure that the planning of C&Q activities, 
whether a separate plan or part of another plan, fits seamlessly into the overall Project Execution Plan. C&Q 
activities should be properly reflected in the Project Execution Plan at an appropriate level of detail to be able to 
identify interdependencies between C&Q as well as to other project activities. Commissioning is normally occurring 
throughout construction and needs to occur when it is appropriate; e.g., commissioning the agitation system cannot 
be completed until it is energized.
Having these activities in the Project Execution Plan supports:
•
Coordination of test activities among C&Q to prepare for FAT or SAT events
•
Ability to schedule sequence of testing for multiple systems (e.g., electrical supply before energizing a system)

6.3.1	
Scope of the Effort
The scope captures all systems that are covered within the scope of the project. This includes both direct impact and 
not direct impact systems (as described in Chapter 3). More details are provided below.
6.3.1.1	
Additional Scope Considerations
PQ activities may be included in a C&Q Plan for pharmaceutical grade utilities (e.g., WFI, Purified Water), support 
systems (e.g., autoclaves, depyrogenation ovens, washers), environments (e.g., non-environmental monitoring in 
clean rooms), and non-product PQ (e.g., media fills for filling lines). Individual organizations/sites need to decide if 
these are in qualification or a separate validation program.
Cycle development for areas such as sterilization and cleaning also need to be considered. Cycle development can 
identify factors that require qualification or that modify the qualification. These are often executed after the equipment 
qualification effort. It is recommended to not perform the final qualification testing until after cycle development since 
changes identified by cycle development can be captured in the Engineering Change Management system (see 
Chapter 12). Individual organizations/sites need to decide if cycle development is part of the C&Q effort and capture 
the decision in the C&Q Plan.

6.3.2	
Strategy and Approach
The C&Q Plan needs to define C&Q strategy and approach to aid in overall project planning. The C&Q Plan captures 
whether systems will be commissioned only or commissioned and qualified. The system classification (as described 
in Chapter 3) and System Risk Assessment (as described in Chapter 4) have identified the CPPs and CDEs that 
need to be supported by the C&Q effort.
The rigor of testing depends on the user requirements, CDEs, and system characteristics (e.g., packaged system, 
modular, or installed system) as well as experiences with the vendor.
An aspect that needs to be captured in the C&Q Plan is the identification of the documentation source that will be 
utilized to satisfy the C&Q requirements. Appropriate prerequisites, means, and checks should be described in the 
plan to ensure reliable results, e.g., the pre-approval of vendor/manufacturer documents by an SME. If vendor-
provided documentation or testing is to be accepted to support qualification, the criteria for determining if it can be 
utilized needs to be established in the Plan. Refer to Chapter 10 for details regarding vendor assessments for C&Q 
documentation purposes.
The C&Q Plan should also capture if individual pieces of equipment or systems will be tested separately because 
they are delivered and installed at different times by different vendors or if testing will be executed once all 
components are installed. Dependencies within the scope of work (e.g., if one system needs to be tested before 
another system is tested) should also be included.
6.3.3	
Documentation
The C&Q Plan should capture document planning and control as well as execution of testing. The C&Q Plan should 
reference appropriate document control and content procedures to satisfy engineering, commissioning, qualification, 
operations, and maintenance requirements. Utilizing electronic storage of documents is recommended as it allows for 
searching and easier retrieval. If electronic storage will be utilized, ensure the vendors and contracting companies are 
aware and can support the delivery of their documentation in that means.
The Plan should define responsibilities for document preparation, collection, review and approval, and how the 
documents will be organized. This Guide recommends that a document matrix be considered as a means of planning 
and communicating these responsibilities. Documents also need to be tracked to ensure they are completed when 
they are needed.

6.3.4	
C&Q Plan Contents
The C&Q Plan should define the systems that will be commissioned and qualified based on the system or area 
boundaries. The plan should include a description of systems including their means of automation. The system 
overview should be a high-level process narrative, including system boundaries, based on operational/functional 
requirements. The C&Q team representatives should provide input in defining system boundaries (which can be in 
a system description, marked on P&IDs, or highlighted on system drawings) in the early project phases to develop 
an executable plan. It is often possible to gather much of the system overview information from other project 
documentation.
The C&Q Plan should address the level of testing for each system. This may range from detailed descriptions to 
references to standard tests. An alternative is to provide only high-level descriptions in the C&Q Plan and leave the 
detailed descriptions to execution documents. The selected approach should increase the level of efficiency and 
effectiveness. The plan should also include project schedules by system (as defined by the system boundaries) that 
capture the dependencies between systems (i.e., ones that need to be completed before completing others).
Commissioning is an important aspect that requires planning, documentation, and managed resources. 
Commissioning encompasses testing from FAT, testing during construction, SAT, installation testing, and much of 
the start-up activities (described in Section 7.3) of a project lifecycle including those that will be used to assess the 
qualification of a direct impact system. The C&Q Plan should also establish the framework necessary to ensure clear 
consistent communication and coordination required between predecessor and successor lifecycle activities (e.g., 
construction, C&Q, Process Validation, operations/maintenance).
Testing during commissioning should be used to support qualification. If commissioning documentation will be used 
to support qualification, it is critical to involve the leader of the qualification effort and the Quality Unit in the planning 
and coordination of commissioning activities and deliverables. A periodic commissioning planning and status meeting 
should be established to ensure appropriate planning for this stage of the project.
The C&Q Plan needs to include C&Q steps. There are two typical phases:
•
Installation: Documentation supporting installation is successfully completed. This may include FAT and SAT
documentation as well as site commissioning for systems built at the site.
•
Operational: Documentation supporting the functionality and performance of the system after the system has
been mechanically completed. This may include testing performed in FAT and SAT.
The plan should address the steps to follow when test results do not meet the excepted results or acceptance criteria 
(discrepancy). There should be a predefined methodology for how discrepancies should be handled (see Section 7.4).
