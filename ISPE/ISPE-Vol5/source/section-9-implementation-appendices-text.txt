and Risk-Based C&Q Process
14.1	
Introduction
This chapter describes strategies how organizations can effectively transition to the science and risk-based C&Q 
process described in this Guide. Implementing change successfully often requires high-level sponsorship due to the 
numerous stakeholders impacted.
The key to a successful transition is to recognize that there are multiple ways that the regulatory expectations can 
be met; adopting the science and risk-based approach does not invalidate current work or existing documentation. It 
should be noted that any process used for C&Q is expected to be science and risk-based.
Qualification documents represent a picture of the system at a point in time; this information changes with time 
and there are quality systems in place to manage this change (including associated revision of the qualification 
and engineering record documents), such as change management, maintenance, calibration, and periodic review. 
Therefore, existing documentation can remain acceptable to provide the documented evidence that the system is 
suitable for the intended purpose.

approach described in this Guide.

14.2	
Preparing for Implementation
The suggested steps for implementation preparation are described in more detail as follows:
1. 
Define the organization approach:
	
This Guide describes a suggested approach for organizations to adopt, but it may need to be tailored to fit the 
specific structure of the organization.
2. 
Evaluate the organizational maturity:
	
It is useful to perform a documented assessment of organizational maturity, considering the quality systems and 
GEP, to determine the degree and pace of organizational transition to a science and risk-based C&Q approach. 
The assessment scope is based on the process that the organization intends to implement and should consider 
all relevant aspects (e.g., experience, personnel resources, organizational culture). Examples of criteria for the 
relevant departments include:
•	
Research and Development/Process Development (i.e., the department that provides the science and 
process knowledge for which the C&Q program is based): Does this department have robust documentation 
and clear communication channels to provide the data to enable the process?
•	
Quality: Is the Quality Unit open to an alternative approach to C&Q and to the different documentation 
deliverables?
•	
Engineering: Is the engineering department ready to deliver documents per Good Documentation Practice? 
Do they have adequate change management systems in place? Is there adequate document storage?
•	
System Owners: Are the system owners adequately trained to perform the necessary reviews?
3.	
Develop an implementation plan:
	
A critical requirement for successful implementation is to have a well-defined implementation strategy with 
support from senior management. The use of initial small-scale proof of concept projects can help to identify any 
program aspects that require augmentation for a specific organization.
4. 
Develop supporting quality systems and associated procedures:
	
The procedures defining the engineering, quality, and documentation standards, as covered in this Guide, should 
be developed.
5. 
Train staff on the approach:
	
An important aspect for implementation is to provide overview training and ensure there is a good understanding 
of the key concepts. Considerations should also be made for how to present the approach to regulatory 
inspectors.
6. 
Train staff on the procedures:
	
The relevant staff should be sufficiently trained on the new procedures and have a good understanding of the 
overall program and its goals.

14.3	
Approaches for Implementation
For new projects or facilities, the use of a champion for the new approach is recommended. This individual helps 
with the transition and implementation of the new approach and should have a good understanding of the process 
concepts/goals.
While implementation is more straightforward for new systems (for which there are no existing procedures/
documentation), organizations also need to determine how the approach can be applied to a change on an existing 
qualified system. The most cost-effective way for implementation of the risk-based approach is to supplement the 
legacy approach as gaps are identified or changes to the system are made, thus maximizing the use of existing 
documentation.
A best practice approach would be to first assess if the system requires qualification –does the system have the 
potential to impact product quality? (see Chapter 3 for system classification criteria)
•	
If it does not have the potential to impact product quality, then recategorize the system and commission the 
changes
•	
If it has the potential to impact product quality, then it requires qualification:
-	
Review the existing documentation to determine if it meets current expectations
>	
If it does not, then qualify the system using the new approach, and remediate any gaps identified

requirements are met. A system URS brings together multidisciplinary system requirements into a single document to 
support system design, commissioning, verification, operations and maintenance.

•	
The requirements must clearly distinguish business requirements from quality requirements
•

identified in the requirements document

This document is the basis for the design, commissioning and where appropriate verification of the system

Process Air System
The process air system is direct impact and provides compressed air to meet predefined quality specifications. Note 
that this type of system is typically industry standard and the air generation associated equipment is commissioned 
only (not qualified) since the quality requirements are either verified during start-up (e.g., hydrocarbon content) or 
monitored where there is a risk of them not being met through use of the system (e.g., pressure dew point of the air 
from the dryer, total and viable particle count from critical point of use).
Operations Sequence/Process Flow
The start-up and shutdown conditions are not considered important from a quality perspective, only the in-operation 
state hence this has been considered.

Detailed Meeting Agenda Items for GMP Design Review Meetings
The concept GMP design review is anticipated to be a high-level review of the concept package against the user 
requirements and GMP expectations in the regulatory design basis, quality guidelines, and engineering design 
standards. It is important to obtain consensus on major approaches and decisions to avoid later rework. The meeting 
agenda list is not meant to be all inclusive and items may need to be added or deleted based on the project scope 
and type. Items that should be covered include:
Concept GMP Design Review
A. General Project GMP Requirements
• 
Overall project scope and business case requirements
• 
Project type
• 
Regulatory design basis review
B. Facility Layout and Material Handling
• 
Overall facility layout
• 
Room classification/hygienic zoning schemes
• 
Product, people, material, and waste flow
• 
Transitions requirements
• 
Gowning levels
• 
Cold rooms, freezer rooms, and incubator rooms (locations and requirements)
C. Process and Operations
• 
Overall process summary
• 
Identification of sterile process steps
• 
Overall equipment cleaning philosophy
• 
Review of product quality risk assessment including risk of contamination from (1) particulates internal and 
external to the equipment, (2) microorganisms (for sterile products), and (3) other products or residues (i.e., 
cross-contamination)
D. HVAC
• 
Room pressurization
• 
Air handling unit zoning and segregation
• 
Temperature and relative humidity requirements
• 
Filtration
E. Support Functions
• 
Identification of approach for required support functions including Quality Operations labs, in-process test 
labs, warehouse space, sampling and dispensing areas, and offices impacting manufacturing areas
F. 
Automation and Documentation Systems
• 
Automation strategy
• 
Critical parameter monitoring approach
• 
Building Automation System
