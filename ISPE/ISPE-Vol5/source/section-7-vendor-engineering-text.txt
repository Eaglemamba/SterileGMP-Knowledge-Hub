10	 Vendor Assessment for C&Q 
	
Documentation Purposes
10.1	
Introduction
This chapter describes vendor assessments3 and controls for work where vendors follow their own procedures to 
perform testing and produce documentation that may be used to support system qualification. When vendor tests are 
used to directly support qualification where appropriate, the result is reduced on-site testing and reduced cost and 
time in the schedule without impacting quality. In addition, the SMEs on the project team can contribute to improved 
product quality by focusing their attention on other higher risk quality critical activities.
Vendor assessments (for C&Q documentation purposes) do not include the vendor selection and/or commercial 
assessment, though the results may impact those assessments. Vendor assessments for C&Q documentation 
purposes may be integrated into this broader vendor selection and qualification process or comprise a standalone 
activity for C&Q planning and execution purposes.
Regulatory expectations for vendor assessments are provided in Chapter 15 (Appendix 1). For detailed information 
on assessing vendors of automated systems, refer to ISPE GAMP® 5: A Risk-Based Approach to Compliant GxP 
Computerized Systems [6]. For detailed information on vendor assessments for design services, construction 
services, and equipment supply, refer to ISPE Good Practice Guide: Project Management [27].
Note: It is not necessary to perform vendor assessments, as described in this Guide, for vendors that provide 
qualification services (sometimes referred to as secondary services) when those vendors are contracted to follow the 
regulated owner organization procedures and practices or applicable specialist standards, such as the US National 
Environmental Balancing Bureau (NEBB) [28].
10.1.1	
Purpose and Benefits
The objective of a vendor assessment is to establish confidence in the vendor and the vendor quality systems to 
perform testing, manage changes, and provide documentation that is accurate, complete, and unambiguous, with 
results matching what was expected. Vendor verification documentation assessments are highly recommended when 
documents may be used to support qualification. Purchaser and vendor cooperation is key to successfully achieving 
this goal.
Vendor assessments can minimize business/project risk associated with vendor performance, including project cost 
and schedule risk, by performing testing and completing documentation deliverables required to support verification 
of CAs (qualification) right the first time.
The use of vendor documentation can prevent repeating work that was appropriately performed and documented 
and can help to achieve GMP compliance at a reduced cost and in less time. Vendors are the experts in their 
particular systems and the use of vendor testing and documentation should be encouraged. As an example, consider 
verification of product contact materials. Accepting a certificate of conformity from a quality vendor can be an 
acceptable, low-risk approach for material verification; checking 100% of the related heat certificates would therefore 
be unnecessary.
3	 The use of the term vendor assessment throughout this chapter relates specifically to the use of vendor-provided documentation to support system C&Q.

10.1.2	 Principles/Concepts
It is good engineering practice to assess vendor’s capability for testing and documenting the systems they provide for 
conformance to requirements and specifications, based on the criticality of the system. The decision and justification 
to use vendor documentation to support the verification of CAs and associated CDEs should be based on the 
intended purpose of the manufacturing system and the suitability of the vendor quality systems. The decision and 
rationale should be documented in the C&Q Plan and approved by SMEs and the Quality Unit.
The Quality Unit and Engineering/Technical SME should agree on the type (e.g., checklist, test plan, execution 
report) and quality (Good Documentation Practices) of the deliverables. The Quality Unit should review the 
vendor assessment and confirm the extent to which the vendor’s documentation package may be used to support 
qualification and verify suitability for the intended purpose. This review and confirmation should be conducted and 
documented as early as possible.
Quality Unit oversight in the use of vendor verification documentation is a risk-based decision, with consideration of 
risk to product quality. For example, there is less product quality risk in documenting pipe boroscoping or passivation 
activities as compared to an SAT for a sterile filling isolator.
10.2	
Communication of Requirements, Scope, and Deliverables
The project team should ensure that the fundamental project requirements are communicated effectively to all parties, 
both internal and external. In addition to the overall project requirements, specific items that should be communicated 
to vendors involved in the project should include, at a minimum:
•	
Specific scope of work, including requirements and specifications as appropriate
•	
Specific deliverables, including testing and documentation, that are in the scope of work
•	
Quality assurance requirements for the work and deliverables
•	
Project controls which will apply
•	
Schedule of deliverables, including documentation deliverables and lead times for review and update
Depending on the size of the project, these items may be contained in a single document or detailed in several 
project plans, procedures, or specifications. Confirmation should be obtained that this communication has been 
understood; provisions should be made in the schedule for dialogue and/or meetings to provide clarification and 
confirm comprehension by the vendor.
From a project risk perspective and to help to ensure that quality is built in at the outset, arrangements should be 
made for supervision, inspections, and/or surveillance for the scope of work and associated deliverables across the 
lifecycle of their development.

10.5	
Use of Vendors Unfamiliar with GMP
The use of vendor assessments, quality assurance plans, and/or vendor quality plans provide a means either to 
accept or reject a vendor, or to identify where additional resources should be applied to ensure success. Certain 
vendors may not operate within a GMP environment for most of their products and services to customers. A QMS 
may be an unfamiliar or informal practice. The vendor needs to be informed that they are supplying goods or services 
to a pharmaceutical manufacturer.
Deficiencies should be recognized early and appropriate support measures established to help to deliver the 
necessary requirements. Where deficiencies are identified, additional controls and resources should be applied as 
early as possible. The vendor needs to support this approach and their agreement should be obtained. Additional 
control measures, designed to mitigate the risks presented, may include:
•	
Vendor training and guidance
•	
Additional review and approval
•	
Additional auditing of the vendor
•	
Attending/witnessing critical phases of the design, fabrication, and testing at the vendor site
•	
Support by third-party service providers
•	
Additional owner testing to compensate for being unable to rely on vendor generated test documentation

11	 Engineering Quality Process
11.1	
Introduction
When the C&Q process is considered as a part of an organization-wide PQS, the result is an achievement of both 
GMP and GEP concepts. A separate quality system is not required for C&Q; instead, it can be part of the overall PQS 
or a subset of the PQS. For the purposes of this Guide, the term Engineering Quality Process is used to describe 
the process within the PQS that covers C&Q. This Guide recognizes that this function is referred to differently across 
organizations which have their own structure and assignment of roles and responsibilities. This chapter intends to 
focus on the concepts that should be addressed for this function.
The overall organization PQS program provides the quality practices for processes in order to consistently deliver 
quality products. The Engineering Quality Process, as a subset process of the PQS, addresses the quality practices 
for the C&Q process and is intended to support the overall PQS program.
Both the PQS and Engineering Quality Process need to emphasize quality (i.e., the product or service meeting the 
specified requirements) versus compliance (i.e., meeting current procedures or organization requirements). Good 
quality practices and methods through the PQS produce robust reliable systems and ensures compliance. The 
Engineering Quality Process needs to be established as part of the PQS; the Engineering Quality Process can then 
be used for developing sustainable, consistent, cost-effective, and practical engineering processes for project and 
operational lifecycle management.
11.1.1	
Rationale
Current regulatory authority and ICH [12] guidances emphasize a focus on building quality into the product4 and 
not by inspection. ICH Q10 [9] augments the defined GMPs by describing specific quality system elements and 
management responsibilities that should be in place to achieve three main objectives; it states:
“Implementation of the Q10 model should result in achievement of three main objectives which complement or 
enhance regional GMP requirements:
	
Achieve Product Realisation [Section 1.5.1]
	
Establish and Maintain a State of Control [Section 1.5.2]
	
Facilitate Continual Improvement [Section 1.5.3]”
The FDA 2006 Guidance for Industry: Quality Systems Approach to Pharmaceutical CGMP Regulations [15] states 
the following as the goal of the guidance:
“This guidance describes a comprehensive quality systems model, which, if implemented, will allow 
manufacturers to support and sustain robust, modern quality systems that are consistent with CGMP [Current 
Good Manufacturing Practice] regulations.”
In alignment with ICH Q8 [4], the FDA guidance states:
“The overarching philosophy articulated in both the CGMP regulations and in robust modern quality systems is: 
Quality should be built into the product, and testing alone cannot be relied on to ensure product quality.”
4	 The term “building quality into processes” was first coined in June 1960 by Eiji Toyoda.

Another useful resource in understanding Engineering Quality Process as a philosophy is the work of Dr. W. 
Edwards Deming [29]. Deming believed in improving the process and in doing so using process measures to guide 
improvement efforts. In his book [29], Deming states:
“Conformance to specifications may be achieved in several ways:
1.	
By careful inspection, sorting the bad from the good. Dependence on inspection is hazardous and costly.
2.	
By work on the production process to shrink variation about the nominal value.”
The approach presented in this Guide embraces regulatory guidance and Deming’s principle that demonstration of 
conformance to specifications can be achieved by developing a process, which addresses causes and concerns for 
variability and reduces reliance on inspection. Thus, the focus of the Quality Unit should be to ensure adherence to a 
process that identifies CAs/ CDEs and confirms that they have been satisfactorily tested.
11.2	
Application
The Engineering Quality Process should include aspects that support the C&Q effort, such as maintenance and 
calibration, change management program, critical utilities, and the procedures for the C&Q process.
The approach described in this section is based on the six-system inspection model described in the FDA 2006 
Guidance [15], whereby an organization’s overall quality system provides the foundation for the five manufacturing 
systems that function within it. The six systems are as follows:
•	
Quality system
•	
Production system
•	
Facilities and equipment system
•	
Laboratory control system
•	
Materials system
•	
Packaging and labeling system
Product and process development/knowledge may be added to this list or may be included as part of the production 
system. The concept is addressed in Chapters 4 and 6.
As part of the overall PQS, the Engineering Quality Process plays a significant role in providing the practices and 
controls within the facilities and equipment system.
The application of an Engineering Quality Process also supports other requirements, such as for HSE and efficiency. 
Understanding this broader context and embracing Engineering Quality Process as a philosophy, rather than simply a 
collection of tools and templates, supports the design of a sound C&Q program that delivers systems suitable for the 
intended purpose using cost-effective solutions.
An Engineering Quality Process can help to transform the way in which organizations currently operate and to 
reinforce the efficient implementation of a science and risk-based approach to facility start-up. The Engineering 
Quality Process has additional objectives, including to:
•	
Provide a lean framework to foster innovation, encourage (rather than obstruct) continual improvement, and 
facilitate the effective, cost-efficient, and focused application of time and resources

Provide the framework for linking the CAs to the CDEs and the mechanism to capture the traceability

the C&Q activity currently being undertaken. This referencing of knowledge imposes a necessary discipline in the 
assessment, management, and maintenance of the knowledge, particularly on the change management practices used.

11.3	
Deliverables
The main deliverable from a quality system is to ensure that systems are operating as intended as captured 
in documentation. A document hierarchy within the Engineering Quality Process helps to identify where the 
requirements/expectations are derived. Figure 11.1 illustrates a typical document hierarchy for a quality system.
Figure 11.1: Typical Document Hierarchy for Quality System
On the top of the hierarchy are policies/directives, which are the high-level documents that capture the expectations 
of the organization. The expectations for C&Q may be that the systems used to manufacture products have been 
qualified to be suitable for the intended purpose. These expectations may be part of an overall validation policy or 
detailed in an independent policy.
The next level is standards, which identify the minimum deliverables that are required for implementation of the 
program. The intent is to avoid different parts of the organization interpreting the intent of the policies/directives 
differently. For C&Q, the deliverable may be a Qualification Summary Report. Standards should:
•	
Reference any specific relevant regulatory authority guidance
•	
Be used to implement a harmonized approach throughout the organization – this should be an organization-wide 
model with a process for sites to learn from each other and practice a wider continual improvement path
•	
Limit special conditions – the standard should not make allowances for individual site preferences or 
commitments, but should include a process to grant exceptions after proper review and approval
The next level is procedures, which may establish steps to be followed for the implementation of standards. 
Procedures may be corporate or site-specific documents. Procedures may be used for multiple sites but should avoid 
the term global since not all sites in the organization may follow them. Examples of expected procedures are for:
•	
URS
•	
System Risk Assessment (identification of CAs/CDEs)
•	
DR/DQ
•	
Protocols/Plans
•	
Engineering Change Management

The bottom level of the hierarchy is executable documents, which are the plans, test scripts, reports, etc. that

In summary, the Engineering Quality Process should:
•	
Define how oversight by the Quality Unit on the program or project will be accomplished – this is especially 
important on large projects that may last for an extended period of time and may include personnel changes
•	
Define approvers of documentation – the Quality Unit does not need to approve every document; the documents 
that require Quality Unit approval are described in various chapters of this Guide (see also Section 6.4 for an 
example approval matrix)
•	
Establish the linkage to the QRM process for C&Q (System Risk Assessment as described in Chapter 4)
•	
Address integrated C&Q efforts – e.g., integrate the C&Q process so that one set of testing and documentation 
can satisfy C&Q requirements
•	
Address what can be done as part of start-up and how much shakedown is done before documentation starts
•	
Identify the level of testing and expectations for documentation – this should be commensurate with the risk 
to product quality; the objective is to provide confidence that overall delivery of the program delivers a working 
system and a quality product
•	
Define the vendor selection process – it should address the selection and decision-making process; then based 
on the information available, decide on the level of oversight and use of vendors and their documentation
•	
Provide a means for measuring progress and maintenance in the C&Q program – it should establish a metric 
control plan to measure progress
•	
Ensure continual improvement – senior management should be responsible for the quality system governance 
through management review to ensure continuing suitability and effectiveness of Engineering Quality Process
11.4	
Roles and Responsibilities
The roles and responsibilities for the Engineering Quality Process should be defined. The functions that contribute to 
the Engineering Quality Process are:
•	
Engineering: Align engineering systems to support the Engineering Quality Process
•	
Manufacturing: (representative(s) of the group that will use the system to manufacture products) Ensure the 
Engineering Quality Process will be able to deliver a system suitable for the intended purpose
•	
Technical Support/Research: Provide technical guidance for product and process requirements and critical 
parameter ranges based on Basis of Design studies during product development
•	
Quality Unit: Ensure the Engineering Quality Process fits within the QMS and agree with the provisions of the 
Engineering Quality Process
•	
HSE: Ensure the Engineering Quality Process addresses personnel safety and impact to the environment and 
includes provisions to meet local, state, and federal requirements
