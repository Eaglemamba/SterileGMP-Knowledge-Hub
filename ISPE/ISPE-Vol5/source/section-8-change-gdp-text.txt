12	 Change Management
12.1	
Introduction
Change management processes should be established and applied throughout the lifecycle of manufacturing 
systems. The risk-based application of SME-led change management is one of the biggest challenges in the 
implementation of QRM-based C&Q as it applies the principles of ICH Q9 [3]. This Guide presents an approach which 
involves two modes of change management:
• 
Engineering Change Management (ECM)
• 
Quality Change Control (QCC)
Project change management that covers the elements of project scope, cost, and schedule is outside the scope of 
this Guide.
A system is considered to be changed when it is modified, altered, added to, removed, or improved in a way that 
makes its functions, physical features, or performance different from what they were prior to the change.
Replacing an existing part of a system with an identical one (like-for-like) may not be considered as a change. 
Organizations should define and follow a robust assessment process for classifying changes as like-for-like.
12.1.1	
Purpose and Benefits
Change management is a documented program that:
• 
Defines what is subject to change
• 
Determines the risk for product impact caused by the change\
• 
Establishes a process for initiation, review, and approval of the request for change
• 
Defines when the Quality Unit should be involved
• 
Establishes a process for evaluation of the actual impact of the change
Change management is a well-managed process for instituting changes and is needed for the following reasons:
• 
Ensures control of the impact on the current state of the system and the process it belongs to
• 
Provides a basis for peer (SME) review and approval
• 
Is a regulatory requirement (e.g., 21 CFR 211.100 [30] and EU GMP Volume 4 (§5.23) [31])
• 
Is a good business practice
Change management processes should not be (or perceived to be) an obstruction to fostering innovation and 
continual improvement. All stakeholders in the change management system should understand the purposes and 
benefits of effective change management. Efficient use of change management adds value by delivering technically 
sound solutions in an efficient manner and makes innovation and continual improvement less cost or time prohibitive.

12.2	
Engineering Change Management
ECM is a key component of the Engineering Quality Process (as described in Chapter 11). It provides the primary 
control mechanism within the overall Engineering Quality Process and is fundamental in supporting the execution of 
regulatory/quality change control for CQAs. Appropriate SMEs, process owners, and the Quality Unit (where CAs/
CDEs are impacted) should be involved in the evaluation, review, and approval of the potential impact, the intended 
purpose of the change, and the work required to implement the change.
There are three primary areas of application for ECM:
• 
Provide lifecycle management for systems supporting both GMP and non-GMP regulated processes during 
operation
• 
Provide the primary change management mechanism during a project phase including work intended to support 
C&Q (e.g., during DR/DQ, fabrication, FAT/SAT, and verification)
• 
Manage and document technical changes for non-critical aspects of qualified systems
The review and approval method for the ECM system should enable effective and rapid change management that is 
sufficiently flexible to adapt to:
• 
The stage of the project and related regulatory impact
• 
The impact on manufacturing, maintenance, and system lifecycle
The level of documentation of the change should be commensurate with the level of risk related to the change.
It is important to understand the following:
• 
When and where ECM may be applied within the organization
• 
The point in the project lifecycle at which QA-managed QCC applies
12.2.1	 Scope and Sequence of ECM
International GMPs require that changes that have the potential to impact product quality and patient safety be 
handled within the PQS (refer to Chapter 15 (Appendix 1) for the regulatory basis). This drives the need to establish 
a defined endpoint for application of ECM to GMP and non-GMP changes within the project lifecycle; from this point 
forward, full application of QCC to CAs/CDEs within systems commences.
Figure 12.1 (in Section 12.1) highlights the potential benefits to be gained when applying an SME-led science and 
risk-based approach, by not commencing QA-managed QCC until the acceptance and release phase at the earliest. 
From a regulatory perspective, it is only after the acceptance and release of a system (for execution of PQ/Process 
Validation, if applicable, or for manufacturing for commercial use) that changes to systems with CAs/CDEs should go 
through QA-managed QCC.
During the operational phase of a system, ECM should continue to be applied to non-critical aspects of the system. 
Each change to the system should therefore be evaluated on whether CAs/CDEs are affected by the change.

12.4	
Change Management during Operation
After acceptance and release and after the system is placed into production use, any changes to the qualified 
system has the potential to affect product quality and patient safety. GMP regulations usually require Quality Unit 
pre-approval (approval prior to non-emergency implementation) of changes with the potential to affect product quality 
(refer to Chapter 15 (Appendix 1) for the regulatory basis). When using a risk-based approach, QA-managed QCC 
should be applied when the change impacts CAs/CDEs.
After acceptance and release, there are scenarios that allow for application of ECM without Quality Unit involvement. 
Organizations should establish procedures that state the specific requirements for use of ECM within the organization 
or facility. Routine operations that may allow for application of ECM without Quality Unit involvement include, for 
example:
• 
Changes to non-GMP aspects of systems
• 
Changes to non-critical aspects of systems (as established by the System Risk Assessment)
• 
Certain categories of changes, such as like-for-like, as documented in the completed verification and approved 
acceptance and release documentation
When QCC is used to manage changes, a log or tracking system should be used to maintain change histories for 
systems with CAs/CDEs. IT tools providing this functionality may be commercially available. A complete record of all 
changes to qualified systems should be able to be retrieved on demand.

•

are assessed, implemented, and approved as to their potential impact on product quality and patient safety.
•

the system owner, should be notified of approved changes. It is important to recognize the difference between 
design development and change. The final design (including the implemented changes) will be assessed by DR/
DQ to verify that the documented user requirements and associated design intent are met.
 
At the completion of the design phase, DR/DQ is completed. At this point, ECM is applied to GEP changes, and 
ECM with Quality Unit approval is applied to changes in CAs/CDEs (with Quality Unit approval).

13.2	
C&Q Document Development Basics
Testing and verification documentation are usually written in the local language. Summary documents are generally in 
English to facilitate regulatory review.
The use of forms/templates is encouraged to promote consistency. Forms/templates can be approved once when 
created and then utilized multiple times without pre-approval if the relevant content is not changed. Where this 
approach is used, there should be a procedure to ensure that the form/template used for data entry is current at the 
time of use.
When forms/templates are used, the SME should review the form to ensure that it includes all of the required 
testing for the system being verified; if additional tests are required, the form/template should be supplemented with 
additional testing.
Test scripts may be approved individually and assembled into a test plan/report. If test data sheets are used to 
supplement test scripts, they should be approved in accordance with the requirements defined in the test plan.
The test procedures should be clear and unambiguous such that the results are repeatable and reproducible, as 
explained below:
• 
Repeatable: The same result would be achieved if the same procedure is repeated by the same trained and 
qualified personnel.
• 
Reproducible: The test procedures should be performed the same way each time and the same result would be 
achieved by different trained and qualified personnel.
The test document should indicate the order that the cases are to be executed. Acceptance criteria needs to be 
established and it should be clear that the tests demonstrate that the acceptance criteria have been met.

13.3.1.1	 Executor Qualification and Signature Log
Individuals who enter information in a test document (i.e., executors) must possess the education, training, 
experience, or any combination thereof to perform their work [32] and record their printed name, signature, and initials

Documents should be completed as work is performed. Results should be recorded at the time of activity. Backdating 
is not permitted.
The steps within a test case should be executed in order unless specified otherwise. Provide an explanation for any 
step or task performed out of sequence.
If a test script takes more than one calendar day to execute, indicate where testing stopped and initial and date this 
entry at the appropriate test step.

dated. The date of the actual data should be included. Transcribed data should be checked by a second person. If 
data is transcribed, the raw data should be included –labeled, initialed and dated.

Approvers should have the education, training, experience, or any combination thereof to review and/or approve

have been obtained.

Methods of document storage should be established, as dictated by organization/site policies and procedures and 
in a manner that allows documents to be located and retrieved within a reasonable time period, as required. The 
methods should include access control and tracking. The storage area should be secure.
Copies and original documents should be labeled as such. Executed documents can be scanned after final approval 
and archived as electronic records in a validated document management system. Once archiving is completed, the 
hard copy can be destroyed. Retention of records should follow organization/site specific records management policy. 
Check the specific requirements of authorities for the regions that the site will supply before destroying GMP records/
documents.
13.8	
Disaster Recovery
There should be a system in place to handle the reconstruction of records. All measures should be taken to recover 
the original document. For a record that is lost or destroyed and not recoverable, a copy of the original record is 
acceptable as the official copy, which should be approved by Management.
Note: Storage and management of documents during the execution of a project should be defined to minimize risk of 
loss and/or damage.
