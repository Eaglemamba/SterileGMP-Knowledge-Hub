Q2-1: What should the classification of the room surrounding the isolator be?
Recommendation
Determination of the classification level should be risk-based, taking into consideration the risk of
introducing contaminants during preparation of the isolator with open doors, as well as movement
into and out of the isolator during operation.
Note: Current FDA guidance recommends a back ound environment of no less than ISO 8 in operation; current
gr
EU GMP Annex I recommends ISO 8 at rest (Grade DJ.
Rationale
When isolator systems are properly designed, contaminants aer not exchanged between the interior
of the isolator and the surrounding environment. Robust qualification of the isolator system provides
assurance that the classified space within the isolator can be maintained.
Additional considerations for the surrounding room include maintaining a clean and organized space
and controlled access. Temperature and humidity settings are generally for the comfort of the operators,
but potential impact to decontamination processes of the surrounding environment must be considered.
The quality of the air inside the isolator is more critical to the process than the outside air. When the isolator
functions properly and monitoring and alarm systems are integrated, the risk of the room air causing an
issue to the process is minimal. Nonetheless, the space surrounding the isolator should be controlled and
meet airborne particle limits for deanrooms, as defined in ISO 14644, in order to provdi e a high degree of
assurance that surrounding conditions and practices will not negatively affect the aseptic processes within
the isolator. Although Annex 1 requires only Grade D "at rest" determination of airborne particle count, it is
common practice to apply at least the ISO 8 limit "in operation'' for open and closed isolators with gloves.
Higher risk operations may require more rigorous control. However, a lower Grade surrounding area/con­
trolled environment may be acceptable for gloveless isolators that present no access during aseptic operation.

Q2-2: Should the isolator fill zone have unidirectional flow?
Recommendation
Unidirectional airflow may not be required within an isolator fill zone where it has been demonstrat­
ed that nonunidirectional (i.e., turbulent) air does not present the risk of product contamination. In
all cases, however, the airborne particulate limit should not exceed that for ISO 5. Likewise, the zone
surrounding an isolator need not provide unidirectional air. In this zone, the airborne particulate
limit should not exceed ISO 8 in operation.
Rationale
Airborne contamination is the major risk in the fill zone and other critical zones within an isolator (i.e.,
zones where product or product contact components may be exposed), because the physical barrier
l
provided by the isolator eliminates potential contamination directy from personnel. Air in the isolator
is filtered through HEPA filters, providing the same degree of cleanliness whether or not flow is unidi­
rectional. The purpose of unidirectional flow is to prevent the incursion of airborne contamination in
the form of viable and nonviable particulates from outside the dean air stream. In addition, all outside
items introduced into the isolator are decontaminated, entering either through rapid transfer ports
or a sterilizing tunnel. Nonsterile air incursion through isolator openings (e.g., an exit mousehole), is
prevented by a positive pressure differential to the outside. The risk that surface contamination will
become airborne in a turbulent air flow is obviated by effective chemical decontamination.
With proper risk analysis, an effective isolator decontamination process, and a rigorous aseptic fill
challenge, turbulent air flow may be demonstrated to be acceptable in an aseptic fill isolator. ISO 5
is required in order to achieve an airborne particulate level equivalent to that prescribed for unidirec­
tional airflow.
Similar logic may be applied to the area around an isolator. The barriers provided by the physi-
cal isolator structure and air pressure differential provide adequate protection against the ingress of
contamination. This position must be supported by appropriate challenges to confirm the integrity
of the isolator (e.g., glove integrity testing, where appropriate). ISO 8 in operation is recommended
to provide a controlled environment even though air exchange between this zone and the interior of
the isolator is not possible.

Q2-3: Should airflow pattern visualization/demonstration be performed in
an isolator?
Recommendation
Unidirectional airflow should first be verified by measuring air velocity 150-300 mm from the HEPA fil­
ter face as described in ISO Standard 14644-3. Once the airflow velocity distribution has been established,
the airflow may be further verified by an airflow visualization or "smoke" stud
y.
If nonunidirectional airflow is employed, only directional airflow at the boundaries of the isolator,
e.g., mouse-hole exit, need be performed. Differential pressure between the isolator and the sur­
rounding wne should be monitored continuously.
Rationale
For unidirectional isolators, the airflow velocity test will establish the airflow streamlines as delivered
at the HEPA filter. Airflow visualization is used to ensure that any disruption to these streamlines,
e.g., by a stationary object in the flow path, does not present a risk of contamination. This type of
test may be extended to observe the effect of routine and expected non-routine glove-aided interven­
tions. Where unidirectional flow is specified, "first-air" principles should be maintained. An airflow
visualization test will demonstrate that interventions do not violate those principles. Airflow visu­
alization may be able to demonstrate disruption to air direction, e.g., by a stationary object in the
flow path. Whether or not this disruption presents a risk to product is subject to interpretation. The
results of such studies should be considered as merely one factor in determining system acceptance.
Nonunidirectional isolators should be demonstrated with a high degree of assurance to not pres-
ent a risk of product contamination (see Q2.2). In such isolators, airflow will not necessarily follow
defined streamlines and may be subject to random backflows and eddy currents. Visualization of
these flows is of no value. On the other hand, the user must be sure that air from outside the isolator
cannot get inside. Measuring pressure differential satisfies this concern, but airflow visualization at
the points determined by risk analysis to be vulnerable to incursion adds a high degree of assurance.
Air flow visualization studies should be performed at system qualification. In general, they do not
have to be repeated except where a risk assessment has determined they are required, as in the case of
a significant equipment change.

Q2-4: Should the isolator environment qualification runs (at rest and in
operation) include the sample sites established according to ISO
14644-1 or can a reduced sampling plan be followed based on a risk
assessment?
Recommendation
For total particle count, the firm should use the methodology defined in ISO 14644-1. Because
ISO 14644-1 only defines limits for total airborne particles, qualification of performance for viable
counts should require a risk assessment to determine sampling methodology and location.
Rationale
ISO 14644-1 is the international consensus standard recognized by the European Union, PIC/S, and
the U.S. FDA to define cleanroom classification and performance. ISO 14644-1 provides for a spe­
cific and well-defined sampling plan, including the minimum number of sample locations, sampling
pattern, sample size, and methodology to interpret the data. By following the ISO standard, a firm
may be highly confident in the isolator design and, upon recertification, continuing performance.
Qualification and recertification according to the ISO standard for total particle count provides as­
surance that pockets of potential contamination do not exist.

Q2-S: What techniques should be used to identify, track, and evaluate air
flow during qualification and maintenance of the isolator?
Recommendation
As with any pharmaceutical operation, a risk-based approach should be applied to isolators. Because
airborne particulate presents a significant risk to pharmaceutical operations carried out within isola­
tors, airflow visualization studies are often considered essential to assessing that risk. Video record­
ing these studies provides even more value by allowing further analysis by more reviewers, as well as
providing permanent documentation.
Appendix B-7 ofISO 14644-3 describes various test methods applicable to airflow visualization.
These include: (a) tracer thread method, (b) tracer injection method, (c) airflow visualization method
by image-processing techniques, and (d) airflow visualization method by the measurement of velocity
distribution. Appendix C-7 of the ISO standard describes various apparatus used for these methods.
The tracer injection ("smoke" study) method is most frequently recommended for pharmaceutical
isolators, for both unidirectional and nonunidirectional isolators. Aerosol fog covering a larger area
to test unidirectional flow may be generated from deionized water or from commercial organic and
inorganic solutions. Commercially available "candles" or "sticks" generate lesser quantities of visible
fog and are usually limited to testing airflow direction at critical interfaces (e.g., mouseholes, trans­
fer isolators). In either case, the value of airflow visualization may be enhanced by photography or
videography.
Evaluation of test results will depend upon the perceived risks. At critical isolator interfaces, such as
mouseholes or transfer points, directional airflow can be visualized and recorded, adding evidence to
air pressure differential measurements. Inadequate flow or even unacceptable reversals may be discov­
ered and corrected before committing actual product to the isolator.
Airflow visualization within a turbulent flow isolator, if and when employed, most likely would
not provide valuable information other than the discovery ofleaks into or out of the isolator. In a
unidirectional isolator (the more common design for aseptic operations), airflow visualization studies
can provide valuable information besides leaks. The streamlined flow of air from the HEPA-filcered
diffusers can be confirmed to flow uniformly down and around objects in its path. Any eddies,
turbulence, "dead zones," or backflow can be assessed to determine whether or not they present a
significant risk to exposed product or product contact surfaces. If a risk is determined, corrections
may include flow rate adjustment or repositioning items within the isolator.
In operation, airflow visualization can help in the evaluation of the effect of normal movement (e.g.,
conveyed vials and stopper transport), as well as routine and nonroutine interventions. Particular
attention should be paid to human activity through glove ports and the introduction of items or
components through transfer ports. This can help distinguish between safe and unsafe technique and
provide a valuable training aid, especially in video form. It can also help pinpoint areas of risk where
environmental monitoring may be focused. Discovering areas of risk during the test phase allows for
correction, both mechanical and procedural.
Rationale
Physical measurement, such as airflow velocity and pressure differential, provide important data
regarding isolator operation. Experience has shown, however, these measurements alone may not be
adequate unless correlated to actual experience. The FDA guidance on aseptic processing strongly
implies that air visualization studies be conducted and emphasizes the "in operation" application of
the test. EU GMP Annex 1 requires it in Grade NISO 5 environments.

Q3-1: How should personnel be gowned when working in the isolator or the
background environment area during aseptic processing?
Recommendation
Depending on the classification of the surrounding area, it may be prudent to gown according to the
company's Grade CID gowning requirements. In addition, it is a good practice for operators work­
ing in isolator gloves to perform interventions or other activities inside the isolator to wear a separate
set of sterile, cleanroom gloves. Street clothing should not be worn in the environment of produc­
tion isolators to minimize the bioload in the room and in the isolator during cleaning, loading, and
unloading.
For loading, unloading, and cleaning opened isolators, an additional hood, sterile goggles or glasses,
and gloves, in addition to Grade C garments, should be considered to reduce the risk of particles and
microbial load.
Rationale
The isolator provides a good physical barrier between the environment and the critical area; there­
fore, a Grade NB garment is not required. Gloves worn underneath the isolator gloves will reduce
the microbial load within the isolator gloves. Wearing sterile gloves provides an additional layer that
can help to protect against potential ingress of microorganisms from the environment in the event of
a pinhole in the isolator glove.
During teh loading of the isolator, the aim is to keep the microbial load low. Skin Rakes, hair, and particu­
lates from operators may result in microbial or particulate contamination after the vapor hydrogen peroxide
(VHP)-decontamination cycle. Operators should wear gloves to reduce the risk of passing oily substances/
sebum onto the surface of the isolator as this coudl prevent the surface from being decontaminated.

Q3-2: Should operator gloves be disinfected prior to entering isolators
gloves?
Recommendation
Sterile gloves should be disinfected before entering isolator gloves. Use of sterile 70% isopropanol or
70% ethanol is recommended, or another sanitizing/sporicidal agent can be used. 1he disinfectant
can be applied by spraying or wiping.
Rationale
Disinfected gloves worn underneath isolator gloves will better reduce the microbial load within the
isolator gloves than nondisinfected gloves. In the case of a pinhole in an isolator glove, a reduced
microbial load on the inside of the isolator glove will reduce the chance of microbial ingression into
the isolator.

Q4-3: What is the response to a glove integrity failure?
Recommendation
A procedure should detail the actions required when a glove failure is discovered. Physical failure of
gloves should be treated as a deviation, and the associated investigation must be documented. The
response and actions taken may vary, depending on when the failure is identified.
If
the integrity failure is discovered prior to a production run, the run should not proceed until the
failure is investigated and resolved.
If
the integrity failure is discovered during line setup or prior to isolator decontamination, the nonin­
tegral glove should be replaced.
If
the integrity failure is discovered subsequent to isolator decontamination, but prior to product
exposure inside the isolator, the glove should be replaced and the decontamination cycle repeated.
Conducting a full investigation may not be necessary if the previous post-batch physical integrity test
and visual check passed. However, an investigation to determine the cause of the failure may be help­
ful for identifying actions required to prevent a repeat of the failure.
If
the failure is discovered during/after a production run, an investigation should be conducted to
determine the cause of the failure and the impact on the filled product. The investigation should
consider the location of the glove, the activities performed by those wearing the glove, and the tim­
ing of integrity tests that passed. Prior to resuming production, the glove should be replaced and the
conditions that caused the failure should be resolved.
Rationale
Quality risk management should be applied for each scenario and corrective action should be taken
in order to minimize the risk of contamination. Each site should evaluate the specific processes and
procedures used. It is not acceptable to use nonintegral gloves or gloves with pinhole leaks during
media fills or manufacturing processes. Validation activities should ensure adherence and perfor­
mance to aseptic processing requirements.
