1.0  Introduction
The distribution of life science products is extremely diverse, ranging from raw materials to finished 
product through the supply chain to the end user. Typically, as the distribution process moves from 
shipments on pallets to individual packages to delivery to the end patient, the term “Last Mile” is 
applied. The Last Mile defines the point at which products have reached their final destination (e.g., 
pharmacy, medical practice, private residence). Product temperature must continue to be maintained 
within registered storage and/or transport conditions. This may include shipments from a wholesale 
distributor to a pharmacy where the products are held awaiting customer pick-up or, as the Last Mile 
process has become an even more complex system, home delivery of products that also include cell 
and gene therapy. 
1.1	
Purpose
This technical report provides guidance for establishing a quality management system for distribu-
tion of all types of temperature-sensitive medicinal and pharmaceutical products, from preparation of 
products for transportation at shipping sites to the shipment of products to receiving sites for storage 
or distribution to patients (end users). 
The examples accompanying the points to consider provided herein on how to protect the quality of 
the product are for demonstrational purposes only. 
1.2	
Scope
This technical report offers a resource for all stakeholders involved in the pharmaceutical supply chain 
and provides a model for qualifications from the process design through implementation and opera-
tion to verification. The stakeholders include, but are not limited to, the following organization types: 
• 
Pharmaceutical and biotech companies of commercial and clinical medicinal products, including 
manufacturers, contract manufacture organizations (CMOs), and repackagers 
• 
Health care providers (HCPs) 
• 
Logistic service providers 
• 
Wholesalers 
• 
Distributors (including subcontractors) 
• 
Pharmacies, including mail order, infusion, specialty, and compounding pharmacies 
• 
503B pharmacies currently in the United States 
• 
Nongovernmental organizations such as the United Nations International Children’s Emergency 
Fund (UNICEF) and Pan American Health Organization (PAHO) 
The stakeholders referenced are responsible and accountable for the processes that control the han-
dling, transport, and storage of the product from the point of manufacture to distribution to the 
patient. The stakeholders need to work closely with the manufacturers who have all the stability data 
and knowledge necessary to evaluate the excursion.

process design, process qualification, and ongoing process verification for thermal protection systems 
in the pharmaceutical supply chain.

2.0  Glossary 
The terms and definitions below are in common use within the industry and represent industry best 
practices in the experiences of the technical report team.
503B Pharmacy 
The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) 
created this designation to, “recognize the differ-
ences between outsourcing facilities and conven-
tional drug manufacturers, while maintaining the 
minimal standards necessary to protect patients 
from the risks of contaminated or otherwise sub-
standard compounded drug products.” US Phar-
macies with this designation must comply with 
State Board of Pharmacy guidelines.
Acceptance Criteria
Predefined criteria that must be met by testing; 
numerical limits, ranges, or other suitable mea-
sures for acceptance of test results.
Ambient Temperature Profile 
The temperatures selected to simulate the an-
ticipated and experienced extremes used to chal-
lenge a thermal protection system in a testing 
chamber.

Corrective Action/Preventive Action 
(CAPA)
A corrective action is taken to eliminate the root 
cause of a nonconformance and prevent recur-
rence. A preventive action is meant to eliminate 
the cause of a potential nonconformance or 
another potential undesirable situation (hence, 
aimed at preventing an occurrence).
Conditioning/Preconditioning
The process to bring the payload or a thermal 
protection system component(s) to a specified 
temperature condition by keeping it for a given 
period of time in the conditioning environment. 
Data Acquisition System
A combination of sensors, measurement hardware, 
and software used to measure physical conditions 
that convert the results into digital numeric values. 
Data acquisition systems are used in thermal pro-
tection system qualification to measure tempera-
ture using, for example, thermocouples.
Data Loggers
Measurement instrument that is capable of re-
cording temperature over a defined period of 
time based on memory capacity. The data can be 
retrieved, reviewed, and stored for accept/reject 
decision-making along with analysis of route.
Design Qualification (DQ)
Tests or assessments that are performed prior to 
operational qualification to ensure that the user 
requirements are met by the proposed thermal 
protection system.
Equilibration Time
The duration required for a thermal protection sys-
tem with product load to reach the required product 
temperature range within the thermal requirements.
Indicators
A measurement instrument capable of compar-
ing environmental conditions (e.g., temperature) 
to alarm settings to determine if those settings 
have been exceeded. Limited data may be stored. 
Installation Qualification (IQ)
The process of obtaining and documenting evi-
dence that the premises, equipment, and/or sup-
porting systems have been provided and installed 
in compliance with their design specifications.
Logistics Service Provider (LSP)
Providers that contribute to the transportation 
and storage/distribution process including but 
not limited to carriers, integrators, forwarders, 
and custom brokers. LSP’s may contract aspects 
of the logistics process to Subcontractors.
Management Review
A formal, documented review of the quality sys-
tem’s performance executed periodically at senior 
management level according to a planned schedule.
Mechanical Testing (also Distribution 
Testing, Physical Testing, or Dynamic 
Testing)
Testing that demonstrates and documents, with 
a high degree of assurance, the ability of a ther-
mal protection system to withstand the distribu-
tion environment in compliance with the user 
and design requirements. Some typical examples 
are shock, drop, impact, compression, and vibra-
tion testing.
Nonconformance
Any unexpected and/or unwanted deviation 
from established processes, procedures, regula-
tion, laws, requirements, standards, or specifica-
tions including but not limited to temperature, 
anomalous trends, and quality system failures. 
Operational Qualification (OQ)
Testing that demonstrates and documents, with 
a high degree of assurance, that a system consis-
tently performs as intended under the anticipat-
ed shipping conditions in compliance with the 
user and design requirements. 
Pack-Out
The detailed instructions on how to precondi-
tion and assemble the packaging system with the 
payload to be shipped. With a seasonal pack-out 
configuration, the preconditioning and assembly 
may change depending on the time of year, am-
bient temperatures, and/or transportation lane. 
A universal pack-out configuration remains un-
changed throughout the year. 
Passive Thermal Protection System 
(see Thermal Protection Systems, 
Passive Payload (also Product Load))
The variable amount of product to be trans-

ing. PCMs are used in thermal protection sys-

A placebo product is used to replace the actual 
product during qualification. Rather than use 
expensive pharmaceutical products, a placebo 
product provides a cost-effective way to test 
cold-chain solutions during the qualification 
process. The placebo product should imitate as 
closely as possible the dimensions and thermal 
behavior of the actual product and its packaging 
(primary and secondary). Preliminary tests may 
be necessary to prove that the placebo product is 
an acceptable emulation of the real product and 
its packaging (primary and secondary) that will 
be shipped in the qualified solution. 
Proportional Integral Derivative (PID) 
Controller
The control loop mechanism that provides feed-

chamber control system.
Qualification

will function to meet its predetermined accep-
tance criteria.
Shipper
An individual or company that tenders products for 
transportation (common term also used to identify 
a package containing products to be shipped). 
Stability Budget
A stability budget considers the results of long-
term, accelerated, extreme excursion, and tem-
perature cycling studies to determine the amount 
of time out of storage that a drug product may ex-
perience without any significant risk to its quality. 
Standard Operating Procedure (SOP)
A written set of step-by-step instructions that de-
scribe the requirements necessary to complete an 
activity, task, or process.
Subcontractor 
An entity that takes a portion of a contract from 
the principal contractor such as an LSP or from 
another subcontractor. 
Temperature Excursion 
An event in which product is exposed to tem-
peratures outside of the recommended storage 
and/or transport temperature range. 
Thermal Protection System (TPS)
Any active or passive temperature control system for 
the distribution of temperature-sensitive products. 
Active Thermal Protection System
A temperature-controlled container with refrig-
eration and/or heating capabilities and its asso-
ciated calibrated temperature-control devices. 
This term includes commonly used contain-
ers like temperature-controlled trucks/trailer, 
temperature-controlled ocean containers, and 
active-unit load devices for airfreight.
Passive Thermal Protection System
An insulated container that, when assembled, will 
provide thermal protection for its payload from out-
side temperatures. It comprises a set of components 
including an insulated container, spacers, dunnage, 
shipping refrigerants (e.g., gel pack, gel brick, dry 
ice), packaging, and product payload configured in 
a specific manner to function as a qualified temper-
ature-controlled system for a specified duration.
Transportation Lane
The two points that define the origin and desti-
nation for a shipment.

3.0  Process Design
Process design begins with defining the requirements for shipping a product from the origin to the 
destination, which will require multiple inputs from development, logistics, etc. The outcomes of all 
requirements are a proposed thermal protection system (TPS) and the inputs for the transportation 
process qualification.
3.1	
User Requirements Specification (URS) 
The best practice for an organization is to complete a cross-functionally reviewed and approved User 
Requirements Specification (URS) before starting a qualification process (1). The URS defines the 
requirements of the product, packaging, and transport system for the qualification process. Examples 
of considerations may include but are not limited to:
• Product storage and transportation temperature
• Transportation method and/or lanes
• Performance duration required to perform activities such as pick, pack, ship, or put away
• Expected ambient temperature exposures or ambient temperature profiles
• Minimum and maximum expected shipping quantities
• Product load thermal characteristics and shapes (i.e., payload capacity)
• Temperature monitoring strategy
• Operational, safety, and logistical capabilities, including handling and material conditioning
• Environmental considerations (i.e., reusable, disposable, compostable, or recyclable)
• Other product specific considerations that should have been evaluated at the primary- or second-
ary-packaging level, such as shock, vibration, atmospheric pressure, are outside the scope of this 
document.
3.2	
Quality Risk Assessment
Risk assessment is the process of identifying, evaluating, and mitigating risks that have the potential 
to negatively affect product quality, processes, and/or user/patient safety. It requires proactively iden-
tifying risks that have the potential to occur, evaluating their relative risk impact (using qualitative or 
quantitative scoring), and implementing effective design, process, and/or operator/user controls to 
minimize or eliminate those risks to product quality and user/patient safety. 
Risk management procedures should be established to provide a standardized process to conduct, 
review, and update a risk assessment. A risk assessment is paramount to ensure that patient safety and 
product quality have been comprehensively considered and met through internal qualification testing, 
or equivalent vendor qualification testing. 
Risk review sessions should be ongoing as well as event-driven (e.g., additional requirements, event-
based failures, nonconformances, corrective/preventive actions (CAPA), changes). This will ensure 
previously evaluated risk levels are still representative of, or updated to reflect the current state of a 
product and/or process by means of the quality management system, which subsequently demon-
strates the product and/or process is kept in an acceptable state of control. 
The outcome of the risk assessment should be documented. Plans to handle, store, and distribute the 
product should be made accordingly. For more information about risk management, see PDA Techni-
cal Reports No. 54 and No. 58 and ICH Quality Guideline Q9: Quality Risk Management (2-4). 
3.3

Design qualification (DQ) is the process of obtaining and documenting evidence that the TPS has 
been designed in accordance with user requirements. Principles discussed in this section apply to both 
active and passive TPS solutions.

Initial selection and comparison of a potential TPS may often be made using a customized profile or 
one of several standard thermal profiles, e.g., International Safe Transit Association (ISTA) Testing 
Standard 7D or 7E (5-6).
Design testing results should demonstrate the best and worst-case locations to be monitored during 
OQ testing. Best practice considers using more than the minimum number of probe locations as de-
scribed in Section 4.0 Process Qualification. 
When a placebo product is used for testing, understanding its thermal dynamic compared to real prod-
ucts is crucial. For example, a box of 30 prefilled syringes of 1 ml will not behave exactly the same as a 
placebo product using a 30 ml vial of liquid. Assumptions used to design the placebo products should 
be well explained with preliminary test results or thermal calculations. The size of the placebo product 
should also correlate with the dimensions of the intended payload. In some cases, qualification will be 
done using minimum and maximum payload dimensions. In other cases, however, the use of a placebo 
payload with average dimensions is acceptable if most shipments have fairly constant payload sizes 
throughout the intended season(s).
DQ testing results are also used to define the components to be used as part of the TPS and in the 
OQ. Qualification results are only valid for the specific components tested. For example, an ice pack 
of a specific volume must also be described with the correct dimensions for a thermal packaging solu-
tion. An ice pack containing the same thermal mass but with a different shape or dimensions cannot 
be considered equivalent since a change of shape may have a substantial impact on the qualification 
results. Consequently, component specifications and diagrams should be written for each component 
of the successful design that is critical to the performance of the TPS. Component specifications are 
not required prior to design testing.
A qualification strategy for reusable temperature-controlled shippers should additionally include as-
sessment on re-certified for use, recovery, and refurbishment services provided by the shipper, packag-
ing vendor, or logistics service providers.
4.0 
Process Qualification
The qualification exercise can be regarded as the deliberate and purposeful collection and evaluation 
of data from a process in order to establish evidence that the process can consistently satisfy the user 
requirements. The subtle difference between OQ and PQ is that the former is intended to examine the 
process at the extremes, and the latter is intended to ensure process technical transfer to the process 
owner. The OQ of a thermal protection system is typically executed in a climate chamber at the antici-
pated extreme conditions, whereas the PQ is executed in real life according to the Validation Master 
Plan. Collectively, these qualifications may be referred to as “qualification.” A risk assessment should 
examine events that are exogenous to the process yet may impact product quality, such as equipment 
failures or unplanned shipping delays.
A qualified shipping system should have an approved set of qualification documentation. Typical parts 
of the documentation may include the URS, risk assessment, DQ, OQ, PQ, mechanical testing (for ex-
ample shock and vibration), complete qualification plan, and final control strategy; (see Figure 1.0-1).

the tests were done, and the rationale for why specific procedures were selected (e.g., real or placebo

ments. The description of the preparations, measurements, and protocol should be organized chrono-

location of temperature probes (if used), and experimental set-ups in environmental chambers should 
be included. Then, the protocol to be followed and a completed report should be approved by all par-
ties involved.
The Validation Master Plan (VMP) encompasses all aspects of validation required by an organiza-
tion. The validation may include, for example, manufacturing processes or equipment, back-up power 
equipment, heating and cooling systems, storage, and distribution. The goal is to make sure that all 
items related to temperature management for storage and distribution have been identified, included 
in the VMP, analyzed and documented, and have produced acceptable results.
Thermal qualification should not use a macroscopic approach (whole or lumped system without inter-
nal spatial variations), which can be misleading. Rather, the thermal qualification should be obtained 
by performing a heat transfer analysis targeting the detection of product temperature excursions at 
critical locations, such as corners and points nearest or farthest from a cold pack in a passive TPS or 
refrigeration unit, for an active system. Critical product temperature locations can be determined us-
ing preliminary test results, thermal simulation software, or other guidance from documents such as 
ISTA Standard 20 (Revision 2.0): Design and Qualification of Insulated Shipping Containers (ISC) (9). It 
means that, in most cases, more than one temperature measuring point is needed for the qualification 
process.
4.1	
Operational Qualification (OQ)
The goal of an OQ is to provide documented testing that demonstrates, with a high degree of as-
surance, that a process or system will function to meet its predetermined acceptance criteria when 
exposed to anticipated environmental extreme conditions (for example temperature, shock, and vi-
bration). The package or system would be run according to the preapproved qualification protocol 
with expected loads to prove reliability and repeatability of performance. The location of products or 
placebo products within the shipper must be clearly documented. Including only the number of prod-
ucts or items is not sufficient; describing their orientation and positions with respect to one another 
(loading configuration) is essential since it will influence the thermal behavior of the product payload 
and the temperature distribution within it. 
The equipment used for an OQ should mimic the expected temperature conditions within the lane 
and record accurate data of the environment and product payload. The materials to be used in the test 
must be preconditioned in the controlled environments defined in the OQ protocol. The protocol 
may include but is not limited to a description of:
• 
Scope and activities required to complete an OQ test
• 
Clear acceptance criteria
• 
Preconditioning of the test materials
• 
Temperature-monitoring methodology

In case the OQ process requires the use of an environmental chamber to perform the selected tempera-

conditions, even though they can replicate the same temperature profile. Such parameters as product 
placement and stacking pattern, airspeed, shelf surface types (e.g., grid, metal, wood, plastic), and tem-
perature accuracy will have a significant impact on test results and should be given considerable atten-
tion. The same also applies for the qualification process of potential handling scenarios such as package 
orientation, temporary storage conditions, and stacking before shipping. For example, most small parcel 
shipments will experience multiple orientations during handling and should be tested to at least identify 
potential temperature excursions that were not observed during the original design orientation. Such 
observations should be communicated to the design team for possible design revaluation.
4.2	
Performance Qualification (PQ)
Performance qualification (PQ) is done using the actual transportation or distribution environment 
and is performed by those who will be executing the day-to-day shipping process. The PQ serves to 
demonstrate that the qualified system is effective and reproducible in real-world application. Testing 
should be performed using typical parameters including product loads, operational handling, trans-
portation routes, and conditioning procedures. 
PQ testing should be performed following a preapproved protocol and the results should be presented 
in a report. The test plan should incorporate standard operating procedures (SOPs) that include:
• 
Actual ambient temperature variances, including seasonal changes and cross-hemisphere lanes, if 
applicable; data should be available to support the lane
• 
Representative transportation load configurations
• 
Defined packaging configurations
• 
Calibrated temperature monitors
• 
Sufficient number of tests to assure reliable results
• 
List of SOPs relevant to the process
• 
Evidence of operator training on the relevant SOPs (extending to all subcontractors)
Other procedures may be used and, if so, they need to be justified.
For further understanding, see Section 8.0 Case Studies.
4.3	
Qualification Plan Complete
To close the qualification of the TPS, all completed documentation and the deviations should be 
summarized in one report, sometimes called Qualification Assurance Summary Report or Validation 
Summary Report. The conclusion should indicate the applicability and shortcomings of the TPS and 
list the deviations from the URS. The report should be approved according to established procedures.

4.4	
Shipping Control Strategy
A shipping control strategy or quality risk control strategy, which is the output of the quality risk assessment, 
for drug product in the supply chain should define the measures to implement the qualified TPS within the 
operational environment to ensure that the product is shipped under the intended conditions within the 
stability budget. This means anything that can increase the degradation rate of the medicinal product must 
be controlled. A control strategy should include the implementation or update of the VMP, SOPs, contracts 
(e.g., quality agreement, technical agreement), training, and maintenance protocols including calibrations. 
In addition, a control strategy may include, in part, allowable times and temperature exposures expressed in 
a tabular form that reflects product stability, citing the stability budget for the product. This will ensure the 
qualified TPS will be implemented and used according to the established qualified conditions.
5.0 
Ongoing Process Verification
After process design and process qualification of a TPS, the shipping organization should implement 
the TPS in operations according to the control strategy. By applying continuous process verification, 
this will ensure that the TPS will protect the product throughout transit—from its preparation at the 
shipping site, during transport, and until its arrival at the receiving site—as intended during the quali-
fication for quality, effectiveness, and safety. 
Continuous process verification applies to all transportation modes and to all parties involved in the 
transportation of medicinal products and its related activities, such as (but not limited to):
• 
Preparing the goods for transport
• 
Loading/unloading goods into shipping equipment
• 
Loading/unloading from one piece of shipping equipment to another
• 
In-transit handling between transportation (e.g., airport transit, harbor transit)
• 
Receipt of goods
Each participant in the distribution process has an obligation to support the goal of protecting the 
product, minimizing opportunities for exposure to temperatures outside the required range, and to 
assess and respond appropriately based on the impact of such exposures, should they occur. In par-
ticularly cold or hot climates, it may be advisable to extend the temperature protection to the docking 
areas of the vehicles (complete enclosure)
Participants in the transportation process can be categorized as shippers, logistics service providers 
(LSP) and receivers. LSP refers to the providers that contribute to the transportation and storage–dis-
tribution process, including carriers, integrators, forwarders, and customs brokers. 
There are also considerations specific to transportation using vehicles or containers providing active 
temperature-controlled environment as well as specific aspects for transportation using passive TPS.
In order to support implementation, shippers must thoroughly document the processes used and 
improvement modifications made, as well as the reasoning for those changes, during the qualification 
process. This documentation is the critical reference for the creation of a process SOP, which should in-
clude procedures for process monitoring, environmental monitoring, nonconformance management, 
training, and the application of these procedures by the managing stakeholders. 
Process monitoring must demonstrate that the state of control is being maintained where the set of 
controls consistently provides assurance of continued process performance and product quality. The

5.1	
Standard Operating Procedures (SOP)
SOPs should be in place to describe in detail all steps of the process execution. SOPs should be re-
viewed and revised periodically according to the organization’s policies and guidelines. A key part of 
an SOP implementation would include training and a comprehension evaluation section to assess the 
knowledge of the SOP users (e.g., shippers, logistic service providers, carriers). A summary of changes 
and versioning is required for easier tracking. The review and approval system requires a defined pro-
cess of retrieval and removal of obsolete SOP versions.
5.2	
Monitoring
Data loggers and indicators, commonly referred to as monitors, are instruments that use micropro-
cessors with sensors and internal memory capacity to collect data on predefined attributes, such as 
temperature and/or humidity. They can be connected to a computer system and use software to col-
lect, store, and analyze data as, in an example of temperature-controlled storage premises, part of a 
continuous monitoring requirement. In such cases, computer/electronic system validation is required 
as a part of the GxP. 
In different circumstances, monitors can be connected wirelessly to a modem, device, or cloud-based 
system, or monitors can operate independently as stand-alone instruments. Data loggers can record 
and transmit predefined attributes in a real-time environment providing certain predispositions are 
fulfilled, such as connectivity to a GSM/Wi-Fi/Bluetooth signal operated within the restrictions of 
airfreight transport. Unlike temperature, humidity monitoring and control is predominately reserved 
for storage premises. Like shock and vibration, humidity monitoring during transport is a product-
specific consideration, not a standard in attribute monitoring.
When supported by the risk assessment, a time–temperature indicator (TTI) can be used as an alter-
native to data loggers. A TTI is a device or smart label that shows the accumulated time–temperature 
history of a product. TTIs are commonly used to indicate exposure to excessive temperature and/or

Each monitor is required to have a certificate of calibration and must not be used beyond the date

Training

5.5	
Management of Nonconformances
From a temperature perspective, distribution must be designed to keep the product within the defined 
limits described by the manufacturers, applicable regulations, or visible on the outer packaging. The 
distribution process is a complex and dynamic process, where product is exposed to the external envi-
ronment, less controllable than e.g., a typical drug manufacturing process, not managed directly by the 
product manufacturer and exposed to various entities in the supply chain. Due to the inherent vari-
ability of the external environment, nonconformances should be expected and documented according 
to the level of impact on the product.
Written procedures should be in place so that nonconformances are defined, their impact on product 
quality is assessed, and their root cause investigated, determined and, as applicable, eliminated (see 
Section 5.5.1).
5.5.1	 Categorization of Nonconformances
Nonconformances can be categorized by their type (e.g., temperature excursions outside the defined 
limits described by the manufacturer or visible on the outer packaging, logistic process deviation, 
equipment breakdown, theft/loss, anomalous trends, noncompliance with a good-practices require-
ment), as well as their criticality (e.g., no impact or potential or determined minor or major impact 
on product quality or patient safety). The categorization applied by each company must be described 
in a written procedure.
5.5.2	 Impact Assessment
An impact assessment must be performed following written procedures. The impact assessment must 
also consider and define the boundaries of the nonconformance in terms of which products may have 
been affected and which are out of scope.

The level of impact determines the level of investigation and correction needed. A prompt correction 
of the nonconformance to limit its immediate impact is required, and all actions undertaken in that 
respect must be properly documented. 
All products impacted (or potentially impacted) must be individually assessed. An investigation should 
be conducted involving the appropriate subject matter experts (SME) for the product(s) to determine 
the impact on product quality. Stability data might be used to assess temperature excursions. Never-
theless, the cumulative impact of any possible previous excursion must be assessed. All investigations 
including temperature excursions should be properly documented and approved by the Quality Unit.
When product quality, compliance, or patient safety is not impacted, it is considered acceptable to close 
the nonconformance with minimal or no root cause investigation (as applicable and described by the 
procedure). The impact assessment must be formalized and approved as required by the local procedure.
5.5.3	 Corrective and Preventive Actions (CAPA)
If a root cause of a nonconformance is determined, a CAPA should be considered and implemented 
in order to remove the root cause of the nonconformance. A corrective action is taken to prevent 
recurrence, whereas a preventive action is taken to prevent occurrence. Depending on the CAPA, the 
implementation of the CAPA should be monitored for its timeliness and effectiveness. The thorough-
ness of a CAPA must be commensurate with the risk posed by the nonconformance.
A CAPA is used to address quality and compliance issues and should result in process improvements 
and process understanding. It should be used in conjunction with the root cause analysis. To facilitate 
the trend analysis, a root cause should be identified according to the elements, for example, the route 
where the nonconformance occurred, root cause location (where it happened), and root cause owner 
(who or what is at the origin). The level of effort, formality, and documentation of the actions should 
be commensurate with the level of impact of the issue on product quality.
The development, implementation, and verification of the CAPA’s effectiveness will often require col-
laboration with the LSP(s) and/or packaging/container manufacturers.
5.5.4	 Product Control and Disposition Decision
Should an absence of impact for products in the boundaries of the nonconformance be determined, 
the product can be distributed after that no-impact determination has been confirmed, according to 
product specifications and local procedures.
Should an impact be determined, product(s) must be held and can be distributed only after a correction 
of the impact, if possible, has been successfully executed and product quality impact no longer exists.
When correction of the impact is not possible, impacted product(s) must not be released. 
5.5.5	 Approval and Closure
The responsibilities for approving nonconformances and their investigations must be described in a local 
procedure. The Quality Unit is typically responsible for approving all nonconformances as described.
The person responsible for the approval and closure of a nonconformance must have access to all 
information and investigation elements as required to perform the closure. When more information 
or investigations are needed to enable the approval and closure of a nonconformance, these must be 
requested from and provided by the investigation team. 
The approval can be granted only after all investigative elements are available and formalized and all 
applicable CAPAs have been satisfactorily identified and approved.

5.6	
Complaints
Similar to nonconformances discovered during distribution operations, procedures should be documented 
that describe the processes for receiving, evaluating, investigating, addressing, and trending of complaints 
from customers. A CAPA should be considered to remove root causes of complaints as appropriate.

5.7	
Periodic Review
Distribution performance must be monitored and reviewed periodically to ensure the state of control 
is maintained as expected by the qualifications. The extent and frequency of the distribution process 
capability should be reviewed at regular time intervals using a risk-based approach.
Distribution nonconformances (e.g., number, trends) and the system for the management of non-
conformances must be periodically reviewed. The aim of the review is identifying negative patterns or 
trends in the processes and highlighting areas for improvements.
Implementing a process to monitor the occurrence of nonconformances, by tracking appropriate met-
rics and KPIs, is necessary. Mechanisms for escalation should be included to discuss the trends.
Aspects to be reviewed include, but not limit to:
• 
Number or frequency of nonconformances for each process or product, as applicable
• 
Adequacy (effectiveness) of CAPAs (e.g., by measuring recurrent nonconformances)
• 
Adherence to the standards required by the local procedure (e.g., timeliness for investigating and 
approving nonconformances)
For nonconformances classified as minor, trending should be performed to ensure they do not repre-
sent (or hide) a major nonconformance that would warrant more incisive actions. 
This periodic review must be an integral part of the management review.
5.8	
Change Control
An effective change control system must be in place to control changes made to the qualified systems. 
Each change to a qualified system must be described and documented. 
According to the type of change, different systems can be used to record and manage changes (e.g., 
distribution change control, system logbook).
In distribution, a good practice is to maintain a list with all the routes/shipping lanes and their descrip-
tion (e.g., shipping/receiving site, LSP, airlines/haulers, cold chain solution). 
Changes made to the system may render the distribution process no longer qualified. All changes 
(planned or unplanned) and their potential impact on the qualification status must be documented 
and approved by the Quality Unit.

• 
New transportation lane with a specific transportation mode (e.g., air, ocean, rail, road) or major 
modification of an existing qualified transportation lane (e.g., shift from air to ocean)

New type of data logger

New passive TPS

6.0 
Conclusion 
This technical report provides guidance for establishing a process for the qualification of distribution 
of temperature-sensitive medicinal and pharmaceutical products. It offers a resource for all involved 
stakeholders in the pharmaceutical supply chain, providing a model for qualifications from the process 
design through implementation and operation to verification including the shipment preparation of 
the products and thermal protection systems, their transportation and arrival at the receiving site for 
storage and further distribution to the patient (end user). 
The technical report addresses “what” is needed to qualify thermal protection systems and processes, 
but not the “how” as this may vary depending on local regulations, business requirements, and product 
characteristics. By applying the guidance provided in this technical report, the reader will ensure that 
product quality is maintained during the distribution through the pharmaceutical supply chain in 
compliance with current regulatory expectations.

8.2	
Case Study 2: Passive Shipper SOPs
When using passive shippers, the assembly diagram, photos, and a description should be included 
within either the SOP or protocol. As with a puzzle, the multiple pieces must be assembled correctly. A 
written description of the process to assemble a passive shipper must accompany the diagram. Training 
should be completed and documented for all employees who will assemble passive shippers. Retraining 
schedules should be developed and maintained. Audits need to be performed on the accuracy of the 
passive shipper assembly. Random checks may be done after a passive shipper has been assembled and 
finalized. The Quality Unit should randomly select shippers and open them to verify proper assembly.
Transport modes need to be defined prior to the execution of any qualification. A carrier quality 
agreement may be helpful to understand exactly how logistic service providers and freight forwarders 
will consistently handle each shipment. Things to consider are the route and the risks with that route 
related to time in transit and/or security. 
Gel pack and phase-change materials are used inside passive shippers to maintain temperature. Frozen 
gel packs and phase-change materials can be at different temperatures, such as -15 °C to -10 °C. Know-
ing at what temperature these materials should be, according to the passive shipper requirements, is es-
sential to understand. Consistency is needed to assure that equipment used to condition and store gel 
packs and phase-change materials are capable of producing gel packs and phase-change materials at the 
proper temperature. The next step is to determine how long gel packs and phase-change materials need 
to stay inside the equipment to ensure the proper temperature. In most cases, it may require many days 
to condition these products depending if they are stored on a pallet, in a box, or individually. 
The goal is to make sure the materials are at the correct temperature prior to use. Using gel packs and 
phase-change materials prior to being at the proper temperature could result in failed shipments. If gel 
packs and phase-change materials are used frozen, it is imperative to determine whether or not they 
will cause irreversible damages to the payload. Gel packs and phase-change materials may freeze if 
they are come in direct contact with products affected by freezing (thermal shock and denaturing the 
product); this should have been tested during prior qualification processes. Thermal shock can occur 
unintentionally if the cold bank moves during handling and ends up touching the payload, or if the 
shipper is placed in a cold room prior to shipping. The layout and written plan should include a pro-
cess to warn users that the cold bank may require material to separate it from the payload. Introducing 
gel packs and phase-change materials at the wrong temperature into a pack-out or improperly placing 
them can result in adverse temperatures.

q	Perform a monthly review report based on a rolling 12-month period of

q	Sort the deviation percentage (by decreasing order)

q	Identify the root cause owner and root cause location with the highest incident

and create a CAPA accordingly

Case Study 3: Qualification Master Plan
The following case study is an example of how to implement the information contained in this techni-
cal report. Risk analysis and planning for its mitigation should be the first step.
To make validation terminology applicable to a good distribution practices (GDP) environment, a 
qualification master plan (QMP) is written to define the qualification strategy. For each qualification 
step (design, operational, and performance qualifications), a rationale is written to support the qualifi-
cation program according to the type (active or passive) of thermal protection system (TPS).
More specifically, when the qualification program addresses winter and summer conditions in the op-
erational qualification (OQ) and performance qualification (PQ), particular attention is given to the 
PQ done with commercial batches that can last one year or more.
Performing a TPS PQ with consecutive shipments of the product is considered a best practice, because 
it mimics the real situation and therefore confirms the representative OQ result. However, considering 
the recommendations from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration and the World Health Organiza-
tion, which advocate the PQ be completed before commencing commercial distribution of the product, 
it is necessary to document and develop a rationale to ensure the product quality used during the PQ.
After the OQ, an intermediate qualification summary report is written, based on the OQ conclu-
sion, to justify the decision to use commercial product for the PQ. The output of the OQ performed 
considering winter and summer conditions is to evaluate temperature risk during static conditions 
(thermal chamber temperature profiles) and performance of the solution in order to define the number 
of probes and locations that have to be used for routine operations. In order to demonstrate that the 
OQ results are effective and reproducible over the years, PQ field transportation tests are performed 
in real shipping conditions (dynamic condition), during which the temperature is monitored and 
documented in a PQ winter and PQ summer report. PQ will consist of replicated live transporta-
tion tests done as defined in the intermediate validation report. Based on a matrix approach, multiple 
destinations and product loads may be considered. The number of replicate(s) should be governed by 
the regulatory requirements of the specific country or region. Studies should be done in winter and 
summer conditions at the time of the year where the weather and route are challenging to assure the 
results are representative. Regional anomalies should be taken into consideration e.g., during extreme 
weather conditions; monsoon.
Another option to consider is having a continuous performance verification program in place, where 
all shipments are monitored, and the performance of a temperature-controlled shipper is verified based 
on temperature data and the number of nonconformances experienced on regular quarterly/annual 
basis. Deviation wording may need to be changed, as root cause analyses are mainly performed for 
major and trending nonconformances. All minor nonconformances, by nature, do not have product 
quality or patient safety impact and therefore do not require a root cause analysis or implementation 
of corrective and preventive actions (CAPA).
The output of the PQ is to evaluate temperature during real shipment and performance of logistics 
processes with the TPS.
Until the final Quality Assurance Summary Report is approved, in addition to the PQ shipments, real 
transportation shipments will be made based on the output from the OQ validation results. There is 
no product risk, as every shipment is temperature-monitored in a location defined by the analysis of 
the thermal mapping results obtained during the OQ phase. In addition, any temperature nonconfor-
mance will be documented. Based on the risk analysis and mitigation policy of the company, a root 
cause analysis will be conducted, for example, for major excursions or trends, to guarantee that the 
transportation system is under control.

About PDA Technical Reports
PDA Technical Reports are global consensus documents, prepared by member-driven Task Forces (listed on inside 
front cover) comprised of content experts, including scientists and engineers working in the pharmaceutical/bio-
pharmaceutical industry, regulatory authorities and academia. While in development, PDA Technical Reports are 
subjected to a global review of PDA members and other topic-specific experts, often including regulatory officials. 
Comments from the global review are then considered by the authoring Task Force during the preparation of the 
final working draft. The level of expertise of the Task Force and those participating in the global review ensure a 
broad perspective reflecting best thinking and practices currently available.
The final working draft is next reviewed by the PDA Advisory Board or Boards that sanctioned the project. PDA’s 
Advisory Boards are: Science Advisory Board, Biotechnology Advisory Board, and Regulatory Affairs and Quality 
Advisory Board. Following this stage of review, the PDA Board of Directors conducts the final review and deter-
mines whether to publish or not publish the document as an official PDA Technical Report.
While PDA goes to great lengths to ensure each Technical Report is of the highest quality, all readers are encouraged 
to contact PDA about any scientific, technical, or regulatory inaccuracies, discrepancies, or mistakes that might be 
found in any of the documents. Readers can email PDA at: TRfeedback@pda.org.
