Leak Test Methods 
The final section is a series of summaries that describe several commonly used microbial and 
physical integrity test methods. 
These methods are listed alphabetically. In each summary, 
information is included on the validatability 
of the method, the limitations that influence the 
method’s sensitivity, and whether it is useful for quantitative leakage measurement, for 
qualitative leakage detection, or for leak location. 
Critical test parameters are listed followed by 
the time required to complete the leak test, and special equipment required. The package stress 
created by the leak test is stated, along with specific product/package types that are most 
commonly tested by the given method. 
Finally, pertinent literature references from the 
document’s general reference list are given that relate to the method being summarized.

Bubble Test 
Description: The package is submerged in water or other suitable clear, colorless solvent. 
A 
vacuum is exerted on the test system, and the package is examined visually for evidence of 
gaseous leakage. Alternatively, surfactant may be added to the seal in question, a differential 
pressure is applied across the seal, and evidence of gaseous leakage is detected by visible 
inspection. 
Validation 
This method determines the presence and location of leaks 
that allow sufficient gaseous flow to permit the formation of 
visually detectable bubbles in the submersion fluid or in the 
applied surfactant. This method can be validated for the 
detection or location of larger leaks or package 
imperfections. Validation will depend on the test method 
parameters and the reliability and sensitivity of visual 
detection. 
Sensitivity 
The sensitivity of the method is based on the ability of gas to 
move out of a leak to form visible bubbles in the submersion 
fluid or applied surfactant, and the ability to identify positive 
leak controls

8.2 
Helium Mass Spectrometry 
Description: Helium is used as a tracer gas for detection and measurement of leakage across a 
package seal. Helium is detected by mass spectrometry. 
There are four ways of testing for 
helium leakage rate by mass spectrometry: Tracer Probe, Hood, Detector Probe, and Inside Out. 
Tracer Probe: The package itself is placed in-line with the mass spectrometry unit and helium 
is sprayed around the outside of the package. The mass spectrometer vacuum pump applies 
vacuum to the package and helium leakage into the package is measured by mass 
spectrometry. 
Hood Method: The package is placed in-line with the mass spectrometer and is enclosed in a 
“hood” or chamber into which helium can be introduced. The mass spectrometer vacuum 
pump applies vacuum to the package and helium leakage into the package is measured by 
mass spectrometry. 
Detector Probe: The package is flooded with helium and a detector probe connected to the 
mass spectrometer is used to sample the atmosphere around the package perimeter. 
The 
package is not under vacuum in this case. 
Inside-Out: The package is flooded with helium and subsequently placed into a test chamber 
directly connected to the mass spectrometer. Helium can be introduced into the package 
headspace by penetrating the package and flooding the headspace, by flooding the headspace 
prior to sealing the package, or by bombing, i.e. exposing the sealed package to an 
environment of pressurized helium. During the Inside-Out method the package is subjected 
to vacuum generated by the mass spectrometer.

High Voltage Leak Detection (HVLD) 
Description: Containers are exposed to a high frequency, high-voltage current near the area of the 
package to be detected for leaks. The presence of a leak will result in a flow of current above 
base noise detected by the HVLD system as a reject. 
This test method is only feasible for 
containers constructed of relatively nonconductive materials and for product that is significantly 
more conductive. The product must be at or very near any potential leaks to allow conductance 
to occur.

8.4 
Lid Deflection 
Description: To perform lid deflection tests a pressure differential is applied to a package and the 
resultant movement of the package’s flexible lidding material is monitored and used as an 
indication of the absence or presence of unacceptable levels of leakage. Lid deflection sensors 
can work by any of a variety of methods, including strain gauges for monitoring lidding 
movement, and sensors that work by contact, capacitance, inductance, electromagnetic wave 
motion, photoelectricity, and ultrasonic pulse echo. 
Validation 
Lid deflection methods can be validated for the detection of the 
absence of lidding material or for the detection of gaseous 
leaks large enough to result in minimal or no flexible lidding 
material displacement when a package is subjected to a 
reference differential pressure. Validation studies should 
examine the ability of the lid deflection instrument to detect 
control leaks as a function of instrument sensor capabilities, 
differential pressure testing conditions, package headspace, and 
package component geometry.

Challenge, Liquid Immersion 
Description: Microbial challenge testing by liquid immersion involves immersing filled package 
units into a liquid concentration of microorganisms for a specified period of time, removing the 
units, rinsing them, incubating the units and examining them for microbial growth. These units 
typically contain nutritive growth media, but may alternatively contain product, if not inhibitory 
to the growth of the challenge organism. During immersion, the packages may be exposed to 
extremes of pressure to simulate anticipated product processing or distribution conditions.

8.8 
Package Headspace Analysis 
Description: The headspace content of a package is analyzed and monitored for change over 
time. Package headspace analyses are useful for the measurement of leakage and permeation 
from packages that require a rarefied gas headspace to ensure long term product stability. 
The 
packages may be stored in a challenge environment of vacuum or concentrated rarefied gas to 
accelerate leakage/permeation. 
Validation 
This method can be validated for the measurement of gaseous 
leaks and permeation large enough to result in an unacceptable 
shift in headspace content over time. Validation studies should 
examine the ability of the analytical method to accurately 
sample and measure rarefied gas content in the package 
system. 
Sensitivity 
The sensitivity of headspace analyses is based on the ability to 
accurately sample and measure gas headspace content in the 
package system. Sensitivity can be verified using reference 
gas mixtures of known concentration. Gas headspace content 
tests can be very sensitive indicators of package leakage and 
permeation, especially after exposure of product to longer 
times or to a challenge environment designed to accelerate 
leakage and permeation.

Particulate Transmission 
Description: Package challenge testing by aerosolized dust involves placing the package units 
into a specially designed aerosolization chamber and charging the atmosphere within the 
chamber with aerosolized dust for a specified period of time. 
During the challenge test, the 
packages may be subjected to differential pressure challenges. 
The atmosphere within the 
chamber must be kept well circulated. 
After the challenge, the units are removed from the 
chamber and examined for evidence of dust ingress. 
Validation 
Particulate transmission tests determine the presence and 
location of leaks that can be penetrated by particulates. This 
method can be validated provided the particulate size is 
uniformly small, the dust concentration is sufficient and 
uniformly dispersed within the chamber, and the chamber 
circulation system settings and controls are adequate. Positive 
controls, where appropriate, should demonstrate dust ingress. 
Sensitivity 
The sensitivity of the method is based on the incidence of 
penetration of aerosolized dust into positive control package 
defects and the ability to detect such penetration.
