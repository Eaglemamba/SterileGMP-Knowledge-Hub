Residual Gas Ionization 
Description: A high voltage, high frequency field is applied to packages sealed under vacuum. 
The field causes residual headspace gas to ionize. A conducting plate located near the package 
detects the level of ionization current. Ionization above an established limit triggers rejection of 
the package. 
Validation 
The residual gas ionization method can be validated for the 
detection of insufficient vacuum in packages designed to 
maintain a minimal vacuum level. Validation studies should 
examine the ability of the ionization sensors to differentiate 
between packages containing various known levels of vacuum 
as a function of voltage, frequency, or package headspace.

Sensitivity 
The sensitivity of a residual gas ionization method is based on 
the ability to accurately detect pressure exceeding a desired 
acceptance limit in evacuated packages. Instrument sensitivity 
can be determined using control packages containing known 
pressure levels. It is possible to detect smaller leaks and 
permeation rates with this method as the time interval from 
when the package was originally evacuated to the test time is 
lengthened.

Description: Containers under pressure are placed near an ultrasonic sensor capable of detecting 
acoustic energy in the ultrasonic range of 20-100 kHz. Ultrasonic sensors will detect movement 
of air or gasses out of package leaks large enough to create turbulent gas flow. 
Smaller leaks 
may be detected by the application of a special surfactant to the seal. Bursting of surfactant 
bubbles is detected ultrasonically. There must be enough package headspace to permit detectable

8.13 
Ultrasonic Imaging 
Description: Very short ultrasound pulses are generated at frequencies up to several hundred 
MHz. These ultrasound frequencies are focused on the container seal by acoustic lenses. Once 
inside the package the ultrasonic pulses may be absorbed, scattered or reflected depending on the 
change in the material. The reflected ultrasound echoes are used to create an image that can be 
analyzed for the presence of improper sealing, delamination, gaps, or product-contaminated 
seals. Alternate ultrasonic inspection methods work by contact transmission of ultrasonic energy 
through the package seal using an array of transmitters and receivers in direct contact with the 
seal region. 
Validation 
Sensitivity 
Ultrasonic imaging methods can be validated for the detection 
and location of seal imperfections in flat thermal or adhesive 
bonded seals, for instance a pouch. Validation studies should 
examine the ability of the ultrasonic sensor to detect control 
leaks as a function of ultrasound pulse generation and 
transmission variables, instrument sensor sensitivity settings, 
transmission and detection sensor positioning, package 
materials of construction, and package geometry. Validation 
studies should reflect the range of acceptable package sealing 
parameters. 
Sensitivity of an ultrasonic imaging method is based on the 
ability to detect positive control package defects such as gaps, 
seal contaminants, package material defects, and other seal 
inconsistencies.

8.14 
Vacuum/Pressure 
Decay 
Description: A pressure differential is applied to a package and the change in pressure or vacuum 
is measured either inside the package itself using a test fixture, or outside in a sealed package test 
chamber. 
The change in test chamber pressure or vacuum can be determined by absolute 
pressure measurement, or it can be determined using a differential pressure manometer in which 
the test chamber pressure change is compared to that of a control package. 
Validation 
Vacuum/pressure decay methods can be validated for the 
detection or measurement of gaseous leaks large enough to 
allow a measurable change in pressure in the package or the 
test chamber over the duration of the test. Validation studies 
should examine the ability of the vacuum/pressure decay 
instrument to detect control leaks as a function of test chamber 
geometry, instrument sensor capabilities, differential pressure 
testing conditions, test time, and environmental temperature 
conditions. 
Sensitivity 
Sensitivity of the vacuum/pressure decay method is based on 
the ability to detect and/or measure a change in pressure or 
vacuum resulting from gaseous leaks. Instrument sensitivity 
can be verified using positive control package defects or 
gaseous reference leaks. Test method sensitivity is greatly 
influenced by a number of factors. These include those 
mentioned under validation above as well as the critical 
parameters listed below. Generally, pressure decay is 
considered a more sensitive test that vacuum rise, and 
differential pressure is potentially more sensitive than absolute 
pressure sensors.

Description: The package is examined visually by human or by electronic inspection devices for 
evidence of product leakage. The test may be accompanied by additional package challenges of

Validation

location of leaks penetrated by the contained product. This 
method can be validated for the detection of larger leaks or 
package imperfections. Validation will depend on the 
reliability and sensitivity of the mode of leakage detection. 
Sensitivity

product migration through positive control package defects and 
the ability to detect such migration.

Volumetric 
Gas Flow 
Description: A pressure differential is applied to a package and the outward flow rate of gas is 
measured. 
Validation 
Volumetric gas flow methods can be validated for the 
measurement of gaseous leaks large enough to allow a 
measurable flow of gas out of the package over the duration of 
the test. Validation studies should examine the ability of the 
volumetric flow rate instrument to measure control leaks as a 
function of instrument sensor capabilities, differential pressure 
testing conditions, and test time. 
Sensitivity 
The sensitivity of volumetric gas flow methods is based on the 
ability to accurately measure gaseous flow out of a package 
under positive pressure. Instrument sensitivity can be verified 
using gaseous reference leaks or positive control package 
defects. Test method sensitivity is influenced primarily by 
flow meter accuracy, test time, and the differential pressure 
applied to the package. Generally, volumetric gas flow is not 
as sensitive a test as vacuum / pressure decay methods.

Weight Loss/Gain 
Description: The container is filled with either a volatile liquid or solid, or a hygroscopic 
material such as a desiccant. The container is stored’ in an environment intended to promote 
migration of the substances out of the package containing volatile materials, or to promote 
migration of water vapor into the package containing hygroscopic materials. 
Leakage plus 
permeation are measured in units of package weight loss or weight gain. For greater accuracy, it 
is recommended, although not required, that control packages filled with inert materials be tested 
at the same time as the test units. Each time the test units are weighed, the control packages’ 
weight change should be subtracted from the change in test unit weights. 
Validation 
Weight loss or weight gain methods can be validated for the 
measurement of leaks of a wide variety of sizes, as well as for 
permeation measurement. Validation will depend on the test 
method parameters and the sensitivity of the balance used to 
monitor weight change. Acceptance limits for weight loss or 
gain are generally established as a function of the product- 
package.system.
