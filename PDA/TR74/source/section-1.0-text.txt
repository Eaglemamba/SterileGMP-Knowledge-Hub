1.0 Introduction 
Reprocessing and reworking are tools that biological manufacturers can use when a manufacturing process 
step has operated outside of its validated operating ranges. For reprocessing, rather than proceeding with the 
next manufacturing process step, the product is rerun through the previous manufacturing process step 
operated within the acceptable operating ranges. For reworking, the product is subjected to one or more 
processing steps that are different from the established manufacturing process to obtain acceptable product. 
In biological manufacturing, however, reprocessing, and especially reworking, should be considered only in 
exceptional circumstances. Reworking can be considered during events such as critical equipment failures, 
out of specifications of critical quality attributes of drug substance or drug product, and/or deviation from in-
process controls that impact quality of the drug substance or drug product (further conditions to consider are 
stated in Section 5.0).  
Reprocessing and reworking are deviations that are managed within the corrective and preventive actions 
(CAPA) of the manufacturer’s quality management system. An essential prerequisite for the acceptance of a 
reprocessing or reworking step is a clear indication of the root cause. Due to the size and complexity of 
biological products, including the limitations in testing methods (especially measurement of functional 
biological activity, i.e., potency), reprocessing, and especially reworking, are limited for biologicals. Thus, 
they should be entered into with caution so as not to impact the product’s critical quality attributes (CQA), 
and the costs and risks should be carefully evaluated and documented. 
Effective reprocessing and reworking strategies should integrate supply chain resilience considerations, 
including forecasting, inventory buffers, and manufacturing scheduling. Combining quality risk 
management assessments (e.g., a FMEA assessment) with supply risk scoring can support balanced 
decisions that protect both compliance and patient access. 
Reprocessing and reworking also require regulatory authority approval before the manufactured product can 
be released for human use. In limited cases, regulatory authority approval for reprocessing can be acquired 
ahead of time by presenting a comprehensive change management plan (proactive approach detailed in 
Section 3.6). This plan should explain the reason for reprocessing, such as refiltration or reconcentration due 
to technical failures or mechanical breakdown of a chromatography column (1). It must also specify the 
frequency and nature of the reprocessing procedures for potential events, as well as detail the steps for 
validation and ensuring product comparability. 
1.1 Purpose 
PDA Technical Report No. 74 (Revised 2026): Reprocessing and Reworking of Biologicals (TR 74) presents 
a strategy based on the experiences and practices of a PDA Technical Report Team representing a cross-
section of industry professionals. The intent is to provide guidance on the design, development, controls,

The information discussed represents what the authors consider a balance of scientific, regulatory, and

1.2 Scope 
The previous version of TR 74 included only such biopharmaceutical products as recombinant proteins and 
polypeptides. Because the principles of reprocessing and reworking are applicable across a wider array of 
manufacturing processes, the scope has been expanded to include guidance on biological products. In 
addition to recombinant proteins and monoclonal antibodies (both as innovators/novel and biosimilars), 
biological products (2-4) encompass a wide range of other products such as vaccines and blood-derived 
proteins.  
The quality of biologicals is defined by the chosen production and manufacturing process. Minor changes in 
the process can affect the quality of the drug product; therefore, the development of the manufacturing 
process is of paramount importance for biologicals, including the implementation of any reprocessing or 
reworking. Incorporating the topic of reworking further expands the scope of this technical report, but 
because the reworking of biologicals is far more challenging than reprocessing, the authors emphasize that 
extra caution should be taken. 
The guidance in this technical report is general in nature, and the case studies illustrate how the general 
principles may be applied. No attempt is made to encompass all types of unit operations for which 
reprocessing or reworking could be considered.  
This technical report is not intended to establish mandatory standards but serves as a single-source overview 
that complements existing guidance documents listed in Section 8.0.  
The principles discussed in TR 74 are applicable to both clinical and commercial manufacturing processes, 
but the authors recognize that reprocessing and reworking during the clinical manufacturing of biologicals is 
often more challenging than during commercial manufacturing. This is primarily because the understanding 
of the product and the process is very limited, so extra caution is emphasized.
