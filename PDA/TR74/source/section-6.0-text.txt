6.0 Appendix I: Hypothetical Case Study (Reprocessing 
Framework) — Anion Exchange Chromatography) 
Section 6.0 (Appendix I) focuses on the design and implementation considerations for a reprocessing step. 
Adapted from the published monoclonal antibody manufacturing (mAb) process, A-Mab: A Case Study in 
Bioprocess Development, for this case study, a reprocessing framework has been created using an anion 
exchange chromatography (AEX) step (19). This reprocessing framework is intentionally designed to 
require a more comprehensive analysis than a simpler reprocessing step, such as refiltration described in 
Section 7.0. 
Reprocessing of a major unit operation, such as an AEX chromatography column, requires a detailed 
assessment, significant resources, planning, and commitment. Simpler unit operations require a subset of the 
planning and activities outlined here. Regardless, quality management systems, regulatory requirements, and 
a comprehensive process understanding should drive the overall design of the reprocessing procedure. 
6.1 Unit Operation Characteristics 
Chromatography is used to purify a product (e.g., mAb, target proteins, compounds) considering the 
properties of the target solute, feed stream, required purity level, productivity, costs, process sequence, 
process parameters, and implementation of the designed purification process. Biomolecules are purified 
using chromatography techniques that separate them according to the difference in their specific properties 
as summarized in Table 6.1-1. In ion exchange chromatography (IEX), biomolecules are separated 
according to the difference in their net surface charges.

The overall goal of a chromatographic step is to remove impurities, such as host-cell-derived proteins 
(HCP), nucleic acids, carbohydrates, and other undesired compounds.  
In general, a full-scale chromatography system will consist of the following components: 
• 
Buffer and Product Reservoirs: Containers (hard-piped or single use disposables) used to 
supply the buffers, product feed, and for elution collection required during the chromatography 
operation.  
• 
Pumps: Peristaltic pumps used to feed the buffers into chromatography columns. Depending 
on scale, other types of pumps may be utilized considering process requirements and equipment 
design.  
• 
Column: The chamber where the chromatographic media is loaded. The size (diameter and 
height) of the column will be defined based on the process requirements. The material of 
construction (e.g., glass, polycarbonate, steel) is selected based on process conditions (e.g., 
pressure and flow rates). 
• 
Resin: Chromatography media intended for the purification process. The packaging of the 
columns is commonly conducted by creating a slurry where the particles are suspended, that is, 
poured into the column.  
• 
Valves: Sanitary systems used to control the flow of materials during the operation of the 
chromatography system. These may be manual or pneumatic units.  
• 
Sensors: Applications intended to monitor process parameters required for the chromatography 
process. These may include pH, air, temperature, flow, pressure, and UV monitor. 
• 
Controller/Computer: Used to control and monitor the operation of the system. 
The purification strategy involving a chromatography step is based on the characteristics (e.g., properties 
and stability) of the product under different conditions, final specifications, feed properties (e.g., 
concentration of product and impurities), nature of the process (e.g., continuous or discontinuous), and 
nature of the substances (e.g., hazardous). When designing the reprocessing step for the AEX 
chromatography case study, consideration was given to the quality attributes for mAbs listed in Table 6.1-2 
(19).

6.2.1 Column Packing 
The resin utilized for chromatography is loaded into the column in a controlled manner to ensure a uniform 
distribution while preventing the formation of channels that will affect the performance of the unit 
operation. Commonly, a slurry containing resin in a process buffer is poured into the column. Once the resin 
is settled, the integrity of the packaged column is confirmed. In the case of reprocessing, the integrity of the 
column bed should be verified and confirmed prior to its use. Any issue affecting the bed should be solved 
prior to reprocessing, considering resuspension and repacking of the resin if applicable. 
6.2.2 Cleaning and Sanitization 
Full cleaning and sanitization of the column must be conducted in preparation for reprocessing. Cleaning 
and sanitization will follow existing procedures and practices, including the regeneration of the resin. (These 
elements are discussed further in (68, 69).) 
6.2.3 Performance 
Performance of the chromatography step must be monitored according to the routine established procedures 
and must satisfy predefined acceptance criteria. The criteria should include, but not be limited to, the 
operational conditions (e.g., asymmetry, height equivalent to the theoretical plate, and/or transition analysis) 
and quality attributes (65).

6.2.4 Regeneration and Lifecycle 
The resin must be regenerated consistent with established procedures and must satisfy the acceptance 
criteria defined and validated. The use of the resin for a reprocessing activity will be within the validated 
number of reuses if the reprocessing activity is not associated with a malfunction of the chromatography 
column and resin. 
6.2.5 Validation and Filings 
Validation of reprocessing will be performed under a prospective protocol. Detailed guidance for validation 
activities of chromatography processes for the purification of proteins is provided in PDA TR 14 (68). In the 
event that changes are required to an existing filing, guidance is provided in PDA Technical Report No. 38: 
Manufacturing Chromatography Systems Post-Approval Changes (ChromPAC): Chemistry, Manufacturing, 
and Controls Documentation. 
In this example, a protocol for prospective validation of reprocessing at the manufacturing scale was written 
and approved; however, the potential of a concurrent validation could be considered if the frequency for 
reprocessing did not justify a prospective protocol. The protocol required a single GMP run at 
manufacturing scale, including all routine in-process and release testing as well as additional nonroutine 
testing, for example, analysis of HCP, acidic variants, aggregate levels, fragmentation, oxidation, and 
potency in reprocessed material. The protocol also required placing the resulting drug substance in the 
stability program. The protocol acceptance criteria for nonroutine testing were based on the comparability 
criteria established in Section 6.4.3 and demonstrated at small-scale. (See Section 6.5.6.)  
Yet, a full prospective validation approach may not be feasible or practical for reprocessing in all cases. 
Instead, a concurrent validation strategy may be more appropriate, allowing the manufacture and testing of 
products and only reprocessing when and if necessary. In the case of concurrent validation, the acceptance 
criteria shall be aligned with the approved specifications for the process. 
The initial BLA to the FDA and MAA to the EMA for this new biologic included a complete manufacturing 
process development section for the AEX unit operation within Section 3.2.S.2.6 of the CTD. A 
comparability protocol was provided post-licensure to gain agreement with FDA on the implementation 
strategy and to request a reduction in reporting requirements from a PAS to a CBE-30 (i.e., documentation 
of changes being effected, 30-day response from FDA required). In the EU MAA, a Change Management 
Protocol was included that also outlined the implementation strategy to seek a reduction in reporting 
requirements from a Type II Variation to a Type IB Variation upon successful completion of the protocol.

The FMEA risk assessment for the A-Mab manufacturing process is illustrated in Table 6.3.1-2 and only 
concerns the purification steps.  
The AEX chromatography step was operated in the flow-through mode, binding impurities such as DNA, 
HCP, endotoxin, and viruses to the resin while the antibody passed through. In addition, this step was 
designed to reduce levels of aggregate and acidic variants. Within the operating parameters of the AEX step, 
in this example, high levels of HCP in the column effluent are possible on rare occasions, and therefore, 
HCP is the quality attribute most likely to trigger reprocessing.  
The risk assessment identified the AEX column (Table 6.3.1-2) as the step with the highest RPN. 
Development data indicated that the risk of high HCP levels was an inherent process risk and a rare event 
attributable to multiple, interacting sources of variability in the unit operation. Thus, the conclusion was that 
the AEX unit operation was a candidate for reprocessing. 
The assigned scores are multiplied together (S x O x D = RPN) to determine the risk priority number (RPN). 
For example, for the “anion exchange chromatography” reprocessing step in Table 6.3.1-2, S x O x D = 5 x 
5 x 5 = 125.

to account for assay variability and process drift, enabling corrective actions before reaching the <100 
ng/mg action limit.

6.3.2 Business Case and Project Charter 
Three options were considered in assessing the business case for reprocessing at the AEX step:  
1. Discarding the batch upon failure to meet the HCP in-process control requirement of 
≤80 ng/mg  
2. Redesigning the AEX step or adding additional steps  
3. Reprocessing during the AEX step 
From a business perspective, it was confirmed that all three options could be managed to ensure acceptable 
product quality at release, but Option 3 was the most cost-effective and could be designed to meet quality 
and regulatory requirements. A project charter was created, and a cross-functional team was formed to 
develop a reprocessing step.

6.4.3 Comparability Criteria 
For laboratory-scale models, the intermediate quality attributes had to remain consistent with AEX eluate 
that had not been reprocessed, with the exception of HCP, which was expected to be reduced by the 
reprocessing event. 
For manufacturing-scale studies, all drug substance release specifications continued to be met. Additionally, 
the intermediate quality attributes remained consistent with AEX eluate that had not been reprocessed, with 
the exception of HCP, which was expected to be reduced by the reprocessing event. 
Drug substance from the first reprocessed batch was placed on the established real-time and accelerated 
stability protocols. It was determined that evaluation of the resulting drug product stability was not required.

6.5.2 Reprocessing Triggers 
Based on a review of the development history and the prior risk assessment, an HCP critical in-process test 
following the AEX step was designed and implemented to serve as the trigger for an investigation (i.e., if 
HCP is >100 ng/mg). The test results were available within eight (8) hours of sampling. The requirement for 
this additional eight-hour hold time was considered when designing the reprocessing step, as described in 
Section 6.4.3 and Sections 6.5.6-6.5.9. The introduction of this IPC test was a permanent process change 
that affected all production batches and not just the reprocessed batches. The investigation procedure was 
defined in an SOP. If the investigation confirmed the positive result, then the reprocessing step would be 
initiated.

6.5.3 Reprocessing Design  
The design of the AEX reprocessing step took into consideration the issues described in Section 6.3.1, 
Section 6.3.2, Section 6.5.1, and Section 6.5.2. Since the step operated in flow-through mode, the AEX 
eluate was considered to be suitable as a feed-stream for AEX reprocessing without any adjustments. Prior 
development studies reviewed in Section 6.5.1 showed that all ranges for feed-stream conditions expected 
for the reprocessing operation were evaluated and shown to produce drug substance with acceptable quality. 
In addition, studies for the reprocessing step demonstrated that the AEX column—after being stripped, 
cleaned, and re-equilibrated—would readily remove HCP to well below acceptable levels. All other aspects 
of the design of the reprocessing step, including operating conditions, buffer solution, equipment sizes, and 
others were similar to the design of the original AEX step. 
6.5.4 Design Impact Assessment  
After completing the design of the reprocessing step, the broader impact of the design on manufacturing 
operations was assessed. Since the AEX is a flow-through step, no adjustment to the buffer composition was 
required. The protein concentration was expected to be 30% lower than the original feed-stream but still 
within acceptable limits. The AEX load concentration is not considered a CPP. 
A summary of the design impact assessment is included in Table 6.4.1-1 in the “Other” category. The two 
most significant areas of concern identified were Buffer/Solution Supply and Equipment Cleaning. The 
maximum hold time for AEX eluate had to be increased by 48 hours to allow for completion of the HCP 
IPC test and to complete preparation of equipment and buffers in the event that the reprocessing trigger 
occurred. An evaluation of the manufacturing-scale microbial hold studies supported the longer hold time.

Based on the AEX reprocessing risk assessment in Section 6.5.5 (Table 6.4.1-1 and Table 6.5.5-1),

− Consider reprocessing the material through the step twice (i.e., double-reprocessing) as a

− Verify that the use of reprocessed AEX eluate did not have an impact on virus removal LRF

− Evaluate the need for this viral-clearance study based on development data encompassing 
relevant process conditions.  
6.5.9 Development Data Assessment  
The data from the development studies outlined in Section 6.5.8 were evaluated to determine whether 
product quality and process performance of the newly designed reprocessing step met all the comparability 
criteria described in Section 6.4.3. 
6.5.9.1 Intermediate Hold-Time Study  
Based on the design impact evaluation in Section 6.5.4, it was determined that the maximum hold time for 
AEX eluate had to be increased by 48 hours to allow for completion of the HCP IPC test and to complete 
preparation of equipment and buffers from the end of the first AEX elution. The original PAR for ambient 
hold time was ≤72 hours. A study was therefore performed to extend this ambient hold-time PAR to ≤120 
hours. The resulting evaluation of the relevant intermediate quality attributes (identified in Section 6.5.2) 
showed that the PAR extension to ≤120 hours was acceptable. No additional CPPs were identified as a 
result of this study. 
6.5.9.2 Reprocessing at Laboratory Scale  
AEX eluate derived from the pilot run described in Section 6.5.7 was collected and tested for all relevant 
intermediate quality attributes (identified in Section 6.4.2). This intermediate material was required to have 
acceptable levels of all relevant quality attributes, except for HCP, which was manipulated as described in 
Section 6.5.7 to have higher than normal levels of HCP.  
The intermediate material was then reprocessed twice through the qualified reduced-scale AEX step with no 
manipulation of the process intermediate between runs. This double-reprocessed material was used as it 
theoretically represents a worst-case scenario and provides stronger evidence that the reprocessing has no

impact on product quality. The reprocessing runs were performed under target conditions each time, and 
samples for quality attribute testing were collected after each AEX run. The double-reprocessing of the 
laboratory-scale AEX eluate was performed in duplicate (four reduced-scale AEX runs in total). The test 
results from each run (i.e., from the original pilot run AEX eluate, from the single-reprocessed AEX eluates, 
and from the double-reprocessed AEX eluates) were compared. The acceptance criteria for the testing 
included demonstrating the reprocessed AEX eluate quality attributes were consistent with nonreprocessed 
material, except for the determined levels of HCP. HCP levels were required to meet drug substance release 
limits after the first reprocessing runs. These acceptance criteria were met. No additional CPPs were 
identified as a result of this study.  
6.5.9.3 Virus Removal Verification  
Since the reprocessed AEX eluate could impact subsequent viral removal steps, an evaluation of the 
performance of the reprocessing step for potential impact on the subsequent virus removal filter was 
performed. It was found that the minor changes to the virus filter step load pH, conductivity, and product 
concentration were within the originally studied virus filter step parameter ranges. Therefore, no additional 
virus removal studies were required for the implementation of this AEX reprocessing step.  
6.5.10 Finalize Design  
Based on the outcomes of laboratory-scale development, the proposed manufacturing-scale procedures were 
finalized. This included creation of an SOP describing reprocessing, an SOP describing the new HCP IPC 
test, and creation of a batch record to be used in the event of a GMP reprocessing step. No operating ranges 
were changed except for the maximum AEX eluate hold time at ambient temperature, which was extended 
from 72 to 120 hours.  
6.5.11 Transfer to Manufacturing  
A technology transfer project team, including all relevant stakeholders, was formed. All standard company 
guidelines and procedures for technology transfer were followed. As part of the technology transfer project, 
one intermediate pilot-scale run was executed by manufacturing personnel under draft procedures and batch 
records. A technology transfer report was written and approved, and all relevant staff was formally trained 
on the new procedures. 
6.6 Conclusions  
Reprocessing activities involving AEX is a feasible option during routine manufacturing. However, proper 
documentation aligned with GMPs is required to demonstrate the controls in place and effectiveness of the 
step. Among the considerations for reprocessing are: 
• 
Prospective or concurrent validation protocol  
• 
Clear rationale for reprocessing (e.g., reason for reprocessing) 
• 
Proper management of the column and resin 
• 
Proper management of the intermediate material requiring reprocessing 
• 
Proper documentation (e.g., procedures and batch records in place)
