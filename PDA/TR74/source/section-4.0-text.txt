Guidelines published by ICH contain harmonized guidance specifically for to-be-marketed and marketed 
pharmaceuticals, including biologicals, specifically for submissions involving reprocessing of biologicals. 
Some of the common characteristics harmonized to enable efficient reprocessing submissions include: 
• 
To-Be-Marketed: Initial market application to contain details of any anticipated future 
reprocessing in the relevant sections of the Common Technical Document (CTD) referred to as 
a Post-Approval Change Management Protocol (PACMP). Allowing this inclusion of 
reprocessing protocols for relevant steps provides reprocessing flexibility and minimizes post-
approval reporting.  
• 
Marketed: Post-market clear guidance and well-defined timeframes for review and approval of 
Post-Approval Changes (PAC), as well as PACMPs  
• 
Acceptance of science- and risk-based approaches for developing data to support PACs and 
PACMPs  
• 
Relatively fewer requests for additional documentation not included in CTD Module 3

Before a decision is taken to rework batches that do not conform to established 
standards or specifications, an investigation into the reason for non-conformance 
should be performed.  
Batches that have been reworked should be subjected to appropriate evaluation, 
testing, stability testing if warranted, and documentation to show that the 
reworked product is of equivalent quality to that produced by the original process. 
Concurrent validation is often the appropriate validation approach for rework 
procedures. This allows a protocol to define the rework procedure, how it will be 
carried out, and the expected results. If there is only one batch to be reworked, 
then a report can be written and the batch released once it is found to be 
acceptable.  
Procedures should provide for comparing the impurity profile of each reworked 
batch against batches manufactured by the established process. Where routine 
analytical methods are inadequate to characterize the reworked batch, additional 
methods should be used. 
ICH Q7: Questions 
and Answers (9) 
Does the definition of expiry date in ICH Q7 preclude the rework or reprocess of 
an expired API? 
According to the definition, material should not be used after the expiry date. The 
original intent of this definition in ICH Q7 was that expired API should not be used 
in drug product formulation.  
It may be acceptable to reprocess or rework the expired API where the API 
manufacturer has all related historical GMP documentation and additional stability 
data on the reworked or reprocessed API. There may be registration/filing 
considerations that are beyond the scope of ICH Q7 in addition to the GMP 
considerations.

4.3 Specific Guidance from the United States (FDA) 
In addition to implementation of the ICH Guidelines, the FDA provides general guidance for biologicals 
during the clinical investigation period and specific guidance for biologicals during the post-market 
approval period. 
4.3.1 During Clinical Investigation Period 
In 2008, FDA published Guidance for Industry: CGMP for Phase 1 Investigational Drugs that stressed the 
importance of documenting how the manufacturing process was carried out during the clinical 
investigational period (29): 
• 
A record of manufacturing data that details the materials, equipment, procedures used, and any 
problems encountered during manufacturing. We recommend that manufacturers retain records 
sufficient to replicate the manufacturing process. Similarly, if the manufacture of a phase 1 
investigational drug batch is initiated but not completed, we recommend that the record include 
an explanation of why manufacturing was terminated.  
• 
A record of changes in procedures and processes used for subsequent batches along with the 
rationale for any changes. 
Since reprocessing and/or reworking are significant changes, they are required to be reported to the FDA 
under an information amendment to the Investigational New Drug (IND) Application on file to carry out the 
human clinical trials (21 CFR 312.31). Although there is no required time period of delay of implementation 
for these information amendments, manufacturers typically wait for a period of time to allow the FDA 
reviewers opportunity to review the amendment (30). 
Note: Consider the impact of changes to be discussed with regulatory authorities in order to determine the 
need for additional toxicology studies. 
FDA in 2020 published Guidance for Industry: Chemistry, Manufacturing, and Control (CMC) Information 
for Human Gene Therapy Investigational New Drug Applications (INDs), the scope of which included all 
gene therapies, including ex-vivo genetically modified human cells. There is no mention of reworking in 
this guidance; however, it provides the following guidance for reprocessing (31): 
• 
Drug Substance: For early stage INDs, there may be differences between the manufacturing 
and testing of the toxicology lots and the material you plan to use in the clinical studies. For 
later stage INDs, there may be changes to the manufacturing process as part of process 
development, optimization, or under certain conditions there may be reprocessing step(s). In 
both situations, we recommend that you describe how manufacturing differences are expected 
to impact product safety and activity and to provide batch analysis information in the “Batch 
Analysis (3.2.S.4.4)” section of the CTD. 
• 
Drug Product: Under certain conditions, repeating sterile filtration (if applicable) may be 
critical. We recommend that you clearly describe the conditions and justifications for each 
reprocessing procedure (32) and demonstrate product consistency between reprocessed lots and 
normal production lots.

Changes that have a minimal potential to have an adverse effect on product quality (i.e., minor 
changes) shall be documented by the applicant in an annual report (21 CFR 601.12(d)).

potential to adversely affect product quality, safety, or effectiveness (34).

4.4 Specific Guidance from the European Union (EMA) 
In addition to implementation of the ICH Guidelines, EMA provides specific guidance for biologicals both 
during the clinical investigation period and the post-market approval period. 
4.4.1 During Clinical Investigation Period 
EMA in 2022 published the Guideline on the Requirements for Quality Documentation Concerning 
Biological Investigational Medicinal Products in Clinical Trials – Revision 2. Included in the scope were 
proteins and polypeptides (produced from recombinant and nonrecombinant cell-culture expression 
systems), their derivatives and products of which they are components (e.g., conjugates), and auxiliary 
medicinal products containing these proteins and polypeptides as active substances. There is no mention of 
reworking in this guideline; however, the following guidance is provided for reprocessing of these 
biological manufacturing processes (35): 
• 
Drug Substance: Any reprocessing during manufacture of the active substance (e.g. filter 
integrity test failure) should be described and justified. Reprocessing could be considered in 
exceptional circumstances. For biological products, these situations are usually restricted to 
certain refiltration and re-concentration steps upon technical failure of equipment or mechanical 
breakdown of a chromatography column. 
• 
Drug Product: Reprocessing may be acceptable for particular manufacturing steps (e.g. 
refiltration) only if the steps are adequately described and appropriately justified.

In 2025, EMA published the Guideline on Quality, Non-Clinical and Clinical Requirements for 
Investigational Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products in Clinical Trials the scope of which includes gene 
therapy medicinal products, somatic cell therapy medicinal products, tissue-engineered products, and 
combined ATMPs. There is no mention of reworking in this guideline; however, the following guidance is 
provided for reprocessing of these biological manufacturing processes (36): 
• 
Drug Substance: Any reprocessing during manufacture of the active substance (e.g., filter 
integrity test failure) should be described and justified. Reprocessing could only be considered 
in exceptional circumstances. These situations are usually restricted to refiltration and re-
concentration steps upon technical failure of equipment or mechanical breakdown of a 
chromatography column. 
• 
Drug Product: Reprocessing may be acceptable for particular manufacturing steps (e.g. 
refiltration) only if the steps are adequately described and appropriately justified. 
4.4.2 Post-Market Approval Period 
Under the EMA Variation Regulation, each post-approval change in the production process established in 
the approved Marketing Authorisation Application must be reported using the submission type associated 
with one of the three tier-based reporting categories. The category assigned depends on the potential (i.e., 
risk) of the change to have an adverse effect on the identity, strength, quality, purity, or potency of the 
product as they may relate to the safety or effectiveness of the product (i.e., a substantial potential, moderate 
potential, or minimal potential). The reporting categories in this regulatory provision also specify when the 
product made using the change can be distributed. The submission types and associated reporting categories 
are as follows (37):  
• 
Type II Major Variation: Change must be approved by EMA prior to distribution of the 
product manufactured using the change 
• 
Type IB Minor Variation: Change must wait 30 days after submission to EMA 
• 
Type IA Minor Variation: Change must be submitted within 12 months from the date of 
implementation 
There is no mention of reworking in the Variation Regulation. Table 4.4.2-1 provides guidance for

4.5 Other Guidance 
Biological manufacturers wish to operate across many geographical areas, yet not all countries participate in 
ICH (e.g., Russia). Keeping track of each country’s or region’s CMC regulatory compliance review 
procedures and oversite for reprocessing and reworking of biologicals has proven challenging.  
Various specialized government agencies and industry associations, such as those listed here, provide 
recommendations on effective control and safety when considering reprocessing and/or reworking of 
biological manufacturing processes:  
• 
World Health Organization (WHO) is a United Nations agency to support health around the 
world, including the safety of manufactured drugs 
• 
Pharmaceutical Inspection Co-Operation Scheme (PIC/S) is an informal, nonbinding 
cooperative arrangement between regulatory authorities in the field of GMPs for medicinal 
products 
• 
BioPhorum is a collaboration of industry leaders and subject matter experts working together to 
pool knowledge, best practices, and ideas on global biopharmaceutical industry practices 
• 
Parenteral Drug Association (PDA) is the leading global provider of science, technology, and 
regulatory information detailed in technical reports and points-to-consider documents  
Overall, carrying out reprocessing or reworking according to the principles indicated in the ICH guidelines 
and the FDA and EMA guidances is generally a safe approach. However, if there is any doubt about 
proceeding with reprocessing or reworking a biological manufacturing process, contact the respective 
regulatory authority for specific guidance.  
Note: If reprocessing or reworking has not been explicitly mentioned in the manufacturer’s submitted 
documentation on the manufacturing process, correctly assume that it has not yet been approved.
