3.0 Reprocessing Considerations 
Reprocessing (3, 9, 11-15), or repetition of an existing biopharmaceutical unit operation, requires a well-
designed process, supportive data, and validation to augment the existing manufacturing process. 
Importantly, the supportive data must provide the manufacturer with a high degree of confidence 
(assurance) that the drug substance or drug product produced by reprocessing meets the predefined quality 
standards for the drug substance or drug product and is comparable to a nonreprocessed batch. The 
reprocessed and reworked biological drug substances and sterile drug products should adhere to all CQAs 
associated (including bioburden and endotoxins) to normal batch disposition and release. Enhanced testing 
protocols are often required to assess the quality attributes of the reprocessed material, ensuring that it 
remains consistent with the established manufacturing process. 
Reprocessing in pharmaceutical manufacturing can occur under various scenarios that can be broadly 
categorized as proactive and reactive approaches. 
In GMP, a risk-based approach (6), to reprocessing is incorporated by systematically assessing and 
managing potential risks associated with reactive reprocessing activities. This begins with identifying and 
evaluating the nature of deviations and their potential impact on product quality and patient safety. By 
prioritizing areas of greatest concern, manufacturers can focus resources and controls where they are most 
needed. Decisions regarding reprocessing are made based on these risk assessments, ensuring that only safe 
and effective practices are implemented. 
For any event that requires reprocessing, the manufacturer must perform a formal root-cause investigation of 
the unit operation excursion if the excursion if not within the design space. Additional development studies 
may be required to supplement the root-cause investigation. Consideration of any proposed reprocessing by 
regulators will likely be based on the root-cause analysis and the impact of reprocessing on product quality. 
3.1 Proactive Approach 
In a proactive scenario, the potential need for reprocessing is identified in advance through predetermined 
criteria. The execution of the reprocessing activities occurs through a predefined concurrent validation 
protocol with clear acceptance criteria or through previously validated procedures may be reviewed and 
approved by applicable regulatory agencies. This approach includes: 
1. Predefined Criteria: Manufacturers outline specific conditions or triggers that would 
necessitate reprocessing, allowing for a structured response to an event that may require 
reprocessing. Post reprocessing, there should be sufficient data to support a conclusion that 
any given reprocessed batch of a drug substance or drug product is uniform and meets the 
defined acceptance criteria.

2. Validated Procedures: Reprocessing is executed according to a validated procedure that 
has undergone detailed review by the manufacturer’s quality unit. This concurrent 
validation ensures that the process is capable of consistently producing a product that meets 
established quality standards. The validation protocol should include the scope of the 
manufacturing process that is being reprocessed, number of batches required, tests to be 
performed, and acceptance criteria. The proposed acceptance criteria for all tests should be 
appropriately justified. The concurrent validation approach should be described within the 
Validation Master Plan. An example of reprocessing that might be specifically covered by a 
Biological License Application (BLA) would be repeating viral filtration following a failed 
filter-integrity test. Because viral filters occasionally fail, manufacturers may be approved 
for refiltration of the protein drug substance or drug product (following successful 
validation). The approach to validation should be consistent with a lifecycle approach to 
validation, as discussed in the U.S. Food and Drug Administration Guidance for Industry: 
Process Validation: General Principles and Practices and PDA Technical Report No. 60 
(Revised 2026) Process Validation: A Lifecycle Approach (10). 
When reprocessing has been prospectively validated, it is appropriate to provide the full 
validation protocol and report in a regulatory submission and to follow the validated 
process where indicated. When reprocessing is anticipated to undergo concurrent 
validation, the protocolinclusive of initial starting conditions and appropriate acceptance 
criteria to determine whether successful completion of a reprocessing step has been 
achievedshould be provided. 
3. Regulatory Guidance derived from Established Protocols: An essential prerequisite 
for the acceptance of a reprocessing step is a clear identification of the root cause. For 
biological products, these situations are usually restricted to some refiltration, 
reconcentration steps upon technical failure of equipment, or mechanical breakdown of a 
chromatography column. These steps should be appropriately described, validated, and 
included in the regulatory submission. Such documentation should include the description 
of conditions for which reprocessing could be applied and a demonstration that the 
reprocessing step(s) does not impact the quality of the drug substance and/or drug product. 
The sequence of steps detailed in Section 3.6 provides a roadmap for implementing 
proactive reprocessing, ensuring that all necessary evaluations and approvals are addressed 
before action is taken. 
3.2 Reactive Approach 
Reactive reprocessing occurs in response to unforeseen circumstances during the manufacturing process. 
The same categories of data and decisions required for a proactive reprocessing approach apply to this 
approach, except that less time and information may be available for performing assessments. As a result, 
reactive reprocessing is better suited for steps that are simple (such as microbial or virus filtration after 
failure of filter-integrity testing) and have a low likelihood of affecting product quality. The sequence of 
steps discussed in Section 3.6 may be used to guide the approach to reactive reprocessing, starting with the 
design of a reprocessing unit operation (see Section 3.6, Step 6).

Two distinct reactive reprocessing scenarios are described as follows: 
1. Nonprospectively Validated Reprocessing at an In-Process Hold Point 
a. In this scenario, a need arises for a nonprospectively validated reprocessing unit 
operation while a product is held at an in-process stage. This may involve scenarios 
such as freezing the product for an extended period. 
b. During the hold period, manufacturers can develop and execute a plan to validate 
the reprocessing step. This provides assurance that the product meets all the 
required quality attributes subsequent to the product concurrent release. 
2. Immediate Reprocessing During a Manufacturing Run 
a. This scenario occurs when a reprocessing need emerges unexpectedly during a 
manufacturing run. In such cases, the reprocessing step may need to be 
implemented before its validation. 
b. If a rapid design and implementation of the reprocessing option is feasible, it may 
be executed "at risk." This means that the reprocessing can occur with the 
understanding that supportive data must be generated and reviewed to ensure 
product quality before the batch is released to the market. 
Of the two reactive reprocessing scenarios described above, the second scenario (#2) carries the highest 
degree of risk and is likely to be implemented only with relatively simple unit operations that affect a 
limited number of CQAs, such as dead-end filtration for drug substance or drug product. Release of the 
reprocessed batch using a reactive scenario should only be performed upon receiving regulatory approval. 
(See Section 4.0 for strategies on regulatory submissions). 
A proactive process design approach that accounts for reprocessing could apply to more complex (such as 
ultrafiltration, diafiltration, concentration, chromatography, viral removal steps, and polishing) steps with 
significant benefit to the manufacturer. 
In principle, almost any unit operation may be considered for potential reprocessing as long as it is 
demonstrated to consistently deliver a drug substance or drug product that meets the same specified quality 
commitments of the standard manufacturing process. Reprocessing simple unit operations is relatively well 
accepted and understood by regulators and the industry. Reprocessing a sterile drug substance and 
reprocessing and reworking of sterile-biological batches requires critical attention to maintaining sterility, 
endotoxin, and particulate controls. Reprocessing of nonsterile drug substances manufactured in low 
bioburden facilities also requires special attention to be paid to microbial and particulate controls. 
3.3 Reprocessing Frequency 
A manufacturing process that requires frequent reprocessing is considered to be poorly controlled and not in 
a validated state. While there is no formal definition of “frequent,” it would be reasonable to question the 
state-of-control for a unit operation that requires reprocessing as often as 10% of the time (16). (Ten percent 
is given here as an illustrative example and is not intended to be a prescriptive limit.) 
It is important to distinguish between two types of reprocessing frequency: 
• 
Number of reprocessed unit operations within a batch 
• 
Number of batches that require reprocessing over a defined time period

Given that the Annual Product Review/Product Quality Review provides a one-year performance summary, 
it may be appropriate to evaluate reprocessing trends within that annual timeframe. Under circumstances 
where reprocessing becomes recurring or trends upward, a reassessment of the process design should be 
strongly considered. If reprocessing is carried out frequently or reaches a specified percentage of 
manufactured batches, it must be considered part of the normal process, described as such in the application, 
and receives approval. 
3.3.1 Reprocessing Frequency per Batch 
The frequency of reprocessing for the same batch at commercial-scale reprocessing should be restricted to a 
maximum number of reprocessing events according to process development data for small-scale 
reprocessing.  
Validation of the reprocessing step should be supported by a scientifically justified number of 
demonstrations runs. While no fixed minimum is mandated by regulatory agencies, performing at least two 
independent runs is common practice to demonstrate reproducibility. The exact number should be based on 
process complexity, risk assessment, and historical data to ensure the process consistently achieves 
predefined acceptance criteria. 
The reprocessing frequency is established by the manufacturer based on appropriate studies at either small 
scale or same scale. Small-scale validation studies are commonly used to establish frequency for a 
reprocessing event for a particular unit operation. These studies may be conducted before implementation of 
a reprocessing approach at manufacturing scale. In a reprocessing event, product quality can change after 
repeated run exposures during filtration or chromatography processing steps and may impact the stability of 
long-term storage. There is no rule-of-thumb for frequency determination; the manufacturer must establish it 
for each project based on its impact on quality attributes. For example, a simple dead-end filtration, typically 
a maximum of three reprocessings, can be included in a small-scale reprocessing study. Some protein 
molecules may not be stable for a second reprocessing; in that case, maximum reprocessing may be 
restricted to only one reprocessing event.  
These studies are used to obtain data on product quality attributes (e.g., aggregation) resulting from 
exposure to physical/shear stress during repeated unit operations. Small-scale validation-study design should 
be predictive of the commercial process. This will ensure that the data generated is relevant to large-scale. 
Small-scale reprocessing studies must use the same protein solution for the repeated number of runs and the 
samples to be withdrawn after each run. Product quality attributes of each run should be compared against 
the initial and intermediate feed-material to understand the impact of multiple exposures and to determine 
the frequency for the large-scale reprocessing event. All reprocessing events, including repeated runs, need 
to be completed within the allowed chemical and microbial hold time for intermediate pools or scientifically 
justified if there is any excursion from validated hold time.  
When developing a proactive reprocessing step, a review of the development data and history may identify 
process steps with inherent variability that may, on rare occasions, lead to a need for reprocessing (e.g., 
failing in-process control (IPC) assay results, filter integrity failure, difficult separation step). The variability 
must be scientifically understood.

3.4 Continuation vs. Reprocessing (Repetition) of a Unit Operation 
Most unit operations include some sort of in-process control measurement to determine whether the step 
meets the objective (e.g., a concentration, pH). An adjustment to bring a product within acceptable limits is 
not considered reprocessing and, instead, should be designed as a process “continuation.” Procedures should 
account for continuation to perform an adjustment within defined limits. Without appropriate instructions, 
continuations in processing may be erroneously considered as reprocessing and lead to procedural 
deviations. 
For example, during a step in the ultrafiltration-diafiltration operation, concentration is measured at different 
volumes to check for the desired target concentration. If the concentration does not meet the target 
specification, the process can be continued until the target specification is met, and it is considered a 
continuation of the process, not reprocessing. Similarly, in the titration process, the addition of titrant to 
achieve the desired pH or conductivity set point may be considered a continuation of the process. Likewise, 
adding a buffer dilution to achieve the target product concentration is considered a continuation of the 
process, not a reprocessing activity. 
In the case of drug product manufacturing, when large-volume containers are thawed and only a partial 
amount is used for filling, the remaining formulated drug substance may need to be refrozen for subsequent 
use. This practice may be considered reprocessing if not included in an approved application. Supporting 
freeze-thaw stability data from reduced- or-at-scale studies can help justify the impact on drug product 
quality. 
3.5 Quality Concerns During the Reprocessing of Biologicals 
Protein aggregation is a key concern during manufacturing and quality assessment, particularly in the 
context of reprocessing. Most therapeutic proteins are only marginally stable in solutions and are susceptible 
to chemical instabilities (e.g., deamidation, oxidation, hydrolysis) as well as physical instabilities (e.g., 
denaturation and noncovalent aggregation).  
During reprocessing, proteins may be exposed to various stress conditions. Process controls play a critical 
role in mitigating the effects of destabilizing factors, such as pH, buffer components, and denaturants, that 
can induce protein unfolding and subsequent aggregation.  
Several stress factors during reprocessing can contribute to protein aggregation and instability. Key 
considerations include: 
• 
Increased Viscosity During Reprocessing: Higher protein concentration can increase 
viscosity, affecting subcutaneous formulation. Evaluate using rheology or dynamic light-
scattering. 
• 
Thermal Stress: Elevated temperatures (e.g., during viral inactivation) can promote protein 
denaturation and aggregation. Effects are difficult to quantify and should be controlled. 
• 
High Ionic Strength: Use of high ionic strength eluents (e.g., in ion exchange or hydrophobic 
interaction chromatography) may induce aggregation. 
• 
pH Sensitivity: Eluents with pH near or far from the protein’s isoelectric point can promote 
aggregation. 
• 
Filtration Stress: Filtration processes may lead to aggregation due to membrane contact, air 
bubbles, or shear stress. Ultrafiltration at high protein concentrations adds risk, especially 
before filling or during buffer mixing.

• 
Formulation Handling: Aggregation can be triggered by agitation, freeze-thaw cycles, and 
prolonged storage of bulk solutions before formulation. 
• 
Air-Water Interfaces: Exposure to air-water interfaces during formulation can reduce protein

Failure to manage these factors may result in out-of-specification (OOS) drug substance or drug product, 
visible particulates, reduced storage stability, loss of efficacy, and potentially increased immunogenicity risk 
(17, 18). Therefore, before performing any reprocessing, evaluating and understanding the potential impact 
on product quality is essential. If a reactive reprocessing strategy is used, the sampling plan should include 
specific analytical tests to demonstrate that reprocessing does not adversely affect the quality, safety, or 
efficacy of the drug substance or drug product. 
Note: For reprocessing, product release may occur in parallel with ongoing stability studies, since the 
process remains within the validated manufacturing design space. For reworking, stability data must be 
available prior to release to define retesting intervals, as the introduction of new steps is equivalent to a 
modified synthesis with potentially higher risk (see Section 5.0). 
3.6 General Steps to Consider for a Proactive Design and Validation of a 
Reprocessing Step 
A general outline for designing, validating, and filing a reprocessing procedure with the regulators using a 
proactive approach is provided in this section. Figure 3.6-1 provides an example of a generic sequence of 
activities that could support a proactive reprocessing approach. Steps 1-20 provide an illustration of 
reprocessing a biopharmaceutical product. 
Section 6.0 (Appendix I) provides a comprehensive example of an approach to reprocessing using a 
hypothetical chromatography step that was created for TR 74 based on the publication A-Mab: A Case Study 
in Bioprocess Development (19). 
In the ideal situation, a manufacturer would plan for reprocessing during development prior to licensure of 
the product. However, it may also be possible to prospectively define a new step using supplementary 
submissions created for reprocessing in manufacturing. 
The following 20 steps are presented as recommended considerations for evaluating and implementing a 
reprocessing strategy for a biopharmaceutical product. While the steps are written predicated on a product 
that has already received marketing approval, many of the key concepts are equally applicable during the 
development of reprocessing step(s) for a new biopharmaceutical product. Importantly, these steps are not 
intended to prescribe a fixed sequence but may be adapted to align with the company’s quality system, 
regulatory commitments, and the stage of the product lifecycle.

Step 1: Identifying and Evaluating Reprocessing Need 
The need to reprocess may arise when a biological intermediate/ drug substance/product does not conform 
to specifications or acceptable ranges. Perform an evaluation to narrow the field of feasible and practical 
reprocessing steps. Consider that any reprocessing step candidate should be based on simplicity of the step 
to operate in manufacturing, and past failures. Conduct a technical risk assessment (e.g., FMEA) that 
includes review of related past manufacturing or development issues that may be helpful in identifying a 
potential need for reprocessing. Also, consider the effect on drug supply and whether reprocessing is needed 
to prevent a drug shortage, especially if it is a breakthrough product or an orphan drug. Ensure that further 
development of a prospective reprocessing step would result in an acceptably low regulatory or quality risk. 
(An initial quality and regulatory risk assessment is part of the evaluation discussed in Step 14 and Step 18.) 
For proactive reprocessing, development data must support that the variability is a rare event and is 
scientifically understood. (See Section 3.1.) 
Step 2: Establish a Project Charter (Optional) 
Establish a project team and team charter if the evaluation determines the risks to further develop the 
prospective reprocessing step(s) are acceptable. Development of a reprocessing step involves many 
stakeholders and may require numerous, varying resources and skillsets including those from the Research 
and Development, Process/Analytical Development, Manufacturing, Manufacturing Science and 
Technology (MSAT)/Process Science, Engineering, Quality Control, Quality Assurance, Regulatory 
Affairs, and Project Management groups.

Step 3: Review Development History 
Review the design basis and development history for the existing unit operation to understand the designed 
purpose of the proposed reprocessing steps, especially the control of CQAs. Leverage knowledge obtained 
during the original development of the unit operation to ensure that reprocessing will address the designed                                
purpose of that step to consistently control specific product quality aspects (CQAs) (8, 9). Perform a 
systematic assessment to identify which development studies (e.g., viral/impurity clearance, process 
characterization, resin/membrane cleaning) should be repeated or augmented to support reprocessing. 
Evaluate the assessment for any potential impact of reprocessing to subsequent processing steps. 
Step 4: Identify Reprocessing Triggers 
Identify the in-process testing or monitoring triggers that would indicate a need for reprocessing at a given 
unit operation. It may be necessary to introduce a new in-process test(s) to detect failure modes identified in 
the initial reprocessing risk assessment. The detection method for a failure mode that leads to reprocessing is 
critical to the reliability of the overall process and to the success of a reprocessing step. The method must be 
rapid enough to allow for timely decision-making before the opportunity to reprocess has passed. For 
example, integrity testing of a 0.2 µm filter would be a better detection method to support reprocessing 
because it can be completed in a few minutes or hours compared to microbial testing, which can take several 
days to complete. 
Step 5: Design Reprocessing Procedure 
Begin the initial design with the proposed manufacturing-scale reprocessing procedure including all 
equipment, process parameters, linkages to adjacent unit operations, pre-use/post-use procedures (e.g., 
equilibration/cleaning), and process controls. Give special attention to any procedure required to return the 
process intermediate and equipment to an appropriate starting condition so that the reprocessing step can be 
performed. This restoration step is designed to replicate the intermediate input feed-stream conditions (e.g., 
concentration, volume, pH, conductivity) and is not considered to be a rework step. 
Step 6: Design Assessment 
Consider the broader effect of the reprocessing design outside the scope of its specific unit operation; for 
example, facility design, pre-/post-viral segregation, linkages to adjacent unit operations, draw on utilities, 
and scheduling. 
Step 7: Perform Quality Attribute Impact Assessment 
Perform a risk assessment (6), to identify all quality attributes that may be affected by the proposed 
reprocessing step. In particular, consider such issues as modes of degradation, critical process parameters 
(CPP), exposure to product-contact surfaces, leachables from filters/single-use tubing, and adsorptive losses 
that may be impacted by an additional processing step, intermediate hold point, or freeze-thaw cycle. It is 
not expected that any new CQAs will be identified, but CQAs may take on a higher or lower overall risk 
following reprocessing (8, 9, 20, 21). Consider whether the stability of the reprocessed batch should be 
formally evaluated, especially upon the first instance of reprocessing. 
Step 8: Reassess Criticality 
Review existing criticality assessments conducted for process parameters, raw materials, equipment, in-
process controls, and other factors based on the standard process to determine whether any changes or 
additions are required. The criticality may be influenced by the addition of a reprocessing step.

Step 9: Identify or Develop Analytical Methods 
Identify or develop the analytical methods to monitor all relevant CQAs in intermediate feed-streams and/or 
drug substance/product. Even though no new CQAs are likely to be identified to effectively develop and 
validate a reprocessing step, additional analytical methods capable of monitoring all potentially impacted 
CQAs in the relevant matrix may be needed to effectively develop and validate a reprocessing step. 
Additional development and qualification may be needed to adapt methods to the reprocessed product. The 
risk assessment (Step 7) should determine whether additional characterization methods are required to 
establish comparability and acceptable product quality. 
Step 10: Establish Comparability Criteria 
Establish appropriate comparability criteria for all CQAs. Once CQAs and their corresponding analytical 
methods are identified, it may be possible to develop the key comparability criteria to assess product quality 
during the development of the reprocessing unit operation. All of the original drug substance or drug 
product specifications must continue to be met following reprocessing. Verify that product quality at 
intermediate points in the process meets original expectations or consider new criteria as appropriate. Use 
development studies (discussed below in Step 11) when needed to demonstrate that viral or impurity 
clearance downstream from the reprocessing unit operation is consistent with the original process. 
Step 11: Design Scaled-Down Model 
Design and qualify a scaled-down model, if applicable. To generate additional data, a scaled-down unit 
operation model can be adapted from the previously qualified model of the standard process. The 
requirements for a laboratory-scale model of a reprocessing step are the same as those used in the 
development of a standard manufacturing process (i.e., design a scaled-down model that is representative of 
the manufacturing-scale reprocessing unit operation). 
Step 12: Obtain Representative Materials 
Obtain representative materials for performing reprocessing at the reduced scale. If representative 
intermediate feed-streams from manufacturing cannot be obtained, then it may be necessary to generate the 
materials using a qualified scaled-down model of all, or a section of, the manufacturing-scale process. It 
should also be possible to recreate the failure mode in the feed-stream in a representative manner (e.g., 
spiking with impurities). If the failure mode is based on operational checks, such as integrity tests, rather 
than a product-quality excursion, then use of a standard intermediate feed-stream may be justified. 
Step 13: Execute Developmental Studies 
Plan and execute reduced-scale development studies, if required once the process design elements described 
above in Step 4 – Step 12 are completed. The design of reprocessing development studies (e.g., using a 
design-of-experiments – methodology or worst-case conditions) should result in an understanding of 
robustness across appropriate critical and noncritical process parameter ranges. Evaluate any effects of the 
reprocessing step on quality attributes using the selected analytical methods. 
The design and scope of these studies should take into consideration all elements of the original 
development studies related to the unit operation, including intermediate and drug substance/product 
stability. Determine which prior development studies should be repeated and what additional studies (e.g., 
testing for new degradants) are needed. The data generated in reduced scale studies will become an 
important element of regulatory submissions and may reduce the data needed from the manufacturing scale.

Step 14: Assess Development Data 
Upon completion of the development studies, review the generated data to evaluate whether the scaled-
down process studies indicate the following: 
• 
All predetermined comparability criteria are met by the reprocessing procedure. 
• 
Process parameter operating ranges are confirmed. 
• 
Process intermediate, drug substance, and product stability are potentially impacted. 
Step 15: Finalize Designs 
Adapt GMP manufacturing-scale reprocessing procedures and equipment based upon the results of scaled-
down studies. For example, if the development studies described in Step 13 indicate that process parameter 
ranges must be narrowed or shifted to ensure that all quality and comparability criteria are met, then it may 
be necessary to modify the manufacturing-scale process design and control strategy to reflect this new 
process understanding. 
Step 16: Transfer Technology to Manufacturing 
Execute technology transfer according to company policies and procedures. Transfer of the reprocessing 
step to manufacturing should consider scale-up issues and implementation of associated GMP procedures. 
For more complex reprocessing activities, an intermediate pilot-scale process or non-GMP “engineering 
run” may also be beneficial to ensure a successful transfer into the GMP manufacturing environment (see 
Section 3.8 for more information on contamination control strategies). 
Step 17: Validation, Comparability and Stability Protocols 
Write formal prospective process validation, comparability, and stability protocols for manufacturing-scale 
reprocessing. Validation considerations include the number of lots of resin or other raw materials required, 
the number of runs needed, and the additional nonroutine sampling and testing, beginning with 
consideration of the original validation of the standard process. Additional validation and comparability 
run(s) at manufacturing scale may be supported by the reduced-scale data generated during development. It 
is unlikely that these protocols will be executed before the regulatory submission but, if they are, then 
validation and comparability results could be included in the regulatory submissions discussed in Step 18  
(10, 22). 
Real-time stability studies are considered to be a holistic test of recombinant proteins and, therefore, are 
often used to document that no changes to the protein have occurred. After validation of the reprocessed unit 
operation and subsequent license approval, it may not be necessary for reprocessed lots to undergo further 
stability testing. Ensure the expected stability studies after license approval are clearly stated in the 
submission. Discuss stability requirements, which may vary regionally, with the appropriate regulatory 
body. 
Accelerated and/or stress stability studies for drug substances may be valuable tools for more rapidly 
characterizing changes in a protein that may affect stability. In this case, a comparison of accelerated 
stability data with non-reprocessed control materials, as well as with historical data, may be considered. 
Step 18: Regulatory Submission 
For approval of the reprocessing step, include the supportive scaled-down development data and prospective 
validation/comparability/stability protocols in the submission (3, 8, 9). (The cited publications will be useful 
references to support the submission.)

Step 19: Monitor and Investigate Triggering Event 
Monitor failure modes for signs that the manufacturing process might need reprocessing or, alternatively, 
decide to proactively carry out reprocessing to collect data for a submission. Regardless of whether the 
reprocessing is prospectively designed, it is essential to thoroughly investigate the root cause of any 
triggering event and its impact on product quality following GMP procedures and the principles of 
International Council for Harmonisation (ICH) Quality Guideline Q9(R1): Quality Risk Management (6). 
The investigation outcome, along with CAPAs, should be considered in relation to process validation, 
regulatory commitments (Step 20), and reprocessing design (Step 5 and Step 6) (23). 
Step 20: Validate and Release 
For each reprocessing batch, follow established GMP procedures for executing validation, comparability, 
and stability protocols, and ensuring all acceptance criteria, including release-testing, are met. Document 
validation and comparability results in a final report, completing change control and regulatory 
commitments before releasing the reprocessed batch.

3.8 Impact of Contamination Control Strategy during Reprocessing 
As discussed above, it is critical to ensure microbial controls are considered and protected during 
reprocessing events. The reprocessed biological drug substances and sterile drug products should adhere to 
all CQAs associated with bioburden and endotoxins.  
During the reprocessing of a sterile biological drug substance and drug product, European Union (EU) 
Annex 1 (20), provides general guidance that should be used in the design and control of facilities, 
equipment, systems, and procedures used for the manufacture of all sterile products. The principles of 
quality risk management (QRM) should be applied to ensure that microbial, particulate, and 
endotoxin/pyrogen contamination is prevented in the final drug substance and drug products. QRM is also 
applicable to low-bioburden biological intermediates and drug substances (20).  
Critical aspects considered in QRM include, but are not limited to: 
• 
Process step where reprocessing occurs 
• 
Time of product exposure (open operations) 
• 
Air classification of the reprocessing work zone (where the product is exposed in absence of 
closed system) 
• 
Particulate counts and bioburden trends 
• 
Training records of personnel carrying out reprocessing 
• 
Sanitization and disinfection procedures 
• 
Material movement and area segregation controls (e.g. CCS impact, pre/post viral areas) 
• 
Hold times during reprocessing 
• 
Line clearance before and after reprocessing 
• 
Cleaning and/or change over limits, in cases of multiproduct facilities 
• 
Filter validation studies (critical on operating ranges while handling concentrated protein 
solutions) 
• 
Sampling techniques and number of samples taken for studies

4.0 Regulatory Considerations 
Both reprocessing and reworking decisions require regulatory agency approval prior to release of the 
affected batch(es) for use in humans. Section 4.0 provides regulatory agency guidance about what is 
necessary to report and how to submit this information for approval. In addition, experiences and practices 
from a PDA Technical Report Team representing a cross-section of industry professionals are provided in 
the effective management of this communication. 
For detailed information on reworking, see Section 5.0. 
4.1 General 
Some regulatory agencies provide clear guidance and details on submissions for these scenarios, while 
others provide little to no direct guidance. In the latter case, the applicant should determine if reprocessing 
or reworking can be executed under GMP control only. If in doubt, the wisest practice is to seek relevant 
regulatory agency approval before considering reworking or reprocessing. For products manufactured across 
global supply networks, harmonization of reprocessing/reworking strategies across sites is critical. 
Regulatory submission timelines should be aligned with supply chain planning to avoid stock-outs and 
ensure uninterrupted patient supply. 
Regulatory considerations for several regions are presented in Sections 4.2–4.5. When intermediates are 
manufactured by CDMOs, roles and responsibilities must be clearly defined. This includes identifying the 
stage of processing, the nature of the biological intermediate, and evaluating the potential impact of 
reprocessing or reworking. CDMOs should ensure transparent communication, data integrity, and timely 
provision of information required for regulatory submissions. 
4.2 Guidance for International Council for Harmonisation Regions 
ICH has brought together regulatory authorities and representatives from the pharmaceutical industry to 
discuss scientific and technical aspects of pharmaceuticals. ICH includes regulatory authority members from 
more than 15 countries (e.g., United States, European Union, Japan, Brazil, China, etc.). At Step 5 of the 
ICH process, harmonized ICH guidelines are implemented by ICH Regulatory Members and Observers 
within their respective country or region. ICH Guidelines are implemented in accordance with the applicable 
national/local/regional rules. The stage of implementation of all ICH Guidelines is dependent upon when a 
Member or Observer has joined ICH. Progress in ICH Guideline implementation can be tracked at 
www.ICH.org (27).
