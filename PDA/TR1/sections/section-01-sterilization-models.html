3.1

Sterilization Models | 滅菌模型

Semilogarithmic first-order kinetics of microbial death | 微生物死亡的半對數一階動力學

Original Text (English)

The death of a homogeneous culture of microorganisms exposed to constant lethal stress (also known as the survivor curve) has been shown empirically to follow first-order kinetics. The rate of microbial death is a function of the thermal resistance of the microorganism and lethal stress and is independent of the number of microorganisms in the challenge.

The survivor curve can be described using the following semilogarithmic, first-order model:

Equation 1 — Survivor Curve Model

Log NF = −FT,ref/DT + Log N0

Where:

  • NF = Number of microorganisms after exposure of F equivalent minutes
  • FT,ref = Equivalent lethality of a cycle calculated as minutes at a reference temperature (T), using a defined temperature coefficient (z)
  • DT = Thermal resistance value, in minutes, of the microorganism at a specific temperature (T)
  • N0 = Number of microorganisms prior to exposure

Figure 3.1-1 illustrates the semilogarithmic survivor curve described above for a biological indicator.

DT is a measure (the negative reciprocal) of the slope of the semilogarithmic survivor curve; therefore, it describes the relationship between the number of survivors versus equivalent (F-value) exposure time. F-value is a term used in the model to characterize exposure time to moist heat. By definition, the F-value is expressed by a reference temperature so that it truly represents the equivalent exposure time, in terms of lethality, at that reference temperature.

Since routine operational cycles are not generally square wave cycles (i.e., the load does not come up to temperature instantaneously, remains at the precise set point throughout the exposure phase, and then cools down instantaneously), the z-value, or temperature coefficient, is used in the model to calculate the equivalent lethality at different temperatures.

In order to use this semilogarithmic model for the survivor curve in the scientific approach to microbial destruction, the challenge must consist of a homogeneous culture, and a constant lethal stress (or ability to calculate equivalent lethal stress — F-value) must be applied to the challenge.

The semilogarithmic relationship does not accurately fit all experimental microbial thermal destruction data; but, to date, no other model is known that fits all experimental data. To be useful, a model or mathematical relationship should: (a) represent the system, (b) be predictive of system performance, and (c) be reasonably easy to use and understand.

The semilogarithmic survivor curve model can be used directly and graphically, to both analyze experimental data and design sterilization processes. It allows calculations in both directions — from experimental microbial-destruction data to physical parameters, and from physical parameters to expected microbial survival data.

核心概念：一階動力學

微生物在恆定熱應力下的死亡遵循一階動力學——每單位時間死亡的比例是固定的，與現存數量無關。這就像放射性衰變：不論初始數量多少，每個D值時間段都消滅90%的存活微生物。

生活比喻：銀行利息的反面

想像一個銀行帳戶，每分鐘損失90%的餘額（而非增加）。無論你從100萬還是1元開始，比例損失都相同。這就是半對數模型的直覺——在對數尺度上是直線。

因此，圖3.1-1的x軸（時間）與y軸（log存活數）之間呈直線關係，斜率的倒數就是D值。

方程式解讀

Log NF = −F/DT + Log N0

實際操作含義：

  • 若 N0 = 106，DT = 2.5 min，暴露 F = 30 min
  • Log NF = −30/2.5 + 6 = −12 + 6 = −6

模型的局限性

PDA TR1 誠實指出：半對數模型並不完美地符合所有實驗數據，但目前沒有更好的替代模型。一個實用模型的三個要求：

  1. 能描述系統行為
  2. 能預測系統表現
  3. 合理易用易懂

工業界接受這個模型的原因是其保守性——在多數情況下，實際殺菌效果優於模型預測。

3.1.1

Resistance Value (DT) | D值（熱阻力值）

Time for one-log reduction of microbial population | 微生物族群減少一個對數所需時間

Original Text (English)

DT-value is the time in minutes required for a one-log, or 90%, reduction of a microbial population under specified lethal conditions, i.e., for a temperature (T) in moist heat sterilization. The DT-value is the negative reciprocal of the slope of the survivor curve. One logarithmic cycle on the y-axis (population) represents a ten-fold change in the number of survivors; therefore, the DT-value is the time, or equivalent time (F), on the x-axis for the survivor curve to traverse one logarithmic cycle.

It is important to note that the DT-value is not strictly a genetic characteristic of any particular microorganism. The DT-value is determined empirically using the first-order survivor curve model to characterize the resistance of a BI challenge system to a lethal agent. Refer to Section 3.2.1 for discussion of factors that may affect the resistance of biological indicators.

D-values can be calculated from a survivor curve determined by either:

  1. Direct enumeration
  2. Using two data points (N0 and a point in the quantal range calculated using fraction-negative methods)

3.1.1.1 Direct Enumeration Method

This method involves sub-lethal treatment of the biological challenge followed by enumeration of the survivors (spore counts). The resulting data can be plotted on a semilogarithmic graph (logarithm of surviving population versus equivalent exposure time), and the DT-value determined from the slope of the line. The DT-value is the negative reciprocal of the line that best fits the data points. Linear regression analysis can be used to determine the slope of the best-fit line, when appropriate.

3.1.1.2 Fraction-Negative Methods

Fraction-negative methods use N0 and data in the quantal range to create a two-point line from which the DT-value can be determined. The quantal range is the exposure period over which a set of replicate test BIs exhibit a dichotomous response — some are positive for growth, and the rest are negative for growth. There are two primary methods:

  • Holcomb-Spearman-Karber Method — all data in the quantal range are combined using a weighted average technique to yield the mean time corresponding to an NF value of 0.56 average survivors per BI. Allows for calculating a confidence interval for the DT-value.
  • Stumbo-Murphy-Cochran Method — each set of data in the quantal area is individually analyzed using the Halvorson-Ziegler "Most Probable Number (MPN) Method" to determine NF and, in turn, a DT-value for each set of data. These DT-values are then averaged to determine the test DT-value.

It is important to note that the use of statistical methods for calculating DT-values requires strict adherence to statistical analysis rules. The BIs exposed throughout the study must be replicates and should be from the same BI lot. Recovery and incubation procedures should be identical for all BIs in the study. The only variable should be the exposure time to the lethal agent.

Furthermore, these fraction-negative methods should not be used to evaluate kill in a production sterilizer where BIs are placed throughout the chamber. The multiple BIs are not considered replicates, because there is no assurance of uniform lethality throughout the sterilizer.

DT-value determination studies are usually necessary for:

  • To characterize heat-resistant product isolates (obtained from heat shock testing) during design of sterilization cycles when product-specific approaches are used
  • To evaluate the effect of a formulation change on resistance
  • To characterize heat-resistant microorganisms isolated from the manufacturing environment during routine monitoring
  • To establish D-values for biological indicators prepared by directly inoculating materials or product formulations with heat-resistant spores

3.1.2

Temperature Coefficient (z-value) | 溫度係數（z值）

Degrees of temperature change needed to alter D-value by a factor of 10 | D值改變10倍所需的溫度變化度數

Original Text (English)

Change in the heat resistance of spores, as a function of the temperature, is represented by the z-value. The z-value is the number of degrees of temperature change necessary to change the DT-value by a factor of 10. It is analogous to a temperature coefficient in the semilogarithmic model. The z-value is a component of the F-value calculation that is used to compare spore lethality at different temperatures.

For example, the DT of a BI challenge system with a z-value of 8°C will change by a factor of 10 for each 8°C change in temperature. If the D112°C of the BI is 1.6 minutes, then the D120°C will be 0.16 minutes and the D104°C will be 16.0 minutes. The correct unit of measurement for temperature differences is the degree Kelvin, which is identical in all respects to a change of 1°C. In the interest of simplicity, degrees Celsius will be used throughout this document.

Moist heat sterilization processes are usually carried out within a small temperature range, e.g., 110–135°C, therefore the experimentally determined z-value is usually considered constant for practical purposes. A z-value of 10°C is generally used in routine process design and evaluation.

In studies where the objective is to reconcile the physical and biological F-values delivered to a product, the actual z-value of the biological indicator system must be used in the calculation of the physical FT-values.

The z-value can be determined by several methods; however, determining the z-value from D-values is the most widely-used method. D-values for the challenge microorganism are determined at several different temperatures and then plotted with the logarithmic scale on the y-axis and with the temperature on the x-axis. A straight line is drawn through the data points. The z-value is the number of degrees of temperature for the D-value to change by a factor of 10 (one log cycle), e.g., from 2.0 minutes to 0.2 minute, or from 0.3 minute to 3.0 minutes.

Like the D-value, the z-value is the negative reciprocal of the slope of this line, which is often referred to as the Thermal Resistance Curve (Figure 3.1.2-1). It is important to consider the temperature scale when using z-values, and to express the z-value in terms of either Celsius (Kelvin) or Fahrenheit depending on the intended usage.

z值：熱阻力的溫度敏感性

z值描述微生物熱阻力隨溫度的變化速率。z值越小，意味著溫度對D值的影響越大（對溫度更敏感）；z值越大，微生物的熱阻力對溫度變化較不敏感。

標準值：濕熱滅菌常規使用 z = 10°C（對應嗜熱脂肪土芽孢桿菌）

比喻：z值像音量旋鈕的靈敏度

想像兩個音響：一個旋鈕轉10度音量變10倍（z=10），另一個轉8度就變10倍（z=8）。z值小的那個「更靈敏」——小小的溫度變化就大幅改變殺菌效果。

3.1.3

Lethal Rate (L) and Lethality (F) | 致死率與致死性

Integration of time-temperature data to quantify sterilization efficacy | 整合時間-溫度數據以量化滅菌效力

Original Text (English)

In moist heat sterilization, the ability to relate equivalent minutes at a reference temperature (Tref) with microbial destruction at that same reference temperature is the foundation of all our biological measurements. The development of the measurement and integration of time-temperature data, using the z-value to calculate lethal rates, is what has made it possible to model microbial destruction processes.

Sterilizers are designed to operate at a specific designated temperature; however, the actual temperature may fluctuate around the target temperature within a range. While this fluctuation is usually minimal, it may have a significant effect on F-value determination, especially if the process temperature is predominantly on the low or high side of the target temperature. The F-value calculation considers all of the fluctuations around the target temperature by reducing the individual temperature observations to a common equivalent value in terms of delivered lethality. It is the integration of the lethal rate over the cycle.

3.1.3.1 Lethal Rate (L)

The lethal rate can be calculated by the following formula:

Equation 2 — Lethal Rate

LT,ref,z = 10(T − Tref)/z

Where:

  • T = Temperature of the item being heated
  • Tref = Reference temperature
  • z = z-value of the challenge organism (or 10°C if not known)

The lethal rate is an exponential function, thus small temperature differences can have a significant effect on the delivered lethality. For example, for a BI system with a z-value of 10°C, a 1°C decrease in temperature reduces the lethality by approximately 20%:

L = 10(120−121°C)/10 = 10−0.1 ≈ 0.79

Thus, for a BI with a z-value of 10°C, one minute at 120°C is equivalent to 0.79 minutes at 121°C in terms of lethality.

3.1.3.2 FT,ref Value (Fphys)

The F-value is a measurement of the lethality of a process. FT,ref is the calculated equivalent of time, in terms of lethality, at a reference temperature, Tref, and a temperature coefficient, z, that is delivered to the item being sterilized. F-values calculated from physical data (time and temperature) are also referred to as Fphys.

The F-value is the integration of the lethal rates throughout the process. In practical terms, this integration is carried out as a numerical summation using the Trapezoid model:

Equation 3 — F-value Calculation

FT,ref = d × (ΣL)

Where:

  • d = the time increment between each temperature reading
  • L = the lethal rate calculated for each temperature reading

Due to the inherent inaccuracies of the z-value, which is used to determine the lethal rate, a minimum temperature may be defined (e.g., 100°C) where the integration begins and ends. It is also important to use reference temperatures (Tref) that are close to the operating temperature of the sterilizer during the exposure phase.

3.1.3.3 F0

The term F0 is the number of equivalent minutes of steam sterilization at a temperature of 121°C delivered to a container or unit of product. This is calculated using a z-value of 10°C.

Note: The reference temperature used in calculating F0 is 121.1°C, which is the approximate mathematical equivalent of 250°F. The reference temperature of 121°C for F0 will be used throughout this report for brevity.

Wherever a value is stated in terms of F0, it is referring to the equivalent time at 121°C. If, for example, a cycle has a stated F0-value of 8.0 minutes, then the sterilization effectiveness of that cycle is equivalent to 8.0 minutes at 121°C regardless of the load/product temperature and time actually achieved in the cycle.

3.1.3.4 FBIO

The term FBIO represents the delivered lethality measured by the actual kill of microorganisms on or in a BI system. The FBIO-value is calculated using the following formula:

Equation 4 — FBIO Calculation

FBIO = DT × LR

Where:

  • DT = D-value of the BI at the reference temperature
  • LR = Log reduction of the BI population achieved during the cycle

比較

Overkill vs. Bioburden-Based Approach | 過度殺滅法 vs. 生物負載法

Two primary sterilization validation strategies | 兩種主要的滅菌確效策略

Comparison Overview | 方法概覽

PDA TR1 recognizes two principal approaches to moist heat sterilization cycle design, both grounded in the semilogarithmic model:

  • The Overkill Approach applies a large excess of lethality to provide a high margin of safety, irrespective of the actual bioburden.
  • The Bioburden-Based Approach (also called the Probability of Survival approach) uses knowledge of the actual bioburden count and D-value to design a cycle that achieves a defined SAL.

The choice of approach depends on product characteristics, regulatory expectations, and the manufacturer's risk management philosophy.

The SAL (Sterility Assurance Level) of 10−6 is the internationally accepted standard — the probability that any single unit is non-sterile after sterilization must be no greater than one in one million.

SAL 10−6 的含義

SAL（無菌保證水準）= 10−6 意指：在符合規範的滅菌製程後，任何一個單位產品為非無菌的機率不超過百萬分之一。

注意：SAL 10−6 不代表每百萬個產品中有一個有菌——它是機率概念，不是頻率概念。這個數值本身無法直接測試驗證，只能通過正確設計和確效的製程來保證。

Question 1 | 問題一

A biological indicator has D121°C = 1.8 minutes and an initial population of 106 spores. If the sterilization cycle delivers an F0 of 15 minutes, what is the probability of survival (NF)?

一個生物指示劑的 D121°C = 1.8 分鐘，初始孢子數 = 106。若滅菌製程提供 F0 = 15 分鐘，存活機率（NF）為何？

Show Answer | 查看答案

Log NF = −F/DT + Log N0

Log NF = −15/1.8 + Log 106

Log NF = −8.33 + 6 = −2.33

NF = 10−2.33 ≈ 0.0047

結論：約 0.47% 的機率有一個存活微生物，或每 213 個BI中預期有一個陽性。這遠優於 SAL 10−6，屬於 Overkill 方法範疇。

Question 2 | 問題二

A sterilizer operates at 121°C but temperature data shows the actual product temperature fluctuates between 120°C and 122°C during the exposure phase (z = 10°C, Tref = 121°C). One minute at 120°C contributes how much to F0 compared to one minute at 122°C?

滅菌器設定 121°C，但實際產品溫度在曝露階段在 120–122°C 之間波動（z = 10°C）。在 120°C 一分鐘 vs. 在 122°C 一分鐘，各對 F0 貢獻多少？

Show Answer | 查看答案

L120°C = 10(120−121)/10 = 10−0.1 ≈ 0.794 min

L122°C = 10(122−121)/10 = 100.1 ≈ 1.259 min

結論：122°C 的貢獻（1.259）比 120°C（0.794）高出約 58%。這說明溫度偏差對 F 值計算有顯著影響，即使只差 2°C。確保製程溫度均勻性對準確的 F 值計算至關重要。

Question 3 | 問題三

You are designing a bioburden-based sterilization cycle. Bioburden monitoring data shows an average of 100 CFU per container, with the most resistant organisms having D121°C = 0.5 minutes. What minimum F0 is needed to achieve SAL 10−6?

你正在設計生物負載法的滅菌製程。每容器平均生物負載 = 100 CFU，最耐熱菌的 D121°C = 0.5 分鐘。要達到 SAL 10−6，最低 F0 為多少？

Show Answer | 查看答案

目標：NF ≤ 10−6

Log NF = −F/DT + Log N0

−6 = −F/0.5 + Log 100

−6 = −F/0.5 + 2

−F/0.5 = −8

F = 4.0 minutes

結論：F0 ≥ 4.0 分鐘即可達到 SAL 10−6。這比 Overkill 法所需的 F0 ≥ 12 分鐘寬鬆許多，適合熱敏感產品——但必須嚴格維持生物負載監控計畫，確保生物負載不超過設計假設。

Question 4 | 問題四

Why is it inappropriate to use fraction-negative methods to determine D-values from BIs placed at different locations inside a production sterilizer?

為什麼不能使用 Fraction-Negative 法，對放置在生產滅菌器不同位置的 BI 進行 D 值測定？

Show Answer | 查看答案

Fraction-Negative 法的前提假設是所有 BI 受到相同劑量的致死因子（uniform lethality）。在實驗室用 resistometer 測定時，這個條件可以滿足。

但在生產滅菌器中，不同位置（冷點、熱點、負載中心 vs. 邊緣）可能受到不同的 F 值。放置在不同位置的 BI 並非「複本（replicates）」，而是在不同條件下測試的樣本。

結論：若對這些數據套用 Fraction-Negative 統計方法，計算出的 D 值將是無意義的混合結果，不代表任何真實的微生物熱阻力。這類數據只能用於判斷特定位置是否滿足殺菌條件，而非計算 D 值。
