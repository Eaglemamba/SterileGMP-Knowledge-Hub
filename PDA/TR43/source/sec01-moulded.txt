Appendix
This appendix contains five lexicons that visually and descriptively detail glass nonconformities to ad-
dress the need for this universal guideline. Table 8.0-1 pertains to moulded glass containers, and Tables 
8.0-2–8.0-5 describe tubular glass containers encompassing vials, ampoules, cartridges, and syringes. 
These lexicons depict the most common glass nonconformance attributes in a slide format. Each 
lexicon slide contains the name of nonconformance or attribute, definition, location(s), method(s) of 
identification (where applicable), related attribute(s), and classification(s) of the nonconformance at-
tribute as well as a photographic image(s) and/or drawing(s). 
Nonconformance
The primary name of the attribute.
a.k.a.
“Also known as” attribute name(s). (i.e., Heel Tap a.k.a. Slugged Bottom) 
Definition
Description of the attribute. 
Photographs, Images, and Drawings
It should be noted that the photographs in the lexicon slides are not intended to represent limit 
samples. These photographs were chosen based on the clarity of the defect type to help the reader 
recognize the defect.
Classification
The lexicon provides a classification for each attribute. An attribute may have multiple classifications 
based on criteria specified. In many cases defects are identified during visual inspection and then 
measured to identify a classification. As stated in section 4.0, the classifications are Critical, Major A, 
Major B, Minor, and Acceptable Imperfection.
Related
A reference to a similar looking attribute which has slightly different characteristics to facilitate proper 
designation of attribute. e.g., Loose glass lists related to adhered glass, glass stringer, lehr sticker, spiticule) 
Location
Each lexicon has a nomenclature slide identifying the locations. Under location, a classification of 
“general” indicates that the nonconformity can occur anywhere on the container.

Method
This information is presented in the lexicons to provide potential method(s) for determination of 
acceptability. There are other acceptable methods available to the inspector, for example:
• 
Calibrated measuring device with magnification method such as an eye loupe with 
graduations or microscope with measurement software or graduated oculars. Used for mea-
surement of area by longest length and shortest length or area of circular particles or length 
of attribute. (For attributes such as particles, fibers, chip, contamination, airline, bruise, 
bump check, check, knot, reboil, scratch, scuff, stone) 
• 
TAPPI Chart method (Test Method T564, transparent chart for the estimation of defect). 
Used for measurement estimate of area by longest length and shortest length or area of 
circular attribute. (For attributes such as contamination, particles, fibers, chip, bruise) 
• 
Identification or compositional verification methods or chemical analysis methods 
(e.g., SEM EDS, SEM EDX, FTIR, ICP, or other available analytical techniques) (For attri-
butes such as contamination, water spot, metal mark, glass powder spot, treatment bloom)
• 
Hydrolytic resistance methods (e.g., USP <660> Surface Glass Test, Ph. Eur. 3.2.1 A, 
ICP, or other available analytical techniques) (For attributes such as haze, glass powder spot, 
treatment bloom)
• 
Tactile method of probing a defect with a non-metallic instrument in addition to mag-
nification will be more deterministic of the discontinuity’s depth as opposed to visual only. 
(For attributes such as crack, check) 
• 
Tactile method of probing a defect for adhesion or ease of removal. (For attributes such 
as adhered glass, contamination, loose glass.)
• 
Container Closure Integrity (CCI) Assessment of a defect to determine if seal integrity 
is compromised.
• 
Calibrated measuring device method (e.g., comparator, comparator with templates, 
comparator using immersion fluid, calipers, magnamike, bottom thickness gauge, wall 
weight gauge, runout gauge, offset gauge, ID gauge, OD ring gauge, perpendicularity gauge, 
feeler/shim gauge, Go / No Go Gauges) (For attributes such as flared shoulder, leaner, con-
cave bottom, bullseye, bent neck, offset finish, pulled ID, sunken neck)
• 
Removable method to determine ease of removing attribute or adhesion (e.g., 80 C WFI 
flush, swipe with gloved hand, water flush, air flush, or soft probe) (For attributes such as 
adhered glass, contamination, loose glass, particles)
• 
Flat surface method (e.g., flat surface, footprint stamp test) (For attributes such as rocker, 
wavy bottom, bullseye, wavy top)
• 
Visual with limit sample method utilizes an optional actual physical unit, reference pho-
to, or written description of a defect established between a drug manufacturer and a glass 
manufacturer. The limit sample defines the approximate maximum degree of acceptability 
for an “acceptable imperfection,” that is, an imperfection that is considered acceptable based 
on the size or other criteria noted in the requirements. (For attributes such as bent neck, 
bullseye, fire mark, frost, haze, leaner, malformed shoulder, offset finish, orange peel, sunken 
neck, tooling marks, twisted neck, wavy bottom)

Risk / Impact 
This information provides the risk/impact directly tied to the classification:
• Container Closure Integrity (CCI) / Sterility: This risk / impact is likely to cause harm to a pa-
tient. Corresponding to a Critical classification which is a nonconformity that is likely to result in 
personal injury or potential hazard to the user. This classification is assigned to any nonconformity 
that compromises the integrity of a parenteral container and risks microbiological contamination 
of a sterile product. e.g., seal integrity or defects that may cause plunger damage
• User injury or harm: This risk / impact is user injury or harm to patient or healthcare practi-
tioner. Corresponding to a Critical classification. e.g., microbiological contamination, internal 
contamination (extraneous, not glass or glass container), crack, likely harm 
• Remote risk to user: This risk / impact is remote risk to patient, user, or healthcare prac-
titioner. Corresponding to Major A, a nonconformity leading to serious impairments. e.g., 
check with potential to propagate into crack under stress, internal particle dislodge, cuts/lac-
erations from sharp, not sterile, generation of glass particles 
• Functional: This risk or impact may lead to functional or serious impairment. Corresponding to a 
Major A classification. e.g., package unusable, broken, container stability during processing e.g., rocker
• Machinability: The risk / impact includes yield reduction, increased downtime, line stoppage, 
reduced efficiency, business impact. Corresponding to Major B, a nonconformity leading to 
impairments of a lesser degree. e.g., dimensional attributes are typically classified Major B
• Negligible risk: This risk / impact is negligible due to downstream processing such as wash-
ing. Corresponding to Minor, a nonconformity that does not impact product quality or 
process capability. e.g., particle removed by washing
• Aesthetic: This risk / impact is cosmetic, visual, and/or covered by aluminum seal, label, or 
device. It does not impact product quality or process capability. Corresponding to Minor or 
Acceptable Imperfection. An imperfection that is less than the minimum nonconformity 
size or limit sample, magnitude, and impact of a nonconformity is considered not applicable. 
Therefore, an imperfection that is considered to be nonapplicable is acceptable. 
Source
Attribute may have single or multiple sources:
• Material (Tube and/or Tank): The glass melted in the tank or furnace which is either deliv-
ered to moulds or drawn into tubes. Attributes such as airlines, seeds, knots, stones, inclusions.
• Container Formation
o	 Converting: The forming process of glass tubes into syringes, cartridges, vials, or ampoules. At-
tributes such as adhered glass, alkali spots, bent neck, broken ring off, bullseye, concave bottom, 
fire mark, flared bottom, frost, glass stringer, haze, leaner, lap, malformed finish, malformed 
shoulder, offset finish, orange peel, pressure mark, pulled ID, reboil, rocker, spiticule, sunken 
neck, tooling mark, trapped glass, twisted, unfilled finish, water spot, wavy bottom, wavy top. 
o	 Moulding: The forming process of glass gobs into vials/bottles. Attributes such as wire edge, 
sunken side, rocker.
