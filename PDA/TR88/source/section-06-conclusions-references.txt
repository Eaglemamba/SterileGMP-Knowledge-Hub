6.0 Conclusions
The investigation of microbial monitoring and testing deviations is a critical activity in the pharma-
ceutical industry that is not adequately covered by the FDA Guidance for Industry: Investigating Out
of Specification (OOS) Test Results for Pharmaceutical Production (1). This technical report provides
industry best practices related to microbial investigations to eliminate that gap in guidance.
These investigations are operationally divided into two phases—Phase I laboratory investigations and
Phase II manufacturing investigations—with different teams responsible for each activity. Because of
the importance of timeliness in microbial investigations, the manufacturing investigation may be initi-
ated prior to completion of the laboratory investigation.
The objective of the laboratory investigation is to confirm that the sampling and testing was conducted
correctly, and the reported result was valid. The investigational team is usually limited to the microbi-
ologist conducting the test, the laboratory management, an SME with knowledge and experience with
the test, and a representative of the QCU.
The major points to consider in a laboratory investigation are:
• Integrity of the test sample used in the microbial test
• Educational background, skill, and experience of the microbiologist conducting the test
• Testing and environmental and personnel monitoring history associated with the microbiologist
• Suitability of the equipment, supplies, media, diluents, and reagents used for the test
• Environmental controls, including microbial testing facilities, laminar flow hoods, isolators, and
PPE associated with the test
• Reading of plates, scoring of microbiological broth, recording of data, accuracy of calculations,
and review and reporting of the results
• Microbial identification of the isolate implicated in the deviation
Once the validity of the microbial test is confirmed, a multifunctional team is assembled to conduct the
manufacturing investigation related to the failure. Representatives of the microbiology laboratory, manu-
facturing, engineering, maintenance, and process validation may be members of the manufacturing inves-
tigation team usually led by the QCU. To increase the chances of a successful investigation, an experienced
pharmaceutical microbiologist familiar with manufacturing operations is usually added as an SME.
The major points to consider in a manufacturing investigation are:
• Unit operation and location within the facility directly related to the failure
• Possible contribution of surrounding support areas
• Possible contribution to the failure of deviations in environmental controls, i.e., space pressurization,
airflow patterns, temperature, humidity, power outages, material flow, facility cleaning, and sanitization
• Possible contribution of pharmaceutical ingredients, ingredient water, in-process materials, pack-
aging components, and utilities
• Deviation in critical manufacturing steps, as documented in the batch record
• Recent change controls
• Related in-process bioburden, environmental, and personnel monitoring deviations
• Interviews with operators, laboratory management, and production managers as to the possible
cause of the failure
• Follow-up microbial monitoring and testing
The investigation reports must adequately document the team findings. If the root cause of the out-of-
limit or out-of-specification microbial test result cannot be definitively established, the most probable
cause or causes must be presented. Investigating out-of-trend results may help identify laboratory and/
or manufacturing issue before they become out-of-specification results. To prevent the recurrence of
these microbial failures, a CAPA plan must be implemented, with responsible individuals reporting
their progress on the plan to the QCU and their management.

About PDA Technical Reports
PDA Technical Reports are global consensus documents, prepared by member-driven Task Forces (listed on inside
front cover) comprised of content experts, including scientists and engineers working in the pharmaceutical/bio-
pharmaceutical industry, regulatory authorities and academia. While in development, PDA Technical Reports are
subjected to a global review of PDA members and other topic-specific experts, often including regulatory officials.
Comments from the global review are then considered by the authoring Task Force during the preparation of the
final working draft. The level of expertise of the Task Force and those participating in the global review ensure a
broad perspective reflecting best thinking and practices currently available.
The final working draft is next reviewed by the PDA Advisory Board or Boards that sanctioned the project. PDA’s
Advisory Boards are: Science Advisory Board, Biotechnology Advisory Board, and Regulatory Affairs and Quality
Advisory Board. Following this stage of review, the PDA Board of Directors conducts the final review and deter-
mines whether to publish or not publish the document as an official PDA Technical Report.
While PDA goes to great lengths to ensure each Technical Report is of the highest quality, all readers are encouraged
to contact PDA about any scientific, technical, or regulatory inaccuracies, discrepancies, or mistakes that might be
found in any of the documents. Readers can email PDA at: TRfeedback@pda.org.
