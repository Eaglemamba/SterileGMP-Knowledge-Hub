4.2.2 Bacterial Endotoxins Testing Investigations
Compendial bacterial endotoxins testing (BET) detect and/or quantify lipopolysaccharide cell membrane
components derived from the outer-cell membrane of Gram-negative bacteria that are most commonly
responsible for human pyrogenic responses. This method is more comparable to chemical assay than to the
traditional methods used for the detection and enumeration of microorganisms in pharmaceuticals, bio-
pharmaceuticals, and medical devices. For this reason, the enzyme cascade-based Limulus amoebocyte lysate
(LAL) BET has unique analytical requirements that may affect the reliability of the test results. USP <85>
Bacterial Endotoxin Tests describes the procedures for performing both the gel clot and spectrophotometric
methods for detecting and quantifying bacterial endotoxins. Because the methods are based on enzymatic
components and principles, incubation conditions (e.g., temperature and time) are important, and labora-

product matrix on the product/lysate mixture. Another critical factor is the measurement and, if necessary,
the adjustment of the product–lysate mixture to fall within the required pH range as stated in the lysate
instructions for use. If the pH is not within the specified range, the endotoxin test results can be significantly

A deviation may be due to one of the following from a partial list of reasons:
• Error during product dilution (excessive volume transfer during serial dilution preparation), in-
correct volume (too little or too much) during reagent rehydration, misplacement of handled test

• CSE cross-contamination caused by pipette tips (or pipets) used repeatedly

• Working on a lab bench with a nearby splash sink that creates water aerosols (tap water commonly

• Analyst not performing the test according to the prescribed method

• Unexpected occurrence during the analysis that interrupted the completion of the gel clot (e.g.,

• Glucans, at high enough concentrations, present in the test material may cause false positives
• Contaminated water baths used for incubation of gel clot test tubes

culminate in a faulty analysis.
4.2.2.1 Compendial Bacterial Endotoxins Test

4.2.3 Antimicrobial Effectiveness Testing Investigations
The test methods and acceptance criteria for different dosage forms are defined in USP <51> Anti-
microbial Effectiveness Testing. In general, the test is only applicable to aqueous, multiple-use sterile
and nonsterile drug products that may be subject to microbial contamination during use. USP <51>
defines aqueous as a water activity (A ) of more than 0.6. Products with A < 0.6 will not support the
w w
growth of microorganisms and may be demonstrably self-preserving. The test confirms the effective-
ness of the antimicrobial preservative system and/or other supporting physicochemical attributes to
meet product category criteria. Although a drug product would be required to meet the test criteria
during development and to be demonstrated as effective in a long-term stability study, the test is
a shelf-life specification, but not a release requirement. In contrast, the preservative concentration
required within the formulation to meet the product category criteria at release and at expiry is a
product specification included in the regulatory submission. Typically, USP <51> is looked upon as a
stability-indicating pass/fail test, where the results in terms of log reduction of the challenge organisms
at specified time intervals are evaluated against the log reduction requirement for the product category.
Given the inherent variability of the test method, marginally preserved products have a potential to
fail at one or more time intervals in long-term stability studies supporting marketed products (27).

4.2.5 Biological Indicators Investigations
Biological indictors (BI) are used during the validation of moist heat, dry heat, and gaseous steriliza-
tion processes and during vapor-phase hydrogen peroxide decontamination cycles. BIs may also be
used for monitoring routine cycles to support the release of products that are used by the pharmaceuti-
cal industry. Additionally, a BI can be used as a load monitor to support parametric release of moist-
heat sterilized products in the U.S. Typically, they consist of high numbers of resistant bacterial spores
with characterized calibrated D-values that are included in sterilization loads, retrieved, and cultured
to demonstrate the lethality of their destruction during the sterilization cycle.
It is an expectation that a company will conduct confirmatory testing of the population and purity stated
in the certificate of analysis of the incoming BI lot and growth of the cultured spore strip. Low counts may
reflect the incomplete recovery from the matrix supporting the bacterial spores, whereas the growth of a heat-
resistant spore-forming bacteria like Geobacillus stearothermophilus from a BI is most likely caused by contami-
nation of the laminar flow hood and/or test supplies from positive controls in the testing environment (30).
Probable causes of failure would include gross mishandling, insufficient exposure to the sterilization
parameters, and higher BI D-values that result in a lower probability of destruction in the sterilization
cycle. Other points to consider include:
• Were the BIs stored and handled prior to use, as recommended by the manufacturer?
• Were the BIs incubated in accordance with regulatory expectations and manufacturer instructions?
• Were the BIs used in the manner prescribed by the manufacturer?
• Was the appropriate BI used for the product being tested (e.g., ampoule BI used for liquid products)?
• In cases where the BI is directly inoculated into a solution or onto a substrate, what is the D-value
as used in that form?
PDA Technical Report 51 Biological Indicators for Gas and Vapor-Phase Decontamination Processes:
Specification, Manufacture, Control and Use provides further guidance on this topic (31).

4.2.8 Utilities Monitoring
4.2.8.1 Pharmaceutical-Grade Water Systems
The purpose of the laboratory investigation is to determine whether a definitive source of sampling
and/or laboratory error contributed to the root cause of the deviation. This section addresses the initial
and extended laboratory investigations associated with samples collected from pharmaceutical-grade
water systems, such as purified water and water for injection (WFI).
Microbiological testing of pharmaceutical water systems, including the source water (i.e., potable wa-
ter) and purification steps, typically consists of total viable microbial count, BET, and/or a test for the
absence of coliforms, depending on the grade of water and any local regulatory requirements. After
a pharmaceutical water system is successfully validated, both the system and the source are subject
to periodic microbial monitoring using standard methods. Action limits and alert levels should be
established to recognize adverse trends in the water system. These alert levels or action limits should
be based on historical data, the intended purpose of the sampling plan, as well as the recommended
compendial or regulatory limits. In general, specifications are not set for water systems.
In a well-designed, well-operated pharmaceutical water system, sanitized by maintaining a minimum
turbulence level and elevated temperature (> 65 °C) or periodic heat or chemical sanitization, test
results above the action limit should be very rare.
Several events can signal that a laboratory investigation should be conducted:
• A single test result that exceeds an action limit
• Multiple test results that exceed an alert level, thus indicating an adverse trend
• Isolation of microorganisms of concern based on the dosage forms manufactured in the facility
The cross-functional team conducting the laboratory investigation should review the collection and
microbiological testing of the subject water sample to determine any potential sampling and labora-
tory errors. The data collected should include the sampling location, sample containers, sampling
technique, and any alert level or action limit excursions associated with the sampling location, loop, or
water system, and the operators collecting the water samples.
If this investigation does not identify a definitive source of laboratory error to explain the deviation
result, the laboratory should immediately notify the owner of the utility system, operational users, and
the QCU.
4.2.8.1.1 Evaluation of Sample Handling
The methods used for preparing, storing, and attaching hoses and sampling apparatus to the sample
ports are critical. Regulatory guidance recommends that each point of use should be flushed using the
same flushing procedure used in the manufacturing process (35).
The potential for changes in the microbial population in samples should be limited by restricting the
holding time before testing and controlling the storage temperature. If the samples cannot be tested
within two hours, they should be transported and stored at refrigeration temperature for a maximum
of 24 hours. In situations where even 24 hours is not possible (such as when using off-site contract
laboratories), it is particularly important to qualify the microbiological sample hold times and storage
conditions. (See APHA/AWWA/WEF Standard Methods, USP <1231>, EMA 496873, and Japan: G8
for guidance (28, 36-38). Prior to removing the test aliquot, all samples should be adequately mixed
to ensure a homogeneous mixture is achieved. Any parameters for storage and transportation of water
samples that are different from standard methods should be qualified to ensure the samples are not
negatively affected, resulting in potentially invalid results.

4.2.8.1.2 Evaluation of Test Methods
Review of all materials associated with the testing and their appropriateness for the grade of water
tested is required in a laboratory investigation. Sterility of the membrane filters, sample containers, pi-
pettes, and utensils used to perform testing must be suitable for their intended use and must conform
within sterilization dating. Also, all diluents and reagents used must be suitable (such as sterile and/or
endotoxin-free) and within their expiry dating.
Any deviation from these requirements must be evaluated and investigated. Confluent microbial
growth, in contrast to discrete colonies on the membranes or pour plates, may be indicative of equip-
ment contamination. The position of the analyst’s head, hands, and arms in the path of the airflow
(preferably in a laminar flow hood with horizontal air flow) over the test materials may also be a source
of microbial contamination (personnel-related considerations are discussed further in Section 5.2.1).
The identity of the microorganism(s) associated with the investigation may also reveal information
about the source of contamination. The most common water isolates are Gram-negative, nonferment-
ing, rod-shaped bacteria historically described as pseudomonads, which may be indicators for a biofilm
formation within the water system. The recovery of organisms that do not thrive or survive under the
low ionic strength/low carbon conditions found in a pharmaceutical water system may suggest con-
tamination of the sample or insufficient control or flushing of the sampling apparatus and/or hoses
attached at the port. Gram-positive cocci commonly found on the human skin, such as Staphylococ-
cus epidermidis, can be indicative of potential sampling contamination, while Gram-positive spore-
formers, such as Bacillus species or the fungus Cladosporium species, can be indicative of potential
environmental contamination.
4.2.8.1.3 Evaluation of Trending Data
Reviewing the historical test data will help determine if an adverse trend is associated with individual
points-of-use, loops, and water systems. This information will be also useful for the manufacturing
investigation.
4.3 Phase I: Conclusion and Next Steps
Evaluation and questions related to the Phase I deviation investigation must use strong, evidence-
based data. The investigation may not be definitive but may suggest probable cause(s). The Phase I
investigation commonly concludes with one of three possible outcomes:
1. Events uncovered during the investigation may be responsible for the deviation and present a
clear root cause that is directly responsible for the deviation. Examples could show contamination
due to failure to follow SOPs, use of poor aseptic techniques during either sampling or testing, a
recognized laboratory error, or the use of nonsterile testing materials. There may be a direct link
between isolate identification and the same contamination from the testing environment. In these
cases, the test may be considered invalid.
In the case of invalid tests, the Phase I investigation would conclude without advancing to the
Phase II manufacturing investigation, or where Phase I and Phase II investigations were under-
taken concurrently, the cancellation of the Phase II investigation would be warranted. Depending
on the sample type and source of contamination, resampling or retesting may also be considered
as long as the QCU agrees. CAPAs must be implemented (preferably prior to any retesting or
resampling), however, in order to remediate the original cause of the invalidation.
2. While the investigation might have revealed issues, there may be insufficient evidence to directly
correlate the investigative findings to the deviation. For example, if the EM conducted during the
testing showed microorganisms present but the organisms were a different genus and species than the
contamination within the sample, the environmental results may cast doubt on the sample, but they
24 © 2022 Parenteral Drug Association, Inc. Technical Report No. 88

Licensed to Kuo, Li-Hung/TWi Pharmaceuticals Inc.: Copying and Distribution Prohibited.
would not invalidate the original result. Thus, the Phase I laboratory investigation would be found
inconclusive, not providing sufficient evidence to invalidate the result, and the result is deemed to be
valid. The investigation would then proceed to a Phase II manufacturing investigation.
3. The results of the investigation did not show any evidence of testing failure. In other words, it can be
clearly demonstrated that all procedures were followed, analysts were properly qualified to perform
the testing, and all materials, equipment, and testing environment were considered appropriate for
the test. The calculations for reporting data and all final results were correct and verified. Therefore,
the deviation is confirmed and deemed valid, so the investigation must proceed to Phase II in order
to determine the source of contamination from the processes used during manufacturing.
