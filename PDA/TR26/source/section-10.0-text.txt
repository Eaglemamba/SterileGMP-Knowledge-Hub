11.0 Appendix II: Nondestructive Physical Integrity Test
Methods

11.1 Bubble Point Test

The bubble point test is based on the premise that membrane pores can be represented as a multiplicity of
capillaries, and the knowledge that liquid is held in a capillary tube due to surface tension and other factors.
The minimum gas pressure required to force liquid out of the pores is a direct function of pore diameter. The
bubble point test is used to determine the pressure at which there is bulk flow of the test gas through the
largest pores in the membrane that are the first from which liquid is displaced. Since the observed bubble
point is a function of the wetting-liquid surface tension, the contact angle, the pore shape, and the pore size,
results are specific to each membrane polymer/surface chemistry, structure, wetting fluid combination,
methods, equipment and surface area. While bubble point is an indication of the largest pores, it cannot be
used directly to calculate their diameter. The bubble point test also does not indicate the number of largest
pores, which can affect the probability of bacterial retention (if the organisms are smaller than the pores).

The test can be performed manually by subjecting a wetted filter membrane to gas pressure on the upstream
side. Tubing on the membrane's downstream side is immersed in a liquid. The differential pressure at which
a steady stream of bubbles, or in some cases the displacement of liquid, is observed is the bubble point. The
typical test equipment for performing a manual bubble point test is illustrated in Figure 11.0-1.

The manual bubble point test is a sensitive, visual technique for disc filters and other small surface-area
membrane filters; it may be performed as one of the quality assurance tests for membranes during the
casting process. The bubble point test detects major and minor defects and out-of-size pores and can be
correlated with the bacterial retention study. It is also a reliable integrity test of larger surface-area filters
until the diffusional flow across the membrane masks the flow rate from the pores from which the wetting
fluid has been displaced.

The following are points to consider when performing the bubble point test manually:

• Use a pressure gauge with easy-to-read incremental steps and increase slowly in a step-wise
manner.
• Allow pressure to stabilize at each step in the pressure increase. The true bubble point value
may be overshot if pressure is increased too rapidly. The test cannot be repeated without
rewetting the membrane.
• Minimize downstream connections and avoid kinking the downstream tubing.
• Check for leaks in the system.
• Clearly define the bubble point as bulk air flow. The most common error is misidentifying
bubbles that are not the result of bulk flow. Some bubbles will appear because of diffusion
across the wetted membrane. Free flowing air is the indication, not the first bubble.
• Manually performed, bubble point-test determination can be subjectively different from one
operator to another; therefore, operators should be properly trained to conduct the test and to
interpret test results.
• Keep upstream volume to a minimum. With extremely high upstream volumes, longer
stabilization between pressure increases may be required.
• Maintain the temperature within the specified range.

Test attributes:

• Relatively fast, with a short stabilization time
• Can be directly related to the membrane pore size
• Relatively easy to perform for small-to-medium area filters
• Easy to establish correlation between bacterial retention and physical integrity test values
• Sensitivity may be limited for large-membrane-area systems if diffusive air flow below the
bubble point is significant
• Manual testing requires downstream (i.e., sterile side) manipulations

11.2 Diffusive Flow Test

In a wetted membrane filter under pressure, gas molecules migrate through the liquid-filled pores at
differential pressures below the bubble point via a diffusion process following Fick's Law of Diffusion. The
overall rate of diffusion for a filter is proportional to the applied test pressure, its membrane surface area and
wetted thickness, and the solubility and diffusivity of the test gas in the wetting liquid. In small surface-area
filters, such as flat-disc filters, this gas flow may be too low to be measured accurately. In large surface-area
filter systems, it is significant and can be measured to perform an integrity test. To accurately measure the
test limit, one must use the correct limit based on the gas type. This only applies to Diffusion/Pressure Hold
test; the bubble point test is not influenced by gas type.

The diffusive flow test quantitatively measures the sum of diffusive flow through the membrane and flow
through any open pores. The filter user should use the manufacturer's recommended testing method for each
filter, ensuring that the method is appropriate and has been validated for the specific case. Diffusive flow

11.3 Pressure Hold/Decay Test

The pressure hold (or pressure decay) test is an indirect method of upstream diffusive flow-testing. In this
method, the filter-housing is pressurized to a predetermined setting and then isolated from the pressure
source. Gas flow across the membrane is quantitatively measured as a decay in pressure over a specific
period of time. This test has been indirectly correlated to bacterial retention through its relationship to the
diffusive flow test.

The allowable pressure drop is not published by filter manufacturers because it is unique to a given filter
assembly and piping system, and it is calculated by taking into account the diffusive-flow specification at a
given pressure and constant temperature, the upstream system volume, and the duration of the test.
Therefore, this test requires a determination of the upstream volume of the system and a calculation of the
allowable pressure drop.

The pressure hold/decay test measures the diffusive gas (typically air or nitrogen) flow through all the
wetted pores, and the bulk gas flow through larger non-wetted pores, when applying a predetermined gas
pressure to a wetted filter in a system that has no upstream leaks. Pressure decay on the upstream side is
then measured, after isolating the incoming gas supply. Upstream integrity tests are particularly useful in
critical fluid applications since they can be performed without compromising the sterility of the downstream
system.

The pressure decay is a function of the gas flow through the filter and the upstream volume of the filter
system. The pressure hold/decay test will confirm the integrity of the entire assembly, including housing
seals. Therefore, failure analysis for lack of seal integrity is required prior to reaching the conclusion that the
filter element itself is not integral.

If the pressure decay is below the maximum allowable value, the filter passes the physical integrity test. If
the decay and concomitant pressure drop exceed the correlated maximum-allowable value, the filter fails the
test, and an investigation should be performed (see Section 7.6).

In performing the pressure hold/decay test, the physical conditions must be held constant, as the test is
affected by interactions of temperature, system volume and filter area. The pressure hold/decay test
equipment is illustrated in Figure 11.0-3. This test commonly is performed preprocess, post-sterilization of
the filter assembly, and again after sterilizing filtration.

The following are points to consider when performing the pressure hold/decay test:

• There should be no leaks in the upstream side of the system.
• Upstream volume should be determined to ensure a proper maximum-allowable-pressure drop
calculation. It is recommended that upstream volume be kept to a minimum.
• The downstream side of the filter should be vented to the atmosphere.
• Sufficient stabilization time should be allowed before beginning the test; stabilization time
increases with larger upstream volumes and increased filter surface-areas.
• A pressure gauge or transducer with the proper degree of accuracy for determining the pressure
drop during testing should be used.
• Test time may need to be extended if the pressure-drop rate is extremely small relative to the
sensitivity of the gauge or transducer.
• The specified temperature should be maintained to ensure that the temperature does not change
during the integrity test. Note that lower upstream volumes will result in increased sensitivity to
temperature changes.

Test attributes:

• Relatively easy to perform
• No downstream manipulation is required
• Can be correlated to bacterial retention (as discussed in Section 7.1)
• Sensitivity of the test decreases as the upstream volume of the system is increased
• Resolution of the test is limited by the resolution of the pressure sensor used
• Process conditions for the test, including temperature and gas used for pressurization, should be
defined for the issuance of integrity test values.

11.4 Automated Integrity-Test Instruments

Numerous automated integrity-test instruments are available for use in the pharmaceutical industry to
determine a bubble-point pressure value using incremental pressure hold/decay tests. The pressure
hold/decay tests are performed at increasing pressures, with the bubble point identified where the pressure
decay become nonlinear, based on an algorithm specific to each instrument manufacturer. Automated
integrity-test units determine diffusive-flow values, either by measuring pressure hold/decay at a specified
test pressure and calculating the diffusive-flow value using a predetermined measurement of the upstream
volume of the filter assembly or by direct-flow measurement using an upstream mass flow meter. Another
method to determine diffusive-flow values is a system with additional small volumes to maintain constant
test pressure during the test, which eliminates the need to measure upstream volume.

Irrespective of the technique used to determine filter bubble point or diffusive flow, the automated integritytest
instrument must be qualified against an independent reference method.
