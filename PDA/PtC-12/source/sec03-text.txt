Topic 2. Physical Environment 
Q 2.1. What should be the classification of the room surrounding the restricted access 
barrier system (RABS)? 
Recommendation 
The minimum classification of the room immediately surrounding the RABS must be Grade B. However, a 
manufacturer may also choose to maintain these areas at more stringent levels of control when assessed for 
risk, especially in the area immediately surrounding the RABS doors, which may require execution of open-
door interventions (i.e., during setup or filling) (1, 4).   
Rationale 
A RABS provides an enclosed and separated environment designed to reduce the risk of contamination to 
product, components, and product-contact surfaces when compared to conventional "curtained" aseptic 
processing operations. However, RABS are not fully sealed from the immediate surrounding environment 
and, in some designs, allow for open-door interventions by aseptic operators. As such, the adjacent 
background cleanroom environment must be appropriately classified and maintained to protect the critical 
processing zone(s) within the RABS; therefore, the cleanroom environment should not be lower than Grade 
B.   
Companies should provide the maximum assurance possible and practicable so that the controlled RABS 
environment is not impacted by the surrounding environmental conditions since the RABS is not completely 
isolated from the surroundings, unlike closed isolators, for example.   
Note: The minimum regulatory expectations (e.g., FDA, EMA) for the classification of manufacturing 
spaces immediately adjacent (i.e., background) to the Grade A critical processing zone is Grade B.

Q 2.2. Which environmental monitoring criteria are required in extended unidirectional 
airflow surrounding restricted access barrier system (RABS)?  
Recommendation 
Due to the presence of the UDAF around the open-door portion of the RABS, total particulates and viables 
should always reflect Grade A limits at the working position, at rest. Locations that are found to be critical 
based on the risk assessment, should have tighter limits than Grade B under the UDAF when the RABS 
door is in the open position, while in operation.  
The remainder of the room should be subjected to sampling frequencies and limits (e.g., total particulates, 
viable air samples (active and passive)) and surface samples at a frequency appropriate for Grade B and 
adhering to Grade B limits.   
Rationale 
In the extended UDF surrounding the RABS, EM should be conducted to demonstrate the maintenance of a 
protective environment during the times when the doors are opened. The function of the extended UDF is to 
provide enhanced protection around critical zones where there is a break in the integrity of the Grade A 
RABS zone (i.e., during open-door interventions). During times when the doors are closed, this environment 
can be monitored as a more traditional Grade B environment, whereas during open-door interventions, it 
should be treated as an enhanced Grade B environment with limits (alert and action) defined based on a risk 
assessment.

Q 2.3. How should airflow pattern visualization/demonstration be performed in a 
restricted access barrier system (RABS) for airflow visualization studies (AVS)? 
Recommendation 
The purpose of airflow visualization studies (AVS) (also known as “smoke testing”) is the verification of 
unidirectional airflow maintained during all required equipment functions and manual interventions. This is 
aligned to the principle of assuring First Air in contact with product and direct and indirect product-contact 
equipment. The intention is to demonstrate that all permitted interventions are appropriately designed and 
can be executed according to the approved methodology or procedure.   
AVS should verify protective airflow is maintained at RABS entry ‘mouseholes’ without compromise to the 
process environment that protective airflow is applied to.   
An additional reason to perform an AVS is to have a visual record of process operations conducted per 
requirements to allow the following:  
•
Used as training material to demonstrate correct process execution. An important aspect about AVS
is that they should be exactly representatives of operational conditions, so people should have the
right gowning, validated loads and packages should be used.
•
Presenting to internal or external inspectors who may require visualization of a process that may not
be (fully) running at the point of inspection
•
Assess EM sampling site locations and design of interventions or intervention discard strategies. 
Note: It is important to know what the air velocity is at the working position during the airflow 
visualization testing so that a correlation can be established in routine requalification studies to demonstrate 
the continued applicability of the AVS.  
Airflow Pattern Visualization Process Activity Considerations 
It must be recognized that the act of performing airflow visualization disturbs the environment that it is 
designed to visualize due to the purposeful injection of a smoke or fogging spray into the critical zone that 
would self-evidently not occur during routine operations.   
To minimize the impact of this disturbance in the air supplied to the RABS, the following should be 
considered: 
1.
Develop a Qualification Protocol
̶
Work with affected departments (Manufacturing, Quality Assurance, Engineering, 
Microbiology) to determine and document the setup, operation, and intervention steps 
necessary for the process. The test objective is to show unidirectional airflow sweeping 
over critical surfaces.  
2.
Select Smoke-Generation Equipment Type (e.g., Fogger, Cartridge, Dry Ice)
̶
There are multiple acceptable equipment options for the generation of "smoke" for airflow 
pattern visualization testing, including foggers that use dry ice, vaporization of water for 
injection (WFI) or deionized water, or liquid nitrogen. 
̶
The use of foggers that generate glycerin-based vapor is not recommended because they 
can leave an oily residue on the test area, personnel, and/or floor areas. Such residues can 
potentially impact equipment performance, equipment cleanliness, and personnel safety and 
should be avoided.

3. Select Water Vapor-Generating Equipment 
̶ 
Water vapor-generating equipment has a variety of generation mechanisms and design 
styles, including cartridge-based (handheld) and continuous supply via external devices.   
̶ 
Depending on the specifics of the application and the RABS design, one of these styles 
may be the better choice as there would be no residue formation.   
4. Amount of Smoke  
̶ 
The amount of smoke generated should be sufficient to visualize the airflow (and possible 
turbulence), but not too much smoke to obscure the operators’ actions or machine 
operations.  
5. Smoke Speed  
̶ 
The smoke should be generated at slow speed in the opposite direction to the airflow, at 
approximately 45° against the direction of a vertical airflow and at a speed that minimizes 
disturbance. The speed of smoke injection can be checked and adjusted before the test, with 
the objective of not creating turbulence by the act of injection.   
̶ 
It should be noted that if the smoke flow was in the same direction as the normal airflow, 
that can reinforce the airflow directionality. Any perturbation to airflow (e.g., injection of 
smoke) changes the system compared to the routine operation.  
6. Smoke Generator and Smoke Diffuser (i.e., Manifold)  
̶ 
Movement of the smoke generator and smoke diffuser should be reduced to be as slow as 
possible, as recommended by good aseptic practices.   
̶ 
To limit movements, the use of a smoke diffuser of sufficient size is suggested such that it 
becomes unnecessary to move it during the AVS. 
7. Smoke Generation Location 
̶ 
The smoke is to be generated as high as possible above the working position, according to 
the design of the equipment, but not too close to the HEPA filter to avoid filter-clogging 
due to residue accumulation.   
8. Air Movement at Interfaces  
̶ 
To check air movement at interfaces to the Grade B surrounding environment (e.g., when 
doors are open), the smoke is injected inside and outside the RABS. This is done at 
different locations to demonstrate the flush of the air from the Grade A RABS and its 
demonstrated unidirectionality (when injected inside) and the absence of air ingress (when 
injected from outside) into the RABS zone.   
9. Wall and Vertical Panel Considerations 
̶ 
Injecting smoke near walls or vertical panels should be avoided, as this may create 
additional disturbances in airflow that would not occur in routine operations.  
Note: Air will flow slower along the RABS walls, and there will be minor eddies along the 
walls. This is to be expected by the application of physics. Therefore, it should never be 
necessary to perform critical operations immediately adjacent to any RABS walls.

10. Airflow Visualization Study Frequency
̶
The frequency of re-executing an AVS is not timebound in the first instance; it should be 
based primarily on events (equipment or process change control).   
̶
As periodic validation review is regarded as a qualification study, the study may need to be 
repeated for other reasons (e.g., improvements in film-capture quality over time, or 
perception that the filmed operations and manipulations are not sufficiently “current”.   
11. Open-door Intervention Considerations
̶
In the case of open-door interventions, airflow visualization qualification should be done to 
ensure that there is a unidirectional airflow flush away and out of the RABS during the 
intervention.   
Selection of Video Recording Equipment and Cinematography   
The record that exists after the execution of airflow visualization testing is a GMP record that assures the 
data integrity, a video record that may need to exist for many years. As such, careful thought must go into 
both the selection of the recording equipment and the modalities concerning film cinematography to 
maximize value of what is shown to the reviewer in the video footage. The following points and 
recommendations should be followed:  
1.
Equipment and Physical Setup
̶
The equipment used and the physical setup to video-capture the performance of the airflow 
visualization testing must be considered to ensure that the record contains sufficient detail 
to demonstrate that unidirectional flow is maintained in all required areas. A good strategy 
to ensure this is to set up multiple cameras at different angles with significantly overlapping 
fields of view.   
̶
Where applicable, built-in cameras and recording devices can be used to capture video of 
smoke studies; however, complete and detailed coverage of the study area must be 
accomplished.   
2.
Visibility of Vapor on the Video Recording
̶
The rear glass doors of the RABS may need to be covered by an opaque backing or a 
contrasting color in order to facilitate visibility in the final video footage.   
3.
Cinematography Considerations
̶
Contextualizing the cinematography by filming a timepiece that indicates the time and date 
before panning the camera to visualize the smoke directionality is recommended. Filming a 
schematic drawing of the RABS system and then the equipment section can also assist the 
viewer in this regard.  
̶
Ensure that the differential pressure across the filter and velocity were within standard 
operating limits during the AVS.  
̶
Each camera shot should be carefully prepared to ensure that no unnecessary activity is 
performed in the background (e.g., maintenance and/or cleaning).   
̶
 The video should be taken using two to four (2-4) different camera angles to facilitate, as 
much as possible, a three-dimensional view of the unidirectionality of airflow over the 
critical zones.

Practical Considerations   
Similarly, the logistics of the study execution require careful planning so that the captured film footage is 
integral and does not allow for any misinterpretation or gaps to the record. The following should be 
considered for implementation:  
•
Simulate a walk-through of interventions (dress rehearsal) to assist with seamless professional
execution that minimizes the need for extensive editing. Consider the floor communication method
(i.e., radios, hand signals) during execution if participating individuals are on opposite sides of a
RABS.
•
Simulate all manipulations strictly following good aseptic behavior, including glove
decontamination, and limit the personnel in the space to the smoke-equipment operator, aseptic
processing operators, video operator, and reviewer/approver. 
•
Filling line operation at maximum speed will require airflow visualization studies because of
potential impact on protective airflows, including 'First air'.
•
Consider bringing a cleanroom mobile device (e.g., tablet) to the film location so the video footage
can be downloaded and reviewed by approvers on the floor immediately after the test study has
been performed (subject to room classification).
•
Verify that all support systems (e.g., room HVAC, unidirectional airflow) are in a controlled and
qualified state and operational. Verify all personnel are present and gowned per routine procedure. 
•
Verify that the materials needed (i.e., smoke vapor source) are available and in sufficient
quantities. 
•
Verify any smoke-detection equipment that could trigger an alarm and/or evacuation are switched
off for the zone being filmed, in collaboration with the site security personnel. Particle and viable
counters should be in operation to mimic the routine, but additional precautions may need to be
taken to avoid damage to sensor heads.
•
Verify that the room is in an acceptable state (i.e., normal housekeeping, no work in progress in the
background of the video, no materials linked to shutdown activity stored in area being studied). 
Rationale 
Focus of the video reviewer may be diverted from the smoke study evaluation if simulated operations are 
not done with the expected level of control for the operation to be simulated (e.g., untidy areas, personnel 
not wearing required gowning). Additionally, abnormal conditions may not be considered representative of 
the routine when performing AVS.   
The integrity of the airflow visualization film footage requires assurance (e.g., date, time, continuity, 
accuracy) for both inspectional and training purposes. The firm should assess periodically the need to repeat 
smoke testing to provide assurance that they reflect current operational characteristics.   
This assessment should consider the following: 
•
Cumulative effect of process and/or facility changes
•
Quality of the video footage being improved over time
•
Improvements in the methodology of smoke generation and insertion techniques
