Q 4.4. What is the response to a glove-integrity failure? 
Recommendation 
In response to a glove-integrity failure, the actions to be taken will vary depending on when and where the 
failure is identified. The impact on previous batches must be always considered unless there is an approved 
end of batch glove integrity test available. A risk-based site policy and/or procedure should detail the actions 
required when a glove failure is discovered (1, 6). Typical responses to a glove failure include, but are not 
limited to the following:   
•
If discovered during line setup and prior to RABS cleaning, disinfecting. and/or bio-
decontamination, the nonintegral or damaged glove should be replaced, and the replacement should
be documented per the site’s procedures. 
•
If discovered after RABS cleaning, disinfecting, and/or bio-decontamination but prior to product
exposure inside the RABS, the glove should be replaced, disinfected or sterilized, and the action
documented per the site’s procedures. An investigation, including a root cause analysis of
procedures, process, and equipment, is recommended. 
•
If discovered during or after a production run, an investigation, including a root cause analysis of
procedures, process, and equipment as well as the impact on the filled product, is recommended. 
During the investigation process, the evaluation of the following elements should be considered: 
•
Time and process step when the glove failure was identified 
•
Who identified the glove failure and the associated interview 
•
RABS location of the failed glove 
•
Failure location on the glove (e.g., fingertip, palm, glove port interface) 
•
Activities performed by those wearing the failed glove 
•
Review of the gloves and RABS preventive maintenance records, including: 
̶ 
Recent glove integrity test results  
̶ 
Last glove changeout  
•
Review of the batch records, including: 
̶ 
RABS gloves Incoming Quality Assurance documentation (e.g., material specifications) 
̶ 
RABS setup/glove installation  
̶ 
RABS cleaning/disinfecting  
̶ 
Filling interventions, both qualified and unqualified  
̶ 
Any batch-related deviations  
•
Review of the operator’s qualification and training records 
•
Review of the glove integrity-test system qualification studies 
•
Review of the current unidirectional air visualization studies 
•
Review of batch EM data, both viable and total particles (See Topic 5.)
•
Review of batch bioburden samples data 
The evaluation of these materials and records should be conducted according to the site's QRM system. This 
glove-integrity investigation should be conducted such that the impact on the product is clearly understood 
and suitable corrective and preventive actions are put in place (4).

Q 4.5. How do you prevent restricted access barrier system (RABS) glove damages 
after installation? 
Recommendation 
To keep the gloves in good condition during the processing of a batch or maintenance intervention, any 
operation that could compromise glove integrity should be avoided. For example, tools with sharp edges 
(e.g., scissors), should be those with rounded or covered tips designed specifically for cleanrooms.   
Any operation that may overstretch gloves should be avoided, using instead activities within the specific 
glove range of action or using another glove in closer proximity of the intervention. When not in use, RABS 
gloves should be retracted to avoid possible interference and damage due to moving parts as well as 
disruption to First Air. When the gloves are retracted, best practice is to keep the fingers of the gloves 
extended for ease of the next use.   
See Topic 1 of this PtC for RABS design considerations related to gloves. 
Glove Replacement Criteria 
Gloves should be replaced whenever the relative conditions are different from the technical characteristics 
defined in manufacturer data sheets or from the internal risk assessment that could compromise the RABS 
aseptic internal conditions and product quality (1, 3).   
Glove replacement frequency is based on the following recommendations, which include, but are not limited 
to:   
•
Physical attributes 
•
Manufacturer expiry date 
•
Color variation from specification 
•
Loss of elasticity 
•
Viable and total particle counts trending higher 
Prior to removal of gloves from the RABS, a visual inspection and/or pressure test should be conducted 
based on an approved risk assessment of any damage. Gloves should be removed gently from the RABS 
station and carefully placed on a sterile or sanitized cart or in sterile pouches. Any other hard or sharp object 
should not be placed over the gloves after removal from RABS station as that may damage the glove during 
handling for washing and sterilization processes.   
Rationale 
Integrity of the gloves is key in order to maintain a Grade A environment within the RABS enclosure. 
Maintaining glove integrity includes procedurally controlled glove use and replacement, as well as both 
periodic and routine glove inspection or testing based on an internal risk assessment.

Q 4.6. What methods should be used for verifying the integrity of restricted access 
barrier system (RABS) gloves? 
Recommendation 
Integrity-testing of the RABS gloves should be performed by the operator using visual inspection and 
physical (integrity test) methods that are defined in the CCS or by quality risk assessment.   
There are three recommended processes to verify glove integrity; each method provides a different level of 
inspection:  
•
A visual assessment should be performed by the operator before and after each glove use (18). This
is a relatively quick visual verification for holes, tears, abrasions, and discolorations. This visual
assessment is done from the outside the RABS.
•
A thorough visual inspection should be performed by trained and qualified personnel either before
or after each system usage, batch, or campaign, including after the installation of gloves on the
RABS (1, 6, 17). It is recognized that visual inspection methods can be very effective at identifying
glove defects.
•
A physical test (pressure hold test) should be performed by using a commercially available
automated system that uses either the pressure-decay method that measures a pressure drop inside
the glove/sleeve, or the constant-pressure method that measures the leak rate as a flow. Physical
tests should be conducted for integrity-testing periodically based on a quality risk assessment or
CCS, with the trending of real-time data as an input (1, 3, 6, 17). Performing the physical test, at a
minimum, not longer than 30 days or at the end of the campaign is recommended. However, the
detection of failures, without the identification of a root cause, should result in more frequent
integrity-testing. Alternative physical tests, as long as validated, can be used.
There are two ways to perform the physical integrity-testing with a commercially available, automated 
system:  
1.
RABS gloves remain installed and are tested in place.
2.
RABS gloves are disassembled and are tested remotely.

Q 4.7. What are the acceptance criteria for pinhole detection in restricted access 
barrier system (RABS) gloves? 
Recommendation 
While there is no industry standard or governing health authority acceptance for pinhole-size breaches, any 
breach in integrity should be appropriately investigated, including holes in gloves and seams, to determine 
the impact to the RABS environment (14). While the glove material of construction and the manufacturing 
process within the RABS enclosure impact the potential glove defect size, it is recommended that, at a 
minimum, a pinhole detection system should detect a 200 µm pinhole.   
Physical glove integrity testing can be completed by using automated glove integrity-test systems that can 
detect pinhole sizes lower than what is detectible through visual inspection. Many factors affect the 
determination of the pinhole size to be qualified (e.g., glove material, glove length, glove thickness).   
Key consideration should be given to the different risks presented by pin holes. Primarily airborne 
contamination transfer that can result in a momentary pressurization of gloves or finger pockets at glove 
entry, reversing the pressure differential. In addition, surface contamination transfer from cleanroom gloves 
fingers can transfer contamination by touch if the pinhole is at or greater than the glove thickness. For each 
glove type intended for use, a testing cycle should be developed and qualified with the glove integrity-test 
system to determine the appropriate pinhole detection limit based on the risks of contamination transfer that 
glove pinholes present.  
Additionally, any proposed acceptance criteria for pinholes should be evaluated through a comprehensive 
risk assessment. This risk assessment should consider the manufacturing process, personnel training and 
qualification, specifications of the RABS enclosure, gloves, and use of an automated glove integrity-tester. 
The glove management strategy should include an integrity testing procedure (which combines both visual 
and physical testing means) and the rationale for detection size.  All of these must be documented in a CCS. 
There are many other areas of control that should be evaluated prior to justifying pinhole acceptance 
criterion including, but not limited to (4):  
•
Quality of glove 
•
Tools 
•
Training (ensuring gloves stay away from critical surfaces)
•
Detection (visual and physical) 
•
Preventive maintenance
•
Gowning 
•
Materials 
For example, if a company selected a thinner commercially available glove (e.g., 0.2 mm) when there are 
other commercially available gloves (e.g., 0.4 mm), the increase in thickness can assist in the reduction and 
overall control of the glove. Furthermore, an evaluation of the use of tools to support aseptic operations may 
be appropriate, for example, using a needle recapping device as opposed to an operator manually recapping, 
in order to reduce pinholes.

Q 4.8. What is the appropriate process and frequency for restricted access barrier 
system (RABS) gloves sterilization, bio-decontamination, sporicidal disinfection, and/or 
disinfection? 
Recommendation 
For RABS, gloves used in the Grade A area should be sterilized before installation and sterilized or 
effectively biodecontaminated again by a validated method prior to each manufacturing campaign (1, 9). If 
exposed to the background environment during operation, disinfection using an approved methodology 
following each exposure should be completed (1).   
The frequency of sterilization, bio-decontamination-sporicidal disinfection, and disinfection is based on an 
approved risk assessment considering the following:   
•
Sterilization or bio-decontamination
•
Prior to batch or campaign operation. In between batches of a campaign, no bio-decontamination or
disinfection is required, assuming the physical integrity and system functionality of the RABS has
not been breached (e.g., doors remained closed, HVAC system functioning).
•
Sporicidal Disinfection or Disinfection
•
Either between batches of a campaign or between batches
•
Open-door intervention when exposed to external background during batch operation such as an
open-door intervention. Each intervention should be individually assessed for its impact and
determine the full scope of disinfection needed after the intervention. At a minimum, the
intervention requires the disinfection of all Grade B-exposed surfaces inside the RABS (e.g., RABS
door, RABS glove port). If cleaning or disinfecting agents are likely to leave residues, a follow-up
wipe remove step (e.g., with sterile 70% IPA) may be required.
•
If RABS gloves are exposed during the intervention, their disinfection is required as part of the
intervention. Typically, RABS gloves are inverted (turned “inside out”) when not in use and should
not require disinfection even in an open-door situation (See Figure 1.4-1 for visual representation
of inversion).
•
The application of either sporicidal disinfection or disinfection must be defined in the CCS.
When new RABS gloves are installed (e.g., glove replacement during batch operation in the Grade A 
environment), they are required to be sterilized before installation. Additionally, RABS gloves are to be 
sterilized or effectively biodecontaminated by a validated method prior to each manufacturing campaign 
(1).  
Rationale 
To ensure product quality, gloves should be effectively sterilized before installation and biodecontaminated 
during usage (e.g., prior to each manufacturing campaign). The effectiveness of this sterilization, bio-
decontamination, sporicidal disinfection, and disinfection regime should be verified through a qualified EM 
program.

Topic 5. Environmental Monitoring 
Q 5.1. What should the environmental monitoring (EM) frequency be in a closed or 
open restricted access barrier system (cRABS or oRABS)? 
Recommendation 
The frequency of EM for oRABS and cRABS should be the same; continuous viable and total particle 
monitoring is required for the full duration of critical activities, including setup (1). (See Question 5.5.)  
Critical activities start at the point when a product-contact part and/or a primary container or closure are 
transferred and are exposed to the inside of the RABS. Where the RABS is subdivided into discrete zones, 
the point where critical activities start and end may well be different within the different zones; hence, the 
continuous EM may start at different times in each zone (4).  
Continuous viable (i.e., a combination of active and passive air monitoring) and total particle monitoring 
should begin at the start of the aseptic setup and should continue up until the end of filling. Typically, this is 
the end of the batch or campaign.  
The RABS should be well designed to minimize the need for interventions involving opening the doors. By 
exception and where required, the doors should only be opened under strictly defined conditions, which 
should be procedurally defined and pre-assessed as part of the QRM. The conditions for the opening of the 
doors and controls in place to mitigate risks should be considered and documented as part of the overall 
CCS.  
Appropriate controls should be in place to minimize the risk posed by the intervention through the opening 
of the doors and should extend to consideration of further disinfection steps. Additionally, controls should 
extend to the need to undertake a partial line clearance, for example, to discard exposed primary packaging 
containers or other direct product-contact parts located close to the intervention (e.g., vials, syringes, 
stoppers located on the stopper track). While sterility testing cannot be used to rationalize poor practices or 
otherwise support the undertaking of a high-risk intervention, based on risk, additional sterility samples 
should be considered as part of an additional control measure for critical interventions (4).   
The same EM program applies to both product manufacture and APS.  
Rationale 
There is no difference in expectations around the frequency of EM inside oRABS and cRABS. 
Once a critical surface is exposed inside the RABS, that surface is potentially at risk of contamination, so 
continuous EM is required.

Q 5.2. Which surfaces should be monitored in a restricted access barrier system (RABS) 
(open or closed)? 
Recommendation 
Firms should conduct a risk assessment to define which surfaces of a RABS to monitor after use. Swabs 
should be used for complex geometric surfaces; contact plates should be used for flat surfaces. Sampling is 
only representative; not all surfaces can be sampled (e.g., not all filling needles and not all other surfaces 
above the critical surfaces).  
Typically, based on risk, firms will sample: 
•
Direct- and indirect-contact parts
•
Sterile tools that contact or breach First Air over contact parts or sterile primary packaging during
the batch/campaign
•
RABS intervention gloves used during the batch/campaign for critical interventions*
•
RTP doors, if used, where the door breaches First Air over a critical surface
•
Parts above the critical surfaces that cannot be sterilized
*Note: When RABS intervention gloves are used ambidextrously, considerations should be given to EM
sampling on multiple sides of gloves. This should be assessed using QRM principles.
Other locations linked to the risk assessment may also be sampled (e.g., occluded/hard to reach surfaces). 
Surface samples are taken at the end of use (i.e., the batch or campaign). The surfaces sampled should be 
returned to a state suitable for aseptic manufacturing before the parts are reused. Typically, sterilized 
surfaces will be removed, subjected to a qualified cleaning process, and then resterilized. Other surfaces, 
including barrier gloves, should be cleaned and returned to a state suitable for aseptic manufacture.  
Rationale 
Surface monitoring is typically aimed at surfaces that have the greatest potential impact on product sterility 
because they directly or indirectly contact the product, or they are permitted to breach First Air (e.g., 
forceps).  
A risk assessment is required to select and scientifically justify both the surfaces to be sampled and locations 
on those surfaces that are most representative of the risk. Sampling the entire product-contact surface is not 
necessary; instead, risk-based sampling should occur on the parts most likely to have been put at risk during 
installation or use.  
RABS gloves are used for interventions; therefore, they are required to be sterile before installation and bio-
decontaminated in situ thereafter. During use, RABS gloves are likely to contact surfaces inside the RABS 
that have been disinfected, not sterilized. Where open-door interventions are permitted, a barrier glove may 
well reside in a lower grade for the duration of the intervention and then be disinfected as the door is shut. 
Also, RABS gloves are made of flexible materials prone to puncture. RABS gloves that may have come into 
proximity with or breach First Air over sterile primary packaging, open product, or product-contact parts 
potentially puts the product at risk and should be sampled; such events should be avoided whenever 
possible.  
Tools used for critical interventions should be sampled. Consideration should be given to the use of 
dedicated sterile tools that are used exclusively for conducting certain types of interventions (e.g., involving

Q 5.3. What factors should be taken into consideration when transferring 
environmental monitoring (EM) plates into and out of a restricted access barrier system 
(RABS)? 
Recommendation 
Plates should be brought into the surrounding cleanroom sterile and suitably packaged for transfer into the 
RABS.   
Plates should be transferred into and out of the RABS following QRM principles and good aseptic working 
practices. Suitable methods include, but are not limited to:   
•
Plates sterilized in beta bags or containers that are transferred via alpha RTP ports in the RABS.
•
Plates sterilized in impermeable bags that enter and exit the RABS via an integral bio-
decontamination (e.g., VPHP), rapid transfer airlock, or transfer chamber.
•
Transfer into the RABS occurs prior to line setup, with the outer bag being removed or disinfected
at the Grade transition point. Exit occurs after the batch is complete. Transport to the testing
laboratory should be done in a manner that prevents contamination.
•
Using plate sliders, with the outer surfaces of the plate being disinfected at the Grade transition
point. Care must be taken to ensure that no disinfectant goes inside the plate. Design should ensure
that no airflow enters the RABS from the cleanroom. An example of a plate slider can be seen in
Figure 5.3-1.
Figure 5.3-1: Plate Slider 
It is not recommended to open RABS doors to transfer EM plates in or out of the RABS after line setup is 
complete or when critical surfaces are exposed.  
Rationale 
To provide reliable results, plates need to be sterile at the point of exposure.  
RABS doors should not be opened after line setup; this includes the transfer of EM plates. 
RTPs provide a method for safe entry of materials, including EM plates.   
Transfer chambers provide a method whereby the risk posed by the entry of material previously exposed to 
Grade B conditions can be controlled by a combination of outer bag removal and sporicidal disinfection.
