原文內容 Original Text

5.0 Qualification and Verification of Suppliers, Materials, Components, and Completed Assemblies

5.1 Introduction

The qualification and verification of suppliers, materials, components, and completed assemblies require attention to several factors. It is important to use a risk-based and science-based approach to qualify both materials and completed assemblies for SUSs in pharmaceutical development and manufacture (18). The assessment of risk must be based on the complexity of the system and its intended use (e.g., product contact versus non-product contact, upstream versus downstream use, short-term contact versus long-term storage). See PDA's Technical Report No. 54 on Quality Risk Management for more information (7). In addition, scientific principles must be applied to 1) identify and monitor extractable materials from films and other components; 2) investigate potential interactions with product CQAs or significant process parameters like cell culture media; and 3) assess the effects of assembly processes such as welding, fusing, mechanical stress, and sterilization (e.g., gamma irradiation) on the materials during manufacturing. In assessing the suitability of the SUS with respect to potential leachables in the final product, extractables may be considered as potential in-process leachables. Finally, the design and functional integrity of the completed SUS must be verified and maintained by appropriately trained end users via suitable processes for construction, packaging, shipping, and deployment.

The supplier of the SUS must provide sufficient data to qualify the materials and components used to assemble the final SUS. In this regard, the end user may use the documents from the supplier to meet most of the verification criteria required to make sure that the SUS is fit for the intended application and purpose. This requires a thorough qualification of the supplier by the end user to ascertain an acceptable supplier quality system, documentation, and an appropriate level of technical capability that extends all the way to the original sources of materials to the supplier. In addition, the suppliers must be able to provide documented evidence of performance claims for off-the-shelf component designs for the SUS. A supplier quality agreement must underpin any custom designs that the end user may need from the supplier.

The first step in a science- and risk-based approach is to identify and assess the risks associated with using an SUS. The end user must identify that the SUS supplier's approach to the design and selection of materials of construction includes components and materials such as:

  • Filters
  • Sensors
  • Polymers
  • Lubricants
  • Slip agents

Supplier quality, manufacturing and analytical capabilities should also be evaluated. The end user must fully qualify and audit a number of potential suppliers before embarking on extensive studies of specific materials because it is preferable to have more than one supplier for the SUS.

It is important for the end user to have access to controlled documentation of the qualification studies conducted on the materials and components of the assembled SUS. Such documentation may include:

  • Batch numbers traceable to the raw materials
  • Product shelf life
  • Certificate of quality
  • Certificate of integrity
  • Endotoxin certification (to required and specific endotoxin level)
  • Animal-free certification
  • Sterilization certification (if sterility is claimed)

A robust change control communication and assessment process between the end user and the supplier (including sub-suppliers) must be established to monitor any changes in processing conditions, equipment, analytical methods, manufacturing site, resins, films, additives, and more. If the supplier does not have an appropriate assessment process in place, the end user may have to perform independent testing. After performing material and component qualification of the assembled products, a functional verification of the SUS should be performed according to the following:

  • Risk assessment — To confirm that the assembled products are fit for purpose
  • Process verification and validation — To confirm that the completed SUS functions within process constraints, including repetitive use or extended storage (if required)
  • Safety — To make sure that the SUS conforms to safety requirements as a pressure vessel (especially for larger volumes) or for containment in the event of rupture or leak

If the SUS assembly includes SU sensors, calibration and additional verification documentation must be supplied.

核心概念解析 Key Concepts

Risk-based and Science-based Approach (風險導向與科學導向方法)：這是整個 SUS 確認流程的核心原則。不是所有 SUS 都需要相同程度的測試 — 風險越高的應用（例如長期儲存藥物原料藥），確認的深度與廣度就越大。科學導向則強調以數據驅動決策，而非單純依循慣例。

Supplier Quality Agreement (供應商品質協議)：這是終端使用者與 SUS 供應商之間的正式契約，規範品質期望、變更控制通知義務、稽核權利等。對於客製化設計尤其重要。

Change Control (變更管控)：SUS 的供應鏈涉及多層次的供應商（樹脂廠、薄膜廠、組裝廠），任何一層的變更（如更換添加劑或製造廠址）都可能影響最終產品品質。

比喻說明 Analogy

想像你經營一家高級餐廳，決定從陶瓷餐具（傳統不鏽鋼設備）改用高品質免洗餐具（SUS）。你不能隨便在大賣場買塑膠盤子就開始用 — 你需要確認供應商的食品安全認證、材料成分、耐溫性能，還要確保供應商若更換原料配方時會通知你。這就像 SUS 的確認流程：從原料樹脂到最終組裝，每一環都需要被驗證。

重點提示 Key Notes

確認文件清單是稽核的基礎：批號追溯、保質期、品質證書、完整性證書、內毒素認證、無動物源認證、滅菌認證 — 這些文件不僅是合規要求，更是製程出問題時追根溯源的關鍵工具。

功能驗證三要素：風險評估（確認適用性）、製程驗證與確效（確認功能性）、安全性（確認壓力容器合規）。這三層驗證缺一不可。

5.2 Risks Associated with Using Single-Use Systems 5.2 使用一次性使用系統之相關風險

原文內容 Original Text

The risks associated with the use of SUSs include that to the patient, the process, and operators. The risk to the process may occur as a result of process failure, but it rarely affects patients because of process discontinuation. Normal safety precautions for handling large volumes of liquids, which may also be hazardous, must be maintained. The main focus of this discussion is the risk to the patient. Begin risk assessment by considering the potential for the SUS to interact with product or process fluids. Some of the important parameters are point of use, duration of contact, process conditions (e.g., temperature, presence of solvents, and the ratio of surface area to volume). The greatest risk is associated with long-term (>30 days) storage containers of certain drug products (19). The degree of risk is different for upstream applications because of the ability to use further processing steps to remove modified products or leached compounds and the dilutive effect of multiple filtration and washing steps. Ishikawa (fishbone) diagrams can be used to map the process and then identify and characterize potential areas of concern (Figure 5.2-1).

For assembly error or individual component failure, complex systems with multiple valves, manifolds, and sensors may present more risks than simple systems (horizontal axis). Applications that are more demanding or close to the final step may pose higher risks (vertical axis). Condensates may get back to the process if fluid pathways are not designed properly. In order of increasing risk are the following components:

1. Nonfluid contact systems

  • Vent tubing and filter assemblies
  • Samples and sampling systems for repetitive use

Sampling bags are considered a separate category, as the contained product is removed from the process; however, the potential for interactions with bags to interfere with the outcome of assays must be determined.

2. Fluid contact systems

  • Short-term contact, simple components (e.g., bags for cleaning materials, buffer storage and concentrates, antifoam, additives, and media)

Mixing systems have additional requirements for establishing functional performance and are available in a range of designs (refer to Section 4.8.3):

  • Bottom and top mixing
  • Rocking mixing
  • Cleaning, flushing, and draining
  • Long-term storage (storage, holding, and delivery systems with impact on temperature, moisture loss, and gas adsorption)

3. Complex systems

  • Bioreactors, including instrumentation (pH, temperature, pressure, conductivity, gases, nutrients with impact on mixing, cell viability, cell integrity, adhesion, and shear)
  • Predispensed buffer or media additives (concentrates as well as commercial and proprietary blends of media)

4. Product contact and storage systems

  • Tubing, filters, valves, connectors, and manifolds
  • Process intermediate storage containers
  • Bulk drug-substance storage containers
  • Shipping containers
  • Disposable filling needles

核心概念解析 Key Concepts

三類風險對象：SUS 使用的風險可分為對病患（最關鍵）、對製程（導致批次失敗）、對操作人員（安全危害）。TR66 特別聚焦於病患風險。

風險評估的關鍵參數：

  • 使用位置 (Point of use)：上游 vs 下游，離最終產品越近風險越高
  • 接觸時間 (Duration of contact)：>30天的長期儲存風險最大
  • 製程條件：溫度、溶劑存在、表面積/體積比

Ishikawa Diagram (魚骨圖)：一種品質管理工具，用於系統性地識別造成問題的所有可能原因，將原因分類為人、機、料、法、環等大類別。

風險分級就像建築安全等級：臨時工棚（非接觸系統）的安全要求最低，住宅（流體接觸系統）中等，醫院和核電廠（產品接觸與儲存系統）的安全標準最嚴格。越接近「最終使用者」（病患）的環節，品質要求就越高。

重點提示 Key Notes

上游 vs 下游風險差異：上游應用（如細胞培養）的風險相對較低，因為後續還有多道純化、過濾和清洗步驟可以去除或稀釋 leachables。但下游應用（如藥物原料藥儲存）沒有後續的純化機會，風險最高。

四級風險分類（由低到高）：

  1. 非流體接觸系統 — 排氣管路、取樣系統
  2. 流體接觸系統 — 緩衝液袋、培養基袋
  3. 複雜系統 — 生物反應器、預配培養基
  4. 產品接觸與儲存系統 — 管路、閥門、原料藥儲存容器

5.3 Single-Use System Assembly 5.3 一次性使用系統組裝

原文內容 Original Text

SUSs include fittings, tubing, filters, connectors, bag material (film), and outer packaging. SU components are normally received and stored in areas with minimal environmental control. It is critical for the components to remain in their sealed packaging, and they should be handled with gloves to avoid contamination. Transport into the assembly room must follow standard clean-room practices. All the external packaging of the SU components should be wiped with a suitable antimicrobial or cleansing agent before moving them into the assembly room. The assembly rooms are typically ISO 7 or 8 with a combination of particle and microbial sampling. It is important to design manufacturing suites with HEPA-filtered air, low returns, smooth surfaces, and airlocks for entry and exit. Avoid introducing any sharp edges or corners into the assembly room because they may perforate the SUS bags. Personnel must be trained and wear appropriate clothing for an ISO 7 operation and the assembly room must be cleaned regularly. Where SUSs are preassembled, the supplier takes these steps.

導師解析 Tutorial Commentary

核心概念解析 Key Concepts

SUS 組裝環境要求：SUS 元件通常在環境控制要求較低的區域接收與存放，但組裝必須在 ISO 7 或 ISO 8 等級的潔淨室中進行。這與傳統不鏽鋼設備的製造環境（通常為一般工廠環境）有顯著不同。

關鍵控制措施：密封包裝保持完整、戴手套操作、外包裝消毒擦拭、HEPA 過濾空氣、人員培訓與著裝、避免尖銳物品。

比喻說明 Analogy

SUS 組裝就像在手術室中組裝無菌器材 — 你不能在走廊上打開無菌包裝。即使元件本身已經包裝好了，組裝的環境也必須達到一定的潔淨度，因為組裝過程中的任何污染都可能被密封在最終的系統裡。特別提到「避免尖銳邊角」就像手術室不允許有可能刺破手術手套的物品一樣。

重點提示 Key Notes

CDMO 實務考量：如果 CDMO 自行組裝 SUS，就必須維護符合 ISO 7/8 標準的專用組裝室，包括環境監測計畫（粒子計數 + 微生物取樣）。若選擇由供應商預組裝 (preassembled)，則這些責任轉移給供應商 — 但稽核責任仍在 CDMO 身上。

5.4 Supplier Qualification of Single-Use Systems 5.4 一次性使用系統之供應商確效

原文內容 Original Text

Since a typical SUS installation requires the acquisition of several components from various external suppliers, it is important for the end user to qualify suppliers to ensure that they can deliver SU components that meet or exceed regulatory and customer expectations.

5.4.1 Supplier Audits and Technical Diligence

It is important to audit the supplier's quality systems as part of the supplier qualification process to ensure delivery of consistent SU components to the end user. An understanding of the supplier's capabilities may lead to a better understanding of end-user expectations and can help identify any gaps in the capabilities of the supplier. Therefore, the auditing step must encompass manufacturing and technical aspects, and both parties must agree on corrective actions as appropriate. Microbiological control measures for SUSs must be evaluated as part of a supplier audit.

The auditor should review the procedure and records for quarterly dose audits where the SUS is subjected to gamma-irradiation sterilization.

Standard auditing practices, extending to a review of quality systems and complaint handling, should be followed. Unique to SUSs for inclusion in audits would be the supplier's definition of change and how the change-control process functions, including customer notification. The specific particulate control and reduction schemes in place at the supplier should also be evaluated.

Many SUS suppliers may either be ISO 9001 or medical device manufacturers under FDA and ISO registration. ISO 9001 registration means a company operates in a quality management framework that has been audited by a registrar and confirmed as adhering to ISO 9001 guidelines (20). The manufacturing facilities of registered medical devices are subject to inspection and follow 21 CFR Part 820 (cGMPs for Medical Devices) and ISO 13485 (21,22). SUSs, including bulk drug containers, are considered process equipment rather than medical devices; therefore, the manufacturing facilities of SUS suppliers are not subject to regulatory registration or inspection. Although SUS manufacturers tend to design their quality systems along GMP guidelines, the auditor should not expect these systems to adhere strictly to cGMPs for drug or medical device manufacturing (21,23). ISO 9001 is considered a minimum requirement.

Pertinent factors for a technical diligence assessment and an interpretation of its implementation over the clinical development lifecycle are reviewed in greater detail in Section 7.7.3.

5.4.2 Supplier Quality Agreements and Responsibilities

The purpose of a supplier quality agreement is to set quality expectations, identify and develop a mutual understanding of pertinent regulations and guidance documents, and provide advice on managing quality concerns such as audits, complaints, and change control. Change control can be a complex process for an SUS because of the diverse number of suppliers (e.g., compounders, film producers, and bag assemblers) and materials (e.g., resins and additives) involved in the supply chain. The supplier must provide full transparency on the full supply chain of the SUS and guarantee quality oversight of the supply chain. The supplier must have a robust supply chain, which may require improvements, which can be developed with the support of the customer in a partnership mind-set. The extent to which the primary supplier manages the overall supply chain should be assessed, and if necessary, audit and technical diligence activities should be extended to the original supplier of materials and components. It is important to establish a cordial working relationship between the end user and supplier because an SUS supplier will inevitably become an extended part of the end user's manufacturing and quality systems.

導師解析 Tutorial Commentary

核心概念解析 Key Concepts

Supplier Qualification (供應商確認)：這不只是簡單的「核准供應商清單」管理。對 SUS 供應商的確認需要深入了解其品質體系、製造能力、技術專業度，以及最重要的 — 變更管控流程。

ISO 9001 vs ISO 13485 vs cGMP：

  • ISO 9001：通用品質管理體系標準，是 SUS 供應商的最低要求
  • ISO 13485：醫療器材品質管理體系標準
  • 21 CFR Part 820：FDA 醫療器材 cGMP 法規

重要區分：SUS 被視為製程設備而非醫療器材，因此其製造設施不受法規註冊或查廠的約束。這意味著終端使用者的稽核責任更重。

比喻說明 Analogy

SUS 供應商的角色就像餐廳的食材供應商 — 他們不受「餐飲業衛生法規」直接管轄（SUS 不是醫療器材），但你的餐廳（藥廠）出了食安問題，最終責任還是在你身上。所以你必須主動去稽核你的食材供應商的衛生管理、冷鏈控制、變更通知機制等，而不能只依賴他們的「自我聲明」。

重點提示 Key Notes

SUS 稽核的獨特重點：

  • 變更定義與通知：供應商如何定義「變更」？多小的改變需要通知客戶？這在 SUS 中至關重要，因為即使微小的樹脂配方變更也可能改變 E&L profile
  • 微粒控制：SUS 中的微粒是長期以來的產業痛點，必須評估供應商的微粒控制與減少方案
  • 伽馬射線滅菌劑量稽核：季度劑量稽核記錄是確認滅菌有效性的關鍵

合作夥伴心態 (Partnership Mindset)：TR66 特別強調 SUS 供應商將「不可避免地成為終端使用者製造和品質體系的延伸」。這不是一般的買賣關係，而是深度合作關係。

實務應用 Practical Application

CDMO 場景：假設你的 CDMO 同時服務 5 個客戶，每個客戶使用不同的 SUS 配置。你不可能為每個客戶維護完全獨立的供應商確認體系。實務上，你需要建立一個「平台化」的供應商確認框架 — 先確認核心供應商（薄膜、管路、連接器等），再根據個別客戶的產品風險等級決定是否需要額外的專案級確認。

5.5 Qualification of Alternative Suppliers 5.5 替代供應商確效

原文內容 Original Text

The use of SUS permits considerable flexibility in the choice of materials, designs, and suppliers. This flexibility can be used to minimize risk by ensuring that multiple configurations are viable options for any given requirement. There are multiple combinations of films, bag designs, tubing, filters, connectors, and so on, that are capable of delivering or receiving a specified volume of process fluids to the required standard. Therefore, it is advisable for the end user to qualify an alternative supplier as part of the SUS risk management system (7). Risk- and science-based assessment must be used to determine the level of testing and validation required. In a case involving minimal risk, it may be sufficient to treat an SUS from a secondary supplier as representative of the same class of system or material from the primary supplier. The data generated for the original SUS can be used and compared to that from the secondary supplier. With higher-risk cases (e.g., bioreactor bags or product storage), the secondary supplier's component must be subjected to the same comprehensive qualification process as that of the primary supplier. Note that different suppliers may adopt different approaches to tackle a particular issue and still remain within the bounds of an acceptable supplier quality system. For instance, supplier A may test systems for bioburden from every lot whereas supplier B may test representative samples from different lots as proof of an overall bioburden management system. Both approaches may be acceptable and should be reflected in the user specifications (Table 5.5.2-1🔍). An industry effort to standardize SUS testing methods would improve end users' ability to compare alternative SUS suppliers and adequately assess testing differences between suppliers.

5.5.1 Qualification of Alternative Sources

End users typically have two options when it comes to alternative sources: 1) the same SUS supplier with alternative sources of raw materials, or 2) an alternative SUS supplier. Regardless of the choice, the qualification of an alternative source should be part of the drug substance manufacturer's risk-management system. The user should qualify the alternative supplier or raw material source in the same thorough way as the primary supplier or source.

With each option, the risk- and science-based assessment will continue to determine the level of testing and validation required.

The issues under consideration differ somewhat for each option. Suppliers may qualify alternate raw-material sources as part of their risk mitigation strategy at the level of the resins, additives, or the film itself (if sourced separately). For applications with low potential for process or product impact, it might be sufficient to review the data from the supplier's qualification. For applications with high potential for impact, the user may need to consider additional testing (e.g. cytotoxicity, stability). For films sourced separately, differences in formulations and manufacturing processes may occur, and the physiochemical properties and extractable profile of the alternate film can be quite different from the original. Such differences, however, should not be the deciding factor for the suitability of this alternative material for the SUS. The suitability of the alternative material should be determined by the end user depending on the intended use of the SUS.

For an alternative supplier, data generated for the original material can be used and compared to the data of the alternative supplier source. In high-risk cases (e.g., bioreactor bags or storage of bulk drug substance), the second supplier's component has to be treated as a specific instance, and full-scale qualification is required.

5.5.2 Interchangeability

It is important for end users to qualify alternative sources of films to safeguard against supply disruptions and possible shortages. In some situations this may be a minimal risk (e.g., sampling bag used for monitoring cell growth or nutrient conditions), so a comparison of extractables and leachables data from different suppliers may be acceptable; other situations may be more demanding (e.g., storage of bulk drug substance). SUS interchangeability can be considered if functionally equivalent substitution provides a contingency or process alternative while ensuring process performance and product quality. When considering SUS interchangeability, it is important to assess the component's intended function, anticipated processing conditions, and fitness for use (Table 5.5.2-1🔍). In every case, the approach taken should be supported by a sound risk- and science-based assessment with appropriate evaluation and performance verification for the type of substitution desired.

For example, a bag or assembly used simply as a container for process fluids with no product contact has limited critical attributes—the bag should not leak and there should be an acceptable leachables profile. If the assembly has the additional function of acting as a mixer, the design would need to be assessed for functional performance, in this case minimum mixing conditions (time, speed, and so forth, depending on the mechanism) to achieve homogeneity. Once the design is qualified, this would not need to be established for every lot. For an in-process product container potential interaction with the product would need to be examined and deemed appropriate for the conditions (e.g., liquid, frozen, storage time). The presence of particulates may also need to be assessed, depending on where the alternative component is used in the process. For a complex assembly such as a bioreactor, a full-scale assessment should be conducted on the design for performance parameters and attributes (mixing, pH control, sparging, temperature control, cell growth, viability, titer/yield, etc.). The potential impact of differences in growth conditions on product quality should be included.

*Risk Assessment Focus Descriptions:

  1. Design, dimension, performance, and materials claims of the proposed substitute (form, fit, and function)
  2. Materials of construction and process contact fluid compatibility, contact area, and time
  3. Availability and extent of supplier biosafety (USP <87> and/or <88>, as appropriate for the application) and extractables data (24,25)
  4. Suitability of supplier extractables data to bracket process fluid and conditions
  5. Application and potential for subsequent leachable removal or dilution
  6. Product impact assessment

When considering interchangeability, in most cases other than like-for-like, the end user will need to do some level of operational or extractable/leachable testing to verify fitness for use. Different dimensions or even top or bottom mixing can have product impact. As discussed in Section 5, Business Drivers for the Adoption of Single-use Systems, the cost of quality can be defined as "any cost that would not have been expended if quality were perfect." The additional end-user operational testing of SUS and related quality control activities to ensure process comparability for SU interchangeability could be included in the cost of quality and should then be considered in the overall SUS implementation costs. After all, if a supplier had perfect interchangeability, this end-user cost would be zero.

5.5.3 Using Supplier Quality Documentation

The end user may employ the supplier documentation as part of the SUS document verification process so long as the end user has audited the supplier and collected the requisite evidence of an acceptable supplier quality system, technical capability, and application of Good Engineering Practices (GEP) guidelines (Table 5.5.3-1🔍) (18). If there is not strict adherence to GEP, then there should be assurance that documentation is accurate and suitable to meet the purpose of verification.

5.6 Extractables and Leachables (E&L) 5.6 可萃取物與溶出物（E&L）

原文內容 Original Text

Extractables may be defined as chemical entities that can be extracted from the components of a material by exertion of an exaggerated force (e.g., organic solvent, extreme temperature, ionic strength, pH, contact time). Leachables may be defined as chemical entities that can migrate from product-contact or non-product-contact surfaces into a process stream. Leachables may also include chemical entities that can migrate from contact surfaces into the bulk drug substance or final product under specified conditions of production, storage, and use. While leachables are considered a subset of extractables, they can also be derived from product-contact or non-product-contact materials, under typical process conditions and detected in final dosage. The evaluation of extractables is an essential step in the accurate prediction of leachables and in the selection of a suitable SUS dedicated to the manufacture of a given product. When chemical substances are identified that could be cleared or sufficiently diluted by downstream processes so as to be undetected, these can be termed as in-process leachables for the final dosage and may need to be accounted for during risk assessment. Refer to Table 4.8-1🔍 for a summary of common materials of construction and comments regarding their respective E&L properties.

Drug manufacturers are encouraged to perform a risk-assessment analysis as part of an E&L evaluation within the overall context of enhancing process performance and product safety and efficacy. The accumulation of extractables and leachables in a process fluid is governed by thermodynamic (i.e., the extent to which the materials would migrate) and kinetic (i.e., the rate at which materials would migrate) factors, as well as the amount of time during which such migration would occur. Higher temperatures increase the migration rate of leachables from the bulk of the plastic to the surface in contact with the process stream or formulation. The user can determine the worst-case extent of the extractables by determining the highest temperature at which extractions cease and the entire additive has been removed from the plastic. E&L risk factors can be generally categorized as risk to the patient and risk to the process. In evaluating risks, an extractable or leachable and the level at which it appears will need to be evaluated for impact on process and product quality and for patient safety. The following risk factors may be considered:

  • Proximity of the process steps undergoing a change to the final product
  • Storage and processing conditions (e.g., duration of exposure, temperature, pressure, pH extremes, buffer extraction propensity)
  • The type of process fluid (e.g., purification buffer versus formulated drug substance, presence of solubilizing agents)
  • Construction materials (e.g., PVC containers, bags, and tubing are prone to leaching diethylhexyl phthalate, which has adverse effects on the liver, testis, mammary, nerve, immune system, blood, and fatty tissue) (26,27).
  • Potential adverse effects, including synergistic and additive affects
  • Drug dose, mode, and frequency of administration
  • Therapeutic necessity of the drug (included in drug dose, mode, and frequency)

The presence of leachables during any stage of the production process or storage may pose a safety risk due to their potential to cause toxicity, carcinogenicity, immunogenicity, and/or endocrine dysregulation (28,29). In addition, these substances may adversely impact the physicochemical characteristics (e.g., via aggregation, oxidation, degradation, and formation of particulates) of the final protein product. It has been demonstrated that leachables have adverse effects on cell growth and viability (30). Lastly, the potential for leachables to impact quality control tests should be considered. For these reasons, proper selection and handling of an SUS that supports the production and processing of safe and efficacious therapeutic biologic products is of paramount importance.

Potential sources of extractables and leachables include SU materials used in manufacturing, packaging, storage, filtration, and transfer systems. These include but are not limited to SU bioreactors, bags for intermediate and long-term storage, containers, filters, transfer tubing, valves, fittings, housings, and so on. Extractables and leachables are chemically diverse compounds that may include but are not limited to phthalates (e.g., plasticizers), organic acids, hydrocarbons, cyclic esters derived from urethane adhesives, antioxidants, residual solvents, antistatic and cleaning agents, as well as trace metals and degradation products from irradiation.

5.6.1 Material and Supplier Qualification

It is important for end users to qualify alternative sources of films to safeguard against supply disruptions and possible shortages. There will be situations where this may be a minimal risk (e.g., bags used for sampling), so a comparison of extractables data from different suppliers may be acceptable; other situations may be more demanding (e.g., storage of bulk drug substance). In every case, the approach taken should be supported by a sound risk- and science-based assessment.

Detection, characterization, identification, and quantitation of leachables may present analytical challenges, since these substances represent diverse chemical classes of organic and/or inorganic compounds that coexist in complex mixtures in trace amounts (31). Therefore, well-designed extractables studies are important to provide an analytical road map and to identify early warning signs regarding leachables released during upstream or downstream operations or in storage. Emerging analytical technologies with appropriate sensitivity and specificity may be considered in addition to the currently available methods during the design of such studies. If an extractable may have an adverse effect on the physicochemical or biological properties of a protein, performing characterization studies in which the extractable is spiked into the product may be of value in assessing the potential risk to product quality.

In order to isolate chemicals from plastic components, the study of extractables begins with the selection of a suitable exaggerated condition that is not stipulated for the normal manufacture, storage, or use of the SUS. It is recommended that the extraction studies be performed with material components using the exaggerated conditions with regards to pH, ionic strength, contact time, temperature, surface area–to–volume ratio, and, if applicable, organic solvents of varying polarity and surfactant properties as the extraction media. If the material component in question is not compatible with organic solvents, aqueous media may be used. If a prerinse step is used in routine manufacturing and use of material component, it should be included in the design of the leachables studies but not in the extractables studies because the latter are intended to represent a worst-case situation. Additional pretreatment of plastics such as gamma irradiation and steam treatment can also impact the E&L profile.

A representative sample of process fluid (e.g., cell culture media and buffer) can be used as the extraction media in addition to others listed above for accurate prediction of extractables. If a representative process fluid cannot be obtained (e.g., due to interference with the analytical method), a similar solution to the original may be an acceptable alternative. The extraction study may be performed using a soaking or a recycling method for a specific contact time, under accelerated temperature conditions and preselected surface area–to–volume ratio. USP chapters <661> and <381> contain recommendations for extraction conditions with regards to surface area–to–volume ratio, temperature, and time of extraction (32,33). The USP <661> monograph also includes general analytical testing for the content of nonvolatile residue, residue on ignition, buffering capacity, and heavy metals. However, because such methods are not equipped to identify specific contaminants, their scope and applicability may not be sufficient in every instance. Several industry organizations, such as BioPhorum Operators Group (BPOG) and the Bio-Process Systems Alliance (BPSA), have developed standardized testing approaches. Please see the latest protocols at their websites as they are periodically updated (33-36).

Testing of E&L substances includes the analysis of nonvolatile, semivolatile, and highly volatile organic compounds as well as analysis of trace light and heavy metals. Less specific tests such as pH, conductivity, chemical identity via Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, and analysis of total organic carbon (TOC) may be performed. TOC analysis cannot be used with extract solvents that contain carbon. Highly selective analytical techniques for the detection, characterization, and quantitation of these chemical entities include high-performance liquid chromatography coupled with mass spectroscopy (HPLC-MS), gas chromatography coupled with mass spectroscopy (GC-MS), inductively coupled plasma with mass spectroscopy (ICP-MS), proton nuclear magnetic resonance (1H-NMR), and atomic spectroscopy (e.g., atomic absorption and atomic emission spectroscopy). For instance, nonvolatile compounds may be analyzed with HPLC-MS, whereas highly volatile and semivolatile organic compounds may be resolved with GC-MS. Other methods such as ICP-MS may be used for the detection of residual metals.

The extractables study must be complemented with an appropriately designed leachables study. In addition to monitoring the substances that are leaching under the recommended conditions of use and storage, leachable studies must be designed to identify any interactions and the resulting effect of such interactions on the in-process material and/or on the product. In many cases, drug substance and drug product stability studies may be used to support conclusions concerning the effects of these substances on product quality over time. This is particularly important where there is no downstream purification process to eliminate impurities (e.g., drug product formulation buffer storage container).

In addition to determining the chemical identity, quantity, and composition of E&L, and the impact on process fluid or product quality, E&L must be assessed for cytotoxicity and toxicity in animals in order to preserve patient safety and clinical efficacy (24,25). This information may be obtained from publicly available literature sources such as ICH Q3C (R3) or Product Quality Research Institute Leachables and Extractables Working Group, which stipulates the safety threshold levels for such impurities (37,38). In the absence of specific toxicity data, the threshold of toxicological concern (TTC) approach may be applied in order to judge the risk associated with a particular leachable.

When an SUS is used to grow cell culture, the end user must determine the effect of cell growth on both individual components and the complete SUS. This is important because a recent study showed that the breakdown products of gamma-irradiated antioxidants can have adverse effects on the viability of the cell culture. Spiking studies showed that the phenomenon can occur at levels well below parts per million in a cell line-specific manner (39).

Cell-based assays can be used to identify the particular combinations of cell lines and plastic extractables that may reduce cell culture viability and growth profiles. End users must evaluate the compatibility of the supplied SUS with their specific application, because certain applications (e.g., media storage, bioreactor bags) have been reported to be sensitive to certain combinations of materials and cell lines (30).

The mechanism of interaction of the SUS and contact solution can include leaching from the plastic into the contact solution or the adsorption of the contained solution or its constituents onto the SUS. These interactions can be modeled through science-based assessments of the physicochemical interactions of the solution and SUS. 核心概念解析 Key Concepts

Extractables (可萃取物)：在極端條件下（強溶劑、高溫、極端 pH）從材料中「強制提取」出來的化學物質。這代表了最壞情況的完整化學圖譜。

Leachables (溶出物)：在正常製程或儲存條件下，從材料表面遷移到製程流體或產品中的化學物質。Leachables 是 extractables 的子集，但也可能包含在製程條件下產生的新反應產物。

In-process Leachables (製程中溶出物)：在中間製程步驟中溶出、但可被後續純化步驟清除或稀釋至不可檢測水平的物質。雖然最終不會出現在成品中，但仍需在風險評估中考量。

關鍵區別：Extractables 研究用極端條件建立「化學地圖」，Leachables 研究則用實際條件確認「真實風險」。兩者互補，缺一不可。

比喻說明 Analogy

想像一個新買的水壺：

  • Extractables 研究：就像用沸水、醋、酒精分別浸泡水壺 90 天，看看到底能「逼」出什麼物質 — 這是壓力測試
  • Leachables 研究：就像正常使用水壺泡茶、裝水，看看日常使用下會有什麼物質溶出 — 這是真實情境
  • In-process leachables：就像水壺裝的水還會再經過濾水器 — 即使溶出了東西，後續過濾也能去除

你需要先做壓力測試（extractables）建立完整的風險圖譜，再做日常測試（leachables）確認實際風險。

重點提示 Key Notes

E&L 遷移的科學原理：

  • 熱力學因素：決定物質遷移的程度（平衡狀態下能遷移多少）
  • 動力學因素：決定物質遷移的速率（多快達到平衡）
  • 時間因素：接觸時間越長，遷移量越大
  • 溫度效應：溫度越高，遷移速率越快

Leachables 的危害：毒性、致癌性、免疫原性、內分泌干擾。此外還可能導致蛋白質聚集、氧化、降解和微粒形成 — 直接影響產品 CQAs。

重點提示 Key Notes

Extractables 研究設計要點：

  • 使用極端條件（非正常製程條件）：極端 pH、高溫、長時間、高表面積/體積比
  • 使用不同極性的有機溶劑作為萃取介質
  • 如果常規製程中有預沖洗步驟 → 應包含在 leachables 研究中，但不應包含在 extractables 研究中（因為 extractables 代表最壞情況）
  • 伽馬射線照射和蒸汽處理等預處理也會影響 E&L 圖譜

毒理學評估：

  • 已知物質：參考 ICH Q3C (R3) 殘留溶劑指南中的安全閾值
  • 未知物質：使用 TTC (Threshold of Toxicological Concern，毒理學關注閾值) 方法評估風險

重點提示 Key Notes

細胞培養的特殊風險：研究已證實伽馬射線照射分解的抗氧化劑可在 sub-ppm（低於百萬分之一）的濃度下，以細胞株特異性的方式影響細胞培養活力。這意味著：

  • 不能僅依賴 chemical E&L 數據 — 必須進行細胞功能性測試
  • 不同細胞株的敏感度不同 — 需要針對特定細胞株測試
  • 培養基儲存袋和生物反應器袋是高風險應用

雙向交互作用：不僅塑膠會向溶液釋放物質（leaching），溶液中的成分也可能被塑膠表面吸附（adsorption）。兩個方向都需要評估。

實務應用 Practical Application

CDMO E&L 策略：作為 CDMO，你可能同時生產多種生物製劑產品。建議建立一個「平台 E&L 資料庫」：

  • 對所有常用的 SUS 元件（薄膜、管路、連接器、過濾器）進行一次全面的 extractables 研究
  • 建立基準數據庫，新客戶產品導入時只需進行針對性的 leachables 確認
  • 這可以大幅降低每個新專案的 E&L 評估時間和成本
