6.0 
Risk Triage Model 
A preventive approach to managing the risk of drug shortages caused by manufacturing or quality issues 
includes a risk assessment method called “triage.” A triage approach allows a quick and easy assessment 
of the shortage risks of a product and prioritizes actions for proactive drug shortage management (risk 
control). Not all products require proactive drug shortage management. Only medically necessary and 
critical products and those with the greatest impact to patients require commensurate levels of effort 
and planning to ensure that the supply is adequate, and the availability component of the risk calculus 
is assessed and appropriately managed. The method and tools proposed in Section 6.0 are intended 
to help determine whether a proactive Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan is required for a 
product.
Companies should have robust QRM and knowledge management systems to both prevent (proactive) 
and respond (reactive) to drug shortages (see Section 5.0). Proactive QRM for drug shortage prevention 
should involve the following:
•	
Categorizing each product by criticality based on its indication, marketplace importance, profile, 
current supply situation, and patients’ needs
•	
Establishing and periodically reviewing a Drug Shortage Risk Register—a single source of 
information on hazards and the related risks that can result in drug shortages—by proactively 
identifying and assessing risks to quality, compliance, and supply that could lead to a shortage. A 
Drug Shortage Risk Register (see Section 11.0: Appendix 1 for a sample risk register template) 
should provide the following information, which should be updated and reviewed on a regular 
basis:

6.1	
Description of the Risk Triage Model 
The risk triage method uses a preliminary hazards analysis (PHA) approach, which simplifies the 
process after information is collected on the potential or actual drug shortage. A four-step PHA triage 
approach is used to evaluate the relationship of three risk elements—therapeutic use of a product, 
availability of alternatives, and likelihood of occurrence—to determine the risk of a drug shortage. The 
steps in the triage process depicted in Figure 6.1-1 are as follows:
1.	 Identify impact level (impact on a patient) based on therapeutic use and availability of alternatives.
2.	 Determine the likelihood of a drug shortage for the product, taking into account any drug 
shortage prevention controls that are in place and their likely effectiveness.
3.	 Determine the risk priority level based on a combination of impact on the patient and the 
likelihood of a drug shortage for the product.
4.	 Plan and implement the suggested risk controls for the assessed product based on the risk 
priority level.
The details of the triage model displayed in Figure 6.1.1-1 are described step by step in Sections 
6.1.1-6.1.4.
The triage method incorporates key words used to create a simple risk assessment design approach. 
Rather than using very general, subjective terms to describe priority levels for each of the three elements 
used in the triage model (e.g., high, medium, and low severity), using discrete information allows the 
assessment to focus on product information rather than defining “high,” “medium,” and “low.” Table 
6.1-1 shows how the key words are used to assign a priority level for each of the three elements in the 
risk triage model (45). The current Shortage Prevention Controls will be listed in the drug shortage 
prevention plan.

“Medically necessary products,” “critical medicines,” and “life supporting or life sustaining products” 
have the highest criticality based on patient need; therefore, their shortages present the greatest risk. 
(Definitions of terms are provided in Section 3.0.) Ensuring an uninterrupted, reliable supply of these 
products at all times to all patients who need them is key; therefore, robust controls must be in place 
to proactively manage the risk of shortages of these drugs. 
Products used to treat acute short-term conditions or manage chronic long-term diseases (e.g., Parkin-
son’s disease, diabetes, multiple sclerosis) constitute the next level of product indication-based critical-
ity. Although the criticality of these products is not at the same level as that of life supporting or life 
sustaining therapies, the unavailability of these products over an extended period could harm patients; 
therefore, shortages of these products cannot be tolerated for long periods. 
The lowest level of product indication-based criticality is assigned to medicinal products used for other 
minor indications (e.g., self-care therapies for minor ailments such as cough and cold). If a product is 
registered for more than one indication with different levels of indication-based criticality, the more 
conservative or higher level of criticality should generally be applied for this assessment. For example, 
if one of the product indications classifies a product as medically necessary, and the second indication 
classifies it for treatment of a chronic long-term condition, risk planning should be done assuming the 
higher level of product indication-based criticality, that is, for its use as a medically necessary product.
In addition to the therapeutic use of the product, the second dimension in ranking the impact of a 
shortage to patients is access to and availability of alternative therapies. Relevant health authorities may 
need to be consulted to determine where alternatives may exist for a product and for what markets 
they are available. Where no alternatives are available, especially for products that are medically neces-
sary, life supporting, life sustaining, or used to treat acute short-term or chronic long-term conditions, 
the impact to the patient is the highest (Risk Level A – red). Therefore, the margin for tolerance of a 
drug shortage with these products is minimal, and controls for these risks should ensure that:
•	
Focus is on proactive prevention of a shortage
•	
Time to remedy and return supply conditions toward normal is as short as possible in the event 
of a shortage
Where an alternative therapy is available in the form of a similar product or other presentations of the 
same product, the impact to the patient is lower (Risk Level B – yellow). In the event of a shortage, it 
is important to ensure that these alternatives are accessible to patients in a timely manner to minimize 
impact (e.g., by preventing interruptions in treatment).
The lowest level of impact (Risk Level C – green) is assigned to medicinal products used for other 
indications (e.g., self-care therapies for minor ailments such as cough and cold) for which alternatives 
are readily available or for products used to treat acute short-term or chronic-long terms conditions 
that have the same product readily available in other presentations.

6.1.2	 Step 2: Assess the Likelihood of Shortage 
In the second step of the triage, the likelihood of shortage for a product is assessed. Hazards that could 
increase the likelihood of a shortage should be assessed and current controls should be assessed to identify 
the hazards. Where good, robust controls exist for a hazard, the likelihood of occurrence would be low; if 
there are limited to no controls for a hazard, the likelihood of occurrence increases. Historical data may 
be available for certain hazards, and this historical data should be leveraged to assess the relative likelihood 
of risks. For example, product sourcing may be one of the controls assessed to determine the likelihood of 
a shortage. Single-sourcing of a product or key material supplier increases the likelihood of occurrence of 
a shortage in the event of an issue at that sole-sourced facility or supplier. Other controls may be related 
to infrastructure maintenance and improvements, technology updates, business continuity planning and 
capacity assessment, process capability, inventory levels, or supplier diversification. 
This assessment of likelihood of occurrence may not be quantitative, which is considered acceptable 
because it is more important to assess the controls and their likely effectiveness and to understand the 
relative likelihood of the occurrence of a shortage for identified hazards. In this risk triage model, a simple 
three-level (high, medium, low) scale for assessing the likelihood of occurrence has been proposed.

A single-sourced product with no backup or a product with insufficient safety stock presents the 
highest risk and, in combination with Risk Levels A and B (from Step 1 of the triage), would result 
in the highest risk priority level (Risk Priority Level 1). A multisourced product with a low capacity 
reserve (e.g., preparation of a backup manufacturing facility to produce the product will take at least 
six months) or with sufficient short-term inventory has a medium likelihood of occurrence and, in 
combination with Risk Levels A and B (from Step 1 of the triage), would result in a medium risk 
priority level (Risk Priority Level 2). A multisourced product with an adequate capacity reserve (e.g., 
an active backup manufacturing facility or will require less than four months to prepare to produce 
the product) and a sufficient inventory level represents the lowest risk and, in combination with Risk 
Levels B and C, would result in the lowest risk priority level (Risk Priority Level 3).

After the risk priority levels are assigned to the product in Steps 1-3 of the triage, appropriate risk 
control strategies (may be predetermined in some instances) should be developed and implemented. 
Some actions to consider based on the risk priority level are:
•	
Risk Priority Level 1
—	 Develop a value stream map (VSM) (starting materials through final drug product packag-
ing and distribution) to identify and reduce inventory and manufacturing risk stress points.

of controls, probability of drug shortage, and how long a shortage can be tolerated without 
adverse effects on patients. In addition, the need for a multisourcing strategy should be as-
sessed, taking business considerations into account with senior management involvement.
