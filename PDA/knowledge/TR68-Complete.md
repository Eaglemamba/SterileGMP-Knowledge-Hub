## Recognizing Drug Shortages as a Global Problem

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

The primary objective in the management of drug shortages is preventing harm to patients. In any situation, it is important to maintain the continuity of supply such that all patients receive the medicines they need every time they need them to manage their medical condition. The risk of potential drug shortages should be considered when making decisions related to the supply and/or manufacture of drug products and their corresponding active substances. This technical report addresses how drug shortages caused by manufacturing and quality issues can be anticipated and avoided using a risk-based approach at a product level.

                    

A good example of a shortage that had global impact and needed to be managed by multiple health authorities was **"The Great Acetonitrile Shortage"** in 2008–2009. Acetonitrile is used as a solvent in many analytical procedures that are included in submissions for the approval of drug products and in chemical reactions for the manufacture of active pharmaceutical ingredients (API). The two main manufacturers of this solvent, both located in the same area in China, were required to stop manufacturing acetonitrile to reduce air pollution for the 2008 Summer Olympics. A few months later, in September of 2008, acetonitrile production in Texas was interrupted due to hurricanes in the Gulf of Mexico. The combination of these two events caused shortages of several APIs and drug products. Only a few companies were aware that the product would not be manufactured for a year and, subsequently, had built up their inventories of the product in advance. Most regulatory agencies accepted alternative analytical methods on a short-term basis. The FDA also allowed changes to existing validated test methods within an approved application, either a new drug application (NDA) or an abbreviated new drug application (ANDA), to accommodate using less acetonitrile or an alternative solvent to acetonitrile. The Agency allowed companies to process this as a minor change in the Annual Report for the given drug application as long as the change met the criteria stated in the FDA Guidance for Industry: Changes to an Approved NDA or ANDA and Code of Federal Regulations [21 CFR 314.70(d)(2)(vii)].

                    

This example illustrates that, in some cases, drug shortages can occur very suddenly and for reasons that are difficult to predict. Common antibiotics such as amoxicillin have also experienced shortages globally due to supply-chain disruptions. Hence, it is important for companies and regulators alike to do their best to anticipate potential drug shortages and prevent them from happening. Improved surveillance and communications through forums and consortiums, such as the International Supply Chain Consortium (Rx-360), can help in managing widespread impact in such instances.

                    

The 2013 Birgli® report identified three principal types of causes for drug shortages—economic, business, and manufacturing supply chain issues—that are still highly relevant today. Since then, an awareness of the diversity of possible root causes for shortages has continued to evolve. Figure 3.0-1 illustrates the impact across manufacturers, MAHs (Marketing Authorisation Holders), wholesale distributors, hospitals and pharmacies, governments, and other stakeholders based on the current understanding of multifactorial root causes for driving drug shortages. It is important to acknowledge the role of the media in public perception of shortages. Figure 3.0-1 illustrates why it is important to recognize the landscape of stakeholders and stakeholder interdependencies as a complex ecosystem. It underscores the need for appropriate and timely communications among all the players to better manage unintended consequences of actions that any one stakeholder might take to prevent or manage drug shortages.

## Evolution of Terminologies

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

Though single, internationally harmonized definitions do not currently exist for "drug shortage," "meaningful disruption," and "medically necessary product," various definitions of these terms have been published.

                    

The **FDA** defines drug shortage as *"a period of time when the demand or projected demand for the drug in the United States exceeds the drug's supply."* In general, the FDA Drug Shortage Staff focuses on shortages of medically necessary products that have a significant effect on public health.

                    

The **EMA** earlier described drug shortages as *"disruptions to the manufacturing supply chain"* and updated the definition of shortages in 2019 to be more specific in terms of the gap between supply and demand. In addition, individual EU member states have local definitions of drug shortages. The EU's current definition of "critical medicines" also includes the criteria of therapeutic use, availability of alternative(s), and supply-chain vulnerabilities.

                    

The **World Health Organization (WHO)** refers to a "shortage" as a situation where the supply of medicines, health products or vaccines identified as essential by the health system is considered to be insufficient to meet public health and patient needs. WHO indicates that:

                    

                        On the demand side: a "shortage" will occur when demand exceeds supply at any point in the supply chain and may ultimately create a "stockout" at the point of appropriate service delivery to the patient if the cause of the shortage cannot be resolved in a timely manner relative to the clinical needs of the patient.
                    

                    

The understanding of these terms may also vary in some respects by the different stakeholders in the supply chain (MAHs, regulatory authorities, distributors, wholesalers, providers, and patients). However, there is alignment on the concept that these definitions are based on the fundamental importance of ensuring an uninterrupted supply of medicines to prevent harm to patients. They are also aligned with public-service obligations held by MAHs and wholesale distributors in relation to ensuring the supply of medicinal products in the markets in which they operate.

                

                
                

                    

                    

                        

## Reporting Requirements and Current Regulatory Landscapes

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

Reporting potential shortages to relevant health authorities is a **legal requirement**. In the United States, Section 506C of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act establishes notification requirements to manufacturers of product discontinuance. Title X of the Food and Drug Administration Safety and Innovation Act, enacted in July 2012 to address drug shortages, also led to Section 506C being amended to require annual reporting of drug shortages to the U.S. Congress. However, it has been noted that manufacturers are not required by law to report increased demand or export restrictions for life supporting or life sustaining products.

                    

In the European Union, Article 13 of EU GMP Directive 2017/1572 defines the legal requirement for a manufacturer "to inform the competent authority and, if applicable, the MAH of any defect that could result in a recall or an abnormal restriction on supply." Local legislation implementing this directive in the member states has demonstrated individual approaches being followed by each country. Additionally, in August 2014, Chapter 5: Production of EudraLex – Volume 4 was updated to introduce guidance in relation to supply restrictions and their notification to the MAH (Sec. 5.71). It specified that such notifications "should be done in a timely manner to facilitate reporting of the restriction in supply by the MAH, to the relevant competent authorities." Other health authorities (e.g., individual EU member states, Health Canada, Swissmedic, Therapeutic Goods Administration) are also implementing regulatory requirements in this regard. ICH Q9(R1) further highlights the need for appropriate and timely management of health authority notification.

                    

While reporting requirements and mechanisms for drug shortages vary across different health authorities and are subject to varying legal requirements, the expectation of visibility to shortage information has increased over the last several years. Other associations, such as the American Society of Health System Pharmacists, also may provide information on drug shortages and their management through their websites. Health Canada has provided a multistakeholder toolkit that provides strategies and tools for key players in the supply chain. The differences in reporting requirements and approaches in different countries continue to make it challenging to draw meaningful comparisons of data on drug shortages or to quantify the problem at a global or regional level.

                    

The European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations (EFPIA), European Generic and Biosimilar Medicines Association (EGA), Association of the European Self-Care Industry (AESGP), and Plasma Protein Therapeutics Association (PPTA) jointly developed a harmonized communication framework for potential drug shortages, which includes harmonized trigger points for notifying competent authorities of drug shortages that are due to quality and manufacturing problems.

                    

In the United States, Congress legislates the FDA's authority to act in response to drug shortages. In October 2013, FDA published its Strategic Plan for Preventing and Reducing Drug Shortages. It addresses mitigation strategies for manufacturers, such as notifying the FDA of potential shortages promptly so that manufacturers and the FDA can jointly take steps to reduce supply disruption. Another element of the FDA's drug shortage strategic plan focuses on preventive strategies, such as providing positive incentives for manufacturing and quality improvements and using proactive risk-based approaches for early identification and prevention of manufacturing and quality problems.

                    

In 2012, the EMA published a Reflection Paper on Medicinal Product Supply Shortages Caused by Manufacturing/Good Manufacturing Practice Compliance Problems and a supporting Implementation Plan 2012–2015. In 2019, HMA and EMA published a Guidance on Detection and Notification of Shortages of Medicinal Products for MAHs in the Union (EEA), which also recommends that MAHs utilize PDA TR-68 as a reference.

                    

The increased transparency of shortages, coupled with the mandatory requirements for early notification to health authorities of potential shortages, has allowed authorities and MAHs to work together more proactively to control and prevent potential drug shortage risks before they result in a supply disruption.

                    

Although not all shortages result in patient harm, health authorities try to determine the causes of shortages that do affect patients and "near misses" that could have resulted in patient harm. The objective of doing so is to be prepared to proactively implement actions that can prevent recurrences. For this reason, FDA, EMA, and many other health authorities have taken the crisis around drug shortages seriously and regulations continue to evolve to heighten the attention, sensitivity, and response to shortages.

                

                
                

                    

                    

                        

## Framework for Managing Drug Shortages due to Manufacturing and Quality Problems

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

As illustrated in Figure 3.0-1, the potential causes of drug shortages are often multifactorial and complex. Business, environmental, and economic policies are also important in controlling the risks to drug availability across the end-to-end product value chain, manufacturing being a key step. Therefore, efforts must be concentrated on maintaining uninterrupted manufacturing and distribution and minimizing the risks that packaged drugs will be unavailable.

                    

In the context of product value or supply chain, a drug shortage starts whenever an API, excipient, API starting material, reagent, solvent, component, or even packaging material cannot be made available for the manufacturing process. This can occur due to several reasons, including:

                    

                        
- Manufacturing, quality, and GMP compliance issues

                        
- Inadequate manufacturing capacity to meet increased product demand

                        
- Lack of redundancy

                        
- Complexity of the supply chain

                        
- Evolving global regulatory requirements and the complexity associated with implementing them

                        
- Dynamic market conditions

                    

                    

Country- or region-specific import testing requirements can add complexity to supply chains and restrict companies from recovering quickly, potentially turning "near misses" into actual drug shortages.

                    

Manufacturing and quality issues are often listed as a cause of drug shortages in many regions. In this context, shortages might occur as a result of issues related to such factors as the supply of materials, maintenance and technical operation of facilities and equipment, unavailable equipment, or variable manufacturing processes. Any of these factors may result in the generation of nonconforming batches or in GMP- or regulatory-compliance issues being identified by the company, health authorities, or others.

                    

As part of the EMA interassociation drug shortages project, **PDA** and the International Society for Pharmaceutical Engineering (ISPE) coordinated efforts at both the product and systems levels to address the prevention of drug shortages caused by manufacturing and quality problems. They advised that companies need to focus on operating effectively in an environment of increasing global complexity while continuing to supply products.

                    

TR-68 provides a holistic, **risk-based framework** at a product level for the prevention and management of drug shortages caused by manufacturing and quality issues. Risk-based approaches and risk-based decision-making are more broadly expected, accepted, and integrated into regulations and regulatory oversight mechanisms. However, the industry and regulators are still on a learning curve around the consistent application and dynamic use of risk-based approaches and risk-based decision-making in this area.

                

                
                

                    

                    

                        

## Management Responsibilities

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

A drug manufacturing company's interest is in developing, producing, and making pharmaceutical or biopharmaceutical medicines available to patients in a financially sustainable manner. Once approved by relevant health authorities, a drug should be available with no interruptions to supply; the manufacturer is accountable for producing and making the drug available while the wholesale distributors have a general service obligation to bring the drug to the patients. Senior management of the MAH is accountable for decisions that may impact the company's ability to cause, prevent, and/or recover from drug shortages. It is also accountable for ensuring the availability of their medicinal products in the amount needed and of the right level of quality.

                    

To ensure continued supply, it is important for management to ensure regulatory compliance, as well as compliance to GMP and good distribution practices (GDP), and a robust quality culture that is maintained and enhanced over time. Elements of this quality culture include:

                    

                        
- Competent employees

                        
- Desired behaviors

                        
- Learning environment

                        
- Decision-making (in areas that can impact the availability of drugs, such as decisions related to new supplier approvals, quality defects, deviations, and change controls)

                        
- Proactive QRM (i.e., ability to foresee and prevent drug shortages)

                        
- Environments where people can speak up freely about issues

                        
- Opportunities for improvement and interest in continuous improvements (e.g., technologies, systems, working methods)

                    

                    

Maintaining compliance with GMP requirements across all sites and functions is a prerequisite to ensure uninterrupted supply. Senior management of the MAH is ultimately accountable for maintaining manufacturing sites and other areas covered by pharmaceutical regulations, at a minimum, in compliance with GMPs and relevant regulatory requirements and/or commitments. This includes the MAH accepting accountability for oversight and the mitigation of any product quality and/or supply risks at third-party suppliers or service providers (e.g., contract development and manufacturing organizations (CDMO), contract laboratory organizations). Contracts and third-party relationships should be established with clear expectations and obligations related to quality and managing capacity constraints that might impact product supply or result in shortages.

                    

Where noncompliance is identified, either by company employees via internal audits or other means or through health authority inspections, all efforts should be made to correct it and ensure compliance.

                    

When a drug shortage is anticipated, senior management is responsible for initiating a dialog with the pertinent regulatory authorities. The objective of the dialog should be to reach a viable solution that considers the elements of patient safety, GMP/GDP and regulatory compliance, drug availability, technical abilities, and sustainable business operations.

                    

Applied quality principles aimed at continually improving quality performance are also expected to be fully integrated into business operations and to go beyond compliance with the minimum GMP/GDP requirements.

                    

There are many ways in which the MAH can ensure uninterrupted supply and receive early warning of potential supply issues; this technical report provides a structured approach to achieve such an overview using a risk triage model. Companies can consider assigning a customer service representative, a role analogous to an internal auditor or risk manager, to monitor issues that may lead to drug shortages. Those assessments would provide management with the timely information necessary to understand the risks to patients and the business because of drug shortages. Such a responsibility would be valuable because of the diversity of causes of drug shortages.

                    

Once management obtains information about shortages, it must develop an action plan for addressing the drug shortage risks and make risk-based decisions on how resources will be managed and, potentially, how capital is invested to ensure appropriate capacity and redundancy.

## 6.2 Supply Chain and Value Stream Mapping —

                

VSM as a proactive and reactive drug shortage management tool

            

        

        

            
            

                

                    

### 6.2 Supply Chain and Value Stream Mapping

                    

The MAH is accountable for ensuring that risks for patient safety, product quality, and product availability are adequately managed in the end-to-end product supply chain. In addition to assessing the risks with various suppliers and external service providers (e.g., CDMOs, CLOs) and addressing them through their supplier management controls, the MAH should also review the QRM processes used, especially by their key suppliers and service providers. This can help ensure that these suppliers and service providers have an adequate framework in place, in accordance with the principles of ICH Q9(R1), to assess and manage their quality and compliance risks.

                    

Process mapping, in general, can play a key role in managing the risk of drug shortages particularly as supply chains continue to become increasingly complex. Mapping of the end-to-end product supply processes is one of the most important tools that can be leveraged for managing the risk of drug shortages due to manufacturing and supply problems. **VSM** (also known as material and information flow mapping), a particularly powerful tool, graphically displays the flow of the manufacturing and transportation processes for a product and highlights potential manufacturing, inventory, and supply stress points. Although VSM is often used to reduce amounts of inventory and inefficiencies across the supply chain, it is also used to maintain proper levels of in-process inventory to ensure predefined final product inventories.

                    

While VSM is a great tool for mapping products and information flow, it is not the only approach available. Other tools that could be used are **DRIVE** (Define, Review, Identify, Verify, Execute), **ICOR** (Inputs, Outputs, Controls, and Resources), Cause-and-Effect Diagrams, and Process Flowcharting.

                    

The following elements constitute an effective mapping tool:

                    

                        
- **Team Makeup:** The mapping team should be cross-functional and include individuals who are intimately familiar with manufacturing and supply processes and equipment. The most successful mapping exercises involve laboratory technicians and operators. Mapping requires good facilitation, preferably by an objective, neutral individual.

                        
- **Planning and Preparation:** The scope of the mapping (local to a site or region or broader in the supply chain) should be identified in advance. The team should establish timelines and goals for the mapping (in this case, specific to drug shortages). The exercise will likely become part of a larger initiative for developing a Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan.

                        
- **Evaluation:** The team should perform all steps of the mapping process and capture data and information on how the process works. The team should also have access to existing procedures as well as quality events to capture all stress points.

                        
- **Map:** After a map of the current state is drawn, listing all the steps and handoffs in the process, a future-state map can be drawn using the same steps to identify risk reducing actions that will meet the needs of the product.

                        
- **Action List:** To develop and maintain an effective Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan, an action list needs to be developed to address the gaps and risks based on the future-state map actions.

## 6.3 Managing Drug Shortage Risks Due to Aging Manufacturing Facilities and Technologies

                

Causes, continual improvement, and lifecycle management of analytical technologies

            

        

        

            
            

                

                    

### 6.3 Overview & 6.3.1 Causes and Effects of Aging

                    

Manufacturing and quality risks introduced by aging or legacy facilities and technologies (e.g., out-of-date manufacturing facilities, equipment, analytical technologies) that are not fully up to current standards or expectations can contribute to an increased likelihood of issues that could potentially result in drug shortages. These risks should therefore be proactively identified and managed during the lifecycle of the manufacturing facility, process, equipment, or analytical methods. In order to accomplish the objectives of ICH Q10, continual improvement is an important element of product realization and of maintaining a state of control.

                    

                        *"Product realization is achieved by designing, planning, implementing, maintaining, and continuously improving a system that allows the consistent delivery of products with appropriate quality attributes. Continual improvement is facilitated through the implementation of quality improvements appropriate to the current level of process and product knowledge."*
                        

— EU GMP Guidelines on Pharmaceutical Quality Systems (ICH Q10 context)

                    

                    

### 6.3.1 Causes and Effects of Aging Facilities, Processes, and Analytical Technologies

                    

Some of the main reasons why facilities, processes, or analytical technologies become obsolete are:

                    

                        
- Lack of long-term planning and poor understanding of the importance of reinvestment and recapitalization of facilities and processes

                        
- Concern about the unanticipated negative impact of improvements due to a lack of robust process knowledge or of knowledge and experience with the new technology

                        
- Short-term focus on maintaining or reducing costs, either cost of goods sold or capital expenses

                        
- Negative financial return on investment due to low drug prices (e.g., generics)

                        
- Challenges, long lead times, and a lack of harmonized processes for global regulatory approvals across multiple health authorities

                        
- Absence of positive mindset, attitude, and culture for change and continuous improvement

                    

                    

These factors can produce the decline that occurs over time, for example:

                    

                        
- As a cost-saving effort, a company may decide to move to a limited maintenance schedule on equipment or reduce the availability of critical spare parts and stock of intermediates. The lowered or partial maintenance is believed to save costs; however, breakdowns due to the lack of maintenance or a negative trend in the manufacturing environment may stop production or lead to product rejections in the future, at a far higher cost.

                        
- A conscious decision to drastically reduce or eliminate facility, process, and/or equipment optimization and improvement activities, over the long run, can result in antiquated and noncompliant facilities and processes with high levels of variability and risk. Additionally, aging facilities may limit the ability to increase capacity to meet the product demand.

                        
- Maintaining a false assumption that equipment will not become obsolete in the future and will continue to be supported by the equipment manufacturer is imprudent. Equipment is designed with an intended life span. When the manufacturer fails to plan for equipment obsolescence, it often results in a quick upgrade done at a high cost and with some loss of production time.

                        
- In some cases, a process or analytical technique was designed for one product or output, and a change in a product or output might be incompatible with the old process or product.

                    

                    

Quality and GMP compliance gaps associated with such aging facilities, processes, and technologies should be addressed even though the existing equipment or methods may be functional. Well-trained personnel with a process ownership mentality may make such facilities run, but when such personnel retire or move to other tasks, the facility, processes, or analytical methods see their first excursions.

                    

The continual maintenance and improvement of facilities, processes, and analytical methods is essential to maintain specified product quality and to assure supplies of the product.

## 6.3.2 Continual Improvement of Aging Facilities and Processes

                        

Technology innovation as a driver for quality and supply reliability

                    

                

                

                    

                        

                            

### 6.3.2 Continual Improvement of Aging Facilities and Processes

                            

Processing technology and facility designs are forever evolving. The motivation of suppliers to develop and distribute new technologies is mainly driven by industry seeking new solutions for current processing problems. However, implementation of these solutions becomes challenging in spite of the value they can bring.

                            

Given this, implementation of new technology has resulted not just in financial viability, but an overall improvement in quality and process efficiency. For example, **single-use process technology** has many advantages over traditional stainless steel equipment, such as faster equipment turnaround, which enhances production capacities. Also, single-use components or unit operations can be connected via aseptic connector or tube welders, which creates a closed system that enhances the safety of both the product and the operator. The technological improvements of single-use technologies have been widely embraced, not only for new, but existing processes, as the advantages of using this technology outweigh any challenges change commonly bring.

                            

Facility and process improvements due to technology innovation are not always as dramatic as single-use process technology or new modular cleanroom designs; they can be gradual improvements of existing equipment to the next generation. Subtle, but continual improvements demonstrate the right quality attitude and ownership. Such continual improvement activities inevitably lower the risk of process or facility failure, leading to operations that run more reliably and efficiently and resulting in cost reduction.

                            

[More information about single-use systems can be found in PDA Technical Report No. 66: Application of Single-Use Systems in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing.]

## 6.3.3 Lifecycle Management and Continual Improvement of Analytical Technologies

                

PAC timelines, periodic assessments, and control system updates

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 6.3.3 Lifecycle Management and Continual Improvement of Analytical Technologies

                    

During the commercial lifecycle of a product, analytical technologies continue to improve. However, commercial product analytical control systems—in-process, release, and stability test methods, specifications and acceptance criteria, and reference-standard controls—tend to improve minimally, if at all. This slow pace of change can be attributed to such factors as high cost, resource requirements, and the complexity of the global regulatory environment for making PACs. **Revisions to control systems can take two to five years**, depending on their extent and the number of countries that need to approve the PAC.

                    

A proactive approach to control system lifecycle management is important to ensure alignment with contemporary product knowledge and industry standards. Lifecycle management of analytical control systems should be reviewed periodically (e.g., every five years), not only in response to control system issues identified (e.g., through investigations), during routine commercial use.

                    

Periodic assessments of an analytical control system should determine whether the system continues to control drug substance and product quality at the product's release and at the end of its shelf life. Such determinations should be based on comprehensive reviews of current information about the molecule, including:

                    

                        
- Adequate monitoring of relevant product quality attributes

                        
- Current manufacturing process capability

                        
- Alignment of acceptance criteria with process capability

                        
- Compliance with evolving regulatory requirements and expectations

                        
- The ability to fulfill its intended purpose

                        
- Current analytical technologies

                    

                    

Periodic assessments of the control system should include evaluations of gaps or improvement opportunities, for example, when a new critical quality attribute is identified because of new information on a molecule (such as antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity), when superior technology is available, or when new regulatory requirements are implemented.

                    

Some examples of when an update to the control system may be needed:

                    

                        
- The current method has a high failure rate, low throughput rate, or analyst ergonomic or safety limitations

                        
- The method is no longer suitable for the control system

                        
- The current method validation is no longer applicable

                        
- Superior technology is available

                        
- New or revised specifications or acceptance criteria need to be implemented

                    

                    

Acceptance criteria for current control system test methods should be evaluated against manufacturing and process capability (via analysis of lot-release data) and stability data. Release acceptance criteria can be evaluated by comparing 95 or 99 tolerance intervals of historical batches to current acceptance criteria.

                    

Revisions to current analytical control systems require submission of an application to the relevant health authorities. Therefore, they should be coordinated as much as possible with manufacturing process improvements, transfers, and other activities requiring regulatory approval. Early discussions with relevant health authorities regarding reporting level and documentation requirements for anticipated PACs will allow both the health authorities and the company to plan for the changes in a manner that will help reduce the risk of drug shortages.

## 6.4 Reducing Drug Shortages Through Expedited Post-Approval Changes

                

ICH Q12, global Change Protocols (gCP), and regulatory flexibility

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 6.4 Reducing Drug Shortages Through Expedited Post-Approval Changes

                    

When companies face a potential drug shortage, they can mitigate the risk by expediting a change that may have already been submitted to a national health authority for approval. Current regulatory PAC processes vary among countries in the levels and terminology of change categories, requirements for documentation of changes, the need for preapproval facility inspections, and timelines for approval. For these reasons, companies usually need to run multiple versions of a manufacturing process in parallel for the same product, depending on when each national health authority is likely to approve a change.

                    

In such cases, the company should communicate with the health authorities responsible for trade where a potential drug shortage might occur and request an expedited review of pending PACs or offer a previous version of the product if available in the inventory. Health authorities assess PACs using science and risk-based approaches. However, it would be beneficial if the regulatory approval processes for PACs could be harmonized.

                    

An example of an effort towards harmonization is The International Coalition of Medicines Regulatory Authorities' (ICMRA) Pharmaceutical Quality Knowledge Management System Initiative (PQKMS). According to ICH Q10, Annex 1, companies that demonstrate an effective PQS and process and product understanding, including the use of QRM principles, may be eligible for enhanced regulatory approaches. This is sometimes referred to as **"regulatory flexibility,"** where there may be an opportunity to "optimize science and risk-based post-approval change processes to maximize benefits from innovation and continual improvement."

                    

Additionally, **ICH Q12**: Technical and Regulatory Considerations for Pharmaceutical Product Lifecycle Management provides a framework via PQS to facilitate the management of PACs in a more predictable and efficient manner throughout the product lifecycle. Such opportunities may include managing more PACs in the PQS only, rather than as a prior-approval PAC. The intent of the guideline is to promote continual improvement and innovation, while strengthening quality assurance and reliable product supply.

                

                

                    

### PAC

                    

                        

### Global Change Protocols (gCP) and Streamlined Changes

                    

The industry should work with health authorities to develop predefined **global change protocols (gCP)** that can be available and used as a standard (non-product-specific) template for the categorization, predefined data requirements, and implementation of the different types of changes. Common gCPs that are developed in partnership with health authorities for the different types of changes can help standardize activities and requirements across the industry for a particular change type.

                    

Each company could then use the gCP for the relevant type of change as a starting point in developing the submission for each of the changes in the PAC Management Protocol for their product. The company should update the predefined gCP with the specific aspects of the change for their particular product.

                    

### Streamlined Changes — Categories Eligible for Expedited Review

                    

Companies and health authorities could work together to enable, where appropriate, streamlined review and approval of the following changes, provided that agreed-upon acceptance criteria from health authorities are met and that these changes do not introduce increased risks to patient safety or product quality or efficacy:

                    

**Enhanced Redundancy:**

                    

                        
- Additional manufacturing site or line already approved in at least one significant market or country that can serve as a reference

                        
- Additional raw material or API suppliers

                        
- Additional suppliers of container closure or other processing components

                        
- Additional dosage forms already approved in at least one reference country

                    

                    

**Improved Process and Product Controls:**

                    

                        
- New or improved equipment and technology

                        
- Manufacturing changes that lead to improved process capability

                        
- New analytical technologies that enhance product controls, including adventitious agent controls

                    

                    

**Other Types of Changes (provided that validated and preset criteria are met):**

                    

                        
- Change in reference standards

                        
- Use of new working cell banks

                        
- Addition of a product to a multiproduct facility

                        
- Scale-up of a manufacturing process

                        
- Extension of shelf life with supporting documentation

                        
- Change in utilities (e.g., HVAC, clean steam) or their operation with no impact on product quality

                        
- Change in equipment (design and/or operating principle) with no impact on product quality

                        
- Change in steam-in-place or clean-in-place process

                        
- Change in environmental monitoring program (e.g., sample location and frequency)

                        
- Change in cleaning methods

                    

                    

In all cases, the company concerned should be able to demonstrate that it has an effective PQS in place. The risk assessment for the change(s) should be included in the submission for the change to demonstrate that appropriate risk control measures are in place and provide assurance that patient safety, product quality, and product efficacy are not impacted.

## Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan

                    

                

            

            

                

                    
                    

                        

### 7.0 Overview — Plan Purpose and Scope

                        

The risk assessment triage results in an assignment of a drug shortage risk priority level to each product based on the product's indication, availability of alternative therapies, and probability of a shortage. Section 7.0 describes how a Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan should be developed based on the outcomes of the risk triage. While agencies are working on new guidances, such a plan might also be known as a Shortage Prevention Plan or Mitigation Plan in the future.

                        

A Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan can provide a holistic framework at a product level to address both the proactive and reactive management of a drug shortage. This plan should be proactively developed for medically necessary, life supporting, or life sustaining products and shared with health authorities during inspections. Medium risk products used for short-term acute conditions or long-term chronic conditions can also benefit from a Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan, especially if the risk triage has identified potential drug shortage risks at Risk Priority Level 1.

                        

A Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan can be an effective mechanism for managing a product's continuously changing drug shortage risk profile. Drug shortages may present potential opportunities for product diversion and counterfeiting. Therefore, a company's surveillance program can leverage the Drug Shortage Risk Register as useful input into its process for managing product diversion and counterfeiting risks that may arise due to shortages.

## The 5-Component Structure of a Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan

                    

                

            

            

                

                    
                    

                        

### Plan Components — English Source Text (p34–35)

                        

A Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan consists of the following information:

                        

                            
1. **Identification of drug shortage hazards:** An end-to-end risk assessment of the product's value chain until the first economic customer — its manufacturing operations, storage, and transportation — should identify potential sources of drug shortages. The Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan should address the management of the drug shortage risks listed in the Drug Shortage Risk Register. Typical visibility for a MAH ends after shipment to wholesale distributors.

                            
2. **Assessment of the impact of a potential drug shortage:** Based on the product indications, an assessment should be made and documented of which patient population would be affected by a drug shortage and what this effect would be. Application of the risk triage model would identify the risk priority levels described in Section 6.2. In addition, clinical risk assessments would be helpful in understanding the clinical impact of a drug shortage.

                            
3. **Risk control plan (short-term and long-term):** This section should describe the controls identified from the end-to-end risk assessments of the product, how the risk control strategy will proactively control drug shortage risks, what measures can be taken to ensure product supply resilience, and a plan to respond to a shortage and ensure a prompt return to normal operations in the event of a shortage.

                            
4. **Communication plan:** The Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan should include a communication plan for drug shortages. The AESGP, EFPIA, EGA, and PPTA collaborative document provides guidance on communications related to product shortages, including possible triggers for communication strategies.

                            
5. **Risk review and monitoring:** The Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan should be a living document that is reviewed and updated whenever a product's risk profile changes. Potential causes include approval of a new product or dosage form, a new indication, a new market, shutdown of a facility, increase in product usage, or new quality or manufacturing issues.

## Stakeholder Management and Communications related to Drug Shortages

                    

                

            

            

                

                    
                    

                        

### 8.0 Legal Obligations and Collaborative Dialog

                        

Multiple stakeholders are affected by, and need to be involved in, addressing a drug shortage — manufacturers, health authorities, distributors, pharmacies, healthcare providers and, sometimes, patients, depending on the cause(s) and level of the shortage.

                        

The legal obligations for MAHs to notify the relevant health authorities of a potential drug shortage are described in:

                        

                            
- **Article 13 of Commission Directive 2003/94/EC** — for the European Union

                            
- **Section 506C of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act** — for the United States

                        

                        

In addition, **Part 1, Section 5.71 of the EU GMP Guidelines** has been updated to provide guidance on notification of restrictions in supply.

                        

A collaborative dialog between the company and the relevant health authorities is key to facilitating the control and/or prevention of a shortage and, ultimately, ensuring a continued supply of medicinal products for patients.

                        

Companies need to consider many things when reacting to a current or impending shortage of one of their medicines in a given country or region. Developing a harmonized risk communication mechanism to alert health authorities of a potential or existing shortage is important to creating a shared understanding of the shortage.

                        

The guidance paper *Quality and Manufacturing Driven Supply Disruptions: Industry Communication Principles to Authorities* provides communication principles and a framework to harmonize reporting and communications between MAHs and health authorities regarding drug shortages arising from quality and/or manufacturing disruptions. The paper proposes a harmonized trigger point, time point, and recipient(s) for communicating potential drug shortage events for nationally and centrally approved products in Europe.

                        

Relevant health authorities, those where the product is marketed and where the shortage is expected to occur, must be notified when a meaningful disruption could occur in the supply of medicines due to manufacturing and quality issues, that is, when normal volumes of medicines cannot be delivered for further distribution.

                        

A safety or clinical risk assessment should inform the risk communication strategy so that appropriate risk mitigation actions can be taken at the healthcare provider and/or patient level. For defective products (e.g., those with a potential lack of sterility assurance), a comparison might be needed of the clinical benefits and risks for patients of continued product use, especially when no alternative product or treatment is available.

## Key Risk-Indicating Information to Compile for Health Authorities

                    

                

            

            

                

                    
                    

                        

### 8.1 Information Framework

                        

When anticipating a shortage may occur, companies should compile key pieces of information for health authorities when their involvement is needed. Knowing the reasons for the stock shortage or depletion not only facilitates the implementation of corrective and preventive actions (CAPA), but it can also help develop practical marketplace solutions for the shortage.

                        

Based on product ownership, it is important to openly clarify communication accountabilities between the health authority and the (1) manufacturer or first distributor; (2) wholesaler, agent, trader, distributor, repackager, or relabeler; and (3) suppliers of the product to the patient (e.g., retail pharmacies, hospital pharmacies, physicians).

                        

### Patient-Related Information

                        

                            
- Types of patients (e.g., by disease, demographics, age, genetics, origin) using the medicine. A good level of clinical understanding is important to assess whether any patient groups may be particularly affected (e.g., neonates, patients with cancer or diabetes).

                            
- Scale of the problem:
                                

                                    
    - Likely number of patients treated with the product each month and the percentage of patients that may be affected

                                    
    - Current market share, usage profile, and monthly volume of prescriptions

                                    
    - Monthly sales volume of the product and the proportion affected

                                    
    - Possible local, regional, or global impact on product supplies

                                

                            

                        

                        

### Distribution Information in the Country or Region

                        

                            
- Name and address of the primary distributor of the product, if any

                            
- If other distributors (i.e., secondary wholesalers) supply the product

                            
- If the product is supplied only to hospitals or to both hospital and retail pharmacies

                            
- If the product is supplied through other channels, such as clinics and nursing homes

                            
- Opportunities for parallel import, repackaging, and relabeling, depending on regulations

                        

                        

### Current Stock in Each Country or Region

                        

                            
- Whether the product is currently out of stock in the country or region, or if another version of the product or process is available during the stock-out period

                            
- Whether the product stock is expected to be depleted in the near future (e.g., within the next few weeks or months) because of the supply issue

                            
- Whether the product is in stock but available quantities are insufficient to meet patient needs

                            
- Whether stockpiling is requested by the government, legislation, or guidance or as result of the Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan

## Risk-Based Decision-Making by the Company and Health Authorities

                    

                

            

            

                

                    
                    

                        

### 8.2 Four HA Decision Points

                        

In the event of a shortage, having a product risk assessment developed proactively using the triage method and the risk matrices in Section 6.1, and the key risk-indicating information compiled in Section 8.1 becomes very useful in determining the appropriate actions to take. In addition to the manufacturer or MAHs, CAPAs to address the risks may need to be owned by other stakeholders in the supply chain. Therefore, communication between the stakeholders — including affiliates of the MAH in a country or region, distribution centers, or pharmacies as well as the press — is most important to detect unintended consequences of a corrective or preventive measure early in the system.

                        

Several decisions may need to be made by health authorities in response to reports of actual or pending product shortages:

                        

### HA Decision Point 1: Formal Involvement

                        

Whether to become formally involved in managing the shortage or in coordinating a response. The risk triage method will be useful in determining:

                        

                            
- Whether health authorities need to become **directly involved** (e.g., by taking direct measures, coordinating responses, developing work plans and action items)

                            
- Whether becoming directly involved is **not necessary** at that time

                            
- Whether health authorities need to **monitor the situation closely** without becoming directly involved

                        

                        

### HA Decision Point 2: Alternative Supply Sourcing

                        

Whether to facilitate the sourcing of alternative supplies of the product, involving determining:

                        

                            
- Whether the MAH should be allowed to import and supply a version of the product intended for a **different market** that may not be authorized in the country

                            
- Whether this product could be **batch packaged** for the other market and supplied in that country's packaging and delivery

                            
- Whether the imported product needs to be **repackaged** and, if so, the level of regulatory approval and oversight required

                            
- Whether healthcare professionals should be advised that an **unlicensed version** of the product or a clinically equivalent product is available

                        

                        

### HA Decision Point 3: Expedite Regulatory Approvals

                        

Whether to expedite approval of a request to use an **alternate unlicensed facility** or a **post-approval change** submitted by the MAH. A related decision is whether the approval should be **conditional, temporary, or permanent**.

                        

### HA Decision Point 4: Communication Strategy

                        

What communication strategies will be required and how those strategies will ensure that companies, health authorities, healthcare professionals, and/or patients can take appropriate risk mitigation actions. After the clinical risk assessment, the health authority may decide to advise healthcare professionals to:

                        

                            
- Give priority to certain groups of patients (e.g., patients already treated) for continued treatment

                            
- Use a different medicine or treatment for new (or all) patients

                            
- If a product is defective (e.g., loss of sterility assurance), weigh the **clinical benefits and risks for patients of continued use** when no alternative product or treatment is available

                        

                        

*Any time a shortage situation is encountered it should be viewed as an opportunity for the organization to review their risk register and, where relevant, assess suitable updates to their risk control program to improve prevention practices.*

## 9.0 Conclusions |

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 9.0 Conclusions

                    

The objective of ensuring an uninterrupted supply of safe, efficacious products to patients is shared by regulators, legislators, healthcare providers, and industry bringing them together to communicate for jointly developing solutions that serve the best interests of patients. As the risk to the patient population from a drug shortage rises, the level of rigor, effort, and cross-functional collaboration within an organization to address the risk should also increase. This effort should be coupled with effective communication among the organization, health authorities, and other relevant stakeholders to proactively manage drug shortage risks.

                    

Although challenging, the industry should address the concerns associated with drug shortages, given the objective of protecting patients from harm. Proactively identifying relevant drug shortage hazards and robustly managing drug shortage risks in the end-to-end product value chain, as well as effectively and promptly communicating potential shortages to all stakeholders, is essential to reduce the risk of drug shortages.

                    

TR-68 provides a practical framework that companies can use, a triage model to assess drug shortage risks, and templates for developing a Drug Shortage Risk Register and a Drug Shortage Prevention and Response Plan at a product level. It also describes ways that industry can engage more proactively with health authorities to request expedited review and approval of certain PACs by multiple health authorities to reduce the risks of drug shortages. This could be achieved by using agreed-upon PAC management plans and harmonized gCPs. Such protocols would be helpful for companies to address the risk of drug shortages due to aging facilities, processes, and analytical technologies by modernizing their facilities and implementing new technologies. This technical report also leverages the guidance developed by the AESPG, EFPIA, EGA, and PPTA collaboration for communications between MAHs and health authorities.
