# PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025): Development, Classification, Manufacture, Control, and Testing of Plasmids and Vectors Used in ATMP Production

## Topic 0: Introduction & Scope (p1-p7)

# Section 0: Introduction & Scope

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025) | p1 - p7

    

        

Cell and gene therapies are classes of advanced therapy medicinal products (ATMP) that include numerous different types of products including nucleic acids, viruses, genetically modified microorganisms, and others. Gene therapies are relatively novel technologies that tend to have extremely complex and not well-understood mechanisms of action but hold great potential for treating a variety of serious diseases, unmet medical needs, or simplification of administration and treatment (i.e., "one shot" gene therapy treatments versus a lifetime of chronic treatment for many diseases). The potential applications are diverse and numerous and may reach far beyond any current forms of therapies in terms of disease-state targets, as illustrated by the impact of the handful of currently approved successes. Notable, as well, are the abundant early- to late-stage products currently in clinical development and the anticipation of many more to come.

        

One technology that has shown immense promise within the ATMP space is the use of viral vectors for delivery of a therapeutic gene. Viral vectors have been shown to be useful both in the direct administration of in vivo treatment to patients and in the ex vivo genetic modification of cells that are then administered to the patient. Different viral-vector types have proven effective as gene delivery systems including, but not limited to, adeno-associated viruses (AAV) and lentiviruses, both of which have successfully commercialized products. AAV-based drug products, in particular, have been commercialized successfully in the United States, Europe, and other regions of the world.

        

Plasmid DNA (pDNA) are used as critical materials (i.e., the "building blocks") in the production of viral vectors and may also be delivered as naked DNA or encapsulated (e.g., in a liposome) for delivery purposes at various steps of a gene-therapy manufacturing process. Thus, ensuring consistent control of the quality in the production and use of plasmids is of great importance in the manufacture of many gene therapies, including cell-based, gene-modified therapies.

        

The ex vivo modification of cells was not within the primary scope of PDA Technical Report No. 81: Cell-Based Therapy Control Strategy (TR 81), therefore, some points to consider on viral vectors used in gene therapy were deemed to be of value and are addressed herein. In developing this points to consider document, the intent was to not repeat information already available in TR 81, but rather to supplement it. Likewise, when topics such as process validation, sterile filtration, filtration for viral contaminants, analytical method validation, and phase-appropriate GMPs are addressed in this document, these topics should be deemed supplemental to guidance that can be found in existing PDA technical reports.

        

A team of industry representatives from multiple CMC disciplines comprising product and process development, analytical development, manufacturing, quality assurance, and regulatory affairs was assembled by the PDA ATMP Advisory Board to author this document. This team was tasked not only with identifying the current major problems in the development, qualification, and manufacture of viral vectors that are not adequately addressed in current regulatory guidance, but also with recommending solutions to these problems based on current industry perspectives and best practices with consideration to compliance within a dynamic, global regulatory framework.

        

This document presents the views of the Parenteral Drug Association on the development, classification, manufacture, control, and testing of plasmids and vectors used in ATMP production. It is designed to encourage further dialog with industry, health authorities, and suppliers of technology and materials while taking into consideration the changes and needs of the modern, global, sterile healthcare-product manufacturing industry. **It does not represent a standard or a regulatory guidance.**

## Topic 1: Topic 1: Categorization (p8-p14)

# Topic 1: Categorization of Viral Vectors & Control Strategies

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025) | p8 - p14

    

        

Plasmid and viral vectors (i.e., vectors that consist of or are derived from biological material designed to deliver genetic material) express the desired gene product that is introduced into the ATMP manufacturing process at different stages of production. In some cases, the purified genetic material (i.e., plasmid or vector) is administered directly to a patient whereas in other cases, the genetic material, most commonly a viral vector, is used to genetically modify the cells ex vivo as part of the cell-therapy manufacturing process. This leads to a situation wherein similar material can be classified as a raw material, starting material, critical starting material, or an active substance (drug substance or active pharmaceutical ingredient) with each governed by different GMP requirements within different regulatory frameworks. The regional regulatory jurisdiction(s) will dictate the classification requirement, and therefore, the governing GMPs.

        

A starting material may be deemed a critical starting material depending on an assessment of risk and the particular regulatory jurisdiction. A critical classification may drive tighter controls within the quality system. For example, in the European Economic Area, a viral vector that is used to modify the cells ex vivo will be considered a critical starting material and follow EU EudraLex - Volume 4 - Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) Guidelines, while in the United States, it may be considered a drug substance (3). In some jurisdictions, such as Australia, the difference is even more significant: if the vector is administered in vivo to the patient, it will be considered a medicine and follow the PIC/S code of GMP PE-009 (which is largely aligned with EU Volume IV with part 4 being a notable exception) (4). However, if the same viral vector is used to modify the cells ex vivo, it will be considered a critical starting material and is regulated by a completely different code of GMPs (refer to the Australian Code of Good Manufacturing Practice for Human Blood and Blood Components, Human Tissues and Human Cellular Therapy Products) (5).

        

Considering that various regulatory agencies have different GMP and CMC requirements, it is extremely important to be aware of these nuances to determine the correct classification for each material used during manufacturing in order to develop the appropriate control strategy.

        

While it is not possible to provide a comprehensive framework for categorizing vectors and plasmids, as there are too many jurisdictions with varying requirements, it is possible to show approaches that would facilitate classification of the material, establish the CMC and GMP requirements, and build an appropriate control strategy. That approach can then be adapted for other jurisdictions.

## Topic 2: Topic 2: QbD Principles (p15-p18)

# Topic 2: Quality-by-Design (QbD) Principles

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025) | p15 - p18

    

        

Quality-by-design (QbD), first developed by Dr. Joseph M. Juran, has evolved greatly since its inception and today it is supported by such widely recognized standards as the International Council for Harmonisation's Quality Guidelines ICH Q8(R2): Pharmaceutical Development, ICH Q9: Quality Risk Management, and ICH Q10: Pharmaceutical Quality System. The guiding principle of QbD asserts that quality should be designed into a product and into its manufacturing process. By designing the product and process properly, understanding is increased, thereby simplifying the troubleshooting of process and product problems in the future. This perspective is encouraged by the FDA and by other global regulators. This enhanced process knowledge, in turn, simplifies risk-based quality decision-making, as the risk-based decisions are made within the framework of the effective boundaries of the process.

        

The major deliverables of QbD include:

        

            
- Quality target product profile (QTPP) that identifies the critical quality attributes (CQA)

            
- Product design that includes identification of critical material attributes (CMA)

            
- Process design that includes identification of critical process parameters (CPP)

            
- Control strategy that links CPPs and CMAs to the delivery of CQAs

            
- Process capability

            
- Continuous quality improvement

        

        

Like large-molecule biotechnology products and small-molecule conventional therapies, viral vectors and plasmids benefit from a structured QbD approach to development and continuous improvement. Product development teams make use of prior platform experience and scientific knowledge (e.g., industry standards, published examples) as well as perform studies as needed (e.g., design of experiment) to create the body of knowledge (subject to rugged data analysis) which supports the product throughout its lifecycle. That data is then subject to knowledge management principles and is updated and enhanced by production experience and stability results to support commercialization.

        

At the end of each phase of pharmaceutical development, stage gates should be defined to encourage revision of the QTPP, control strategy, and control ranges. Final revision of the overall initial control strategy should be performed at the conclusion of the process performance qualification (PPQ). Lifecycle maintenance of the control strategy will then be required until product retirement.

## Topic 3: Topic 3: Control Strategies (p19-p23)

# Topic 3: Control Strategies

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025) | p19 - p23

    

        

**Q 3.1. Are control strategies required for viral vectors and plasmid manufacture? If so, at what stage should the control strategy be available?**

        

Viral vector and plasmid manufacture, whether defined as a critical starting material or as a drug substance, requires a robust, holistic control strategy to drive product quality. The control strategy should link the CPPs and CMAs directly to the CQAs. By understanding which process parameters have a direct impact on the CQAs, appropriate process monitoring and controls can ensure reproducible and predictable delivery of the viral vector or plasmid. PDA Technical Report No. 81 Cell-Based Therapy Control Strategy (TR 81) describes the approach for cell-based therapy control strategies; the strategies and concepts described in PDA Technical Report No. 60 Process Validation: A Lifecycle Approach (TR 60) apply to all pharmaceutical products. The principles discussed in TR 81 and TR 60 can broadly be applied to viral vector and plasmid control strategies as well.

        

In addition to the principles mentioned above, phase-appropriate implementation may be warranted. For example, the control strategy may consist of preliminary factors to be monitored and assessed in a phase-appropriate manner. As experience with the production of Phase 1 and Phase 2 material is gained, the control strategy may mature while regional differences may dictate how early the control strategy must be formalized. As additional product and process knowledge is gained through clinical results and through continued small-scale and lifetime studies, process ranges must be established and CPPs defined, so that by the time pivotal clinical batches are produced, product and process consistency are assured. When creating a holistic control strategy for plasmid and vector production, inclusion of microbiological and viral control aspects, in addition to the core manufacturing process elements, is important. The microbiological and viral control strategies can either be prepared as separate documents or they may be written within the overall process control strategy. Either approach is acceptable, provided that all stages of the process and all material handling steps are included in the approaches.

        

Viral and microbiological controls should be in place for the first human clinical trial as they have a direct impact on patient safety. Therefore, these attributes of the control strategy may need to mature earlier than some of the other parameter definitions. In the EU, sterilization and sterility methods are required to be validated at the time of Phase 1 for an Investigational Medicinal Product Dossier. For example, if a vector is defined as a sterile critical starting material, safety-indicating methods (such as sterility and endotoxin) must be qualified. Requirements for a mycoplasma safety-indicating method may vary based on regional requirements. A risk assessment may be needed which takes into consideration the intended market and existing controls. Additionally, the initial control strategy will need to be refined and revised as additional product knowledge and process understanding are gained.

        

Analytical methods and their associated reference standards should mature along with the control strategy. As CPPs are defined for each associated CQA, the analytical methods that confirm CQA achievement must be demonstrated to be reproducible, accurate, and precise. In this manner, should late-stage process changes or process parameter changes be introduced (within or between sites), comparability and bridging can be accomplished with a high degree of assurance. (See Topic 5.)

        

            "Viral and microbiological controls should be in place for the first human clinical trial as they have a direct impact on patient safety."

## Topic 4: Topic 4: Filtration (p24-p30)

# Topic 4: Filtration

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025) | p24 - p30

    

        

For contamination control of proteins and biologically active materials, the use of filtration technologies is commonly preferred over other technologies (e.g., steam sterilization, gamma sterilization, X-ray) because it tends to reduce the impact on the product's quality attributes.

        

Microbial and viral filtration should be included in the design of the manufacturing process from the earliest conceivable timepoint so that its introduction later in development does not introduce any unintended impact on product quality attributes. Establishing filtration for a process may be challenging, as lentiviral, rHSV, and gamma-retroviral vectors may be damaged by filtration or result in such low potency that a lot may be unusable. While AAV vectors can typically be filter-sterilized, potential product changes due to filtration, such as aggregation, should be characterized.

        

PDA Technical Report No. 26 Sterilizing Filtration of Liquids (TR 26) and Technical Report No. 41 (Revised 2022) Virus Retentive Filtration (TR 41) provide in-depth discussions of the theoretical and technical elements associated with the filtration of fluids with the goal of removing microbial and viral contamination. EMA Annexes 1 and PIC/S Annex 2A state compliance expectations when the drug product cannot be filter-sterilized, as in the case in cell therapies. Where filtration is not possible, aseptic processing is expected. In addition, the sterile filtration or aseptic processing of starting materials is also expected (e.g., media used for producer cells, transfection agents, other additives).

        

The decision to aseptically produce without filtration must be accompanied by a risk assessment, including results of the filter development study from the specific product or from platform experience, that demonstrate the product was irreparably harmed by the process of filtration. For more detailed information, see FDA Guidance for Industry: Sterile Drug Products Produced by Aseptic Processing — Current Good Manufacturing Practice and EU Annex 1.

        

            "Microbial and viral filtration should be included in the design of the manufacturing process from the earliest conceivable timepoint..."

## Topic 5: Topic 5: Comparability (p31-p36)

# Topic 5: Comparability

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025) | p31 - p36

    

        

Bridging and comparability are terms commonly used during the development and registration of a new product when comparing and assessing differences between processes or materials. They are used separately (e.g., bridging of reference standards, comparability of lots from different sites) and, more confusingly, can also be used together such as in ICH Quality Guideline Q5E: Comparability of Biotechnological/Biological Products Subject to Changes in their Manufacturing Process where there is a reference to a "comparability bridging study." Therefore, it is important to first define these terms in order to offer clarity about their objectives and execution.

        

**Bridging** is the process of relating earlier data to later data in a way that the ability to extrapolate earlier results to later results is maintained. Common usage includes bridging of clinical studies, bridging of reference standards, or bridging from one test method to a new version of that test method.

        

**Comparability** is the process of comparing technical data from a reference population to a new population for the purpose of ensuring equivalence in quality, efficacy, and/or safety. Common usage includes comparability of product manufactured by different manufacturing sites, particularly when launching at a new site. It may also include comparability (comparing) product quality attributes and patient safety data after a significant process change.

        

In many situations, bridging is achieved when analytical comparability of product quality or analytical method outputs before and after a change is demonstrated. When comparability is not demonstrated by only analytical testing, additional nonclinical and clinical bridging studies are needed.

        

            "A study performed to provide nonclinical or clinical data that allows extrapolation of the existing data from the drug product produced by the current process to the drug product from the changed process." — ICH Q5E on comparability bridging studies
        

        

In early phases of development, establishing in-house or interim reference standards that are useful for the development work, but are not intended for commercial use, is common. When establishing a primary reference standard and subsequent working reference standard, conducting bridging studies of the reference standards to be able to relate lot test results from early safety and clinical work to the testing from batches that represent the commercial process is essential.

        

Similarly, test methods might evolve throughout the development and commercialization program as they are refined or improved (e.g., enhanced method controls or automation, improved precision, greater limits of quantification or detection). Bridging of methods ensures that results from earlier generations of test methods can be interpreted in the context of results generated with the new versions of the test methods.

        

Nowhere is bridging more critical than for stability studies. Data generated with reference standards or test methods at earlier timepoints must be preserved through an understanding of the bridging to the current methods and, therefore, the results at the current and future timepoints. Often stability studies can be performed with both the old and new methods (and old and new reference standards) in parallel from the point of introduction of the new method through the endpoint of the study.

        

Comparability is performed to assess the quality, efficacy, and/or safety attributes of a lot(s) against other lot(s) that are differentiated by process, site of manufacture, raw material, or other potentially significant difference.

        

Some important points to consider for bridging and comparability include the following:

        

            
- Ensure that bridges and comparability are always maintained to clinical materials, especially the pivotal lots that will demonstrate efficacy, safety, and/or immunogenicity

            
- Keep careful track of which lots were tested with which version of the assay or reference standard and know what those differences might mean in terms of bias or offset

            
- Establish written guidelines for when CoAs will or will not be updated based on the introduction of new test methods or new reference standards, and ensure that there is a clear signifier on the CoA that traces to its version that permits ready traceability to the testing that was used to generate the values

            
- Ensure that with each intended change there is a planned strategy for bridging stability data to new methods and reference standards to preserve the integrity of earlier timepoints, *before the change is implemented*

            
- Use bridging and comparability protocols to define the criteria by which comparisons and conclusions are to be made

            
- Ensure that an inventory of clinical lots, reference standard lots, and other representative lots are set aside and maintained for the purpose of establishing the essential bridges required for the program

        

        

Establishing standard operating procedures that include these requirements is one way to ensure success in demonstrating that product, processes, reference standards, and/or analytical results are consistent and traceable.

## Topic 6: Topic 6: Platform Technologies (p37-p40)

# Topic 6: Use of Platform Technologies for ATMP Products

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025) | p37 - p40

    

        

A platform is a model process (or model analytics) from which variations are developed. The term "platform" was adopted as the model represents the jumping-off point for any new development efforts. For example, a model process would have identical unit operations for the majority of the process steps with the same process controls (the process backbone) with only targeted changes, such as an adjustment to an individual process parameter or setpoint, to adapt to the needed conditions specific to the biological entity (e.g., strain).

        

Within the industry, the term platform may also be applied to specific unit operation equipment technology. Although used in this context, the terminology may be imprecise in some cases, as the use of a particular piece of equipment or group of equipment does not ensure that the same process or analytics are maintained. While these equipment "platforms" can help to standardize and streamline qualification, validation, and overall control strategies, they may not provide the same level of regulatory efficiency that are being described in this section.

        

The development of multiple products based on one or more common technologies, or platforms, offers an approach to effectively use prior knowledge from existing products as new products are developed. The interest in pursuing platform technologies has risen significantly in the last several years, particularly for ATMPs, as these products are typically developed under aggressive timelines due to the nature and promise of the therapeutic effect. The use of platforms can allow for:

        

            
- Standardization during manufacturing process development

            
- Standardization during development of analytical methods

            
- Establishment of harmonized CQAs and the associated risk-based assessments

            
- Establishment of consistent process and analytical control strategies

            
- Harmonization of regulatory submission content and technical support documents

            
- Reduced costs and timelines associated with development activities

            
- Reduced time for review and approval by regulators (when prior art has already been approved)

            
- Efficient process qualification approaches (e.g., the inserted coding region in a common viral-vector or plasmid drug substance is produced and purified using the same overall production and control processes)

        

        

The U.S. government has pilot programs promoting the development of platform technology (e.g., the "Paving the Way for Rare Disease Gene Therapies" project) as it is understood that the benefits to patients from the therapies, and the possible shortening of the time to market (which for traditional development pathways has been more than 7–10 years), is in the best interest of the public.

        

The use of a technology platform also contributes to enhanced process knowledge on an ongoing basis as data from additional molecules become available and can be used to further characterize and refine a platform.

        

The use of platforms is consistent with QbD (see Topic 2) and robust control strategies (see Topic 3) as there is a heavy emphasis on prior knowledge and a reliance on effective knowledge management to collect and organize the critical data that ensures appropriate conclusions can be drawn to strengthen the knowledge of the design space (i.e., the combination of critical material attributes and critical process parameter ranges that define the successful product manufacture) and the performance of the process.

## Topic 7: Topic 7: Potency (p41-p43)

# Topic 7: Potency

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 11 (2025) | p41 - p43

    

        

Potency is an expression of the activity of a drug substance or drug product in terms of the amount or concentration required to produce a defined effect. Clinical efficacy, on the other hand, is a measure of the therapeutic effectiveness of the drug in humans. Regulators desire a product-specific potency assay that provides a demonstrable link between potency and clinical efficacy. As such, the development team should first identify the relevant biological property linked to the product's in vivo mechanism of action (MOA) and then establish a method to provide a quantitative measure of that biological property. The assay must demonstrate that its performance remains unaffected by expected variability in product purity or matrix effects.

        

Other than safety, potency is the most critical quality attribute of a product. The ideal potency assay should reflect the MOA of the drug and demonstrate the product has the expected ultimate "functionality" that correlates with clinical efficacy. For an in-vivo gene therapy product, the ultimate functionality means the action of the expressed (or transcribed) gene of interest (GOI). If the product is a protein, then demonstration of the expected bioactivity (e.g., enzymatic activity) of the expressed protein is the ultimate goal of potency assay development. For an activated cell therapy product, for example, this broadly means that there must be an in vitro analytical method that correlates to in vivo reduction of tumor size or number or reducing the progression to metastasis.

        

Potency is a CQA for stability, not just release; therefore, an effective potency assay must also demonstrate that it is stability-indicating. To do this, forced degradation studies should be performed which demonstrate the primary degradation pathways of the product. With the samples showing between 10% and 20% degradation for each condition (where possible), as confirmed by orthogonal methods, the potency assay must be able to accurately detect the associated loss in potency.

        

If the product will be produced across multiple sites, it should be recognized that small differences in process and materials may exist site to site. Ideally, the potency assay should be robust enough to account for these small differences when they do not represent a difference in clinical effect.

        

To effectively quantitate potency, it is necessary to establish a reference standard against which the response from each lot will be quantitated. Initially, the reference standard may be a well-characterized development lot as an interim reference standard. However, as product development advances, the company must qualify a primary reference standard that will be used for commercial production. Bridging will likely be required to link the quantitation of early lots (and their stability studies) to any new reference standard that is introduced (see Topic 5). It is especially important to maintain a quantitative reference to clinical lots.

        

            "Since development of an appropriate potency assay is complex, it is recommended to collect a significant amount of bioanalytical characterization data on early product lots until it is demonstrated that the developed potency assay is robust to the product and process and is predictive of MOA."
