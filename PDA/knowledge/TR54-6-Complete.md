## 3.0 Overall Strategy – A Holistic View

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 3.0 Overall Strategy – A Holistic View

                    

TR 54-6 provides guidance on quality risk management principles that can be used to assess the risks to the quality, safety, and function of an excipient in a drug product. The proposed generic model allows the user to answer the risk question *"Is the excipient fit for use in the drug product?"* through the use of a formalized risk assessment.

                    

The model, as described in Section 6.0 and illustrated in Figure 6.0-1, describes the steps involved in identifying whether an excipient is fit for use, considering:

                    

                        
- An assessment of the excipient's use and function for the excipient user

                        
- An assessment of the entire Supply Chain

                    

                    

In the model, the potential hazards from the excipient itself, as well as the supply chain, are ranked and evaluated using a risk ranking and filtering approach to evaluate the overall risk of harm to the drug product and, through that, to patient safety.

                    

Based on the results of the risk assessment, a pharmaceutical manufacturer can determine what level of GMP will be required. The guides and standards most widely accepted for excipient GMPs include:

                    

                        
- EXCiPACT: International Pharmaceutical Excipients Certification Standards for Pharmaceutical Excipient Suppliers

                        
- NSF/IPEC/ANSI 363: Good Manufacturing Practices for Pharmaceutical Excipients

                        
- IPEC/PQG: The Joint Good Manufacturing Practices Guide for Pharmaceutical Excipients

                        
- USP: <1078> GMP for Bulk Pharmaceutical Excipients and <1059> Excipient Performance

                        
- Japan: Self-imposed Standards of GMP for Pharmaceutical Excipients

                    

                    

Should the risk assessment uncover an issue requiring additional action, a control strategy should be put in place and the affected parties should be notified. Any actions recommended as a result of the risk assessment must be discussed and agreed upon by the excipient manufacturer and the drug product manufacturer or Manufacturing Authorization Holder, and the results documented in a quality agreement or other document.

                    

A global risk assessment of all excipients used by the drug product manufacturer will ensure a standardized assessment across all excipients and drug products and provide a holistic view of the supply chain. This enables the excipient user to provide the excipient manufacturer, with one voice, a single (worst-case) risk assessment — preferable to multiple users within the same company communicating different results to the excipient manufacturer.

                    

                        

                            **Figure 3.0-1 (Holistic View Diagram)**  

                            This figure illustrates the overall iterative risk management process used in excipient supply chain management. It depicts the cycle from Supplier/Excipient Identification through Risk Identification, Risk Assessment (with inputs from QMS, Manufacturing, Supply Chain, Route of Administration, and Function of Excipient), risk scoring (low/medium/high), and implementation of relevant GMP/GDP practices, leading to either risk acceptance or risk reduction, followed by ongoing Control and Review.

### 3.1 Risk Areas Beyond Quality

                    

While the focus of the excipient risk assessment is quality, a company also should consider other aspects that can impact the excipient and, with this, product availability for the patient. These include:

                    

                        
- Excipient availability

                        
- Business continuity risk

                        
- Environmental, socio-economic, health, safety, and geographic risks

                        
- General business data

                    

                    

While consideration of these risks is useful as part of an overall strategy, it is out of scope of this document and is not discussed further within TR 54-6.

## 4.0 Supply Chain — End to End

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 4.0 Supply Chain — End to End

                    

Section 4.0 provides examples of supply chains, helps identify the various actors in the supply chain, and describes their typical roles. Supply chains have become more and more complex over the years. In the past, a manufacturer of pharmaceutical excipients often supplied directly to a drug product manufacturer. Today, the Distributor, Broker, and CMO each play roles in the supply chain, each contributing to the risks to quality in one way or another.

                    

Many materials used as excipients are not manufactured specifically for pharmaceutical use but are intended for other applications such as food, cosmetics, or home care. Intermediaries often become involved, procuring larger quantities from the original manufacturer and repackaging into quantities more suitable for pharmaceutical use. The complexity is magnified for excipients that move internationally.

                    

In both international and domestic supply chains, the point at which legal ownership transfers from one organization to another is a critical juncture. In complex supply chains, legal ownership may transfer several times — for example: original manufacturer → distributor → repackager → end user.

                    

### 4.1 Types of Supply Chains and Risks

                    

### 4.1.1 Direct Supply from Manufacturer

                    

Even when an excipient is supplied directly by a manufacturer to an end user, intermediaries may be involved. Logistics operations may be subcontracted, and one or more warehousing companies may be involved. Because all intermediaries are subcontracted by either the original manufacturer or the end user, they should have good information about how to safely transport the excipient. The supply chain illustrated in Figure 4.1.1-1 is one of the **lowest-risk arrangements** due to the small number of intermediaries and the degree of control that can be applied.

                    

                        

                            **Figure 4.1.1-1: Supply Chain — Direct Supply from Manufacturer**  
  

                            

                                Original Manufacturer
                                →
                                Haulier
                                →
                                End User
                            

                        

                        

Figure 4.1.1-1: Direct supply from manufacturer — fewest intermediaries, lowest risk configuration

                    

                    

### 4.1.2 Supply via a Distributor

                    

When an excipient moves from manufacturer to Distributor, the distributor becomes the legal owner and then transfers ownership to the end user, possibly after repackaging or relabeling. The distributor may have an agreement with the original manufacturer to market the excipient under the distributor's tradename. Usually in these cases the entire supply chain can be mapped with some certainty. This type of supply chain presents *few more risks* than direct supply; however, those risks may be magnified if the distributor engages in repacking or relabeling.

                    

If the original manufacturer has not established agreements with distributors, it will be much harder to fully trace all parties in the supply chain, increasing the risks. The original manufacturer may also change its distributor, and a process for notification of such changes should be agreed between the parties.

Repackaging activities in the supply chain, as illustrated in Figure 4.1.3-1, **magnify the risks to excipient quality**. The end user needs to understand the GMP controls applied by the Repackager in order to minimize the risks to excipient quality, and, if feasible, needs to specify appropriate measures in supply or quality agreements.

### 4.1.4 Supply Via Broker

                    

A Broker usually connects the manufacturer with the customer without physically handling the excipient. The ability to map the supply chain in this scenario will depend on the degree of transparency provided by the broker. When communications are totally open, the supply chain can be traced and any risks assessed clearly — if the broker is fully transparent and the supplier is the original manufacturer, the risks may be as low as in the direct shipment scenario (Section 4.1.1). On the other hand, if the broker is less transparent, it may be very difficult to assess the risks.

                                →
                                End User
                            

                            

Note: Broker does not physically handle the excipient — acts as intermediary for commercial transactions only.

### 4.1.5 Manufacturer Exporting Excipient Directly to End User

                    

An international supply chain usually involves many intermediaries. Unlike previous examples where the parties are relatively fixed, the parties to an international supply chain — including Hauliers, shipping agents, and ships — may vary from shipment to shipment. In these situations, quantifying the risks may be very difficult. If the intermediaries are fixed, however, suitable inspection controls can be applied at those points, reducing the uncertainty.

### 4.1.6 Manufacturer and Contract Manufacturing Organizations (CMO)

                    

When one or more CMOs are involved in the supply chain, it is critically important to clearly identify and understand which party gathers information and who owns the risk assessment.

                    

The Manufacturing Authorization Holder is ultimately responsible for the risk assessment. Depending on the quality agreement and relationship between the CMO and manufacturing authorization holder, there are generally three options:

                    

                        
- The CMO carries out the entire risk assessment, collecting information about the use and function of the excipient in the final drug product — which is then reviewed and approved by the manufacturing authorization holder.

                        
- The risk assessment is carried out by the manufacturing authorization holder, incorporating information from the CMO.

                        
- The risk assessment is carried out by the manufacturing authorization holder based on the risk assessment delivered by the CMO.

                    

                    

In any case, the roles and responsibilities for the CMO and manufacturing authorization holder must be clearly defined, including what actions will be taken if the risk assessments completed by the CMO and manufacturing authorization holder result in different outcomes.

## Chapter Summary

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Key Takeaways — Sections 3–5

                    

                        
- **Section 3.0:** TR54-6 adopts a holistic strategy — risk assessment focuses on quality but must consider supply continuity, regulatory compliance, and environmental/socioeconomic factors. A global, unified risk assessment provides the excipient manufacturer with one consistent voice from the MAH.

                        
- **Section 4.0:** Six supply chain configurations are described (4.1.1–4.1.6), ranging from the lowest-risk direct supply to the most complex international multi-intermediary chain. Each added intermediary introduces potential risks of adulteration, contamination, and documentation gaps.

                        
- **Section 4.1.6 (CMO):** When CMOs are involved, the MAH retains ultimate accountability for the risk assessment. Roles must be explicitly defined in quality agreements, including procedures for resolving conflicting risk assessment outcomes.

                        
- **Section 5.0 (Table 5.0-1):** Each supply chain role has distinct accountability — from the excipient manufacturer (maximum information obligations) to the Broker (minimal, information-flow only) to the MAH (ultimate responsibility).

                        
- **Section 5.0 (Table 5.0-2):** Six common information-gathering challenges are addressed with practical resolutions, including use of quality agreements, onsite audits, provision of declaration templates, and third-party certification programs such as EXCiPACT.

### 6.0 A Model for Quality Risk Management for Excipients

                    

The TR 54-6 Task Force developed this QRM model by surveying industry practices, analyzing real-world company models shared by manufacturing authorization holders, and combining best practices. The model applies a risk ranking and filtering approach to answer the central risk question:

                    

"Is the excipient fit for use in the drug product?"

                    

Risk ranking and filtering involves breaking this question into as many components as needed to capture all relevant risk factors. The two main factors in the model are:

                    

                        
- **Intrinsic excipient risks** — factors inherent to the excipient itself and its use in the drug product

                        
- **Supply chain risks** — factors related to the manufacturer, distributor, and transport of the excipient

                    

                    

These two factors are combined into a single relative risk score. Filters in the form of weighting factors or cut-offs support risk acceptance or mitigation decisions.

## Initiate Risk Management Activities and Identify Assessment Team

                

Who, when, and what triggers the excipient risk assessment process

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.1 Initiate Risk Management Activities

                    

The risk assessment process begins with the identification of a need to qualify an excipient or to review an existing excipient risk assessment. Triggers include:

                    

                        
- New product introduction

                        
- Obtaining a second source for an existing excipient

                        
- Notification from supplier of major changes

                        
- Other drivers discussed in Section 7.1 (ongoing review triggers)

                    

                    

### The Assessment Team

                    

A successful excipient risk assessment requires a holistic approach encompassing knowledge of the excipient's excipient function in the pharmaceutical dosage form, as well as knowledge of the manufacturer and supply chain. The team should include individuals from various functional areas. The risk assessment process may include participants with defined roles such as:

                    

                        
- **QRM facilitator** — knowledgeable about methodology and tools

                        
- **QRM experts** — area or function leads for QRM or decision-makers

                        
- **Quality Assurance** — focused on excipient use and function

                        
- **Quality** — focused on supply chain oversight (excipient manufacturer, supplier, transport)

                        
- **Qualified/responsible person**

                        
- **Material sciences, Quality control**

                        
- **Regulatory affairs, Technical/operations**

                        
- **Sterility assurance** (critical for injectables and sterile products)

                        
- **Manufacturer or supplier auditing**

                        
- **Procurement**

                        
- **R&D, Engineering**

                        
- **Nonclinical statistics, Legal, Manufacturing**

                        
- **Medical/clinical, Safety and pharmacology/toxicology**

                    

                    

Having a multidisciplinary team working collaboratively to gather and review data is critical to ensuring that all risks are considered holistically. The excipient risk management process should be built into the company's existing processes and QTPP-based quality management system (QMS).

                    

### Documentation Required

                    

The output of the initiation phase includes:

                    

                        
- Identification and documentation of the assessment team with defined roles

                        
- A clear statement of the risk question to be answered

                        
- Documentation of the trigger that initiated the assessment

                        
- Scope of the assessment (which drug products or drug product families)

                        
- Timeline and resource plan for completing the assessment

## Identify Intrinsic Risk Factors

                

Factors inherent to the excipient — chemical nature, origin, contamination potential, prior knowledge

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.2 Identify Intrinsic Risk Factors

                    

In identifying the excipient's Intrinsic Risk Factors, numerous factors should be considered. The primary source is input from product and process development.

                    

The QTPP (Quality Target Product Profile) of the drug product(s) in which the excipient is to be used provides requirements for the drug product and forms the basis of information about the excipient's use and function. During drug product design and development, scientific studies are conducted and information is collected about how the excipient may interact with other components of the product.

                    

### Excipient Attributes to Consider

                    

Examples of parameters that can be considered as excipient attributes include:

                    

                        
- Characteristics of excipient needed for use in the drug product

                        
- Manufacturer's excipient specifications

                        
- Factors learned from the manufacture of the excipient

                        
- Existence of multiple and/or global manufacturers

                        
- Lot-to-lot variability

                    

                    

### EC Guidelines Risk Factors (March 19, 2015)

                    

The EC Guidelines specify the following as key intrinsic risk factors that must be assessed:

                    

                        
- **TSE/BSE** — Transmissible spongiform encephalopathy risk (especially animal-origin excipients)

                        
- **Viral contamination** — Potential for viral contamination from biological sources

                        
- **Microbiological / endotoxin / pyrogen contamination** — Risk of microbial growth or endotoxin carry-over

                        
- **Process impurities** — Residual solvents, catalysts, and other process-generated impurities carried over

                        
- **Raw material impurities** — Aflatoxins, pesticides from plant-origin excipients

                        
- **Sterility assurance** — For excipients claimed to be sterile

                        
- **Cross-contamination** — Impurities from other processes in shared facilities (absence of dedicated equipment)

                        
- **Environmental control and storage/transport conditions** — Including cold chain management if appropriate

                        
- **Excipient stability** — Degradation risk over time or under storage conditions

                        
- **Packaging integrity** — Evidence supporting container closure integrity

## 6.3.1 Impact of Excipient on Each Type of Drug Product

                

Correlating excipient attributes to CQAs by route of administration and dosage form

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### Excipient Impact Assessment — Overview

                    

After identification of intrinsic risk factors (Section 6.2), excipient attributes are assessed for their correlation to the CQAs (Critical Quality Attributes) of the drug product in which the excipient is to be used. The rationale of any impact must be explained. This step is taken for each drug product or drug product family, and results can then be consolidated across products.

                    

Initially, all potential risk factors (hazards) are identified and documented as excipient attributes. Then, CQAs are correlated to the pharmaceutical dosage form using a three-symbol notation:

                    

                        
- **+**   Known Impact (documented effect on CQA)

                        
- **–**   No Impact (no indication of impact on CQA)

                        
- **?**   Impact cannot be ruled out; more information may be needed

                    

                    

A "Compliance" column is also included near the CQAs so that compliance with pharmacopeial specifications can be ensured. This column allows assessors to force excipient attributes marked for compliance (e.g., a compendial specification the company will claim adherence to) further into the assessment and specification, regardless of actual CQA impact.

                    

### Handling of "?" Attributes

                    

Only excipient attributes with a correlation marked "+" are brought forward as needed into the specification for further analysis. Attributes marked "?" should be further investigated to obtain data necessary to determine whether there is potential impact. If further data cannot be gathered, "?" will be treated as "+", taking a **worst-case approach**.

                    

### Risk Level by Route of Administration

                    

The Route of Administration is a critical factor in the impact assessment. The same excipient attribute may have very different risk levels depending on the dosage form:

                    
                        
                            
                                
                                
                                
                            
| Route of Administration / Dosage Form | Risk Level | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- |
                        
                        
                            
                                ****
                                
                                
                            
| Parenteral (injectable, IV, SC, IM); Ophthalmic; Open wound | HIGH | Bypasses all body defense barriers; direct contact with blood, tissues, or sensitive organs. Any impurity or endotoxin has immediate systemic access. |
                            
                                ****
                                
                                
                            
| Pulmonary / Inhalation | MEDIUM | Bypasses GI tract; large surface area of absorption; direct access to pulmonary mucosa. Particle size and impurity concerns are heightened. |
                            
                                ****
                                
                                
                            
| Topical, Oral, Vaginal, Rectal | LOW | GI barrier, skin, or mucosal membranes provide a defense. First-pass metabolism (oral) reduces systemic exposure. Topical excipients rarely reach systemic circulation in significant amounts. |
                        
                    

                    

### From Impact Assessment to Specification

                    

Excipient attributes and impact assessments are used to carry out a qualitative failure analysis describing failure options and the effect of failure (consequences). This analysis is based on knowledge from development or literature. From the failure analysis, the excipient user gains understanding of allowed variation in attributes, and specification limits for each material attribute are proposed to mitigate risk. In this way, the excipient impact assessment is used to **justify the excipient specification**.

                    

An impact assessment is conducted for each drug product in which the excipient is used:

                    

                        
- **Retrospective:** The impact assessment leads to evaluation of the existing specification

                        
- **Prospective:** The assessment provides input to one or more new specifications consistent with drug product needs

### ERPN Formula

                    

The Risk Priority Number for the excipient (ERPN) is calculated as the sum of the risk scores for each of the seven identified risk factors (RFE), divided by the number of risk factors. The resulting ERPN will be in the range of 1 (Low) to 5 (High):

                    

                        ERPN = (RFE1 + RFE2 + RFE3 + RFE4 + RFE5 + RFE6 + RFE7) ÷ 7
                    

                    

Note: Users would adjust the calculation as necessary to reflect additional risk factors identified, or to weight individual risk factors based on the company's analysis and risk tolerance. The resulting ERPN will then be used together with the supply chain risk score (SCRPN) in the risk evaluation matrix.

### Key Takeaways

                    

                        
- **Section 6.0:** The generic QRM model applies ICH Q9 principles to excipients, combining both intrinsic excipient risk (ERPN) and supply chain risk (SCRPN) into an overall risk evaluation. The central risk question is "Is the excipient fit for use?"

                        
- **Section 6.1:** Risk assessments are initiated by defined triggers (new product, new supplier, supplier changes). A multidisciplinary team covering QA, supply chain quality, sterility assurance, regulatory, and procurement is essential.

                        
- **Section 6.2:** Intrinsic risk factors are derived from product development (QTPP/CQA analysis) and EC Guideline factors (TSE, viral, microbiological, process impurities, sterility, cross-contamination, stability, packaging integrity).

                        
- **Section 6.3.1:** Excipient attributes are correlated to CQAs using +/–/? notation by dosage form. All "?" attributes are treated as "+" (worst case) if data cannot be obtained. Route of administration is the primary driver of impact level.

                        
- **Section 6.3.2:** Seven RFE factors are each scored 1/3/5 (Low/Medium/High). ERPN = sum ÷ 7. The resulting score drives the depth of supply chain controls and procurement requirements.

## 6.4 Supply Chain Risk Analysis

            

A Supply Chain Risk Score (SCRPN) is created using a risk ranking system similar to the Excipient Risk Profile (ERPN) described in Section 6.3.2. The ranking should draw on input from the supplier, including knowledge from questionnaires, previous experience, Audits, site visits, and media coverage.

            

### Minimum Risk Factors for Supply Chain (RFS)

            

At a minimum, the following six risk factors must be considered when building the SCRPN:

            

                
- **RFS1 — Supply Chain Complexity:** Supplier–broker–manufacturer relationships; opportunity for fraud; presence of repackaging or manipulation; regional security context.

                
- **RFS2 — Prior Knowledge of the Manufacturer:** Track record, prior experience, delivery reliability, evidence of excipient variability, history of complaints.

                
- **RFS3 — Organizational Volatility:** Stability of management; turnover in key quality and technical positions.

                
- **RFS4 — Excipient Manufacturer QMS Performance History:** Regulatory observations, Audit responses, CAPA timeliness, consent decrees, warning letters.

                
- **RFS5 — Packaging Suitability:** Scientific justification; tamper-evident seals; ability to protect against moisture, heat, fraud.

                
- **RFS6 — QMS Standards and Certification:** Whether the supplier holds relevant certifications (e.g., ISO 9001, EXCiPACT), documentation systems, materials management, supplier qualification programs.

                
- **RFSx — Additional factors** as needed for the specific excipient and its use in the final drug product.

| --- | --- | --- | --- |

Each RFS selection yields a value of 1 (Low), 3 (Medium), or 5 (High). The SCRPN is calculated as the arithmetic mean across all identified risk factors:

            

                

#### SCRPN Formula

                

```
SCRPN = (RF_S1 + RF_S2 + RF_S3 + RF_S4 + RF_S5 + RF_S6) / 6

Result: a number between 1 (Low) and 5 (High)
Note: scale is not linear — do not treat as such.
```

            

            

Users may adjust the calculation to reflect additional risk factors or to weight individual risk factors based on the company's analysis and risk tolerance (footnote 3 in TR54-6).

## 6.5 Risk Evaluation

            

The excipient risk score (ERPN) and the supply chain risk score (SCRPN) are evaluated together using a Risk Matrix approach. The excipient risk score appears on one axis; the supply chain risk score on the other. The combination creates an overall risk score that answers the central QRM question: *"Is the excipient fit for use?"*

            

Both ERPN and SCRPN range between 1 (Low) and 5 (High). The scale is not linear and should not be treated as such. Because risk acceptability varies significantly from company to company, the matrix and its cut-offs should be tailored to the individual company's risk tolerance.

            

### 6.5.1 Risk Matrix — Option 1: Calculating the Combined Risk Score (OverallRPN)

            

In Option 1, the combined risk score is calculated by multiplying ERPN by SCRPN:

            

                

#### OverallRPN Formula (Option 1)

                

```
OverallRPN = ERPN × SCRPN

ERPN range:  1 (Low) to 5 (High)
SCRPN range: 1 (Low) to 5 (High)
OverallRPN range: 1 (minimum) to 25 (maximum)
```

### 6.5.2 Risk Matrix — Option 2: Calculating the Final Risk Score

            

Option 2 allows additional customization. The MAH may weight the ERPN and SCRPN differently based on its internal evaluation. For instance, if excipient-related risk deserves greater emphasis, it can be given double the weight of the supply chain risk score.

            

The formula uses user-assigned weights (*wt*) for each score; *n* is the sum of all weights in the numerator:

            

                

#### Final Risk Score Formula (Option 2)

                

```
Final Risk Score = [wt(ERPN) + wt(SCRPN)] / n

Where n = sum of all weights applied.

Example: wt(ERPN) = 2, wt(SCRPN) = 1 → n = 3
Final Risk Score = [(2 × ERPN) + (1 × SCRPN)] / 3
```

            

            

The MAH may also calculate individual scores for each role in the supply chain — for example, a separate risk score for the original manufacturer and another for each broker — and use these individual mean risk scores instead of a single SCRPN.

## 6.6.1 General Considerations on Appropriate GMP Standards

            

Figure 6.6.1-1 in TR54-6 illustrates the extent and degree of risk mitigation required based on the type of drug product and its use. The degree of mitigation — the reduction in overall risk necessary to reach a low residual risk level — increases as progressively more stringent GMP Standards are applied.

            

The mitigation is defined by:

            

                
- The excipient itself — what it is and how it is made

                
- How the excipient is supplied

                
- Why it is used and how it functions in the drug product

                
- The drug product type (e.g., oral, topical, parenteral, sterile)

            

            

The list of QMS standards and GMP definitions is illustrative; other standards providing the required degree of risk mitigation may also be available. The GMP standards form a ladder from lowest to highest mitigation level:

            

                

ISO 9001 — Basic QMS (lowest mitigation)

                

EFfCI GMP for Cosmetic Ingredients (ISO 9001 annex; cosmetic focus)

                

US and EC Food "GMP" + HACCP principles

                

EXCiPACT™ GMP for Pharmaceutical Excipients (ISO 9001 annex; pharma-specific)

                

NSF/IPEC/ANSI 363 (equivalent to EXCiPACT; designed for non-ISO-9001 firms)

                

IPEC-PQG GMP Guide (guide + audit standard; equivalent to EXCiPACT)

                

EC GMP Part II / ICH Q7 (API GMP standard)

                

EC GMP Part I (drug product GMP standard)

                

EC GMP Part I + Annex 1 (sterile materials — highest mitigation)

## Section 6B Summary — Key Takeaways /

        

            

                

### English Summary

                

                    
- The SCRPN is calculated as the mean of six supply chain risk factors (RFS1–RFS6), each scored 1/3/5, yielding a result between 1 (Low) and 5 (High).

                    
- Option 1 (OverallRPN = ERPN × SCRPN) uses multiplicative logic — risks of 1–25 with thresholds at 4 and 10. Option 2 (Final Risk Score) uses weighted averaging with user-defined weights.

                    
- When OverallRPN > 10, risk mitigation is mandatory. Controls are drawn from four layers: drug product manufacturer, excipient supplier supply chain, excipient manufacturing, and MAH internal measures.

                    
- The GMP standards ladder runs from ISO 9001 (basic) through EXCiPACT/NSF/IPEC/ANSI 363/IPEC-PQG (pharma excipient tier) up to EC GMP Part II/ICH Q7, Part I, and Part I + Annex 1 (sterile tier).

                    
- Risk mitigation is iterative: implement controls → re-score → repeat until residual risk is acceptable. All agreements must be documented in quality and supply agreements.

## 7.0 Ongoing Review — Formalized Risk Assessment Lifecycle

                

            

        

        

            
            

                

                    

### 7.0 The Risk Assessment as a Living Document

                    

Once the excipient risk assessment is formally in place, it will be used as living data and will contribute to a knowledge base through ongoing risk review. Figure 7.0-1 shows the overall generic risk model with respect to risk review only.

                    

Risk Review generally will be conducted in two modes:

                    

                        
- **Periodic (monitoring-driven):** Scheduled reassessment at defined intervals based on the excipient's risk tier

                        
- **Event-driven (Trigger-based):** Immediate reassessment whenever a qualifying event occurs

                    

                    

The lifecycle model mirrors the generic risk management cycle: Initiate → Risk ID → Risk Analysis → Risk Evaluation → Risk Reduction → Risk Acceptance → Communication → **Risk Review** → (loop back). This is not a one-time exercise; Lifecycle Management ensures the risk assessment remains current and actionable.

                    

                        "The risk assessment… will be used as living data and will contribute to a knowledge base through ongoing risk review." — TR54-6, Section 7.0
                    

                

                

                    

## 8.0 Benefits and Value / QRM / Knowledge Management

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.0 The Business Case for Formalized Excipient RA

                    

A global excipient risk assessment throughout the drug product lifecycle delivers the following advantages:

                    

                        
- **Documented Knowledge Management:** Structured knowledge about the excipient supply chain, building a growing institutional repository

                        
- **Rapid new-product evaluation:** Ability to rapidly and effectively evaluate the requirements for new products that use existing or new excipients

                        
- **Preferred supply chain identification:** Identification of supply chains that present lower risks and preferred supply chains with which to build long-term relationships

                        
- **Focused oversight:** Focus on specific risk mitigation oversight activities for both manufacturer or supplier and user — directing audit resources to where they matter most

                        
- **Deeper product knowledge:** More detailed product knowledge and increased understanding of how the risks from excipients may affect product quality

                        
- **Formal trigger database:** Formal assessment and database of risk review triggers, such as major changes and critical investigations (CAPA integration)

                        
- **Knowledge-based decision-making:** Proactive rather than reactive risk management — moving from crisis response to anticipatory control

                        
- **Common language for stakeholders:** Provides a common language and approach for clear communication with stakeholders throughout the supply chain

                        
- **Knowledge-sharing opportunities:** Opportunities for comparative assessments between manufacturers, suppliers, and end users

                    

                    

                        "A global excipient risk assessment throughout the drug product lifecycle delivers… knowledge-based and proactive decision-making." — TR54-6, Section 8.0

## Section Summary — Key Takeaways

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### What You Must Know

                    

                        
- **Section 7:** The risk assessment is a *living document*. It must be reviewed periodically AND whenever a trigger event occurs. Triggers include: new supplier, GMP status change, regulatory action, pharmacopeial change, new safety data, product formulation change, and industry-wide quality events.

                        
- **Section 7.1:** All triggering events must be documented along with the decision rationale for whether to revise the risk assessment. This documentation trail is a regulatory expectation.

                        
- **Section 8:** The business case for formalized RA rests on knowledge management, proactive risk identification, supply chain transparency, and regulatory compliance efficiency — not just audit compliance.
