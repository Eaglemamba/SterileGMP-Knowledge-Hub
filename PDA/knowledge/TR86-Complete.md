## Introduction — Historical Context |

                

From TR27 to TR86: Why a new technical report was needed

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 1.0 Introduction (Part 1) — The TR27 Era and Its Successor

                    

For more than a decade, **PDA Technical Report No. 27: Pharmaceutical Package Integrity** served as an influential reference document, representing the best practices in sterile product package integrity testing. At the time it was published, the information it presented was instrumental in shaping the revision of **USP General Chapter <1207> Package Integrity and Test Method Selection**.

                    

As a result, the content of USP <1207> was **expanded in 2016** and currently serves as the primary reference chapter focusing on the basics of package integrity and best practices in sterile product package integrity evaluation and integrity testing.

                    

                        "The intent of USP <1207> is to guide package integrity development away from testing each sample at the smallest possible defect level, because quality cannot be tested into a product."
                    

                    

Rather, using a **risk-based approach** and leveraging testing to better understand, analyze, and eliminate the risks during development phases is encouraged. Use of the appropriate testing during each manufacturing phase can help in evaluating and mitigating residual risks (i.e., in production/stability to proactively address risks/failure modes). Where feasible, regulations and quality risk management principles identified in **ICH Quality Guideline Q9: Quality Risk Management** should be used.

                    

Subsequently, the **PDA Package Integrity Technical Report Team** reexamined the role of TR-27 and determined that a new technical report should be written, building on the package integrity basics updated by USP <1207> and focusing more on *advanced issues* related to pharmaceutical package integrity. They researched the existing sources of information related to the challenges encountered with complex packaging systems, such as large-volume bulk packages and prefilled syringe systems. Currently, no single source is available that thoroughly addresses these issues.

                    

Likewise, the Technical Report Team recognized the increasing number of innovative methods using existing technologies for integrity testing that deserve consideration for their potential utility in the future of pharmaceutical package integrity.

## Introduction — Lifecycle Approach & Three Content Areas |

                

What TR86 covers and how it structures the package integrity lifecycle

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 1.0 Introduction (Part 2) — TR86 Scope & Lifecycle

                    

This technical report is a **consensus-based resource** surrounding the challenges encountered in using complex package systems and introduces important elements to consider in decision-making. It also offers an examination of the technologies available for package integrity testing not yet established by peer-reviewed research.

                    

Although pharmaceutical container closure systems serve a variety of functions, the assurance of package integrity is primarily concerned with **product containment**. However, these systems also serve as an effective barrier against:

                    

                        
- Ingress of **microorganisms**

                        
- Ingress of potentially reactive gases such as **oxygen or water vapor**

                        
- Maintenance of a **vacuum** (where applicable)

                    

                    

**Package integrity** is the measure of the ability of the package to prevent product loss or maintain product sterility and the ability to maintain the internal environment (e.g., loss of vacuum seal). **Package integrity testing** is an ongoing process that starts in package development and continues throughout the life of the product.

                    

When designing a container closure system and selecting and implementing an appropriate method, the goal is to **reduce the risk to product sterility and quality**.

                    

The report addresses container closure integrity in **three main areas**:

                    

                        
1. Challenges with complex systems and package integrity methods

                        
2. Innovative methods for integrity testing using existing technologies

                        
3. Additional considerations for pharmaceutical package integrity

                    

                    

Additionally, how **quality by design (QbD)** relates to package integrity and establishing a **limit of detection (LOD)** are discussed in the final section.

## Introduction — Risk-Based Framework & Patient Safety |

                

Why CCI failure is a critical risk, and what a well-characterized process looks like

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 1.0 Introduction (Part 3) — Risk, Process Control & Patient Safety

                    

Best practices in pharmaceutical packaging suggest that **all activities be evaluated for risk to the product quality as a surrogate to patient safety**. Failure of container closure integrity is a **critical risk**, as the lack of package integrity can lead to contamination of the product which can impact both product quality and potency. Contamination of a sterile product intended for **parenteral administration** could result in dire consequences for the patient.

                    

Demonstration of container closure integrity and ensuring the integrity of the container closure system is required **throughout the lifecycle of a sterile pharmaceutical product**. A well-characterized and controlled process should consist of:

                    

                        
- A **qualified container closure design** with effective, monitored process controls in place

                        
- Process parameters required to produce integral container closure systems are maintained

                        
- Appropriate **specifications for incoming components**

                        
- **Qualification of manufacturing equipment**, including a visual inspection system for detection of irregularities in the process

                        
- Appropriate **sampling strategies** for container closure integrity testing

                    

                    

Package integrity should ensure product quality is maintained through both the **shelf life and life of a product**. Over the past two decades, analytical technology has progressed, improving the ability to evaluate closure integrity. The information provided in this technical report is intended to assist users in advancing their current integrity assessment and testing strategies during the phases of product lifecycle.

### 1.1 Purpose and Scope

                    

This technical report focuses on the challenges facing the pharmaceutical industry that use **complex packaging systems for sterile drugs and biologics** (e.g., syringes, syringe assemblies, bulk containers). It also presents information on some **innovative methods for package integrity testing using existing technologies**, including the potential impact of cryogenic conditions. The intent is to update information and incorporate experiential learning which is not addressed in PDA TR-27 and USP <1207>.

                    

It also serves as a technical resource, focusing particularly on sterile products and encouraging a **risk-based approach** and leveraging testing to better understand, analyze, and eliminate the risks during developmental phases. Use of the appropriate testing during each manufacturing phase can help in evaluating and mitigating residual risks.

### Key Glossary Relationships

                    

                        

#### Deterministic vs. Probabilistic: The Core Dichotomy

                        

The most important conceptual distinction in TR86 and USP <1207> is the difference between deterministic and probabilistic test methods. This distinction drives method selection decisions:

                        

                            
- **Deterministic**: Physics-based, quantitative, predictable, objective. Preferred by regulators. Example: HVLD, vacuum decay, headspace analysis.

                            
- **Probabilistic**: Biology-based (or statistically uncertain), qualitative, random outcomes. Historically used but less preferred for new applications. Example: Microbial Ingress Test (MIT/MIL), dye ingress.

                        

                    

                    

                        

#### MALL and LOD Relationship

                        

The Maximum Allowable Leak Limit (MALL) and the Limit of Detection (LOD) of your test method must be scientifically related:

                        

                            
- MALL = the largest defect that still puts quality at risk → defines the threshold

                            
- LOD = the smallest defect your method can reliably detect → must be ≤ MALL

                            
- If LOD > MALL: your method is not sensitive enough to catch dangerous leaks

### Key Takeaways — English

                    

                        
- **Document Hierarchy**: ICH Q9 → USP <1207> → TR86 (advanced) + TR27 (foundational). TR86 builds on basics established by USP <1207>; it does not replace it.

                        
- **Why TR86 Exists**: No single source addressed complex packaging systems (PFS, bulk containers) or innovative CCIT methods adequately. TR86 fills this gap as a consensus document.

                        
- **Risk-Based Approach**: "Quality cannot be tested into a product." Testing during development should understand and eliminate risks, not just screen for defects at release.

                        
- **Lifecycle Requirement**: CCI must be demonstrated throughout the entire product lifecycle — from package development through commercial production, stability, and beyond.

                        
- **Deterministic > Probabilistic**: Regulators and TR86 prefer deterministic methods (quantitative, physics-based) over probabilistic methods (stochastic, biology-based).

                        
- **LOD must be ≤ MALL**: Method sensitivity must always be justified against the Maximum Allowable Leak Limit established through scientific risk assessment.

                        
- **CCS = all components**: All primary and secondary packaging components and materials are part of the CCS — any change to any component requires CCI re-evaluation.

                        
- **Consensus, Not Regulation**: TR86 represents PDA expert consensus, not regulatory agency policy. Regulatory submissions still require independent scientific justification.

Method selection framework — sensitivity, variability, and known interference sources

      

    

    

      

        
        

          

### 3.0 Challenges with Methodologies

          

The selection of container closure integrity methodology is based on sensitivity, reduced variability, and clarification of the intended result of leak detection. The selected method will present challenges to the investigator, however, inherent characteristics of the chosen method and other unknown factors can increase variability, for example:

          

            
- Formulations that impact flow through a leak or that create blockages within the leak.

            
- Methods that impact formulations, e.g., a method that applies an energy level or introduces a new chemical compound that impacts the original formulation in any way.

            
- Unintended impacts of method on a container closure system (CCS) potentially introducing a defect to or masking a defect in an otherwise unadulterated sample, e.g., pressure changing the shape of the container and potentially the nature of the defect.

          

          

Section 3 addresses the challenges and potential impact of emerging issues on methodology design and selection of container closure integrity testing (CCIT).

        

        
        

          

## Positive Controls |

        

Role, types, and lifecycle applications of leak artifacts in CCIT

      

    

    

      

        

          

### 3.1 Positive Controls

          

Container closure integrity (CCI) methods use positive control units, also called **"leak artifacts."** These controls are useful for different purposes and during different stages of test methods and product development, such as:

          

            
- Design and development of the container

            
- Development of a test method

            
- Determination of the level of sensitivity to various types of leaks

            
- Determination of detection limits

            
- Validation of a test method

            
- Validation or system suitability of the level of sensitivity to various types of leaks

            
- Testing of containers for their stability properties

            
- Development of container manufacturing process (quality control) parameters

          

          

Naturally occurring leaks are valuable positive controls during qualification of a CCIT method. Leaks that occur naturally also have a limited shelf life; they may be measurable today but not tomorrow. These samples might best be used for real-condition comparison of the method to better characterize the capability of the method to detect actual leak paths when engineered leak paths are used to characterize and develop a method.

          

Acquiring a sufficient set of leak artifacts for development purposes is a challenge due to the difficulty of using the normal container production processes to generate consistent, naturally occurring leaks. Therefore, positive control units with simulated defects can be produced to represent potential container and closure defects to use in method development and verification.

          

Naturally occurring leaks can also be used during process and product development. In most cases, the geometry and leak size of naturally occurring leaks are unknown, whereas positive controls with simulated defects (such as hole-type defects with channels) usually have leak paths with known geometry that can be generated using a controlled process. Use of both types of positive controls can provide useful information to develop methods for integrity testing.

          

Some CCIT system instruments can use calibrated leaks, another type of positive control. Calibrated leaks are systematically built into the CCIT system hardware to simulate a leak. The leaks are calibrated against traceable standards and installed inside robust housing (not the product container). If possible, the calibrated leak size should be equal to or less than the maximum allowed leak (MALL) for the specific container design. For a rapid 100% CCIT method, the MALL may not be achieved.

          

The calibrated leaks are used in routine system performance verification and, for some CCIT methods, as tools for routine and frequent calibration. The way in which the leaks were created and calibrated should be completely described.

          

Note: References that provide a broad overview of historical approaches to defect development and application can be found in the additional reading section.

### 3.1.1 Considerations for Selecting Positive Controls

          

Positive controls should be selected based on **product package profile**, **method type**, and **intended outcome of the method** used during the intended lifecycle phase. Positive controls used for method development and validation should include a range of defect sizes and can include a set of different types of defects.

          

At a minimum it should include a set of surrounding leak sizes (above and below) that include the MALL rate for the specific product package and the intended limit of detection for the use of the selected method.

          

There is a limit to these types of defects; therefore, the method cannot be fully characterized to understand its capability or the true MALL of a CCS.

## Considerations When Using Positive Controls |

        

Handling challenges, plugging risk, and detection confidence requirements

      

    

    

      

        

          

### 3.1.2 Considerations When Using Positive Controls

          

Naturally occurring positive controls generated from manufacturing process failures present a challenge when attempting to control the size of leaks. In many cases, these types of defects generate large leaks, or leaks that are fragile and can be easily plugged by product. If found, these types of defects may be valuable to understand method performance against naturally occurring defects but **naturally occurring defects should not be used for method validation** due to the wide variability they exhibit.

          

Positive controls with simulated defects can also be easily plugged during handling and storage; therefore, proper storage and handling precautions should be used.

          

During the development and validation or qualification of a CCIT method, the test system must be challenged with positive controls. Positive controls should simulate large or gross leaks and those that simulate small leaks. Table 3.1.4-1 offers a summary of common positive controls or engineered defects. Engineered defects for positive controls can have the added advantage of being a micron-sized defect, but other factors should also be considered:

          

            
- Percentage of the micron-sized defects that can be consistently and reliably detected using a chosen CCIT method (NOTE: All positive controls with leaks at or above the claimed limit of detection (LoD) of the test method must be detected with high confidence, e.g., 95%).

            
- Size and geometry of leaks that occur in real life and the ability of the test method to accurately detect those defects.

            
- Leak size needed for microbial contamination to occur and pose a risk to product sterility or quality over the entire lifecycle of the product.

## Types of Simulated Positive Controls |

        

Glass micropipettes, wires, laser-drilling — mechanisms, sizes, and limitations

      

    

    

      

        

          

### 3.1.3 Types of Simulated Positive Controls

          

USP <1207.1> describes defect types as "positive controls representing realistic package flaws." Along with the examples given, it acknowledges that a chosen CCIT method may not be able to accurately detect some type defects in a given package design, so an alternative method should be identified. When a method cannot be identified, any information and design that can reduce the possible occurrence of such defects in the CCS should be sought.

          

**Common materials used to create simulated defects include:**

          

            
- Glass micropipette

            
- Wire

            
- Laser-drilled

          

          

#### Glass Micropipettes

          

Glass micropipettes are available in diameter sizes from 0.1 µm to 10 µm. The main advantage of a glass micropipette over laser-drilled and wire-simulated defects is the ability to generate relatively consistent defects at small sizes.

          

Use of a glass micropipette is considered a short microchannel of small diameter (5 µm and under); diameters under 2 µm are not perfectly round.

          

The micropipette is inserted through a protective tool (such as a hollow metallic syringe needle) into the septum or stopper and bonded to the container with epoxy, or through a hole drilled into the side glass or rigid wall and bonded with epoxy. A microscopic examination of the micropipette tip can be used to verify that the micropipette tip was not broken during sample preparation; then, a test cycle can be performed with an equivalent simulated flow rate for comparison to verify the actual performance of the micropipette.

          

Glass micropipettes are fragile, and the tip can be easily broken during sample preparation. Broken micropipette tips will typically yield an unusually higher flow rate; such a sample should not be used as a positive control.

          

#### Wires

          

Wires can simulate foreign objects, such as a human hair (diameter range of 80 µm to 100 µm) at the sealing area. Various wire material and sizes are available in diameters of 10 µm and greater. For some sterile glass containers, a wire can be placed between the plunger and the glass barrel (syringes, cartridges) or between the aluminum-crimped stopper and the glass vial.

          

The elastomeric plunger or stopper, which has been press-fitted to the container, will not fully deform around the wire; therefore, small leak paths can be generated. This type of leak is also considered a microchannel. The defect size is dependent on, for example, the wire size, elastomeric material formulation and hardness, press-fit forces, or assembly forces (crimping for vials).

          

Leak paths with calculated or estimated sizes of 2 µm to 10 µm and less have been created for glass syringes and glass vials using nickel-titanium wires with diameters of 25 µm and greater.

          

Stainless steel wires (typically 50 µm and larger diameter) are used as simulated defects for heat-sealed or ultrasonically sealed trays and pouches. For lidded trays and pouches, where the container and lid material do not conform well to the wires generating a very large defect, the wire is pulled out shortly after sealing (and before full seal-curing) to generate a smaller leak path.

          

#### Laser-Drilled Holes

          

A laser at various wavelengths, when applied to the container surface, generates localized heat, melting the material and creating an opening (or hole) in the package wall. Laser-drilling on the container walls will generate defects from equivalent sizes of 2 µm and greater. Containers are typically filled and sealed after the laser-drilling process.

          

Laser-drilling of glass products (e.g., vials, cartridges, and syringes) is done by drilling a tapered hole in the glass, where the hole does not protrude through the glass wall. The heat generated by this process results in cracking the glass near that hole, creating the leak path through the cracks. The geometry of such cracks is not consistent; however, the defect size and its behavior are hard to define. Its behavior can be a sharp-edge orifice, a very short microchannel, or a combination of both.

          

Updated laser-drilling technology can increase the time interval between laser shots to allow heat dissipation, effectively eliminating glass "cracking." These defects are essentially wormholes that have some consistent diameters across the entire glass wall thickness (i.e., hole-like defects).

          

Laser-drilling manufacturers can correlate the defect "equivalent diameter" using air flow and empirical air flow calibration curves generated from known sharp-edged metallic orifices. Defect sizes less than 10 µm in glass products may show larger variability. The size of laser-drilled defects in glass can be estimated to "equivalent micropipette" size using calibration curves. Calibration curves should not be extrapolated beyond their ranges.

          

Laser-drilled holes are often the most effective and reliable method for simulating positive controls in flexible packaging. They may be the preferred way to introduce engineered defects in such flexible containers as single-use systems (e.g., for bulk storage) or IV bags.

          

An alternative to laser-drilling directly into a container is to use stainless-steel discs. These can be subjected to laser-drilling to create defects as small as 0.5 µm and, generally, are more consistent defects to use in equipment.

## Calibrated Leaks |

        

NIST-traceable standards, He leak rates, equipment integration and system verification

      

    

    

      

        

          

### 3.1.4 Calibrated Leaks

          

Calibrated leaks are designed to yield a given leakage rate calibrated against independent, traceable calibration standards. A calibrated leak is typically an independent component that is introduced into the container barrier or into the test system volume to introduce a certified known flow rate into the test system during a challenge leak test.

          

These calibrated leaks are engineered inside a robust metallic housing and are protected from plugging via filter or clean-supplied gas. Generating a calibrated leak to mimic the headspace of sterile containers (a space with a limited air supply) is difficult because calibrated leaks need an infinite gas supply.

          

The inlet to the calibrated leak is normally open to the gas supply source, while the outlet of the calibrated leak is connected to the equipment test circuit or test chamber via a valve. When this valve is open, a known amount of gas leakage is injected either into the test circuit (in vacuum-test condition) or out of the test circuit (in pressure-test condition).

          

When running a test with a "master container," the calibrated leak simulates a defect that is consistent and robust, which can be used to challenge and verify the CCIT equipment performance. In this condition, the calibrated leak represents a positive control sample for the CCIT equipment to detect that leak.

## Blockage of Leak Paths |

        

Protein aggregates, crystallization, silicone oil — false negatives, CCI strategy beyond CCIT

      

    

    

      

        

          

### 3.2 Blockage of Leak Paths

          

Biopharmaceutical products are formulated with a wide variety of ingredients, such as proteins, nucleic acids, or living microorganisms and buffer solution. Some of these ingredients can present challenges to CCIT. For example, some CCI physical testing methods have difficulty detecting defects in samples formulated with proteins.

          

A study was performed to compare CCIT between a highly concentrated protein solution (120 mg/mL) and a surrogate solution with the same viscosity material property. The surrogate solution could flow out easily through defects, but the highly concentrated protein solution did not flow out. One hypothesis suggests that highly concentrated large molecules in solution are **agglomerated around defect channels, completely or partially blocking the channels**. The result was a solution that had difficulty flowing out through the channels. This supports the idea that proteins may play an important role in clogging the leak path.

          

Other types of defect clogging can occur when a product solution has ingredients that can **crystallize upon drying**. In this case, the solution easily leaks out through the defect channel, but the leaking solution will dry over time and turn into crystallized material in the defect channel. Once the solution has crystallized, it clogs the defect channel. This situation depends on the properties of the solution and the type of defect. Defect samples can be detected if they are tested soon after they are prepared, but the detection probability decreases as sample storage time increases.

          

When defect samples are clogged by proteins, vacuum or tracer gas-based CCI physical tests may not be able to detect defects due to the difficulty creating pressure, mass, or gas changes through the defect channel. Theoretically, if the clogged defect channels have some residual moisture and are still electrically conductive, **high-voltage leak detection (HVLD)** may be able to detect those leaks. Also, solution-based testing, such as the **dye ingress test**, may help to dissolve the crystallized materials in the clogged area if the materials are in direct contact with the dye solution, constituting a valid CCIT method for such products. However, that dye solution can also block small channels under certain conditions, such as drying or vacuum or pressure exposure.

          

When considering clogging by proteins or other ingredients in liquid-filled products over time, seal property, in addition to the CCIT, should be monitored in a stability program. This should be considered in feasibility studies and when selecting the CCIT method. CCIT may not be able to detect a defect in the seal if it is blocked by the clogging. **Sterility could be breached if the product is contaminated prior to the clogging.** Clogging is one of the challenges in CCIT; consequently, the drug manufacturers should not rely on CCIT to ensure the sterility but should develop a CCI strategy.

          

Manufacturers can develop different ways to resolve the clogging challenge. The CCI testing strategy and methods should address this clogging challenge. Visual inspection and RSF are examples. For example, when the seal is visible, like the plunger in a syringe, the plunger-rib contact length can be monitored to verify that the seal contact pressure does not change. When the seal is not visible, like the stopper in a vial or the disc seal in a cartridge, the residual seal force or rubber bulge height from the rubber compression can be used to verify that the seal contact pressure is acceptable.

### Key Regulatory & Technical Takeaways

          

            
- **3.0:** CCIT method selection must account for three variability sources: formulation-induced blockage, method-induced formulation changes, and method-induced container deformation.

Original Text (English) — Stopper & Lubricity

                    

Elastomers and plunger stoppers provide excellent sealing properties when paired with glass and plastic containers. As with other pharmaceutical parenteral closures, halobutyl elastomers are most widely used for prefilled syringe plungers due to good drug compatibility. The elastomeric formulation must be carefully chosen and must be hard enough to provide sufficient rigidity for consistent functionality, while allowing the viscoelastic properties of the plunger material to provide closure properties.

                    

The design of the plunger provides CCI and functionality of the system. To take advantage of the compressibility of the elastomer material, the plunger is designed to be slightly larger in circumference than the inner diameter of the chosen barrel.

                    

ISO 11040-5:2012 recommends that plungers be designed to use all three ribs. In general, the rib closest to the drug is considered the primary sealing rib; the second and third ribs are meant to stabilize the seal and provide secondary CCI assurance.

                    

The use of lubricity coatings on the syringe barrel will influence the ability of the container system to create CCI. Silicone oil and vapor-deposited coatings are the most commonly used. When silicone oil is sprayed on the inside of the barrel, the coating may or may not be heat-treated. Excess lubricity coating may result in the plunger moving too freely during transit, causing a breach in CCI.

Original Text (English) — Polymer PFS

                    

Alternate materials of construction provide additional challenges with design. Prefilled syringes made of glass have long been available. Polymeric (or plastic) syringes in single-use or combination formats are available as an alternate material and have some advantages over glass. These risks are related not only to the syringe barrel design, but to its molding and sterilization processes, downstream handling, packaging, and distribution processes, all of which must be identified, assessed, evaluated, and mitigated.

                    

The supplier of plastic syringe components is responsible for establishing and validating design requirements as well as critical process parameters (CPPs) for ensuring CCI. Appropriate CCIT methods should be applied to verify that the inherent leak rate of the syringe design conforms, at a minimum, to the MALL specified in USP <1207> for preserving product sterility and drug formulation content.

## Test Method Considerations for Prefilled Syringes

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

Decisions regarding which CCIT method to select for prefilled syringes should be based on packaged product physical properties such as density, viscosity, electrical conductivity, molecule size, and evaporation capability.

                    

Although many methods are available for CCIT of prefilled syringe or cartridge systems, CCIT after the assembly of prefilled syringes with auto-injectors or pen-injectors creates a new set of challenges. Disposable auto-injectors are designed to avoid tampering with the primary packaging container. Consequently, using the standard battery of CCIT methods like dye ingress, HVLD, and vacuum decay may not be suitable for this purpose; instead, they must be adapted. An adaptation may involve disassembly of the device before testing, which should verify that the disassembly method does not impact integrity or accept the decreased method sensitivity that testing the intact device would allow.

                    

Risk assessment of a fully assembled syringe and device and its impact on CCIT should be considered. The following factors could compromise the CCIT and may need to be evaluated as part of the design verification:

                    

                        
- Forces applied onto the primary container closure during final assembly, storage, and use of the device that may cause breakage or defects.

                        
- Critical forces on the needle shield (rigid or flexible needle shield), cartridge septum, or cartridge cap.

                        
- Critical forces on the plunger stopper (in cartridges or prefilled syringes).

                    

                    

Testing the actual packaged product for CCI is preferred; however, some solution properties inhibit leak detection, preventing direct testing. When the physical properties of a packaged product inhibit the use of otherwise suitable technologies, surrogate solutions may be used. For example, protein-based drug products are known for their propensity to dry in created defects; this inhibits the ability to detect those created defects by otherwise sensitive, deterministic technologies.

                    

Stopper movement should be deliberately managed during CCIT. If the test methods exploit a pressure differential between the container interior and the test chamber (e.g., vacuum decay and mass extraction), stopper movement should be minimized. Stopper movement, and the resulting change in headspace volume, will reduce the pressure differential, possibly reducing the sensitivity of the method.

                    

The application of a pressure differential to the prefilled syringe during CCIT may cause the stopper to move outward and then move back to its ordinary position once the pressure differential is removed. Stopper movement might allow entry of unwanted foreign matter; in addition, microbial contamination can be caused that could compromise the product. The adoption of technical solutions to prevent stopper movement during testing is recommended. For instance, in a vacuum decay system, one way to overcome this risk is to balance the outward stopper movement with a force equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to that of the stopper itself; this can be achieved by using a contrast pin in the test chamber.

## Additional Considerations for Secondary Packaging

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Original Text (English)

                    

A risk-based analysis should be performed to determine if any of the secondary packaging operations could impact CCI, and if CCIT should be performed to confirm that the integrity of the primary container system is maintained. If such a unit operation is identified, a CCI study should be performed on worst-case samples to demonstrate that the unit operation does not impact container integrity.

Secondary packaging typically consists of (in order) plunger rod placement, finger flange placement, labeling, tray placement, and carton insertion.

                    

Secondary packaging of a prefilled syringe can be performed manually by a technician, by a fully automated packaging line, or by a combination of manual and automated packaging.

The stopper serves as the primary barrier that maintains container integrity. Consequently, the process of inserting the plunger rod should be evaluated to determine if CCIT should be performed on the syringe after the plunger rod has been inserted to confirm that integrity has been maintained.

                    

**Finger Flange Placement:** The main risk this operation poses to CCI is fracture of the syringe. To ensure that no broken or cracked syringes enter the supply chain, a 100% visual inspection must be performed. Given that the glass syringe is the primary container, an evaluation should be performed to determine if CCIT is required.

                    

**Tray Placement and Carton Insertion:** The tray may contain one syringe or multiple syringes. The tray is then placed inside a carton for distribution. The operation of placing the syringe in the tray should be evaluated to determine if CCIT is required on the packaged syringe.

## Key Messages Summary — Section 2a

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

Key Messages (English)

                    

                        

#### KM-1: Stopper as Primary Barrier

                        

The plunger stopper-barrel interface is the primary and most critical CCI barrier in any prefilled syringe design. Its three-rib compression structure provides layered sealing assurance. Any process step that disturbs this interface — filling, stoppering, rod insertion, transport — must be evaluated for CCI impact.

                    

                    

                        

#### KM-2: Multiple Interfaces = Multiple Failure Modes

                        

Unlike a simple glass vial (one primary closure), a PFS has up to three active seal interfaces (stopper-barrel, needle-shield, barrel-shield for staked needle designs). Each interface is a potential failure point. CCI strategy must address all interfaces throughout the product lifecycle.

                    

                    

                        

#### KM-3: Silicone Oil — Dual Role and Blocking Risk

                        

Silicone oil is essential for PFS functionality but creates two CCI-related risks: (1) excess oil allows the stopper to move freely during transport, breaching CCI; (2) silicone oil can physically block small defects, causing false negatives in probabilistic methods like dye ingress testing. Deterministic methods (vacuum decay, HVLD) are preferred for PFS CCIT.

                    

                    

                        

#### KM-4: Stopper Movement in Vacuum-Based CCIT

                        

Applying a pressure differential to a PFS during vacuum decay testing causes the stopper to move outward. This (a) reduces the sensitivity of the test by reducing the pressure differential, and (b) risks introducing foreign matter or microbial contamination. A contrast pin in the test chamber is a validated engineering solution to counteract stopper movement.

                    

                    

                        

#### KM-5: Risk-Based Approach to Secondary Packaging CCIT

                        

Not every secondary packaging operation requires CCIT, but every operation must be assessed for CCI risk. When a risk is identified, a worst-case CCI study must be performed. Semi-finished syringe CCI data does not substitute for finished syringe CCI assessment.

## Test Method Considerations

        

        

          

            

              4.2.3 TEST METHODS
              

Microbial challenge testing is a well-known approach for identifying the MALL if prevention of microbial ingress is the critical container quality attribute. Two types of microbial integrity test methods are commonly used: testing by immersion and testing by aerosol.

              

Recent studies have demonstrated aerosol testing for microbial ingress to be equally as stringent as the immersion method, showing a MALL of 2 µm no matter which method is used. Flexible bulk packaging systems are increasingly being used for drug substance, drug product, and fill-finish applications in the biotech industry. Sound, scientific test methods are needed to ensure their quality throughout the product lifecycle.

              

The current model to ensure integrity is based on a quality by design (QbD) and QRM approach that should be applied by suppliers and users. Suppliers must possess a strong understanding of the design and qualification of a product for its intended use. Likewise, users must ensure their handling and other processes are compatible with the design of the SUS. Both suppliers and users should consider the method and frequency of testing as part of risk mitigation.

              

New technologies will continue to develop to meet the market needs, such as faster inspection times and greater sensitivity. Regardless of the capability of any single test method, it must always fit into an overall approach to quality and risk mitigation through each step of the supply chain.

              

Ongoing research on MALLs and the comparison of real-world to model defects is required to enhance understanding and increase integrity assurance for flexible bulk container systems.

## IV Bags — Flexible Finished Pharmaceutical Containers

        

      

    

    

      
      

        

          

### Overview

          

An IV bag is a pharmaceutical product container made of flexible material, closed at the bottom and along the sides by sealing; the top of the container may be closed by fusion to IV tubing or a medication port, depending on the intended use.

          

Because it comes in direct contact with the pharmaceutical product or formulation, IV bags are made of materials that will not alter the quality (efficacy or stability) of the product or formulation. Some of the information in this section may be relevant to considerations for bulk containers as well.

        

        

          

### IV vs. SUS

          

      

      
      

        

          

4.3.1

          

## Design

        

        

          

            

              4.3.1 DESIGN
              

Flexible packages often undergo temporary shape changes caused by forces within the environment, such as damages incurred during distribution and storage. The magnitude of forces sufficient to cause loss of package sterility by converting a defect into a leak must be considered when trying to determine an appropriate CCIT method that can reliably detect the leak of concern.

              

Imposed pressures capable of initiating liquid or gas flow through a defect provide the necessary conditions for ingress or egress to occur and, subsequently, result in a loss of sterility or quality of the product.

              

For a specific product package system, an understanding of product's liquid properties, such as surface tension and viscosity, is useful to determine the critical leak for that system.

4.3.2

          

## Challenges

        

        

          

            

              4.3.2 CHALLENGES
              

When liquid-filled IV bags are tested for CCI, any leak from the container can pose a challenge if the product leaks and contaminates the test system (equipment and/or test chamber). The residual liquid or vapor that leaks into a test chamber from a defect in the container must be removed and the test system must be cleaned before testing can resume. This activity can affect the throughput of the test method.

              

A defect in direct contact with air may be detected at a faster rate than a defect in contact with a liquid or product solution, and the location of the defect can have a significant impact. The product solution of some IV bags can also dry and crystallize, potentially clogging micron-size defects and making leak detection a challenge.

              

The escape of residual volatile gases and vapors from the outside surface of IV bags when they are exposed to vacuum conditions may affect leak test results by giving false positives during testing (e.g., with vacuum decay or mass extraction test methods). With such methods, the use of special fixtures or the inclusion of equilibration time before the actual test time may be necessary.

              

Positive controls (or engineered defects) for IV bags can pose a challenge when considering leak detection through micron-sized holes. No one method can detect every different type of defect. Hence, the CCIT tool kit should encompass a variety of methods.

## Test Method Considerations

        

        

          

            

              4.3.3 TEST METHODS
              

The condition of the test specimens can have a significant impact on the test results; for example, the temperature of flex bag, time between sterilization and testing, humidity, and many other factors can influence test results. Moisture may be present on the surface of liquid-filled IV bags, especially after moist heat sterilization, which may make it difficult to detect leaks with certain technologies.

              

Certain IV bags may not be suitable for leak detection methods that employ high vacuum or high pressure due to the potential for bursting or dislodging the container closure components.

              

Deterministic method options are HVLD, vacuum decay, mass extraction, force decay, and laser headspace analysis (tunable laser diode spectroscopy). Probabilistic method options are dye or microbial ingress, and internal pressure.

              

Certain CCIT methods, like HVLD, detect leaks in the walls of nonporous, rigid, or flexible packaging containing liquid product. Hence, this method is not compatible with dry pharmaceutical drug products or product packages with liquid on the exterior of the package (e.g., liquid loads immediately removed from a sterilizer).

              

Some films that are used in the manufacture of IV bags are readily permeable to inert gases like helium, which makes application of a helium leak test method a challenge for that specific type of CCS.

              

Natural or real-world CCI defects are generally larger in size than engineered defects and represent all possible CCI failure modes. Natural CCI defects may not be pristine holes but rather long, complex, irregular channels and tortuous pathways.

4.4.1

          

## Design — Cryogenic Storage Observations

        

        

          

            

              4.4.1 DESIGN
              

Refrigeration storage temperatures in the 2 °C to 8 °C range are common for many products. Freezer storage at -20 °C is the next most common temperature. Ultra-cold storage, beginning at -80 °C, is most commonly used for clinical trial samples where complete stability data may not yet be available (e.g., live viral vaccines); it is also the temperature of dry ice, commonly used for the shipment of product.

              

Observations of overpressure have been reported in a significant number of vials after storage at -80 °C. When syringes were inserted into these vials, the plungers pushed outwards and, when the syringe was removed, product sprayed from needle puncture holes. Syringe plunger movement after being inserted into a vial, or product spraying out of a needle hole, indicates a substantial overpressure inside the vial.

              

When vials exhibiting the overpressure phenomena were tested using laser headspace gas analysis methods, the results clearly showed changes in headspace conditions different from the specified manufacturing process parameters. The vials should have, at room temperature, a headspace gas composition of air (20.9% oxygen / 79.1% nitrogen / 0.04% CO₂) and a pressure of 1 atmosphere.

              

Figures 4.4.1-1, 4.4.1-2, and 4.4.1-3 show examples of changes in headspace gas composition and pressure after storage at -80 °C on dry ice. The measurements show excessive levels of CO₂ (atmospheric air has <0.4 mbar), reduced levels of oxygen (atmospheric air has 20.9% oxygen), and elevated levels of total pressure (atmospheric air is 1000 mbar). These headspace changes have been attributed to a CCI failure while the product was stored at -80 °C.

              

The root cause of CCI loss at cold temperatures is believed to be a result of physical changes to packaging components at low temperatures. Elastomers and glass have markedly different thermal expansion coefficients, and stopper elastomers have glass transition temperatures similar to storage temperatures. The greater thermal contraction of elastomer materials, compared to glass, and the material transition below the glass transition temperature to more plastic-like properties, increases the possibility of a CCI failure.

              

When CCI is compromised, nonsterile reactive gases from the surrounding environment enter the package and are trapped; once the vial warms to room temperature, the elastomer regains its elastic properties and returns to its room-temperature dimensions and fit.

4.4.2

          

## Challenges — Thermal Properties of Packaging Components

        

        

          

            

              4.4.2 CHALLENGES
              

Glass vials used to package finished sterile products are commonly made of borosilicate materials. Stoppers are primarily butyl elastomers. Each of these components have coefficients of linear thermal expansion and may also have glass transition temperatures.

              

The change in temperature of a sterile product package is significant, from room temperature of 20 °C to ultra-cold temperature of -80 °C and, possibly, to liquid nitrogen temperatures of -196 °C.

              

**Key quantitative data:** The volume of a butyl elastomer stopper will decrease by 6% when the temperature drops from 20 °C to -20 °C, and decrease by 11% when the temperature drops from 20 °C to -70 °C (the glass transition temperature). Below the glass transition temperature, the thermal expansion-contraction rate of materials decreases, but a further decrease in temperature, to -196 °C, could result in a total stopper volume decrease of up to 20% compared to room temperature volume.

              

In contrast, over the same temperature range (room temperature to -196 °C), borosilicate glass undergoes a volume change of less than 1%.

              

When the temperature drops from 20 °C to -80 °C, the stopper will contract to 88% of its original volume while the glass vial contracts to 99.5%. Below the glass transition temperature, the stopper loses its elasticity and becomes rigid and more plastic-like.

              

As the temperature drops to -80 °C, the headspace gas pressure in the vial drops to 66% of its initial room temperature value (1000 mbar drops to 660 mbar). When a stopper–vial seal loses integrity at -80 °C, the pressure difference between the 1 atmosphere storage environment and the 0.66 atmosphere inside the vial will drive the outside gas into the vial headspace.

              

If a vial is stored at -80 °C on dry ice and loses CCI below the glass transition temperature of -70 °C, the vial will fill with 0.35 atmosphere of CO₂. Over a longer storage period, the oxygen in the vial will diffuse out and be replaced with CO₂. If the vial is then removed from dry ice storage and warmed, the stopper will regain elasticity at the glass transition temperature and the vial will reseal. The headspace pressure will increase from 1 atmosphere at -70 °C to 1.4 atmosphere at 20 °C.

## Test Method Considerations

        

        

          

            

              4.4.3 TEST METHODS
              

Measuring changes in headspace gas composition and pressure after cryogenic storage can provide insight into whether a package has maintained CCI. A key point is that the CCI failure is temporary. As the vial warms up, the elastomer regains its elastic properties, expands, and reseals the vial.

              

Some methods are well suited for measuring CCI failure. When CCI is lost in a sterile pharmaceutical vial stored at cryogenic temperatures, the gas composition and pressure in the vial headspace will change, and headspace analysis can measure those changes. Depending on the cold storage atmosphere (air in a freezer or CO₂ on dry ice), the increases in CO₂ or total pressure and/or the decreases in oxygen concentration can be measured.

## Helium Testing

        

Deterministic, nondestructive CCI for flexible SUS bag systems | p26–28

      

    

    

      

        

          

Original Text — English

          

Helium integrity testing is a well-known, deterministic, nondestructive test method that has been used for many years in several industries to test rigid containers. The technology, which has special applications, now allows the testing of flexible polymeric container systems, such as those described in Section 4.2.

        

        

          

          

            

#### 

            

              

              

              

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

Helium testing is suitable for test units that are dry and can withstand at least 1 psi pressure differential once they are restrained by a special holder. This test method can be used to verify the integrity of fully-assembled 2-D and 3-D single-use bags (such as disposable bioreactor or mixing and storage bags) as well as complex manifold systems consisting of multiple 2-D or 3-D bags connected to a central tubing manifold (Figure 5.1.1-1🔍). Specific components that have a natural leak rate above the determined limit, e.g., aseptic connectors with porous membranes, cannot be tested and must be excluded. Permeation leak rate of nondefective assemblies should also be considered. The complex assemblies, which have more than 100 connection points (such as barb–tube, film–film, and film–fitment joints), can be tested to a defect size of a 2 µm pinhole equivalent flow-effective diameter.

            

The method involves placing a finished biopharmaceutical single-use bag assembly into an enclosed test chamber, evacuating the chamber to create a vacuum, and then introducing a measured quantity of helium gas into the bag assembly being tested. A specialized pumping technique reduces the stress on the bag assembly by reducing the internal pressure of the bag along with the external (chamber) pressure. In addition, the test chamber can be equipped with restraining plates and porous spacers to mechanically support the assembly under test and avoid the masking effect of defects. The helium-filled bag assembly is then held for a fixed time while the surrounding vacuum is monitored for leaking helium using a sensitive mass spectrometer. An automatically preceded pressure-sensing gross leak check prevents the test chamber from being saturated with large quantities of helium, practically eliminating the system down times due to helium pollution. This method has the advantage that it can be made semi-automatic, reducing the role of the operator.

The helium test apparatus includes a vacuum test chamber, pressure sensors, a vacuum pump, and a path providing the helium to escape. ## Optical Emission Spectroscopy (OES)

        

Headspace gas analysis for deterministic CCI | p28–30

      

    

    

      

        

          

Original Text — English

          

The emission spectrum of a chemical element or compound (atom or molecule) is the spectrum of frequencies of electromagnetic radiation emitted due to the atom or molecule making a transition from high energy state to a lower energy state. The energy of the emitted photon is equal to the difference between the two states. For each atom, there are many possible electron transitions, and each transition has a specific energy difference. This collection of different transitions, leading to different radiated wavelengths, makes up an emission spectrum. Each element's emission spectrum is unique; therefore, optical emission spectroscopy (OES) can be used to identify the elements in matter of unknown composition or to perform chemical analysis of substances.

          

OES analysis does not require any specific tracer gas to measure total leakage on sealed containers. Instead, the gas mixture naturally present in the container headspace of the primary container is used to perform high-sensitivity tests over a large detection range.

        

        

          

          

            

#### OES

            

          

          

            

#### 

            

          

          

            

#### OES

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

The OES equipment measures the total leak rate for a specific gas from a sample under vacuum. The test method is deterministic, nondestructive, easy to use, easy to set up, and highly sensitive.

            

OES has been developed to measure or check the CCI of a variety of sterile CCS such as pouches, vials, prefilled syringes, and cartridges. In the case of prefilled liquid products, water leaks coming from the surface and air leaks coming from the air headspace can potentially be measured simultaneously. The ability to analyze the molecular signature of the leaking gas helps troubleshoot the source and location of the leak (e.g., for a CO2-filled liquid package, a higher level of CO2 indicates headspace leakage). In addition, the molecular signature may reduce false rejects (e.g., for dry products filled with nitrogen, an unusually high level of hydrogen may suggest moisture leakage into the chamber due to chamber-seal leakage).

            

For sterile glass products, the low outgassing rate of the glass vials and syringes allows testing of the products in batches of several syringes, potentially increasing the throughput while maintaining the high sensitivity of the test.

          

          

            

              

#### 

              

                

                

                

                

              

            

            

              

#### 

              

              

            

            

              

#### 

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

The OES equipment includes a certified calibrated leak, which is used for the calibration. The leakage is measured in leak flow unit of mbar·L/s.

            

The OES sensor is connected directly to a test chamber in which the container to be analyzed is loaded. The test chamber is evacuated and, at low pressure (<5×10-2 mbar), the OES sensor is able to generate plasma. The excited atoms and ions in the discharge plasma generate light through numerous characteristic emission spectral lines generated by the elements in the gas mixture and captured by a camera. The wavelength and intensity (amplitude) of each emission line is analyzed. The emission intensity (amplitude) is dependent on the partial pressure (mol ratio) of the related element in the gas mixture, and change in intensity over time is proportional to leakage and/or outgassing.

            

Testing time is a direct consequence of the desired LoD in combination with the total outgassing flow, which depends on the materials under vacuum and the relative humidity in the ambient air trapped on the container.

            

The test sequence has three major steps:

            

              
1. **Rough Evacuation Step.** The chamber starts at barometric conditions and is evacuated down to a few mbar. At the end of this step, if the set vacuum level is not reached due to a gross leak, the test will stop and a proper test failure message will be displayed.

              
2. **High-Vacuum Evacuation Step.** When the pressure inside the test chamber reaches the maximum allowed pressure for the turbo pump (typically a few mbar), the OES sensor is in contact with the test chamber and pressure can be measured. It takes one or two seconds to reach steady measurement due to pressure fluctuations.

              
3. **Measurement Step.** The OES sensor is measuring continuously while the pressure is continuously decreasing. The test duration has been defined during the calibration of the equipment in order to achieve a signal-to-noise ratio large enough to reach a good capability.

            

          

          

            

              

#### OES

              

            

            

              

#### 

              

              

              

            

            

              

#### 

              

              

                

                

              

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

The instrument measures the leak rate for any gas in a container (e.g., oxygen, nitrogen or even water vapor) escaping from a leaking sample under vacuum. Because the test is performed under high vacuum, the driving parameter of the OES method sensitivity is the outgassing rate of the container material in contact with or exposed to high vacuum. The smaller the leak, the longer the outgassing takes for creating a measurable pressure difference, and this rate or better the amount of gas escaping from a leak determines as a consequence the LoD for a given test time. Due to the physical constraints under those high-vacuum conditions, water exists only in vapor form, which enhances the LoD towards smaller leak sizes when the leak is filled with water because an evaporating droplet of water creates significantly more gas as if it would escape from the head space of a container. In other words, in the case of a small dry leak, compared to the same small leak with liquid behind it, due to a constant water evaporation during the measurement cycle, the amount of gas escaping through the leak is far higher and the detection limit for those holes is also shifted toward much smaller hole sizes.

            

The upper detection limit, the limit of how large a leak can be to become detected with a differential pressure method, depends on the size of the headspace volume compared to the amount of liquid in the container. If a leak is very large relative to the container size and fill level during this evacuation cycle, all gas and water vapor could be evacuated before the test cycle can start. Under these conditions, the test will produce a false-positive detection, which means a leaking product can pass as an integral product because the container is already empty. Usually those conditions do not occur on all container and fill level variations, but it should be considered as a risk factor before choosing the method.

A number of factors should be considered when using OES, such as safety considerations and the design of the vacuum chamber. Local safety rules and regulations should be followed at all times, and particular attention should be paid when handling toxic drugs, especially if large leaks may be present.

            

Design of the vacuum chamber affects both testing and packaging. Proper chamber design is critical to contain liquid spills and minimize test volume. Tests are performed at high vacuum (under 5×10-2 bar-absolute), during which large-molecule drugs may plug the leak path; consequently, this method should be qualified for the specific drug being tested. In addition, flexible packages, or packages with moving parts (e.g., prefilled syringes), must have proper vacuum chamber design to constrain expansion from causing damage to the package. Flexible packages with large outgassing, such as IV bags, should be tested based on the specific application, as outgassing depends on the package material.

## Airborne Ultrasound

        

Through-transmission seal integrity for flexible and porous packaging | p31–33

      

    

    

      

        

          

Original Text — English

          

Ultrasonic waves are high-frequency sound waves that exhibit behaviors characteristic of sound waves. When a boundary exists between two media with varying acoustic impedances, the ultrasonic waves are partially reflected and partially transmitted through one medium into the other. Additionally, refraction, absorption, and diffraction occur at various instances. Airborne ultrasound is a point-focused signal that pulsates at 500 pulses per second.

        

        

          

          

            

#### 

            

            

              

              

              

              

              

            

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

Airborne ultrasound, a seal integrity test, is a "through transmission technology" that transmits ultrasound through a material and receives the signal on the opposite side of the material. Since, porous packaging film allows the passage of gases (such as air) while maintaining a sterile barrier, traditional vacuum decay or tracer gas leak tests cannot be used to ensure its integrity. Airborne ultrasound seal inspection allows the seals of porous packaging to be inspected online, quantitatively and nondestructively.

            

Porous sterile barrier packaging is one of a variety of flexible package formats available. Creating a functional flexible barrier package typically requires bonding two films together to create a flexible package structure, then sealing it once it has been filled with the product. Each of the pouch seals is critical to the integrity of the pouch, and measuring the quality of those seals is a means of ensuring the integrity of the flexible packaging.

            

Visual inspection of both porous and nonporous flexible packaging assumes that all defects are visible. The quality of a seal is dependent only on physical properties; therefore, the visual appearance of a seal area does not ensure the integrity of the seal. Heat-sealing bars can leave a seal imprint on the outside of a package, while the inner seal fails to reach bonding temperature and never seals completely or has a weak peel strength.

          

          

            

              

#### 

              

              

                

                

                

              

            

            

              

#### 

              

            

            

              

#### 

              

                

                

                

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

The airborne ultrasound system consists of an ultrasound sensor head and a signal processing system. The sensor head is used to pulse sound at a bonded film material and measure the signal strength on the opposing side. The ultrasound signal processor gathers the ultrasound pulsing measurements and processes the measurement into quantitative summary data for evaluation.

            

The sound is focused on an active area relative to frequency (or wavelength) of the sound. The active area determines the resolution of the focused measurement. Airborne ultrasound is primarily suited for film materials, providing an acoustic signature of bond materials or material characteristics based on thickness, density, and material uniformity.

            

The principle of ultrasound measurement relates to double-paned, soundproof windows. A single-pane window allows sound to propagate through the single pane and be measured on the opposing side. A double-pane window absorbs and reflects most of the sound waves, preventing the majority of sound from reaching the opposing side. Relating this principle to airborne ultrasound, the sound waves will propagate through a well-bonded seal (a single pane). The signal transmitted through a bonded seal is measured and used to determine seal quality. A weak seal, seal defect, or point of nonbonding will prevent the ultrasound from propagating through the seal. For multiple layers (no seal), the signal through the seal is blocked completely (like the double-paned window).

            

The ultrasound signal is a point-focused measurement. Test data comprises signal intensity associated with a position on the material or seal area and the decibel-level signal strength. Seal defects can create additional transitions of plastic-to-air or plastic-to-foreign contaminant, causing multiple reflections and refractions. Trapped air or sufficiently large solid contamination (≥1 mm) could also cause the signal to attenuate or decrease in strength. Alternatively, the presence of cuts or holes in the seal would result in a peak in the signal strength.

            

The measurement can be presented in a line graph format (Figure 5.3.2-3🔍), or a composite scan can pair line scans to create an optoacoustic image (Figure 5.3.2-4🔍). The optoacoustic image is a data matrix of ultrasonic signal strength data paired with X-Y coordinates. The data is presented in the form of a bitmap file, making the image quantitative in its base measurement.

            

While an optoacoustic image can be used to evaluate seal quality, the linear scan inspection mode is the most appropriate for discrete defect detection. The leak detection is based on processing a series of high-frequency data points and compiling the population of data into summary statistics for each scan. Summary statistics consist of the average signal, maximum signal, minimum signal, standard deviation of the signal, and length of the scan.

            

A low signal value or minimum value usually indicates the presence of a defect such as contamination, inclusions, delamination, voids, or no seal. A very high signal or maximum value indicates the presence of a cut or gap in the seal, or that the test was taken too close to the edge of the sample.

Defect detection using airborne ultrasound is based on the quantitative assessments of a scanned seal. As described in Section 5.3.2, the defect is detected by breaching a set-point limit for a summary statistic; defects can also be visually apparent in graphical form. The ability of the airborne ultrasound to detect a defect is based on a variety of factors. Defects that can be detected are typically cold seals, channel defects, wrinkles, areas of nonbonding, poor seals, material variations, seal variations, cuts, or holes. The ability to detect these defects is primarily dependent on the physics of ultrasound and the material properties.

          

          

            

              

#### 

              

                

                

                

                

                

                

              

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

Two additional factors that need to be evaluated before an application can be considered are the package contents and the seal geometry. With package contents, many seal defects occur due to product inclusion in the seal area. Liquids can propagate an ultrasonic signal effectively from one material to another, which means the sound would transfer through a defect if liquid were present in the defect. The presence of liquids in the seal can produce test results similar to that of a good seal.

            

Seal geometry can also affect seal inspection. The ultrasonic signal is a focused beam of sound, and approximately 90% of the sound remains within the target focal point. Some sound energy resides outside the focal point. If a seal is located on the edge of the material and a scan is performed on the edge of the material, sound will travel around the edge and will be measured on the receiving side. The ultrasonic sensor must be within 2 mm to 3 mm inside the pouch to produce a useful ultrasonic signal response. As the sensor nears the edge of a seal, the ultrasonic signal is impacted by sound reflecting within the seal, as well as sound passing around a seal edge to reach the receiver. These two factors limit the ability of an ultrasound to scan near the edge of a seal.

## X-Ray Detection X

        

Physical attribute evaluation for glass containers and assembled devices | p34–37

      

    

    

      

        

          

Original Text — English

          

X-ray detection may not be considered a leak test, as it neither quantitatively nor qualitatively evaluates leak rates or leakage; however, X-ray technologies support CCI evaluations in a variety of ways. Technological breakthroughs in manufacturing of less costly and more sensitive large detector arrays, as well as the increased acceptability of products exposed to X-ray radiation, have resulted in an increased potential for its use.

          

X-radiation is a form of electromagnetic radiation that has a wavelength in the range of 0.01 nm to 10 nm, corresponding to frequencies in the range of 30 PHz to 30 EHz and energies in the range of 100 eV to 100 keV. X-rays are shorter in wavelength than UV rays and longer than gamma rays. X-rays can be generated by an X-ray tube, a vacuum tube that uses a high voltage to accelerate the electrons released by a hot cathode to a high velocity. The high velocity electrons collide with a metal target, the anode, creating the X-rays.

        

        

          

          

            

#### X

            

            

          

          

            

#### X

            

              

              

              

              

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

X-ray is applied to package systems to evaluate physical attributes of the test system that may not be visible to the naked eye or even to some visual inspection systems. For this reason, X-ray detection, in its current state, is best used as a supporting technology rather than a leak test, as a supplemental means of characterizing a package system with respect to CCI. Physical attributes may include defects conducive to leakage, such as poor stopper fit or cracks in a glass vial or syringe, but not necessarily the propensity for gas or liquid to leak, or flow, through such defects.

          

          

            

              

#### X

              

              

                

                

                

                

              

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

For pharmaceutical CCI applications, an X-ray image can be produced in a number of ways. The first is a line scan technique (Figure 5.4.2-1🔍), where the object to be inspected is slowly moved through a plane of X-ray radiation and the picture is created in a linear X-ray-sensitive diode array. This technique is called fan or line-beam X-ray scanning. The advantage is high resolution, although from a very low speed, and the scan speed defines the resolution.

            

A variation of this scanning technique known as X-ray tomography, or CT (computerized tomography) scan, employs the use of multiple X-ray scans at different angles to reconstruct a 3-D volume rendering of the external and, importantly, internal geometries of the sample. CT scans have the advantage of high resolution and complete 3-D rendering, enabling the operator to evaluate a single package from an array of angles. However, a single scan may span minutes, hours, or even days depending on instrument capability and resolution.

            

Another technique for X-ray analysis is the area X-ray camera, which works much like a simple photography camera. Here, the object will be penetrated by the radiation, attenuated by the object, and the result captured as a gray-scale image on a large X-ray-sensitive diode array (See Figure 5.4.2-2🔍). This scanning process produces 2-D as well as 3-D pictures.

            

The area imaging technique is fast and reliable because the pictures are taken during a complete standstill of sample and beam. With this technology, a picture can be produced and evaluated within 100 ms or faster.

            

There are two types of X-ray tubes. The first is the closed-tube design, which produces radiation with a focus between 800 µm and 1500 µm. The most useful application field, as it pertains to CCI evaluation, is referred to as the microfocus tube design, which has a focus between 1 µm and 5 µm.

If this resolution is sufficient, X-ray can detect glass defects under the vial crimp due to its ability to easily penetrate aluminum. This ability also provides options to inspect assembled devices or syringes inside blisters or other secondary packs. For this reason, X-ray detection may be a valuable addition to an automated process or may be used as an offline tool to evaluate the physical characteristics behind a known leaking sample.

X-ray technology also has the ability to detect functional defects in pharmaceutical primary packages, especially in syringes. Many defects can be associated with prefilled syringes, ranging from imperfections in glueing the needle socket to defective luer locks around the syringe socket. Other defects can be associated with bent needles within the needle shield or cut-off needle tips that can cause package integrity issues or detrimental effects to the patient.

## CDMO Validation Strategy & Regulatory Considerations — CDMO

        

Practical guidance for implementing innovative CCI methods in a pharmaceutical contract manufacturing context

      

    

    

      

        

          

Validation Framework for Innovative CCI Methods

          

#### General Validation Requirements

          

All CCIT methods, whether deterministic or probabilistic, require a documented method validation package before use in GMP release or in-process testing. For the four innovative methods in Section 5, the following validation elements are universally applicable:

          

            
1. **Sensitivity / LOD determination** — establish the smallest detectable defect using positive controls with known defect sizes (e.g., laser-drilled pinholes of 2 µm, 5 µm, 10 µm).

            
2. **Specificity** — demonstrate that the test discriminates between defective and nondefective units with statistical confidence (false positive rate and false negative rate).

            
3. **Repeatability and reproducibility** — run the same sample multiple times and across different operators/instruments to confirm consistency.

            
4. **Robustness** — evaluate the effect of small but deliberate changes in key parameters (pressure, temperature, test time) on test outcome.

            
5. **Calibration traceability** — all reference leaks and calibrated standards must be traceable to national or international standards.

          

          

#### Reference Standards

          

            
- USP <1207> Package Integrity Evaluation — Sterile Products

            
- ASTM F2338 (vacuum decay), F2981 (OES), F3287 (ultrasound)

            
- ISO 11607 (sterile barrier systems)

            
- PDA TR27 (pharmaceutical package integrity)

            
- FDA Guidance: Container Closure Integrity Testing in lieu of Sterility Testing

          

          

#### Method Qualification vs. Validation

          

For new container/closure systems introduced by a CDMO client, a full method *validation* is expected. For minor changes to an already-validated container (e.g., different vial size but same material), a method *qualification* or verification study may be sufficient, subject to risk assessment and regulatory strategy.

## 6.0 Additional Considerations for Package Integrity Profiling |

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

          

A robust package integrity profile for a given product-package system considers factors experienced throughout the product's lifecycle as required by USP <1207>. As such, a package integrity profile must consider anticipated or experienced stresses, including those resulting from transportation and distribution. Such stresses may include shock, vibration, compression, temperature, pressure, and general package storage environment.

          

            

#### Key Concept: Package Integrity Profile

            

A package integrity profile is the comprehensive, lifecycle-spanning record of a product-package system's integrity status. It encompasses results from development, manufacturing, stability, and distribution phases. USP <1207> requires that this profile account for all stresses the package will experience throughout its life.

          

        

        

          

### — 6.0

6.1

      

        

## 6.1 Transportation and Distribution |

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

          

A package integrity profile is unique to each product-package system, a concept that also applies to storage and distribution considerations. The types of stresses experienced in these lifecycle phases vary widely with dosage form, pack-out configuration, product requirements, and distribution chain. Additionally, the impact of any single stress factor, such as a temperature excursion, can vary with respect to product. An excursion critical to one product may be insubstantial to the quality of another.

          

Given the potential impact to product quality and sterility, appropriate challenges and assessments should be made to ensure that product quality is not affected during the transportation and distribution circuit. The types of challenge conditions and subsequent assessments performed, however, reflect the properly assessed risks for the system in question.

          

As the intent of this report is to focus on package integrity, only those distribution challenges impacting integrity are discussed here. Transportation and distribution challenges are not limited solely to integrity of the package, however. A fully integral package containing a proteinaceous product may experience vibration and shock that does not lead to integrity failure but, for example, may cause aggregation of protein. Thus, in addition to post-challenge CCIT, other assessments may be necessary to evaluate the impact of distribution on overall product quality.

        

        

          

### — 6.1

          

          

            

#### ≠

            

          

          

            

#### Distribution Circuit

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

            

Perhaps the most obvious challenges experienced during distribution are physical stresses such as shock, vibration, and compression. Often, these types of events result in gross leakage, for instance, a vial shattering. This type of gross defect is easily detected and generally prevents final use of the product. The formation of cracks, checks, chips, and other small defects, however, may result in leakage that negatively impacts product quality but is not readily detectable by the end user. Thus, these types of defects present a more latent and use-specific risk.

            

Furthermore, such types of defects may result in more serious integrity concerns further along in the distribution chain, as a check may develop into a crack, or a crack may widen and permit liquid loss or microbial ingress.

            

Consequently, to evaluate and reduce risk associated with a shipment, not only the primary package, but the secondary and tertiary packaging should be considered. Numerous standards are available from such organizations as ASTM and the International Safe Transit Association (ISTA) to reliably and reproducibly subject package systems to physical stresses that replicate a variety of distribution chains, including via truck, rail, air, sea, or others.

            

The effects of temperature on packaging and distribution is also discussed in Section 4.4 on Cryogenic Conditions.

          

          

            

### — 6.1.1

            

            

              

#### 

              

            

            

              

#### ISTA

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

            

In cases where a low-pressure environment could present challenges to the package and product it contains, evaluation of the pressure differentials in a distribution chain could be critical. This is particularly relevant in cases of air shipment, where product may be stored in cargo areas with partial to nonexistent pressurization that, if left unevaluated, may result in unintended consequences. The ability of a sealed flexible container to resist creep or burst during air shipment is an example of risk that should be characterized.

            

Another example, one discussed with increasing frequency as the prevalence of prefilled syringes and prefilled syringe-based devices expands, is the concept of plunger movement. Although the degree to which plunger movement occurs is impacted by fill level, headspace volume, general package design, and pressure differentials experienced, how a low-pressure environment may impact product sterility and quality in these types of systems must also be characterized. As a plunger moves backward, the seal, and possibly the product, may move into an unsterile area of the container. In extreme cases, the plunger may completely exit the barrel.

          

          

            

### — 6.1.2

#### 

              

            

          

        

      

      
      

        

        

          

            

            

Numerous documents from internationally recognized organizations (e.g., ASTM, ISTA, ICH, ISO) are available for reference and provide reproducible ways of evaluating some of these stressors, including physical shock events, thermal cycling, and pressure cycling. However, the simulated distribution chain should reflect the anticipated stressors. Standards are meant to employ reproducible simulations but may not account for the variations and intricacies of specific distribution networks, such as iterative air shipments or a combination of different modes of transport. As with the application of many test methods and techniques, a proper assessment of the risks should inform decisions on evaluating such risks.

6.2

      

        

## 6.2 100% Online Testing | 100%

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

          

A holistic approach to controlling CCI should be taken to ensure package integrity at the time of manufacture as well as over the shelf life of the product. This holistic approach includes consideration of:

          

            
- CCIT method (in-line, near-line, off-line)

            
- Quality of primary package components

            
- Primary package design

            
- Manufacturing process qualification

            
- Product manufacturing process

            
- Change control process

            
- Shelf-life assessment

          

          

The CCIT method is a critical part of the control strategy, and an in-line test can make the overall approach more robust. However, the in-line test should not be the sole point of control.

          

In-line testing is defined as 100% testing of all filled and sealed primary packages for CCI during the production process, after the final sealing operation.

          

Currently, some regulatory requirements state that all container types fused and filled on the filling line (e.g., ampules, blow-fill-seal containers) must be 100% inspected. For other products, no current regulatory requirement exists for 100% in-line testing. The decision to conduct 100% in-line testing for other products should be based on risk assessment, as part of a holistic approach.

          

A package that is accepted at the in-line test station is not guaranteed to maintain its CCI during the shelf life and use of the product. Conversely, a package that is rejected by the in-line test station is guaranteed not to meet the product's CCI requirements. Therefore, the in-line test station acts as a filter for rejected packages, reducing the risk of CCI failures due to random process failure modes.

          

Consistent failure modes that are due to equipment malfunctioning or setup can be detected by other means, such as off-line quality control testing. A particular advantage of in-line CCIT compared to off-line quality control testing is the elimination (and cost savings) of handling suspect lots of products due to sample rejection at the off-line test station.

          

For high-speed lines, current technologies cannot test for the small-sized defects that off-line test methods can. However, since most process failure modes result in medium to large defects in the package, widening the reject limit for in-line testing is a reasonable and low-risk decision.

          

While it can add value in some situations, technology is not currently available to enable 100% in-line testing for all CCS and product types. Where feasible, regulations and quality risk management principles identified in ICH Quality Guideline Q9: Quality Risk Management should be used to determine if 100% in-line testing should be implemented.

          

Integrating 100% in-line testing into the manufacturing process requires a strong commitment. The in-line test station requires capital investment and the operational cost of the equipment, which adds to the cost of the product. Multiple test stations may be required on each filling line to overcome differences between line speeds and test station throughput.

          

No one test method fits all products. In-line test methods must be nondestructive for the specific product to be tested.

## 6.3 Building a Quality by Design (QbD) Approach into the CCIT Program | QbDCCIT

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

          

Pharmaceutical QbD is a systematic approach to development that begins with predefined objectives and emphasizes product and process understanding and control based on sound science and risk management. A QbD concept can be built into a CCIT program, including such elements as:

          

            
- Quality target product profile that identifies the critical quality attributes (CQAs) of the product-package system

            
- Package design and understanding, including identification of critical material attributes that can affect package integrity

            
- Process design and understanding, including identification of CPPs, linking the material attributes and process parameters to the CQAs

            
- Control strategy that includes specifications for the product-package configuration with respect to CCI as well as controls during each step of the manufacturing process

            
- Regular quality checks on process capability and continual improvement initiatives

          

          

One of the ways users can build QbD into a CCIT program is to employ a risk-based approach. A risk-based CCIT program is built on science-based decisions; it offers an ongoing database of product lifecycle CCIT results (also called a package integrity profile) and a risk management tool for package integrity assurance throughout the lifecycle of the product. The program should demonstrate CCI as a function of ongoing, operative variations that takes into consideration:

          

            
- Design and material of the package

            
- Package assembly

            
- Processing conditions

            
- Storage, distribution, and stability conditions

          

          

Key aspects for building a risk-based CCIT program using QbD principles:

          

            
- **CCI over the entire lifecycle of product:** The CCIT tool kit needs to demonstrate CCI over the entire lifecycle of the product. More than one test may be employed during a given product's lifecycle based on the critical parameters to be controlled at that stage.

            
- **Choice of deterministic versus probabilistic methods:** Deterministic methods are those where the leakage event is based on phenomena that follow a predictable chain of events. Probabilistic methods offer qualitative outcomes subject to the probability of a series of events.

            
- **Choice of off-line versus in-line, on-line or at-line test methods**

            
- **Sampling:** Scientifically valid sampling plans should be based on risk assessment in association with appropriate statistical criteria.

            
- **Defining the MALL:** CCI is established when a package meets its maximum allowable leak limit. Product quality requirements define the MALL.

            
- **Designing a CCIT tool kit:** The CCIT tool kit should encompass different test methods that can be employed to detect defect types critical to product quality and sterility.

            
- **Ensuring continuous improvement:** In the event of a change to package design, material, or manufacturing processing conditions, the risk-based strategy should trigger a review of the CCIT tool kit.

## 7.0 Conclusion |

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

          

Best practices in pharmaceutical packaging suggest that all activities must be evaluated for risk to the product quality attributes and, by extension, to the patient. ## Deep Dive: QbD Decision Flow for a CCIT Program | CCITQbD

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Decision Framework: From Product to Validated CCIT Program

          

Building a complete CCIT program under QbD principles requires a structured decision process that begins with product quality needs and ends with a validated, lifecycle-spanning control strategy. The following framework integrates the concepts from sections 6.2, 6.3, and 6.5.

          

            

#### Step 1 — Define the QTPP and CQAs

            

Identify the product's Quality Target Product Profile. For a sterile injectable, key CQAs include: sterility, particulate matter absence, potency, pH stability. The CCS must support all CQAs throughout shelf life. This defines the starting point for integrity requirements.

            

↓

            

#### Step 2 — Determine the MALL

            

Based on the CQAs, determine the Maximum Allowable Leakage Limit (MALL). Common approaches: (a) microbial ingress studies to establish the leak size at which microbial contamination risk becomes unacceptable; (b) moisture vapor or oxygen transmission studies for moisture- or oxygen-sensitive products; (c) headspace pressure requirements for lyophilized products. The MALL is a specific physical parameter (e.g., equivalent helium leak rate, nominal defect diameter) that defines the integrity threshold.

            

↓

            

#### Step 3 — Identify Process Failure Modes (Risk Assessment)

            

Apply HACCP or FMEA to map each step of the CCS assembly, filling, and sealing process. For each step, identify: potential failure modes (e.g., under-crimped vial, cracked barrel, misaligned stopper), potential causes (worn tooling, operator error, component variability), and existing controls. Rank by risk priority number (RPN) or risk level. High-risk failure modes drive the choice of testing approach (100% vs. statistical sampling).

            

↓

            

#### Step 4 — Select CCIT Methods (Tool Kit Design)

            

Select methods whose LOD ≤ MALL. Criteria include: deterministic vs. probabilistic, destructive vs. nondestructive, in-line vs. off-line applicability, testing speed and throughput, compatibility with product and package materials, and regulatory acceptance. More than one method may be needed across different lifecycle stages (development, manufacturing, stability, post-distribution).

            

↓

            

#### Step 5 — Develop and Validate the Selected Method(s)

            

Follow the PharmaCo-A model: feasibility → optimization → LOD/accuracy determination → full validation. Establish LOD using positive controls (laser-drilled or characterized defects) and confirm accuracy using negative controls. Document predefined acceptance criteria. For in-line methods, qualify the equipment and establish alert/action limits.

            

↓

            

#### Step 6 — Define the Control Strategy and Sampling Plan

            

Determine: testing frequency, sample size (based on statistical confidence and risk level), acceptance criteria, and response to failures. Where feasible, consider 100% in-line testing for high-risk or regulatory-mandated container types. For other containers, design statistically valid sampling plans referencing applicable standards.

            

↓

            

#### Step 7 — Build the Package Integrity Profile Database and Continuous Improvement Loop

            

Collect and trend CCIT results over time. Include data from manufacturing, stability studies, post-distribution testing, and complaint investigations. Use this database to trigger reviews when process changes occur, to detect trends before failures become OOS events, and to continuously improve the control strategy as knowledge accumulates over the product lifecycle.

## Deep Dive: Statistical Concepts in LOD — β-Content and Tolerance Intervals | LOD

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### The Statistical Basis for LOD in CCIT

          

The detection limit (LOD) in leak testing is not a single-point measurement. It is a statistical statement about the probability of detecting a defined type of defect with defined confidence. Two key statistical concepts underpin the LOD determination as used in TR86's PharmaCo-A example:

          

            

#### Concept 1: β-Content Tolerance Interval

            

A tolerance interval specifies a range within which at least a proportion β (the "content") of the population falls, with a specified confidence level (1-α).

            

```

In CCIT LOD context:
  β-Content = proportion of defective packages
              of a given size that will be detected
              (e.g., 95% = at least 95% detected)

  Confidence Level = statistical confidence that
                     the β-content requirement is met
                     across repeated studies

PharmaCo-A's definition:
  LOD = smallest defect size where ≥95%
        of that size subset is detected
        on ALL test days (across operators,
        days, instruments)

This provides a conservative, reproducible
definition of method capability.
```

          

          

            

#### Concept 2: False Positive Rate (Method Accuracy)

            

```

False Positive = a good package (no defect)
                 incorrectly identified as leaking

In PharmaCo-A's study:
  Negative controls (n=300 per method)
  were tested across all days

  Method A: 0.3%, 0%, 0.7% false positives
  Methods B, C, D: 0% false positives

Implication:
  False positive rate affects:
  - Yield loss (good product rejected)
  - Investigation burden for QA samples
  - Confidence in positive findings

Acceptance criterion:
  All negative controls pass = 0 false
  positives → confirmed method accuracy
```

          

          

            

#### Concept 3: Sample Size Considerations

            

```

For LOD studies, sample size per defect
size subset affects statistical power.

PharmaCo-A used n=40 per defect size subset.

To detect 95% defect detection with
95% confidence, the minimum sample
size can be estimated using:

  n ≥ log(1 - CL) / log(β)
  where CL = confidence level (0.95)
        β = proportion to be detected (0.95)

  n ≥ log(0.05) / log(0.95) ≈ 58.4 → 59

Note: PharmaCo-A used n=40, which is
a commonly applied practical number.
Consult a statistician for formal sample
size justification in validation studies.
```

          

          

The number of test days and operators also contributes to the statistical robustness. PharmaCo-A used 3 days and 2+ operators, providing a preliminary estimate of intermediate precision (between-day, between-operator variability).

## Distribution Testing Detail: ISTA Standards and Air Transport Pressure | ISTA

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Distribution Simulation Standards Overview

          

The following outlines the major standards and their application to pharmaceutical packaging integrity testing during transportation and distribution:

          

            

#### Air Transport Pressure — Technical Detail

            

Commercial cargo aircraft cargo holds are typically pressurized to approximately **6,000–8,000 feet equivalent altitude**, corresponding to an atmospheric pressure of approximately **0.75–0.80 atm** (75–80 kPa). Some older or smaller aircraft may have unpressurized or partially pressurized cargo holds, resulting in pressures as low as 0.50–0.60 atm at cruising altitude.

            

Key considerations:

            

              
- Pressure differential from sea-level manufacturing to cargo hold: approximately 20–25% reduction

              
- Duration of exposure: may extend 6–12 hours for intercontinental flights

              
- Combined effect with temperature changes in cargo holds (can drop below 0°C on some routes)

              
- Pressure cycles: repeated pressurization/depressurization on multi-leg journeys create fatigue stress on closures

            

          

          

            

#### Prefilled Syringe Plunger Retraction — Calculation Example

            

For a prefilled syringe with:

            

              
- Barrel inner diameter: 6.35 mm

              
- Headspace volume: 0.1 mL (at sea level, 1 atm)

              
- Fill volume: 1.0 mL

            

            

At 0.75 atm (aircraft cargo), the headspace volume expands per Boyle's Law:

            

V₂ = V₁ × (P₁/P₂) = 0.1 mL × (1.00/0.75) = 0.133 mL

            

Volume increase = 0.033 mL, corresponding to a plunger retraction of approximately 1.0 mm — potentially moving the plunger stopper into an unsterile zone of the barrel if the stopper travel clearance is insufficient.
