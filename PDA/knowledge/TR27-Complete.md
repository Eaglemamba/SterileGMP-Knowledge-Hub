## Preface

                    

This report reviews issues of pharmaceutical product package integrity and provides guidance for evaluating the barrier qualities of a pharmaceutical package. It supersedes the previously issued "PDA Technical Information Bulletin No. 4, Aspects of Container/Closure Integrity."

                    

In this updated guideline, the term "product package" is used, rather than the term "container/closure system," as it encompasses any nonporous barrier system that protects a pharmaceutical product from the environment. Similarly, the term "package seal" is used generically to mean all types of seals or closure systems.

                    

Although it is written to reflect the complexity of all pharmaceutical products and packages, the emphasis throughout the document is clearly on packaging intended for sterile products.

                    

                        "This document should be considered as a guide; it is not intended to establish any mandatory or implied standard."

## 2. Introduction

                    

Product quality can be defined in terms of the ability of a product to perform its desired functions despite the stresses to which it has been exposed before and during its intended use.

                    

### Functions of Pharmaceutical Container Systems

                    

Pharmaceutical container systems may have a variety of functions, including:

                    

                        
- Facilitation of product use

                        
- Prevention of improper use

                        
- Light protection

                        
- Containment of the product

                        
- Maintenance of a microbial barrier

                        
- Maintenance of a rarefied product environment (e.g., vacuum or inert gas headspace)

                    

                    

Assurance of package integrity is primarily concerned with:

                    

                        
- Product **containment**

                        
- Maintenance of an effective barrier against ingress of microorganisms

                        
- Maintenance of a barrier against potentially reactive gases such as **oxygen** or **water vapor**

                        
- In some cases, maintenance of a **vacuum**

                    

                    

                        "The information provided in this guideline is intended to assist users in developing integrity assessment strategies for use during the phases of product life."

## 3. Integrity Assurance Through Product Development And Product Life

                    

Product package integrity assessment begins during a product's development phase and continues throughout its life cycle, encompassing routine manufacturing and shelf-life stability assessments.

                    

### The Phases of a Product's Life Cycle

                    

                        
- **3.1** Package design, process development, and durability assessment

                        
- **3.2** In-process control

                        
- **3.3** Product testing

                    

                    

Further reconfirmation of product package integrity should occur when there are major changes in the package system that may affect its integrity, including:

                    

                        
- Package design changes

                        
- Package materials changes

                        
- Changes in manufacturing processing conditions, including sterilization

                    

                    

The following subsections contain information on the critical factors for assessing a product's package integrity at each phase of development and production.

## Package Design, Process Development, and Durability Assessment

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

                    

Package integrity quality assurance involves the selection of appropriate packaging materials, the design of the seal or closure system, and the verification that the incoming components meet specifications. Establishing leakage rate specifications may be desirable early during product development based on the particular product and package performance characteristics to ensure that the product will reflect these limits.

                    

Packages may be closed via either seals or closures. By definition, a seal is the result of the bonding of two similar or dissimilar surfaces. Examples of seals include:

                    

                        
1. Contiguous containers formed from a single material (e.g., glass or plastic ampoules);

                        
2. The direct fusion of two material surfaces, usually by heat (e.g., heat-sealed plastic bags or unit-dose blister packaging);

                        
3. The bonding of two material surfaces with an intermediate adhesive agent.

                    

                    

A closure, on the other hand, is the physical mating of two surfaces. In the strictest sense, all closures are tortuous path barriers. The degree to which a closure can limit leakage is a function of the closure design, the viscoelastic nature of the package components, and the extent of package component fit. For instance, an elastomeric rubber-stoppered sterile vial is capable of achieving a very low gaseous leakage rate due to the viscoelastic nature of the rubber and its ability to conform to the vial surface upon compression.

                    

There are critical factors that impact the quality of each package seal. Such factors typically include package component variables of composition, dimension, and critical defects; and sealing operation variables of time, temperature, pressure, and torque. The impact of processing on package integrity must also be characterized. Package component washing, drying, siliconization and sterilization, and product processing such as terminal sterilization may all impact the ability of the package seal to maintain its effectiveness.

                    

During the package design and process development phases, the acceptable ranges for each critical variable should be established based on package performance criteria that incorporate integrity testing. Wherever feasible, the variables of design and processing should be exaggerated and their impact on package integrity evaluated to help define the margin of safety. Stability studies extending through shelf-life must also include integrity tests of the packaged product.

                    

The assurance of product quality depends on the ability of the product to maintain integrity throughout the stresses anticipated during storage, distribution, and use. Relevant types of stress may include temperature, pressure, and relative humidity extremes, shock and vibrational stress, and exposure to microorganisms. The durability assessment may include exposure of the product to simulated conditions coupled with appropriate leakage detection methodology.

                    

                        "When greater time and effort are spent on package integrity verification during the design and process development phases, fewer problems will likely be encountered after commercialization."

## In-Process Control

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

                    

The processes that have been determined during product development to most critically impact package integrity should be those that are targeted for in-process control. Performance criteria for in-process controls are typically based on the performance of a specific unit operation rather than the performance of the final product. For example, in-process controls and tests may be used to statistically verify that the time, temperature, and pressure variables of a sealing operation are "in control" as opposed to final product testing used to demonstrate that microbial barrier or rarefied internal atmosphere criteria have been met.

                    

During validation studies it must be demonstrated that a process "in control" yields only products that meet the established leakage rate specifications.

                    

For a limited number of products, instrumentation exists that permits high-speed 100% on-line verification of package integrity at the established leakage rate specifications. But for many sterile products, in-process leak test instruments have limitations that preclude high-speed, on-line integrity verification at the required product leakage rate specifications. In-process leak test instruments are designed to check for intermittent defective packages leaking at a gross rate that circumvented the various in-process controls.

                    

There are a variety of logical, validatable in-process integrity tests that can help verify product/package integrity. Examples include:

                    

                        
- Vision systems to cull out defective components;

                        
- Gas flow rate meters installed on ampoule sealing lines;

                        
- Time/temperature/pressure controls on thermal sealing equipment;

                        
- Torque monitors on screw-capping equipment.

                    

                    

These on-line in-process controls, in combination with well-designed product/process validation studies, are an essential part of the total package integrity quality assurance. However, no amount of in-process or final product testing can replace the need for the control and monitoring of various critical package and sealing parameters.

## Product Testing

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

                    

A product that has been properly and carefully designed with critical production processes well characterized and controlled should have little need for final product release testing for package integrity. Certainly the quality of a pharmaceutical product cannot merely be based on the results of a product integrity test, especially when such tests are usually performed on a fraction of the production lot.

                    

Sterility tests have historically been considered an indicator of package integrity; however, due to the insensitive nature of sterility tests, the small number of samples tested, and the possibility of false positives, these results are no longer considered reliable as a final indicator of package integrity.

                    

Generally, integrity test methods selected for final product testing are designed to verify the absence of gross leaks well above permeation rate levels. For those package systems that can be tested 100% on-line with instruments shown to measure near or below the leakage rate specification, there is no need for additional product integrity testing. For other products, a statistically established percentage of the batch may be integrity tested for in-house process control purposes.

                    

Indirect tests of package integrity may also be employed on production lot samples. Examples of indirect measures include:

                    

                        
- Visual inspection;

                        
- Residual seal force of a serum vial with elastomeric closure;

                        
- Seal strength of an adhesive or thermal seal.

                    

                    

The acceptance criteria for such tests should be based on the critical variable acceptance ranges established during the development phases of package design and process development.

## Package Integrity and Leak Rate Specifications

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

                    

A leak is an unintended crack, hole, or porosity in a package capable of passing a liquid or gas through the package. Leakage is the movement of the liquid or gas through the leak. Leakage is defined in units of mass or volume flow of a gas or liquid over time under defined conditions of temperature and pressure.

                    

Leakage is often described in units of standard cubic centimeters of gas (air) that can move through an orifice per second (std cc/s or scc/s) under defined conditions of temperature and differential pressure. Similarly, leakage units of atm cc/s are also used to define leakage measured at local atmospheric pressure and temperature. While the two are not identical, units of scc/s and atm cc/s are often used interchangeably. Another common unit of measure is the SI unit of Pa m³/s (Pascal cubic meters per second). One Pa m³/s is equal to 9.87 scc/s or roughly 10 scc/s.

                    

### Leak Rate Scale Reference

                    
                        
                            
                                
                                
                            
| Phenomenon | Approximate Leak Rate |
| --- | --- |
                        
                        
                            
                                
                                
                            
| Audible sound from orifice | ~1 scc/s |
                            
                                
                                
                            
| Visible liquid flow detectable by human eye | ~0.1 scc/s |
                            
                                
                                
                            
| No visible water leakage (dry air at same pressure) | ~10−3 scc/s |
                            
                                
                                
                            
| Water cannot penetrate (liquid-borne microbial cutoff) | <10−3 scc/s |
                            
                                
                                
                            
| Helium spectroscopy detection limit | ~10−11 scc/s |
                        
                    

                    

Strictly speaking, all packages demonstrate leakage to some extent, although such leakage may not affect product quality. Therefore it is important to establish an allowable leakage rate specification for a given product/package system early during product development.

                    

For sterile liquid products: containers must prevent minute liquid product leakage and microbial ingress. The leakage rate specification limit should be the same as the cut-off for liquid passage, as microorganisms are believed incapable of penetrating a leak smaller in size than can be penetrated by liquids. Water is generally thought to be incapable of penetrating pores that allow less than approximately 10−3 scc/s of airflow at a differential pressure of approximately 1 atmosphere. This relationship between gaseous flow, liquid leakage, and liquid-borne microbial ingress was demonstrated by L. Kirsch et al. and Morton, 1989.

                    

Leakage rate may also be expressed in alternative terms: product weight loss or gain, rise of percent oxygen in inert gas-flushed headspace, or volume of dye that has migrated through a package seal. Such descriptions must clearly and meaningfully define mass or volume flow over time, and are as acceptable as gas leak rate units.

## Test Method Sensitivity

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

                    

In selecting a leak test method it is important to choose a test which is capable of detecting the leakage rates of concern. The leakage rate of concern will depend on the product/package system as well as the phase of the product life cycle in which the test will be utilized.

                    

Most physical leak test methods have an optimum sensitivity range, meaning that a method capable of detecting leaks between 10−6 scc/s and 10−7 scc/s may not be appropriate for gross leaks of 100 scc/s. Some physical test methods have a "gross" and a "fine" leak test mode in order to expand this sensitivity window.

                    

However, it is inappropriate and can be quite costly to select a test method that is too sensitive. For instance, a helium leak test capable of detecting leaks as low as 10−11 scc/s would be an inappropriate test for a high density polyethylene screw-capped bottle merely intended to retain tablets and capsules. But the converse is also true: an airborne microbial challenge test would be meaningless for a product with a gaseous leakage rate specification of 10−7 scc/s.

                    

Because leak test methods have a range of sensitivity, and because the leakage rate of concern may differ depending on the stage of the product's life cycle, it may be necessary to test a given product using more than one leak test method, each designed to detect leaks of varying magnitude. A package containing a lyophilized powder undergoing shipping simulation studies may need to be tested for larger leaks resulting from shock or vibration damage, while long-term stability samples of the same product may need to be checked for leaks indicative of gaseous leakage and permeation.

                    

A test method's sensitivity can be expressed in various units of measure. Optimum sensitivity of a method can only be realized through careful selection of the test parameter conditions. Examples:

                    

                        
- Oxygen analyzer: sensitivity expressed as smallest detectable change in headspace oxygen content;

                        
- Dye test: sensitivity expressed as smallest volume of dye detectable under stated conditions;

                        
- Helium mass spectrometry: sensitivity expressed as smallest measurable leak rate in scc of helium per second.

## Other Test Method Selection Considerations

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

                    

A wide variety of leak test methods are available for the evaluation of pharmaceutical packages. The usefulness of a physical method must be determined on a product-by-product basis by evaluating:

                    

                        
- The ability of a method to test the seal of interest;

                        
- The sensitivity of the instrument or method;

                        
- Any relevant sources of measurement variation.

                    

                    

In some cases, factors such as data storage, analysis and display functions, statistical manipulation of data, practicality, speed, safety, on-line integration feasibility, and expense may play important roles in the selection of the most appropriate leak test method.

                    

Sterile package integrity has traditionally been verified by airborne or liquid immersion microbial challenge tests. Microbial challenge tests have a long history of use and may be an indicator of microbial package integrity. Section 6 reviews both the microbial challenge approach and the alternate physical testing approach for verifying a package's microbial barrier properties.

                    

Section 8 provides summaries of several of the most commonly used physical methods for package integrity testing.

## Section Overview — Methods of Establishing Microbial Package Integrity

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

All sterile pharmaceutical packages must prevent the ingress of microorganisms. This section focuses on liquid immersion and airborne microbial challenge test methods and alternate physical test methods useful for establishing a package's microbial barrier properties.

                    

In subsection 6.1 the rationale for selecting a microbial challenge test versus a physical test method is discussed. Subsections 6.2 and 6.3 describe how one may directly verify a package's microbial integrity through either liquid immersion or airborne microbial challenge tests.

                    

In subsection 6.4 an outline is presented describing how physical leak tests may be validated as an alternative indicator of package microbial integrity. A direct correlation of physical integrity test results to microbial barrier properties can be done by exposing a given population of packages to both physical and microbial challenge tests (subsection 6.4.1). An indirect approach is to compare the physical method's sensitivity to leakage rates previously demonstrated as preventing microbial ingress (subsection 6.4.2).

6.1

            

                

## 6.1 Microbial Challenge vs. Physical Test Methods

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

For most sterile pharmaceutical packages, the leakage rate specification is that which will prevent the ingress of microorganisms. Such packages must therefore be validated as providing an effective barrier against either airborne and/or liquid-borne microbial ingress. The microbial barrier properties of a package may be demonstrated using either microbial challenge tests or physical tests that have been correlated to microbial ingress.

                    

### Reasons to Choose Microbial Challenge Tests

                    

                        
1. **No reasonable alternative:** Certain closures or packages that rely solely upon a tortuous path to prevent airborne microbial ingress may not be reliably tested by a physical method.

                        
2. **Package design incompatibility:** A package may not tolerate the relatively high differential pressures required for testing by vacuum decay or tracer gas techniques.

                        
3. **Limited resources or expertise:** The manufacturer does not have the resources or expertise required for developing and validating an alternate physical test method.

                        
4. **Limited distribution product:** An extensive physical test method validation program cannot be justified.

                    

                    

### Reasons to Choose Physical Tests

                    

Conversely, a physical test may be the preferred approach for microbial barrier package validation. If a sufficiently sensitive physical test method is available that is compatible with the product-package system, it is often more economical in the long run.

                    

Physical tests can be selected that yield qualitative or quantitative results or that can **pinpoint the location of leaks**. Microbial challenge tests are relatively insensitive, yield inconsistent results, are labor intensive, necessitate extensive storage space for sample incubation, and require long test times.

                    

Another disadvantage is that microbial challenge tests must often be done using **media-filled packages** rather than packages containing the intended product.

                    

**Physical tests are the more logical choice** when the leakage rate specification for a product/package system is significantly lower than the limit for microbial ingress. For example, a given sterile glass vial closed with an elastomeric stopper may require a leakage rate specification of 10‑7 std cc/s to maintain an inert gas headspace. The gaseous leakage rate that correlates to the ingress of liquid-borne microorganisms for this package system has been shown to be approximately 10‑5 std cc/s when tested by helium mass spectrometry (Ref. Kirsch, et al).

6.2

            

                

## 6.2 Liquid Immersion Microbial Challenge Tests

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

There is no standard liquid immersion microbial challenge test, but the following guidelines are suggested for the establishment of a meaningful and useful method.

                    

                        
- Select a **small bacterium** that is demonstrated to yield a high population of viable organisms at the end of the study.

                        
- Whenever possible, **positive controls** that demonstrate growth must be included. These may consist of defective packages or packages prepared outside the package assembly process control limits.

                        
- A **statistically representative number of samples** should be tested.

                        
- When packages vary in size, but not in design or package component materials of construction, **worst case container sizes** are to be evaluated, or, preferably, sizes should be bracketed.

                        
- If more than one seal is relied upon for package integrity, **each seal should be challenged**.

                        
- The **position of the container** during the integrity test should ensure that the seal is exposed to the challenge microorganisms.

                        
- The media, incubation, and challenge conditions of time and pressure must be logically chosen keeping in mind critical integrity variables such as package component materials, assembly, processing parameters, and challenge organism viability and morphology.

                        
- Whenever reasonable, **the product itself should be challenged**, as it is possible the product may influence seal integrity. However, this may not be feasible based on the product's inability to support microbial growth.

## 6.3 Airborne Microbial Challenge Tests

## 6.4 Physical Leak Test Detection Methods

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

Unlike microbial challenge tests, physical leak test methods do not directly measure microbial ingress, but rather, they measure some physical property of the leak that may be related to microbial failure. The relationship between the sensitivity of a physical leak test method to the probability of microbial failure can be either directly or indirectly determined.

                

                

                    

### Commentary

6.4.1

            

                

## 6.4.1 Direct Correlation to Microbial Ingress

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

Typically the development of a direct correlation between a physical integrity test and microbial ingress first requires the creation of representative acceptable and defective packages that can be tested by both leak test methods. Then it must be experimentally demonstrated that the measured leak rate by the physical method can be quantitatively correlated to the probability of microbial failure.

                    

To illustrate, the following results would need to be demonstrated by direct correlation of a **liquid chemical tracer test** to a **microbial immersion test**:

                    

                        
1. The presence of tracer in all test units that show microbial ingress,

                        
2. The presence of tracer in some test units that fail to show microbial ingress,

                        
3. The absence of tracer in some test units that fail to show microbial ingress.

                        
4. The absence of microbial ingress in all of the test units that do not show tracer.

                    

                    

A direct correlation of tracer to microbial ingress would need to take into consideration the potential interference of the tracer with microbial growth, and the potential interference of microbial growth on tracer detection.

                    

Liquid chemical tracer tests provide simple pass-fail results; either the tracer is present or it is not. If a physical test method is used that provides a range of leakage rate results, correlation to microbial ingress is based on the **rate of leakage determined by the physical method versus the probability of microbial failure**.

## 6.4.2 Indirect Correlation to Microbial Ingress

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

                    

A physical test method can be indirectly correlated to microbial ingress based on documented evidence of microbial ingress within the physical method's leakage rate detection range. Again, for a sterile glass vial with elastomeric stopper, it has been demonstrated that the helium leak rate (under specific testing conditions) that corresponds to microbial ingress as determined by rigorous immersion challenge testing is 10‑5 std cc/s (Ref. Kirsch, et al). This information can be used to verify the microbial ingress leak detection capability of another physical method by demonstrating that the second method can reliably detect leakage at or below the documented leakage rate of about 10‑5 std cc/s.

                    

It is important to keep in mind that the leak test gas and the test conditions of temperature and differential pressure used for the referee test (in this case the helium test) may differ significantly from those employed by the alternative physical method. Therefore it may be necessary to calculate an equivalent leakage rate against which the alternative physical leak test method must be compared.

                    

Indirect correlation of a physical test method to liquid-borne microbial challenge tests can also be made by demonstrating that the physical method is capable of detecting **microscopic liquid passageways near the limit of microbial ingress**. This relies on the assumption that without a passageway large enough to permit liquid flow, microbial ingress cannot occur. For example, a dye or liquid chemical tracer can be used as an indirect indication of microbial package integrity. In order to rely on such results, the sensitivity of the dye or liquid chemical tracer test must be optimized in order to approach the leakage limit for liquid ingress.

## Overview — Leak Test Methods

            

The final section is a series of summaries that describe several commonly used microbial and physical integrity test methods. These methods are listed **alphabetically**. In each summary, information is included on the **validatability** of the method, the limitations that influence the method's sensitivity, and whether it is useful for **quantitative** leakage measurement, **qualitative** leakage detection, or for leak location.

            

Critical test parameters are listed, followed by the time required to complete the leak test and the special equipment required. The package stress created by the leak test is stated, along with specific product/package types that are most commonly tested by the given method.

## Bubble Test |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.1 Bubble Test

                    

**Description:** The package is submerged in water or other suitable clear, colorless solvent. A vacuum is exerted on the test system, and the package is examined visually for evidence of gaseous leakage. Alternatively, surfactant may be added to the seal in question, a differential pressure is applied across the seal, and evidence of gaseous leakage is detected by visible inspection.

                    

### Validation

                    

This method determines the presence and location of leaks that allow sufficient gaseous flow to permit the formation of **visually detectable bubbles** in the submersion fluid or in the applied surfactant. This method can be validated for the detection or location of larger leaks or package imperfections. Validation will depend on the test method parameters and the reliability and sensitivity of visual detection.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity of the method is based on the ability of gas to move out of a leak to form visible bubbles in the submersion fluid or applied surfactant, and the ability to identify **positive leak controls**.

                    

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Differential pressure exposure

                        
- Differential pressure rate of exposure

                        
- Test time

                        
- Submersion fluid or surfactant surface tension

                        
- Cleanliness of package

                        
- Visual inspection parameters: light intensity, magnification, background, time for inspection, visual inspector training and experience

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Up to several minutes.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Vacuum or pressure source

                        
- Submersion fluid or surfactant

                        
- Submersion vacuum vessel for submersion testing

                        
- Pressure controls and monitors

                        
- Visual inspection: appropriate lighting, light meters, magnification, and color background

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

                        
- Pressure differential

                        
- Wetting of seal or entire package

                    

                    

### Product/Package Types

                    

Applicable to all pharmaceutical packages that do **not** rely on a tortuous path or barrier seal, and that can tolerate wetting and pressure differentials.

                    

*Literature References: ASNT 1982; ASTM D 3078; ASTM D 4991; ASTM E515; Elder 1995; Guazzo 1996; Morton 1987; Morton et al, May-June 1989*

                

                

                    

### 8.1 —

                    

                        

## Helium Mass Spectrometry |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.2 Helium Mass Spectrometry

                    

**Description:** Helium is used as a tracer gas for detection and measurement of leakage across a package seal. Helium is detected by mass spectrometry. There are **four testing modes**:

                    

                        
- **Tracer Probe:** The package is placed in-line with the mass spectrometry unit and helium is sprayed around the outside. The mass spectrometer vacuum pump applies vacuum to the package and helium leakage into the package is measured.

                        
- **Hood Method:** The package is enclosed in a "hood" or chamber into which helium is introduced. The mass spectrometer vacuum pump applies vacuum to the package and helium leakage into the package is measured.

                        
- **Detector Probe:** The package is flooded with helium and a detector probe connected to the mass spectrometer is used to sample the atmosphere around the package perimeter. The package is *not* under vacuum in this case.

                        
- **Inside-Out:** The package is flooded with helium (by penetrating the package, flooding prior to sealing, or "bombing" — exposing the sealed package to pressurized helium). The package is then placed in a test chamber directly connected to the mass spectrometer and subjected to vacuum.

                    

                    

### Validation

                    

Helium mass spectrometry methods can be validated for the detection, location, and measurement of gaseous leaks and helium permeation. Instrument sensitivity can be demonstrated using **calibrated helium leaks**. Positive control package leaks can be used in some cases. Validation studies should examine the ability of the leak test method to accurately measure gas leakage in the package system.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

Helium leak detection methods are potentially the **most sensitive** indicators of package leakage and permeation. Sensitivity is based on the ability to accurately detect and measure helium leakage/permeation rate. General mass spectrometry system parameters that impact sensitivity include:

                    

                        
- Volume of vacuum system

                        
- Pressure in the vacuum system

                        
- Pressure in the sensing element of the mass spectrometer

                        
- Response time of the mass spectrometer

                        
- Effective pump speed of vacuum pump system

                        
- Ratio of the total gas flow of the vacuum pump system to the total gas flow of the mass spectrophotometer leak detector

                    

                    

**Quantitative/Qualitative:**

                    

                        

                        

                    

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

**All methods:**

                    

                        
- Package testing mode; test time duration; temperature of package

                        
- Percentage of tracer gas surrounding the test boundary

                        
- Location, length and size of connection between tester and vacuum system

                        
- Helium background within the vacuum system

                        
- Cleanliness, surface area, surface finish and material composition of test boundary

                        
- Stability of electrical supply to the mass spectrometer

                    

                    

**Hood and Inside-Out methods additionally:**

                    

                        
- Concentration of the tracer gas in the package or hood

                        
- Bombing time; bombing pressure

                        
- Degassing of the package surface (post bombing dwell time or air wash)

                    

                    

**Tracer and Detector Probe methods additionally:**

                    

                        
- Specialized operator training, experience, technique

                        
- Surface scanning speed

                        
- Differential pressure across the package

                        
- Helium gas concentration within the test boundary

                        
- Distance the probe or sniffer is held from the surface

                        
- Length and diameter of sniffer probe lines

                        
- Build-up of helium in the background atmosphere

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Method dependent. Several hours may be required for bombing. Once helium challenge conditions are established, the actual test time is a few seconds.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Helium mass spectrometer with vacuum system

                        
- Package test fixture or probe

                        
- Helium source

                        
- Vacuum (Tracer Probe, Hood and Inside-Out methods)

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

Vacuum is applied to packages in Tracer Probe, Hood, and Inside-Out methods.

                    

### Product/Package Types

                    

                        
- Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that do **not** rely on a tortuous path or barrier material and that are required to maintain a very low leak rate specification.

                        
- **Inside-Out:** Hermetically sealed containers with a defined internal volume that can withstand external vacuum.

                        
- **Tracer Probe:** Items that can withstand a vacuum of 0.1 Pascal (10⁻⁵ torr).

                        
- **Detector Probe:** Items where the effluent side of the leak is accessible with the probe.

                        
- **Hood method:** Packages that can be evacuated.

## High Voltage Leak Detection (HVLD) |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.3 High Voltage Leak Detection (HVLD)

                    

**Description:** Containers are exposed to a high frequency, high-voltage current near the area of the package to be detected for leaks. The presence of a leak will result in a flow of current above base noise detected by the HVLD system as a reject. This test method is only feasible for containers constructed of **relatively nonconductive materials** and for product that is **significantly more conductive**. The product must be at or very near any potential leaks to allow conductance to occur.

                    

### Validation

                    

HVLD methods can be validated for the detection of leaks in product/package systems meeting the conductivity requirements stated above. Validation studies should examine the ability of HVLD to detect control leaks as a function of:

                    

                        
- Voltage level and detection sensitivity settings

                        
- HVLD probe positioning

                        
- Package wall thickness and geometry

                        
- Product formulation variations

                    

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

HVLD can detect leaks large enough to permit **liquid penetration or wetting**. HVLD sensitivity must be demonstrated during validation studies for each product/package system to ensure the detection of all control leaks with a minimum number of false positives.

                    

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- High voltage setting

                        
- Product conductivity

                        
- Sensitivity setting (Gain)

                        
- Test area cleanliness

                        
- HVLD probe positioning

                        
- Cleanliness of the package

                        
- Package geometry

                        
- Access of product to the leak

                        
- Package conductivity

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Test time is **almost instantaneous**.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- High voltage leak detection instrumentation

                        
- Package handling system to ensure package is positioned to wet the seal area and for feeding product into the HVLD equipment

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

Rapid, short exposure to a voltage.

                    

### Other Considerations

                    

It is recommended that the **impact of voltage on product stability** be determined.

                    

### Product/Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that do **not** rely on a tortuous path or barrier seal, and that meet the conductivity requirements described above.

                    

*Literature References: Anonymous 1996; Arndt et al 1996; Axelson et al 1990; Ball 1997; The Parenteral Society 1992; Villiger 1987*

## Lid Deflection |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.4 Lid Deflection

                    

**Description:** To perform lid deflection tests a pressure differential is applied to a package and the resultant movement of the package's flexible lidding material is monitored and used as an indication of the absence or presence of unacceptable levels of leakage. Lid deflection sensors can work by any of a variety of methods, including:

                    

                        
- Strain gauges for monitoring lidding movement

                        
- Contact sensors

                        
- Capacitance sensors

                        
- Inductance sensors

                        
- Electromagnetic wave motion sensors

                        
- Photoelectric sensors

                        
- Ultrasonic pulse echo sensors

                    

                    

### Validation

                    

Lid deflection methods can be validated for the detection of the absence of lidding material or for the detection of gaseous leaks large enough to result in **minimal or no flexible lidding material displacement** when a package is subjected to a reference differential pressure. Validation studies should examine the ability of the lid deflection instrument to detect control leaks as a function of instrument sensor capabilities, differential pressure testing conditions, package headspace, and package component geometry.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity of lid deflection methods is based on the ability to accurately detect flexible lidding material movement when the package is subjected to a standard differential pressure. Sensitivity will largely depend on the **method for monitoring lidding movement** and the capabilities of the particular sensor device selected. Generally, lid deflection tests are used as an indicator of **larger leaks**. This method is sometimes coupled with pressure/vacuum decay leak tests in order to provide both a "gross" and a "fine" leak test detection range.

                    

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Lidding movement sensor accuracy and sensitivity

                        
- Differential pressure applied to package

                        
- Package headspace

                        
- Package component geometry

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

A few seconds.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Lidding deflection instrumentation

                        
- Differential pressure source, monitors and controls

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

Differential pressure.

                    

### Product/Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that do **not** rely on a tortuous path or barrier seal and that can tolerate a differential pressure applied to the seal area. This test is particularly useful for checking critical sizes of specialty packages or devices. The test can often be used instead of vacuum/pressure decay for packages that tend to deform under pressure.

                    

*Literature References: Gnanasekharan et al 1992, 1994; Guazzo 1996; Hoffmann 1996; Rice 1986; Yam 1995*

## Liquid Tracer |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.5 Liquid Tracer

                    

**Description:** A liquid tracer solution, such as **dye, radionuclides, or metal ions**, is exposed to the package seal to be tested. This may require:

                    

                        
- Submersion of the package in the liquid tracer

                        
- Application of a small amount of liquid tracer to the seal

                        
- Filling the package itself with liquid tracer followed by submersion in water or other suitable solvent

                    

                    

Migration of the tracer across the seal is determined either visually, spectrophotometrically, or by other analytical means appropriate to the liquid tracer. Often a **pressure differential** is applied to accelerate tracer migration and improve test method sensitivity.

                    

### Validation

                    

Liquid tracer leak test methods can be validated for the detection or location of leaks large enough to permit **liquid penetration and detection**. For some methods, the extent of liquid leakage can be quantitatively measured. Validation will depend on the test method parameters and the reliability and sensitivity of the liquid tracer detection method.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity of the method is based on the extent of liquid tracer penetration and the ability to detect or measure such leakage.

                    

                    

**Detection limit:** There must be sufficient liquid tracer leakage to permit detection by the selected detection method.

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

**General:**

                    

                        
- Differential pressure exposure

                        
- Time of liquid tracer exposure

                        
- Tracer liquid surface tension, viscosity, hydrophilicity/lipophilicity, and temperature

                        
- Compatibility and stability of the liquid tracer with the product

                        
- Light stability of the tracer with product

                    

                    

**Visual inspection parameters:**

                    

                        
- Type of lighting and light intensity; magnification; background; angle of inspection; time for inspection; training and experience of inspectors

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Several minutes to as long as a few hours.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Liquid tracer solution; vacuum or pressure source; submersion fluid; vacuum vessel

                        
- Pressure controls and monitors

                        
- Visual inspection: lighting, light meters, magnification, color background

                        
- Fluorescent dye inspection: black light of appropriate wavelength and intensity

                        
- Analytical detection: UV/Vis spectrophotometry, atomic absorption, HPLC, or other appropriate instrumentation

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

                        
- Pressure differential

                        
- Wetting of seal or entire package

                    

                    

### Product/Package Types

                    

Applicable to all pharmaceutical packages that do **not** rely on a tortuous path or barrier seal and that can tolerate wetting or submersion.

## Microbial Challenge, Aerosolization |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.6 Microbial Challenge, Aerosolization

                    

**Description:** Microbial challenge testing by aerosolized microorganisms involves placing product-filled or media-filled package units into a specially designed **aerosolization chamber** and charging the atmosphere within the chamber with a nebulized cloud or aerosol of microorganisms for a specified period of time. During the challenge test, the packages may be subjected to **differential pressure challenges**. The atmosphere within the chamber must be kept within defined humidity and temperature ranges and well circulated.

                    

After the challenge, the units are removed from the chamber. If media-filled product is used (or product which supports microbial growth), the units are then **incubated and examined for microbial growth**. Otherwise, the package contents are removed and exposed to media which is subsequently incubated and examined for growth.

                    

### Validation

                    

Microbial aerosolization challenge methods determine the presence of microorganisms that have penetrated a package exposed to aerosolized microorganisms. Critical validation parameters include:

                    

                        
- Concentration and uniformity of aerosolized microorganisms

                        
- Chamber temperature, pressure, and humidity settings and controls

                        
- Ability of the media or product to support microbial growth

                        
- Positive controls should demonstrate growth

                    

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity of the method is based on the **incidence of ingress of aerosolized microorganisms** into positive control package defects and the ability to detect such ingress.

                    

                    

**Detection limit:** There must be sufficient bacterial growth for media to visibly or analytically demonstrate growth.

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Media or product microbial growth promotion

                        
- Stable microbial concentration in the aerosol (level and uniformity)

                        
- Size of challenge organism

                        
- Electrical charge on aerosolized particle

                        
- Extent of microbial drying during test

                        
- Time of exposure to the aerosol

                        
- Chamber air flow patterns and speed

                        
- Chamber temperature and humidity conditions

                        
- Differential pressure exposure (optional)

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Exposure time: typically a few hours. Incubation time: several days, depending on the microbial challenge and incubation conditions.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Standard microbiological laboratory equipment

                        
- Microbial growth support media or product

                        
- Airtight test chamber

                        
- Microbial spore nebulization unit with impinger, motor and fan

                        
- Temperature, pressure, and humidity controls and monitors

                        
- Chamber sterilization equipment

                        
- Air samplers

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

                        
- Pressure differential (optional)

                        
- Challenge Organism: Typically, *B. subtilis* spores

                    

                    

### Product/Package Types

                    

Applicable to sterile pharmaceutical packages that **cannot tolerate liquid immersion** microbial challenge tests and that rely on **tortuous path or barrier seals** to ensure package integrity.

                    

*Literature References: Akers 1985; ASTM F1585-95; ASTM F 1608-95; Blakistone 1995; Chen et al 1991; Chrai et al Sept. 1994; Gnasasekharan et al 1995; Hansen et al 1995; Henke et al 1992; Jones et al 1995; Korczynski 1987; Morton 1987; Placencia et al 1988, 1989; Reich 1985, 1986; Spitz 1994; Spitzley 1993; Tallentire et al 1985*

## Microbial Challenge, Liquid Immersion |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.7 Microbial Challenge, Liquid Immersion

                    

**Description:** Microbial challenge testing by liquid immersion involves immersing filled package units into a **liquid concentration of microorganisms** for a specified period of time, removing the units, rinsing them, incubating the units and examining them for microbial growth. These units typically contain **nutritive growth media**, but may alternatively contain product, if not inhibitory to the growth of the challenge organism. During immersion, the packages may be exposed to extremes of pressure to simulate anticipated product processing or distribution conditions.

                    

### Validation

                    

Microbial challenge by liquid immersion tests determine the ingress of microorganisms into a package that has been challenged with a liquid microbial challenge suspension. A method can be validated, provided:

                    

                        
- The concentration of microorganisms in the challenge suspension is sufficient

                        
- The media or product within the container adequately supports microbial growth

                        
- Positive controls, if appropriate, should demonstrate growth

                    

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity of the method is based on the **incidence of ingress of liquid-borne microorganisms** through positive control package defects and the ability to detect such ingress. Packaged media or product must support microbial growth.

                    

                    

**Detection limit:** There must be sufficient bacterial growth for media to be visibly turbid or analytically detectable.

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Challenge media formulation and surface tension

                        
- Size of challenge organism

                        
- High viable count in challenge media at end of test (≥10⁷/mL)

                        
- Time of exposure to microorganism

                        
- Differential pressure exposure

                        
- Ability of sterile packaged media to support growth of challenge organism

                        
- Sensitivity of analytical method if used to detect growth

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Exposure time: a few minutes to several hours. Usual incubation time: **7–14 days**.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Standard microbiological laboratory equipment

                        
- Microbial growth support media or product

                        
- Challenge microorganism

                        
- Immersion chamber capable of vacuum/pressure cycles and temperature control

                        
- Incubation chamber

                        
- Visual inspection area with appropriate lighting and color background

                        
- Analytical equipment for microbial growth determination (if applicable)

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

                        
- Pressure differential

                        
- Wetting of seals from liquid media

                    

                    

### Challenge Organisms

                    

Organisms reportedly used include: *Escherichia coli, Serratia marcescans, Clostridium sporogenes, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Staphylococcus epidermidis, Brevundimonas diminuta*

                    

### Product/Package Types

                    

Applicable to sterile pharmaceutical packages that can tolerate **liquid immersion**.

## Package Headspace Analysis |

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 8.8 Package Headspace Analysis

                    

**Description:** The headspace content of a package is analyzed and monitored for change over time. Package headspace analyses are useful for the measurement of leakage and permeation from packages that require a **rarefied gas headspace** to ensure long term product stability. The packages may be stored in a challenge environment of vacuum or concentrated rarefied gas to accelerate leakage/permeation.

                    

### Validation

                    

This method can be validated for the measurement of gaseous leaks and permeation large enough to result in an **unacceptable shift in headspace content over time**. Validation studies should examine the ability of the analytical method to accurately sample and measure rarefied gas content in the package system.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity of headspace analyses is based on the ability to accurately sample and measure gas headspace content in the package system. Sensitivity can be verified using **reference gas mixtures of known concentration**. Gas headspace content tests can be very sensitive indicators of package leakage and permeation, especially after exposure of product to longer times or to a challenge environment designed to accelerate leakage and permeation.

                    

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Rarefied gas analytical instrument sensitivity and accuracy

                        
- Headspace sampling technique

                        
- Package headspace volume

                        
- Efficiency of the establishment of initial gas headspace content

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Test times can be a few seconds to a few minutes.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Analytical gas content measurement instrumentation

                        
- Headspace sampling device

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

**Puncture** during headspace sampling.

                    

### Product/Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that require a **rarefied gas headspace** to ensure product stability.

                    

*Literature References: ASNT 1982; Wang et al 1997*

## Residual Gas Ionization |

                

High-voltage field ionizes headspace gas in vacuum-sealed packages — qualitative, non-destructive

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Description

                    

A high voltage, high frequency field is applied to packages sealed under vacuum. The field causes residual headspace gas to ionize. A conducting plate located near the package detects the level of ionization current. Ionization above an established limit triggers rejection of the package.

                    

### Validation

                    

The residual gas ionization method can be validated for the detection of insufficient vacuum in packages designed to maintain a minimal vacuum level. Validation studies should examine the ability of the ionization sensors to differentiate between packages containing various known levels of vacuum as a function of voltage, frequency, or package headspace.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity is based on the ability to accurately detect pressure exceeding a desired acceptance limit in evacuated packages. Instrument sensitivity can be determined using control packages containing known pressure levels. It is possible to detect smaller leaks and permeation rates as the time interval from when the package was originally evacuated to the test time is lengthened.

                    

### Quantitative / Qualitative

                    

Qualitative

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Voltage and frequency

                        
- Conducting plate and voltage field positioning

                        
- Package component ionization potential

                        
- Package headspace volume

                        
- Gaseous content of the residual headspace

                        
- Package component geometry

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Less than a second.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Gas ionization instruments

                        
- Package handling systems

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

High frequency high voltage spark. Impact of the voltage spark on product stability should be considered.

                    

### Product / Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages designed to maintain a partial vacuum in the headspace. The package tested must be relatively non-ionizing.

                    

### Literature References

                    

Wu, 1989 • Crist, 1994

## Tracer Gas Leak Detection |

                

Analytical detection of tracer gas permeating through package — quantitative, non-destructive

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Description

                    

A tracer gas is placed on one side of the package seal. An inert carrier gas is passed across the opposite seal side. Tracer gas is detected by appropriate analytical methods. Examples of analytical methods include coulometric detectors for oxygen detection and photoelectric sensors for carbon dioxide and water vapor detection.

                    

This test is useful for the measurement of leakage and permeation from packages that require a specific gas headspace to ensure long term product stability, or for products that are susceptible to degradation upon exposure to environmental gases or vapors. While oxygen, carbon dioxide, and water vapor are typically selected for testing pharmaceutical packages, a wide variety of other tracer gases can be used as well.

                    

### Validation

                    

This method can be validated for the detection, location, and measurement of gaseous leaks and permeation large enough to result in an unacceptable shift in headspace content over time or in product degradation. Validation studies should examine the ability of the analytical method to accurately measure gas leakage/permeation rate in the package system.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity of tracer gas detection methods is based on the ability to accurately measure tracer gas leakage/permeation rate in the package system. Instrument sensitivity can be established using reference gas mixtures of known tracer concentration. Test method sensitivity is largely dependent on the design of the test fixture.

                    

### Quantitative / Qualitative

                    

Quantitative

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Gas analytical instrument sensitivity and accuracy

                        
- Package test fixture design and dimensions

                        
- Temperature and humidity of the testing environment

                        
- Inert carrier gas temperature and humidity

                        
- Concentration of the tracer gas in the package

                        
- Concentration of tracer gas in the effluent atmosphere

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Several hours to a few days.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Analytical gas measurement instrumentation with package test fixture

                        
- Carrier gas source

                        
- Tracer gas

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

None.

                    

### Product / Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that are required to maintain a specified leakage/permeation rate of gas through the package to ensure product stability.

## Ultrasonic Detection of Gas Flow |

                

Detects turbulent gas escaping from pressurized packages — qualitative, non-destructive

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Description

                    

Containers under pressure are placed near an ultrasonic sensor capable of detecting acoustic energy in the ultrasonic range of 20–100 kHz. Ultrasonic sensors will detect movement of air or gasses out of package leaks large enough to create turbulent gas flow.

                    

Smaller leaks may be detected by the application of a special surfactant to the seal. Bursting of surfactant bubbles is detected ultrasonically. There must be enough package headspace to permit detectable gas flow out of the package.

                    

### Validation

                    

Ultrasonic gas flow detection methods can be validated for the detection and location of leaks in package systems sealed under pressure or in packages which can be "squeezed" or otherwise manipulated to create pressure inside the package. Validation studies should examine the ability of the ultrasonic sensor to detect control leaks as a function of instrument sensitivity settings, internal package pressure, package-sensor positioning, background noise, and line speeds.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

Sensitivity of the method is limited by the presence of turbulent flow gas leakage and the ability to detect such flow using positive control package leaks or standard gaseous reference leaks.

                    

### Quantitative / Qualitative

                    

Qualitative

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Sensitivity settings

                        
- Background noise

                        
- Internal package pressure

                        
- Line speeds

                        
- Package-sensor positioning

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Test time is almost instantaneous.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Ultrasonic detection instrumentation

                        
- Package handling system for squeezing package and/or positioning near the sensor

                        
- Surfactant for ultrasonic detection of bubble breakage

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

Possible squeezing of flexible packages. Possible surfactant wetting of seal surface.

                    

### Product / Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that do not rely on a tortuous path or barrier seal, that contain a headspace volume, that are sealed under pressure or that are flexible enough to permit pressurization by squeezing.

                    

### Literature References

                    

ASNT, 1982 • ASTM E 1002 • Bandes, 1988 • Elder, 1995 • Guazzo, 1997

## Ultrasonic Imaging |

                

High-frequency pulse imaging of seal integrity — qualitative, non-destructive

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Description

                    

Very short ultrasound pulses are generated at frequencies up to several hundred MHz. These ultrasound frequencies are focused on the container seal by acoustic lenses. Once inside the package the ultrasonic pulses may be absorbed, scattered or reflected depending on the change in the material. The reflected ultrasound echoes are used to create an image that can be analyzed for the presence of improper sealing, delamination, gaps, or product-contaminated seals.

                    

Alternate ultrasonic inspection methods work by contact transmission of ultrasonic energy through the package seal using an array of transmitters and receivers in direct contact with the seal region.

                    

### Validation

                    

Ultrasonic imaging methods can be validated for the detection and location of seal imperfections in flat thermal or adhesive bonded seals, for instance a pouch. Validation studies should examine the ability of the ultrasonic sensor to detect control leaks as a function of ultrasound pulse generation and transmission variables, instrument sensor sensitivity settings, transmission and detection sensor positioning, package materials of construction, and package geometry. Validation studies should reflect the range of acceptable package sealing parameters.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

Sensitivity is based on the ability to detect positive control package defects such as gaps, seal contaminants, package material defects, and other seal inconsistencies.

                    

### Quantitative / Qualitative

                    

Qualitative

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Sensor sensitivity settings

                        
- Quantity of transmitters and sensors per seal surface area

                        
- Package-sensor and transmission positionings

                        
- Ultrasound frequency

                        
- Ultrasound pulse amplitude

                        
- Package materials of construction

                        
- Package geometry

                        
- Dry package testing vs. water immersion testing

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Test time is less than one second.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Ultrasonic detection and imaging instrumentation

                        
- Package handling system for positioning packages in the sensor

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

None.

                    

### Product / Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that do not rely on a tortuous path or barrier seal, that contain a headspace volume, that are sealed under pressure or that are flexible enough to permit pressurization by squeezing.

                    

### Literature References

                    

Adams, 1995

## Vacuum/Pressure Decay (Differential) | /

                

Measures pressure change vs. reference — qualitative or quantitative, non-destructive

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Description

                    

A pressure differential is applied to a package and the change in pressure or vacuum is measured either inside the package itself using a test fixture, or outside in a sealed package test chamber. The change in test chamber pressure or vacuum can be determined by absolute pressure measurement, or it can be determined using a differential pressure manometer in which the test chamber pressure change is compared to that of a control package.

                    

### Validation

                    

Vacuum/pressure decay methods can be validated for the detection or measurement of gaseous leaks large enough to allow a measurable change in pressure in the package or the test chamber over the duration of the test. Validation studies should examine the ability of the instrument to detect control leaks as a function of test chamber geometry, instrument sensor capabilities, differential pressure testing conditions, test time, and environmental temperature conditions.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

Sensitivity is based on the ability to detect and/or measure a change in pressure or vacuum resulting from gaseous leaks. Generally, pressure decay is considered a more sensitive test than vacuum rise, and differential pressure is potentially more sensitive than absolute pressure sensors.

                    

### Quantitative / Qualitative

                    

Qualitative or quantitative, depending on the instruments and test conditions used.

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Pressure/vacuum sensor mechanism of operation and capabilities

                        
- Volume of space being tested (test chamber or package headspace)

                        
- Test chamber geometry

                        
- Time allowed for measured pressure change to occur

                        
- Stabilization time

                        
- Differential pressure applied to package

                        
- Water vapor pressure inside package

                        
- Atmospheric pressure and temperature

                        
- Package creep effects when exposed to differential pressure

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

Test time may range from less than a second to a few minutes.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Leak test instrumentation including vacuum or pressure sensors in absolute or differential pressure sensing mode

                        
- Differential pressure source, monitors and controls

                        
- Temperature and humidity controls and monitors

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

Differential pressure applied to package. Consider: headspace volume impact, liquids plugging leaks under vacuum, and package tolerance to differential pressure (vacuum rise tests limited to 1 atm; pressure decay may be much greater).

                    

### Product / Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that do not rely on a tortuous path or barrier seal and that can tolerate a differential pressure applied to the seal area.

## Volumetric Gas Flow |

                

Measures outward gas flow rate under differential pressure — quantitative, non-destructive

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Description

### Validation

                    

Volumetric gas flow methods can be validated for the measurement of gaseous leaks large enough to allow a measurable flow of gas out of the package over the duration of the test. ### Sensitivity

Instrument sensitivity can be verified using gaseous reference leaks or positive control package defects. Test method sensitivity is influenced primarily by flow meter accuracy, test time, and the differential pressure applied to the package. Generally, volumetric gas flow is not as sensitive as vacuum/pressure decay methods.

                    

### Quantitative / Qualitative

                    

Quantitative

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Flow meter accuracy

                        
- Time of test

                        
- Differential pressure applied to package

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Leak test instrumentation equipped with flow meters

                        
- Differential pressure source, monitors and controls

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

Differential pressure.

                    

### Product / Package Types

                    

Applicable to pharmaceutical packages that do not rely on a tortuous path or barrier seal and that can tolerate a differential pressure applied to the seal area. This test is particularly useful for checking critical orifice sizes of specialty packages or devices. ## Weight Loss / Gain | /

                

Gravimetric measurement of volatile loss or hygroscopic gain — quantitative, non-destructive

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Description

                    

The container is filled with either a volatile liquid or solid, or a hygroscopic material such as a desiccant. The container is stored in an environment intended to promote migration of the substances out of the package (volatile materials), or to promote migration of water vapor into the package (hygroscopic materials). Leakage plus permeation are measured in units of package weight loss or weight gain.

                    

For greater accuracy, it is recommended that control packages filled with inert materials be tested at the same time. Each time the test units are weighed, the control packages' weight change should be subtracted from the change in test unit weights.

                    

### Validation

                    

Weight loss or weight gain methods can be validated for the measurement of leaks of a wide variety of sizes, as well as for permeation measurement. Validation will depend on the test method parameters and the sensitivity of the balance used to monitor weight change. Acceptance limits are generally established as a function of the product-package system.

                    

### Sensitivity

                    

The sensitivity is dependent on the extent of weight loss or gain. The weight change of the test units must vary significantly from the control units, and should be above the limit of balance sensitivity.

                    

### Quantitative / Qualitative

                    

Quantitative

                    

### Critical Test Parameters

                    

                        
- Volatility (weight loss) or hygroscopicity (weight gain) of the packaged material

                        
- Time allowed for weight change

                        
- Challenge environment: temperature, and either pressure (weight loss) or relative humidity (weight loss or gain)

                        
- Handling of samples and weighing techniques

                    

                    

### Time to Complete

                    

A few minutes may be required for highly volatile materials (e.g., dry ice). Otherwise, several days or weeks may be required.

                    

### Equipment Required

                    

                        
- Balance

                        
- For weight gain: relative humidity storage chamber; temperature and humidity monitors

                        
- For weight loss: appropriate package storage environment; optional elevated temperature, differential pressure with monitors

                    

                    

### Stress on Package

                    

Environmental extremes of temperature, pressure, humidity.

                    

### Product / Package Types

                    

Applicable to all pharmaceutical packages and seal types intended to contain products susceptible to weight change over shelf-life.

Amini, 1981 • Cilento, 1977 • Guazzo, 1996 • Morton, 1987 • USP <661> • USP <671>
