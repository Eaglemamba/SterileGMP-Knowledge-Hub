This document provides key "Points to Consider" for navigating the complexities of raw materials in the manufacturing of advanced therapy medicinal products (ATMP), aiming to guide stakeholders in establishing robust strategies for raw material management, promoting best practices, and supporting the safe and effective development and manufacturing of ATMPs.

        

The unique nature of ATMPs presents distinct challenges compared to traditional pharmaceuticals, demanding a focused approach to material selection, qualification, and supplier management.

        

Specifically addressed in this document are the intricacies arising from the fact that ATMP raw materials are often:

        

            
- Animal- or human-derived

            
- Not monographed (no pharmacopoeial standard)

            
- Not manufactured for GMP applications (e.g., research-only grade / RUO)

            
- Single-sourced and/or required to be used for aseptic manufacturing

        

        

            "Very often, ATMP material suppliers and vendors are small, specialized companies with roots in academia that do not have a mature GMP quality system."
        

        

These characteristics contribute to a complex regulatory landscape, as regulations for ATMP materials are not globally harmonized. Variations in regulations exist among the:

        

            
- U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)

            
- European Medicines Agency (EMA)

            
- Japan Pharmaceutical and Medical Devices Agency (PMDA)

            
- Pharmaceutical Inspection Co-operation Scheme (PIC/S)

            
- Individual country-specific laws to which companies must adhere

### Topic 1. ATMP Material Categories

        

### Q 1.1. Which ATMP material categories exist and how are they defined?

        

There is no health authority guideline that clearly defines ATMP material categories nor an aligned definition across countries or regions. The material categories listed in **Table 1.1-1🔍** reflect existing regulatory guidance relevant to ATMPs and common industry vernacular.

        

The term critical raw material is used by European Union (EU) health authorities to describe materials critical for product quality in their *Guidelines on Good Manufacturing Practice Specific to Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products*. This guideline does not provide a clear definition of a "critical raw material," however, but lists a few examples such as sera, growth factors, enzymes (such as trypsin), cytokines, and gene-editing reagents. ATMP sponsors may define the criticality of raw materials based on their specific products and internal procedures.

        

The term material is a general term used to describe manufacturing ingredients, raw materials (e.g., starting and ancillary materials, excipients, primary packaging) consumed in the process that may or may not be present in the final drug product. The material categories given in Table 1.1-1🔍, as well as "critical raw materials," fall under "materials."

        

The term animal-derived raw material or "animal-derived material" is widely used, but its definition has not yet been globally aligned by health authorities. PDA Technical Report No. 83 and European Pharmacopoeia, Chapter 5.2.12 provide definitions, which the authors consolidated into the following:

        

### Primary Animal-Derived Materials

        

Any material derived directly from animal sources

        

            
- Bovine serum

            
- Porcine-derived trypsin

            
- Animal-tissue-derived hydrolysates

        

        

### Secondary Animal-Derived Materials

        

Any material manufactured using processes that include materials derived from animal sources

        

            
- Recombinant protein produced in an *E. coli* fermentation that uses a medium containing bovine tryptone

            
- Recombinant protein expressed in plants that are exposed to bovine manure fertilizer

            
- Recombinant CHO-cell culture products

        

        

### Tertiary Animal-Derived Materials

        

Any material free of animal-derived components that has been manufactured using secondary animal-derived materials

        

            
- Tubing sets or other polymeric components that were manufactured using slip agents derived from animal fat (stearates)

            
- Solvent that has been purified using bone-derived, activated charcoal

        

        

The definitions provided above for primary and secondary animal-derived materials are widely applicable to human-derived (raw) materials as well.

        

### Primary Human-Derived Materials

        

Any material derived directly from human sources

        

            
- Human donor cells

            
- Human serum

            
- Human Serum Albumin

        

        

### Secondary Human-Derived Materials

        

Any material manufactured using processes that include materials derived from human sources

        

            
- Recombinant protein produced in a human cell line

        

        

### Tertiary Human-Derived Materials

        

Any material free of human-derived components that has been manufactured using secondary human-derived materials

        

Any of the material categories presented in Table 1.1-1🔍 could also be subclassified as primary, secondary, or tertiary animal-derived or primary, secondary, or tertiary human-derived materials.

### Topic 3. Risk-Based Material Qualification

        

            ATMP material qualification follows a risk-based approach (see PDA Technical Report No. 81: Cell-Based Therapy Control Strategy for risk assessment examples). Besides the general requirements, which apply to all clinical stages and commercial manufacturing, several requirements differentiate between early- and late-stage clinical to commercial transition. These differences must be considered when developing a comprehensive strategy that is also phase appropriate. Further, the sponsor of a clinical trial (manufacturer) is responsible for managing critical material information, like the Certificate of Analysis (CoA), Certificate of Origin, and material numbers, from material suppliers because health authorities require this information in regulatory filings to ensure traceability and consistent quality of material. Appropriate supplier management, along with phase-appropriate material testing, where required, are the key elements of qualification.
        

        

### Q 3.1. What are key considerations for ATMP material qualification and specification setting?

        

            Critical material attributes should be established based on the intended material use and final product quality. Material specifications are intended to ensure that materials meet the compliance requirements of the appropriate market and to ensure materials consistently meet the expected quality. Further, technical information regarding the composition, formulation, and characterization of critical materials is crucial for the assessment of suitability and safety. The level of risk related to the presence of organic and other impurities (e.g., nitrosamine, melamine, heavy metals, human allergens) must also be identified and evaluated.
        

        

            For ATMPs that fall under PIC/S Guide to Good Manufacturing Practice for Medicinal Products regulation, Annex 2A, Part A, Section 5.23 provides additional guidance. For more information on the qualification and specification of plasmids and vectors, see *PDA Points to Consider No. 11: Development, Classification, Manufacture, Control, and Testing of Plasmids and Vectors Used in ATMP Production*.

### Q 3.3. What are specific considerations for ATMP single-use components and final containers?

        

For control of single-use components in all phases of clinical manufacturing, the manufacturing site and supplier for each component should be identified. Full traceability of the supply chain should be known to identify any potential entry routes for adulterants or contaminants. Well-characterized, sterile, pyrogen-free components with intended usage as injectable/implantable medical devices, as well as drug delivery devices, present the lowest level of risk for usage within manufacturing processes.

        

### Late Clinical Stage and Commercial — Container-Closure Specific Requirements

        

Prior to the start of late-phase (pivotal) manufacturing, process-consumable-related risk assessments should be used to identify and evaluate the risks related to each specific scenario of usage, for each component, with consideration for such process parameters as temperature and duration of contact, evaluated against material and component attributes such as materials of construction and composition of the process stream.

        

Components that have contact with the process stream should undergo quality control visual examination and/or testing, based on the specific risks identified through assessment. Primary container closure systems should be tested, at a minimum, for sterility, integrity, and bacterial endotoxin, in addition to visual examination, prior to usage.

        

Leachables and extractables and/or chemical compatibility might be challenged (e.g., for DMSO-containing formulations), and study data might be necessary to demonstrate safety. Testing should aim to establish the worst-case drug-product profile to facilitate development of specifications and acceptance criteria where needed.

        

            "Well-characterized, sterile, pyrogen-free components with intended usage as injectable/implantable medical devices, as well as drug delivery devices, present the lowest level of risk for usage within manufacturing processes."
              
*— PDA PtC-13 (2025), Q3.3*
        

        

### Container-Closure Systems for ATMPs

        

ATMP final containers span a range of formats, each with distinct considerations:

        

        

        

### Sterility Assurance for Single-Use Components

        

Many suppliers of ATMP materials cannot guarantee sterility assurance compliant with Eur. Ph. 5.1.1, EU Annex 1, EMA Guideline on the sterilization of the medicinal product, active substance, excipient and primary container, or similar regulations. Consequently, these raw materials should be considered as nonsterile even if they have been 0.2 µm-filtered.

        

            
- Nonsterile materials should be added to the aseptic process via validated 0.2 µm filtration steps followed by a filter-integrity test.

            
- Primary container closure systems should be tested, at a minimum, for **sterility, integrity, and bacterial endotoxin**, in addition to visual examination, prior to usage.

        

        

### Container Integrity Under Cryogenic Conditions

        

For ATMPs stored at cryogenic temperatures (e.g., −80°C freezer or liquid nitrogen vapor phase), container closure integrity (CCI) under thermal stress is a critical qualification parameter. Freeze-thaw cycling can cause:

        

            
- Seal failures at bag weld points or vial crimp closures

            
- Micro-crack propagation in glass vials under rapid temperature change

            
- Stopper retraction in prefilled syringes at ultra-low temperatures

        

        

Risk assessments should evaluate temperature range, thermal cycling frequency, and storage duration against component material specifications.

        

### PIC/S Annex 2A, Section 5.27 — Individualized Therapies

        

For individualized therapies (e.g., autologous cell therapies), since each batch of product is manufactured for a specific patient, an error-free system for patient-derived materials (e.g., tumor samples or leukocytes) must be in place to maintain the chain of identity and chain of custody.

        

PIC/S Annex 2A, Section 5.27 guidance states: the agreement between the ATMP manufacturer and the supplier should contain clear provisions about the transfer of information, including traceability data and transmission of health donor information that may become available after the supply that may have an impact on the quality or safety of the ATMPs manufactured.

## Topic 5: Topic 5: Lifecycle Management & References (p26-p35)

# Section 5 — Topic 5: Lifecycle Management for Commercial Manufacturing + References

    

    

PDA Points to Consider No. 13: Materials in ATMP Manufacturing (2025) | p26 – p35

    

        

Ideally, lifecycle management of raw materials starts early in the development of an ATMP product. It evolves as the product moves through the early clinical phases, through commercialization, and continues throughout the lifecycle of the product as more data and understanding are gained.

        

When an ATMP product is commercialized, the raw materials should be reviewed from the perspective of lifecycle management and potential new industry guidance. New information may be available that will change the risk-rating for raw materials and, therefore, testing and/or mitigation requirements. Topics to consider include:

        

            
- Raw material that was single-sourced may now have additional suppliers that can be considered as a secondary or tertiary source.

            
- Suppliers may now offer a raw material as GMP grade that was previously labeled for RUO (Research Use Only).

            
- Historical data will now be available on raw materials that may allow for updates to the incoming testing requirements and reassessment of testing specifications, including the widening of specifications.

            
- Historical data will now be available for reassessment of a supplier's qualification that may allow for reduced testing on the receipt of raw materials.

        

        

Product regulatory filings are important to consider with any raw material reassessment, as it may require updates in multiple markets. The timing of these regulatory submissions should also be considered from the perspective of how it will impact the manufacturing and planning schedule and, by extension, patient supply.
