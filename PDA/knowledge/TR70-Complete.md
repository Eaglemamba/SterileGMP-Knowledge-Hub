## 1.0 Introduction

            

This technical report provides comprehensive information and suggested best practices as well as appropriate references to support guidance on cleaning and disinfection programs for aseptic manufacturing facilities.

            

### 1.1 Purpose

            

The purpose of this document is to identify systematic elements that are essential to assuring an appropriate and compliant cleaning and disinfection program for aseptic and bioburden controlled manufacturing facilities and classified environments.

            

### 1.2 Scope

            

The document covers cleaning and disinfection within controlled and noncontrolled environments using chemical agents that reduce or destroy microorganisms. It provides guidance for **non-product-contact surface** cleaning and disinfection.

            

This document is *not* intended to fully address product-contact surface cleaning from a CIP (clean-in-place) or COP (clean-out-of-place) system, which is specifically addressed in:

            

                
- PDA TR No. 29 (Revised 2012): Points to Consider for Cleaning Validation

                
- PDA TR No. 49: Points to Consider for Biotechnology Cleaning Validation

            

            

                This document should be considered as technical guidance; it is not intended to establish any mandatory or implied standard.
            

            

The technical report team consisted of cleaning and disinfection experts from global pharmaceutical and biopharmaceutical companies, academia, and manufacturers of disinfection agents.

## 3.0 Sanitizer, Disinfectant, and Sporicide Claims and Classifications

            

Sanitizers, disinfectants, and sporicides are chemical agents that reduce, eliminate, or destroy microorganisms. Registration testing is performed to meet requirements of organizations such as the:

            

                
- U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)

                
- EU Biocidal Directive

                
- Australian Therapeutics Goods Administration (TGA)

                
- Health Canada

            

            

Registration testing addresses high-bioburden environments (hospitals, food processors, clinical laboratories). Label claims on commercial products are approved through this registration process.

            

                Testing for use of these chemical agents in GMP manufacturing also requires testing prior to use. However, this testing is significantly different — the bioburden load within a cleanroom is much lower.
            

            

GMP-context testing (internal qualification) is performed with:

            

                
- Lower bioburden levels (reflecting actual cleanroom conditions)

                
- Decreased wet contact time periods

                
- On varying substrates (floor, wall, stainless steel, etc.)

            

            

This internal qualification is an expectation of regulatory agencies worldwide (FDA, EMEA, and others) and may be reviewed during inspections.

### Key Takeaways

                    

                        
- **Supplier qualification is a prerequisite, not optional.** A CoA alone is insufficient — audit findings, qualification testing, and change control mechanisms must all be in place before a new agent is introduced to the disinfection program.

                        
- **Requalification triggers are change-driven.** Formulation, packaging, or manufacturing site changes all require evaluation for requalification.

                        
- **In-suspension studies are a screening tool only.** They provide rapid comparative data but cannot replace carrier surface studies as the definitive efficacy confirmation method.

                        
- **Sporicide validation requires spore-forming test organisms.** *Bacillus subtilis* spores (ATCC 6633) are the critical test organism — disinfectants do not kill spores, so this distinction is non-negotiable.

                        
- **Log reduction criterion for disinfectants: ≥ 3-log.** Testing must use agents close to or beyond in-use expiration date (worst-case scenario), in replicate, across multiple lots.

                        
- **Neutralizing agent validation is bidirectional:** confirm (1) it neutralizes the chemical agent, and (2) it does not inhibit organism growth.

                        
- **Three agents is the standard qualification target:** one sanitizer, one disinfectant, one sporicide for a typical pharmaceutical facility.

### Key Takeaways (English)

                

                    
- **In-Use Expiration Dating (Sec. 6.0):** Once a disinfectant container is opened, the manufacturer's expiration date is void. In-use periods must be defined, and containers labeled with preparation date and expiry — a common 483 observation.

                    
- **Environmental Control (Sec. 7.0):** Contamination prevention begins before cleaning starts — at the point of item introduction. Every item entering the APA requires a documented decontamination procedure. The highest achievable level of decontamination is required.

                    
- **Disinfectant solution sterility:** Sanitizers and disinfectants for Grade A/B must be sterile-filtered (0.2 µm) or otherwise sterilized before use. Non-aspirating containers prevent contamination ingress and are preferred.

                    
- **EM Data Analysis (Sec. 7.2–7.3):** EM data must be trended periodically. Environmental isolates should be identified and used to update efficacy test panels, closing the loop between monitoring and disinfection qualification.

                    
- **In-Situ Field Studies (Sec. 8.0):** Before/after EM around cleaning events provides the field-level evidence that laboratory efficacy data alone cannot supply. Both start-up scenarios and routine operations must be covered. This data is central to cleaning validation packages.

### 9.0 Cleaning and Disinfection — Why Cleaning Comes First

                    

Cleaning is a critical step in the cleaning and disinfection process because the buildup of antimicrobial chemical agent residues, product residues, particulates, and other contaminants can inhibit an antimicrobial chemical agent's efficacy.

                    

By lessening the level of particulates, microbes, and residues on the surface, cleaning prepares the surfaces for disinfection because:

                    

                        
- There are fewer organisms to destroy, as most have been removed from the area.

                        
- Obstructions blocking the chemical agent from contacting the organism are minimized.

                        
- Chemical interference that would reduce the stability and effectiveness of the active agents is removed.

                        
- Residual that can interfere with future disinfection, or dry and flake off into the environment, is lessened.

                    

                    

The key to disinfection in the clean room is keeping the surface **wetted for a sufficient period of time** for the chemical to accomplish its mode of action. Drying time is a variable that must be carefully evaluated — especially in areas where unidirectional airflow (UDAF) is present, as air movement tends to dry surfaces quickly.

                    

Cleaning operations should be performed routinely, with **frequency based on area classification, usage, risk, and visible cleanliness**.

## Grade A / B Areas — Highest Rigor

                

Filling zones · LAF workstations · Background environments · Sterile tools and wipers

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Grade A Areas — Most Critical Zones

                    

Grade A is the highest classification, representing the local zone for high-risk operations such as filling, stoppering, and open product transfers. Grade A is typically provided by a **Laminar Air Flow (LAF)** unit or RABS/isolator unidirectional airflow workstation.

                    

Cleaning requirements for Grade A:

                    

                        
- Only **sterile wipers** (pre-wetted or dry) are used — non-woven, low-lint materials validated for use in the zone.

                        
- Cleaning tools (mop handles, buckets, squeegees) must be **sterilized before entry** into the Grade A/B suite.

                        
- **Sporicide** (e.g., hydrogen peroxide vapor, peracetic acid, chlorine dioxide) is routinely employed as the primary antimicrobial chemical agent. Sporicides eliminate bacterial and fungal spores, which neither sanitizers nor standard disinfectants achieve.

                        
- **No floor mop** is used directly within the Grade A zone itself; floor areas under LAF are typically wiped or cleaned by automated VHP/HPV cycles.

                        
- All cleaning actions follow **unidirectional, overlapping stroke technique** — clean to less-clean direction (top to bottom, inside to outside) to avoid spreading contamination.

                        
- Personnel performing Grade A cleaning must wear appropriate gowning (sterile gown, double gloves, face shield) and be qualified to work in the aseptic core.

                    

                    

### Grade B Areas — Background to Grade A

                    

Grade B is the background environment surrounding the Grade A zone during aseptic operations. Requirements are nearly as stringent as Grade A:

                    

                        
- **Sporicide used periodically** (e.g., weekly or per campaign), with **disinfectant rotation** applied between sporicide applications.

                        
- Sterile or sanitized wipers required; low-lint materials.

                        
- Floors: **mop system permitted**, but mop heads must be dedicated to Grade B and either sterilized or replaced between uses.

                        
- Environmental monitoring (EM) frequency is highest — active air sampling every shift, settle plates during operations, surface contact plates after critical activities.

                        
- SOP must specify agent identity, concentration, contact time, wiper type, and cleaning sequence for each surface type.

## Grade C / D Areas — Secondary and General Support

                

Preparation areas · General support corridors · Sanitizer and disinfectant programs

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Grade C Areas — Secondary Support Zones

                    

Grade C areas are less critical support areas where less critical stages of the manufacturing process are carried out. Examples include solution preparation, component preparation, and filling of products for further sterilization.

                    

Cleaning program requirements for Grade C:

                    

                        
- **Disinfectant rotation** is the standard approach — at least two chemically distinct disinfectants (e.g., a quaternary ammonium compound and an alcohol, or an oxidizing agent) are alternated on a defined schedule to prevent the development of resistant organisms.

                        
- **Sporicide applied periodically** — typically monthly or quarterly, or following any adverse EM trend. The sporicide cycle is less frequent than in Grade B.

                        
- Mops are acceptable for floors; mop heads must be disposable or laundered/sterilized on a defined schedule.

                        
- Wipers do not require sterilization but should be low-lint and compatible with the chemical agents used.

                        
- Environmental monitoring at Grade C occurs **on a risk basis** — typically twice weekly for air and surfaces, but frequency is adjusted based on EM data trends.

                        
- Written procedures must document agent identity, concentration, preparation method, contact time, and application sequence.

                    

                    

### Grade D Areas — General Support Areas

                    

Grade D represents the general support environment for aseptic manufacturing — packaging, storage of containers before cleaning/sterilization, and personnel corridors.

                    

Cleaning requirements for Grade D:

                    

                        
- A **sanitizer** (e.g., 70% isopropyl alcohol, quaternary ammonium solution) is generally sufficient as the primary antimicrobial agent.

                        
- Routine disinfectant use is standard; periodic sporicide may still be applied (e.g., biannually) to control environmental bioburden.

                        
- Standard mop-and-bucket systems are acceptable; dedicated equipment per zone is still best practice.

                        
- Environmental monitoring is performed on a risk basis — less frequent than Grade C, typically weekly or monthly depending on operations.

                        
- Operational particulate counts in Grade D are not defined by EU Annex 1 and depend on the specific operation.

## Application Methods — How Agents Are Applied

                

Spray-and-wipe · Mop · Soak / Immersion · Proper wiping technique

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Spray-and-Wipe — Most Common Method

                    

The spray-and-wipe technique is the most widely used method for applying cleaning and disinfecting agents to walls, equipment surfaces, and work surfaces in classified areas.

                    

**Procedure:**

                    

                        
1. Apply the agent to the surface via spray bottle or pre-wetted wiper, ensuring full coverage of the target area.

                        
2. Allow **contact time** as specified in the SOP — the surface must remain visibly wet for the required duration.

                        
3. Wipe in a **unidirectional, overlapping stroke** pattern: strokes must overlap by approximately 30% to ensure no area is missed.

                        
4. Work in a systematic direction — top to bottom, inside to outside (highest risk zone cleaned first to avoid re-contamination from cleaner zone).

                        
5. Discard the wiper after each surface or after a defined number of strokes — never re-dip a used wiper into a clean agent container.

                    

                    

### Mop Systems — Floor Cleaning

                    

Mopping is the standard method for floor cleaning in Grade B, C, and D areas. Key requirements:

                    

                        
- **Dedicated mop heads** per area grade — never use a Grade C mop in Grade B.

                        
- Mop heads should be disposable (single-use) or laundered at high temperature and validated for efficacy.

                        
- Mop head must be pre-wetted or wetted at the point of use from a freshly prepared agent solution — never from stagnant water.

                        
- Use the same **unidirectional, overlapping stroke** technique on floors as for walls.

                        
- In Grade B, mop handle and bucket must be sterilized before entry.

                    

                    

### Soak / Immersion — Small Parts and Tools

                    

Soaking is used for small equipment components, accessories, and tools that can be fully immersed in a disinfectant solution. Requirements:

                    

                        
- Agent concentration and contact time must be pre-validated for the specific material/surface.

                        
- Items must be **pre-cleaned** before immersion — residual soil reduces disinfectant efficacy.

                        
- The immersion solution should be freshly prepared; reuse of solutions must be limited by defined time or contamination threshold.

                        
- After soaking, items are rinsed (if required) with sterile or WFI water before introduction into classified areas.

## Cleaning Materials — Mop Heads, Wipers, Buckets

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### Cleaning Materials — Selection and Control

                    

The choice of cleaning materials — mop heads, wipers, and buckets — directly affects the efficacy of a cleaning and disinfection program. Materials must be selected based on their physical properties, compatibility with chemical agents, and the cleanliness classification of the area in which they are used.

                    

### Wipers

                    

Wipers are used throughout all cleanroom grades. Their primary function is to deliver mechanical action and carry chemical agents to the surface. Key selection criteria include:

                    

                        
- **Grade A/B (ISO 5):** Sterile gamma-irradiated (γ-irradiated) wipers are required. Pre-saturated sterile wipers are preferred to ensure consistent chemical delivery and to eliminate the risk of contamination from the wiper itself.

                        
- **Grade C/D:** Sterile or non-sterile wipers may be used depending on risk assessment. Non-sterile wipers must still be low-particulate and low-linting.

                        
- **Single-use vs. reusable:** Single-use wipers eliminate cross-contamination risk between surfaces. Reusable wipers must be laundered and re-sterilized via a validated procedure before re-use.

                        
- **Material:** Polyester/cellulose blends are common. Fiber shedding and chemical compatibility with the intended cleaning agent must be evaluated.

                    

                    

                        

#### Critical Note — Wiper Residues

                        

Residues from chemical agents, loose fibers from wipers, and released wiper binders can become contamination sources on product-contact or near-product surfaces. Wiper type and chemistry must be part of the formal validation package.

                    

                    

### Mop Heads

                    

Mops are the primary tool for applying cleaning and disinfecting agents to walls and floors. Mop heads must be validated for compatibility with the disinfectants and sporicides used in the program. Key requirements include:

                    

                        
- Mop heads used in Grade A/B areas must be sterile — typically γ-irradiated single-use heads or heads that have been laundered and re-sterilized by a validated process.

                        
- Reusable mop heads accumulate bioburden if not properly laundered. Validated laundering processes define wash temperature, detergent, rinse cycles, and re-sterilization method.

                        
- The wringing mechanism affects liquid retention: poor wringing leads to inadequate surface wetting and insufficient contact time for the disinfecting agent.

                        
- Mop handles and wringer buckets must also be included in the C&D program; they are potential sources of secondary contamination.

                    

                    

### Buckets

                    

Buckets used to prepare and carry cleaning solutions should be:

                    

                        
- Dedicated to specific areas or grades to prevent cross-contamination.

                        
- Cleaned and disinfected after each use using the same validated procedures applied to other equipment in the area.

                        
- Inspected regularly for cracks, pitting, or residue buildup that can harbor microorganisms.

                        
- Properly labeled with the cleaning agent in use, concentration, and preparation date/time to ensure solution is not used beyond its validated in-use period.

## Equipment Surfaces & HVAC / Air Handling Surfaces

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 9.4.1 Non-Product-Contact Equipment Surfaces

                    

Non-product-contact equipment surfaces are those found in close proximity to product-contact surfaces. Because of their critical location, any cleaning chemical, sanitizer, disinfectant, sporicide, wiper, or other material used on these surfaces must not leave a residue that could be inadvertently transferred to a product-contact surface.

                    

The cleaning sequence for non-product-contact surfaces is:

                    

                        
1. **Pre-cleaning:** Remove past product spills, broken glass (from vials, syringes, ampoules), torn stoppers, damaged caps, and other foreign matter before attempting disinfection.

                        
2. **Disinfection:** Once pre-cleaning is complete, spray or wipe the surface with an efficacious disinfectant or sporicide and allow it to remain wetted for the specified contact time.

                        
3. **IPA wipe-down:** After the contact time, all surfaces should receive a wipe-down using 70% isopropyl alcohol (IPA) if a sporicide or disinfectant with residual properties was used.

                    

                    

### 9.4.2 Work Surfaces

                    

Work surfaces — tables, carts, setup areas — may be near product or near components that come into contact with product. Precleaning before disinfection provides a higher level of disinfection efficacy. After cleaning, surfaces should be disinfected using either a disinfectant or a sporicide. A 70% IPA spray-down followed by a dry wipe may be required if the agent used leaves a residue. Frequency is normally daily but should be based on usage.

                    

### 9.4.3 Nonstructural and Hard-to-Clean Surfaces

                    

Structural surfaces (walls, ceilings, floors) along with filling equipment should be routinely cleaned and disinfected. Frequency should be based on environmental monitoring (EM) results and/or a risk-based analysis. Nonstructural surfaces require equal consideration, as they can contaminate the environment.

                    

Surfaces are divided into two categories:

                    

                        
- **Routine nonstructural surfaces:** Tanks, carts, countertops, racks, packaged supplies, storage bins, stairs, work surfaces, non-product-contact surfaces, monitors, gauges.

                        
- **Hard-to-clean surfaces:** Tops of doors, tracks, conveyors, phones, equipment feet and legs, underside of tanks/carts/equipment, wheels, exterior of tubing/pipes, incubators, refrigerators, cold rooms, tools (sterilization may be required).

                    

                    

### HVAC and Air Handling Surfaces

                    

HVAC components in cleanrooms — HEPA filters, air supply plenums, return air grilles, and ductwork — require a dedicated cleaning approach:

                    

                        
- **HEPA filters:** Must not be wetted with cleaning or disinfecting agents under any circumstances. Accidental wetting creates a habitat for mold growth and can damage or compromise the filter matrix, leading to filter integrity failure.

                        
- **Air supply plenums:** Cleaned on a scheduled basis. Visual inspection for dust or particulate buildup informs frequency. Fogging or gassing agents may penetrate into plenum spaces but care must be taken to protect filter integrity.

                        
- **Return air grilles:** Wiped with a disinfectant-moistened wipe. Grilles collect particulates and must be cleaned with a frequency informed by EM trend data — adverse EM trends may trigger increased grille cleaning frequency.

                        
- **Cleaning schedule tied to EM data:** HVAC surface cleaning is not fixed at an arbitrary calendar interval. It is a dynamic schedule responsive to environmental monitoring trends, particularly elevated particle counts or microbial exceedances.

## Floors, Drains & Special Surfaces

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### Floor Cleaning Principles

                    

Floors in classified areas are among the highest-bioburden surfaces due to gravity settling of particles and personnel foot traffic. Effective floor cleaning requires:

                    

                        
- **Directional mopping:** Always mop from the cleanest area toward the dirtiest area, and from the highest-grade room toward lower-grade rooms. This prevents reintroduction of contamination to cleaner zones.

                        
- **Mechanical action:** Mopping provides the mechanical action required to dislodge adhered microorganisms and particulates. Spraying alone is insufficient for floors.

                        
- **Liquid removal:** After mopping with a cleaner, excess liquid and suspended contaminants must be removed using a sterile dry mop, a sterile dry wipe, or a HEPA-filtered wet vacuum. This prepares the surface for the disinfecting agent.

                        
- **Disinfection contact time:** After the floor surface dries, it should be sufficiently wetted with a sterile (Grade A/B) or sterile/non-sterile (Grade C/D) disinfecting agent. The disinfectant must remain in contact with the surface for the validated contact time.

                        
- **Zone boundaries:** Physical entry mats, tacky mats, and step-over barriers define zone boundaries and reduce particulate transfer between areas. These items must also be included in the C&D program.

                    

                    

### Drains

                    

Floor drains present unique microbiological challenges in classified areas:

                    

                        
- Drains are inherently high-bioburden areas. Standing water, biofilm, and organic matter accumulate within drain traps and surrounding surfaces.

                        
- Drains in Grade C and D areas should be cleaned and disinfected regularly. Sporicide application to drain surfaces and interior trap areas is strongly recommended.

                        
- Drains in Grade A or B areas are generally not present (open drains are incompatible with aseptic processing). Where present, they must be sealed or capped when aseptic operations are underway.

                        
- Drain cleaning must be documented with frequency and agent defined in the C&D SOP. Results should be correlated with environmental monitoring data from nearby monitoring points.

                    

                    

### Curtains and Pass-Through Areas

                    

Cleanroom curtains present particular challenges due to their soft surface finish. When viewed microscopically, curtain surfaces are rougher than wall surfaces and provide more protection for dirt and microorganisms against cleaning and disinfecting agents.

                    

                        
- Curtains should be cleaned first using a high-surfactant-based cleaner or 70% IPA applied via a mechanical wipe or mop action.

                        
- After cleaning, curtains should be disinfected with a low-residue disinfectant or sporicide (e.g., hydrogen peroxide or peracetic acid). Contact time must meet validated requirements (minimum 5 minutes).

                        
- If a medium- to high-residue agent (phenolic, quaternary ammonium, or bleach) is used, curtains must be subsequently wiped with 70% IPA and a dry wipe to remove residue. Residue transfer to critical areas or filling equipment must be prevented.

                        
- Curtains should be cleaned with greater frequency than wall surfaces due to higher personnel contact frequency.

## Lyophilizer Cleaning & Disinfection / Corrosion Prevention

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### Lyophilizer Cleaning and Disinfection

                    

Lyophilizers (freeze-dryers) represent one of the most critical pieces of equipment for C&D in aseptic fill-finish manufacturing. The interior chamber creates a complex environment for microbial contamination control due to its enclosed nature, condensation accumulation, and the use of cryogenic temperatures during operation.

                    

### Key C&D Requirements for Lyophilizers

                    

                        
- **Interior chamber cleaning after each cycle:** The lyophilizer chamber must be cleaned after every production cycle. Product residues, stopper fragments, broken vials, and condensed moisture must be removed before the next loading.

                        
- **Door gasket inspection:** The door gasket (seal) must be inspected at each cleaning cycle for integrity. Cracks, cuts, or biological residue on the gasket can compromise chamber sterility and provide a harbor for contamination.

                        
- **Shelf condensation management:** Shelf surfaces accumulate condensation during the freeze and primary drying phases. After cycle completion, shelves must be wiped with a sterile wipe and cleaned before sporicide application. Standing moisture on shelf surfaces can dilute disinfectants and reduce efficacy.

                        
- **Sporicide treatment:** Sporicide treatment of the lyophilizer interior is often required — particularly following product campaigns, environmental monitoring excursions, or scheduled periodic decontamination. Common sporicides include 0.52% sodium hypochlorite, peracetic acid solutions, or vaporized hydrogen peroxide (VHP). VHP offers the advantage of gaseous penetration to all internal surfaces without manual wiping.

                        
- **Condenser cleaning:** The condenser accumulates ice during operation. After defrost, the condenser must be cleaned and disinfected. Condensate water must be drained and the condenser surface treated to prevent biofilm formation.

                    

                    

### GMP Documentation Requirements

                    

Lyophilizer C&D is a critical GMP activity. At manufacturing sites such as aseptic CDMO operations, all C&D activities on the lyophilizer must be:

                    

                        
- Documented in the batch record (batch manufacturing record / BMR) for each production lot processed in that lyophilizer.

                        
- Linked to environmental monitoring results — EM samples from the lyophilizer chamber and loading area should be reviewed alongside the C&D record to confirm that cleaning was effective and the environment was in a state of control.

                        
- Executed according to approved SOPs with version control. Operators performing lyophilizer C&D must be trained and qualified on the procedure.

                        
- Subject to periodic validation review — lyophilizer C&D procedures require validation data demonstrating that sporicide contact time, agent concentration, and application method achieve log reduction targets for target organisms.

                    

                    

                        

#### Regulatory Emphasis

                        

FDA and EMA inspection findings frequently cite inadequate lyophilizer cleaning and disinfection as a contributor to environmental monitoring excursions and sterility assurance failures. The lyophilizer is a confined, low-temperature environment where moisture and residue can persist if C&D practices are not rigorously controlled.

### Corrosion Prevention — Disinfectant Compatibility with Stainless Steel

                    

Many disinfectants and sporicides used in aseptic manufacturing facilities are corrosive to stainless steel equipment surfaces when used incorrectly or in excess concentration. Understanding material compatibility is essential to protecting equipment longevity and preventing product contamination from corrosion byproducts.

                    

### High-Risk Disinfectants for Corrosion

                    

                        
- **Chlorine-based agents (sodium hypochlorite / bleach):** These are highly effective sporicides but are among the most corrosive to 316L stainless steel, aluminum, and other metals. Pitting corrosion (localized corrosion creating small holes in the surface) is the primary failure mode. Risk increases with concentration, temperature, and contact time. Chlorine solutions must not be left on stainless steel surfaces beyond the validated contact time.

                        
- **Peracetic acid (PAA):** Effective sporicide; less corrosive than chlorine at typical use concentrations but still requires rinsing. Higher concentrations used in recirculating systems can accelerate corrosion of welds and surface imperfections.

                        
- **Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂):** Generally lower corrosion risk than chlorine at standard concentrations. VHP applications at high concentrations may affect certain polymers and elastomers rather than stainless steel.

                    

                    

### Compatibility Data and Rinsing Protocols

                    

Formal material compatibility data must be obtained before introducing any new disinfectant or sporicide into the C&D program:

                    

                        
- Compatibility testing should cover the actual materials of construction in the facility: grade of stainless steel, polymers, gasket materials, painted surfaces, anodized aluminum, and floor coatings.

                        
- Validated rinsing protocols must define the rinse agent (sterile water for injection / WFI, or purified water), rinse volume, number of rinse cycles, and acceptable residual limits for the disinfecting agent on the surface post-rinse.

                        
- Contact time limits must be established and enforced. Operators must not allow sporicides or chlorine-based agents to dry on stainless steel surfaces before rinsing.

                        
- Corrosion inspection should be a routine part of equipment maintenance and cleaning verification — visible pitting, discoloration, or surface roughening should trigger investigation and potentially a change in C&D protocol.

## 16.0 Conclusion

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### 16.0 Conclusion — The Role of Cleaning and Disinfection

          

A robust contamination control system starts with controlling contamination from entering classified areas. Such a system stages items from the exterior environment to be cleaned, disinfected, and sterilized prior to entry. Without a system for controlling contamination from entering, loss of control of environmental conditions is very likely.

          

Cleaning complements the control system as the blockage of all contamination from entering is extremely difficult. Cleaning with a non-abrasive action and subsequent lifting of dirtied soils, liquids, particulates, and microbes prepares the surface to be characteristically lower in soil/residue and bioburden, making disinfection of what remains a simpler and more successful process.

          

The disinfection of areas utilizing a validated agent corrects the errors that have occurred during the control and cleaning process.

          

            "Cleaning and disinfection of classified areas is not a preventative measure but rather a **corrective action procedure** done to equalize the failures of the control system."
          

          

Many pertinent details defined in this technical report combine together to help provide the opportunity for success, which is measured as **consistent acceptable environmental conditions**. Consistent control is the ultimate goal.

          

This technical report is not intended to replace any existing requirements or standards; it is a **best practice reference document** regarding the fundamentals of cleaning and disinfection.

## Appendix I: History of Disinfection

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Historical Overview

          

A disinfectant is a substance that kills microorganisms — bacteria, viruses, and other pathogens — on inanimate objects. People were routinely killing microorganisms long before they knew of their existence. Ancient Egyptians, Persians, and Chinese used wine, pine pitch, copper, silver, and mercury as early disinfectants — all toxic at higher concentrations but useful at lower dosages for preventing rot and spoilage.

          

The following timeline traces key milestones in the science of disinfection.

        

        

          

### 

          

          

        

      

      
      

        

          

### Key Milestones — Disinfection Technology

          

            

              

~800 BC

              

Greek poet Homer records first intentional use of **sulfur dioxide (SO₂)** as a disinfectant / fumigant.

            

            

              

AD 500

              

Hindu physician Sushruta Samhita uses fumigation and disinfecting vapors. Venetian cargo ships fumigated to control disease.

            

            

              

Mid-1600s

              

Anton van Leeuwenhoek perfects the microscope — first person to view bacteria ("animalcules"). He discovered that pepper, vinegar, and other chemicals killed them, making him the first person to knowingly disinfect with a chemical substance.

            

            

              

~1600s

              

Sir Francis Bacon experiments with substances preventing putrefaction — astringents, acids, salt, sugar, lack of oxygen.

            

            

              

Mid-1700s

              

Sir John Pringle develops a coefficient table comparing antiseptic solutions, using salt as the standard. Possibly the first to call these chemicals **"antiseptics."**

            

            

              

~1770

              

Carl Wilhelm Scheele discovers **chlorine**. Calcium hypochlorite later used in hospitals, sewers, and stables. During WWI, 0.5% sodium hypochlorite & alkali used to disinfect wounds. Chloride salts still used widely in water treatment today.

            

            

              

1830

              

Tincture of iodine enters the US Pharmacopeia (USP); used to treat wounds during the Civil War.

            

            

              

1832

              

Carl Ludwig von Reichenbach discovers **creosote** (phenols from beech tar) — initially used as a deodorant. Alkylphenols from coal tar later emulsified with soap and marketed as **Lysol**.

            

            

              

~1860s

              

Louis Pasteur advocates heat to destroy invisible disease-causing microbes. Joseph Lister, inspired by Pasteur, uses **carbolic acid (phenol)** to prevent wound infection — a potent germicide that can kill spores, though toxic at full strength.

            

            

              

1879

              

Joseph Lawrence (inspired by Lister's talk in the US) develops **Listerine**. Robert Johnson (also inspired) selects phenols for surgical wound dressings — founding **Johnson & Johnson**.

            

            

              

Late 1800s

              

Robert Koch conclusively demonstrates that bacteria cause disease in live tissues — establishing the germ theory of disease and revolutionizing the scientific basis for disinfection.

## Appendix II: Registration of Sanitizers and Disinfectants

        

      

    

    

      

        

          

### Appendix II — Regulatory Registration Overview

          

Disinfectants and sanitizers used in pharmaceutical and food-drug manufacturing facilities must comply with applicable registration requirements. These requirements differ significantly between the US and EU regulatory frameworks.

          

#### United States — US EPA Registration

          

In the United States, antimicrobial pesticide products — including disinfectants and sanitizers used in drug manufacturing facilities — are regulated under the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA), administered by the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).

          

            
- All disinfectants used in pharmaceutical and food/drug manufacturing environments must hold a valid **US EPA Registration Number**.

            
- The EPA registration process requires efficacy data demonstrating the product kills claimed organisms under labeled conditions, including contact time and concentration.

            
- Manufacturers must provide the EPA-registered product label; claims must not exceed label specifications.

            
- State-level registration may also be required in certain US states beyond federal EPA registration.

          

          

#### European Union — Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR)

          

In the EU, disinfectants used in aseptic manufacturing facilities are regulated under Regulation (EU) No 528/2012 — the Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR), administered at the EU level by the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) with national competent authorities.

          

            
- All biocidal active substances must be approved at the EU level before products containing them can be authorized in Member States.

            
- Product Authorization (PA) is required in each Member State where the product will be placed on the market, unless Mutual Recognition procedures are used.

            
- Product type classification (e.g., PT2 — disinfectants for private and public health areas) determines the scope and use claims of the authorization.

            
- Transitional measures apply for existing products while active substance reviews are ongoing.

- EPA-registered products must be used according to label directions — concentration, contact time, and application method must match label claims.

### Overview: How EPA Safety Labels Are Determined

                    

Labeling requirements for antimicrobial products vary by country. For products registered in the United States, the EPA provides prescriptive precautionary label language. This language is determined by the results of **six acute toxicity studies** performed on the product formulation:

                    

                        
- **Acute oral toxicity** — systemic toxicity via ingestion

                        
- **Acute dermal toxicity** — systemic toxicity via skin absorption

                        
- **Acute inhalation toxicity** (4-hour exposure) — systemic toxicity via breathing

                        
- **Primary eye irritation** — irritation or corrosion of ocular tissue

                        
- **Primary skin irritation** — tissue destruction or erythema

                        
- **Dermal sensitization** — potential for allergic contact dermatitis

                    

                    

With the exception of dermal sensitization, each study is assigned to a Toxicity Category (I–IV) based on study results. Category I is most hazardous; Category IV is least hazardous.

## Appendix VI: AOAC Protocol Testing for Disinfectant Registration (Section 22.0)

                

Use-dilution method, suspension tests, carrier tests, EPA label claim requirements  |  pp. 58–60

            

        

        

            
            

                

                    

### Overview: AOAC and EPA Registration

                    

Firms registering antimicrobial products are required by the U.S. EPA to submit detailed information including:

                    

                        
- Formula and chemical/physical characteristics of the formulation

                        
- Effectiveness data supporting claims against specific microorganisms

                        
- Safety / toxicity data

                    

                    

The AOAC International (Association of Official Analytical Collaboration) develops standardized methods accepted by the EPA for this testing. While EPA registration requires these methods, they should **not** be considered a mandatory requirement for GMP operations. GMP facilities do not utilize AOAC protocols for their own antimicrobial effectiveness programs; however, verifying that a supplier holds U.S. EPA approval is part of the disinfectant selection process.

                    

See www.eoma.aoac.org for specific AOAC methods.

                    

### EPA Label Claim Categories

                    

The general efficacy label claims recognized by EPA are:

                    

                        
- Sporicide (Cold Sterilant) — eliminates all forms of microbial life including fungi, viruses, bacteria, and spores. Spores are the most resistant; sporicide = cold sterilant in EPA terminology.

                        
- Disinfectant — eliminates or irreversibly inactivates infectious bacteria (not necessarily spores). Divided into: Hospital Use and General Use categories.

                        
- Fungicide — reduces microorganisms on inanimate surfaces to safe levels per public health codes.

                        
- Tuberculocide — destroys or irreversibly inactivates *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* in the inanimate environment.

                        
- Virucide — destroys or irreversibly inactivates viruses in the inanimate environment.

## Appendix VII: EN Tests for Disinfection Efficacy (Section 23.0)

                

European standard disinfection tests — EN 1276, EN 1650, EN 13697, EN 13704  |  pp. 61–62

            

        

        

            
            

                

                    

### EU Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR) and EN Tests

                    

The EU Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR 98/8/EC) requires approval for all biocide products — including clean room disinfectants — sold in EU markets. The BPR replaces individual country registration systems for active ingredients.

                    

A key component of BPR dossiers is the efficacy assessment. The EU Commission places **EN (European Norm) test methods** at the top of its preferred efficacy data list. EN tests follow a three-phase evaluation framework:

                    

                        

#### Three-Phase EN Evaluation Framework

                        

**Phase 1:** Basic suspension test to determine bactericidal, fungicidal, or sporicidal activity. No specific contact time; no interfering substances required.

                        

**Phase 2:** Tests for defined applications, split into two steps:

                        

                            
- *Step 1:* Suspension test with specified contact time. Bovine serum albumin (BSA) added as an interfering substance — 0.3% for clean conditions, 3.0% for dirty conditions.

                            
- *Step 2:* Tests simulating practical conditions, e.g., surface/carrier tests.

                        

                        

**Phase 3:** Field trial tests (in-situ field studies).

                    

                    

### Individual EN Test Standards

                    

**EN 1276:2009** — Chemical disinfectants and antiseptics.  

                    Quantitative suspension test for bactericidal activity in food, industrial, domestic, and institutional areas (Phase 2, Step 1).

                    

**EN 1650:1998** — Chemical disinfectants and antiseptics.  

                    Quantitative suspension test for fungicidal activity in food, industrial, domestic, and institutional areas (Phase 2, Step 1).

                    

**EN 13704:2002** — Chemical disinfectants.  

                    Quantitative suspension test for sporicidal activity in food, industrial, domestic, and institutional areas (Phase 2, Step 1).

                    

**EN 13697:2001** — Chemical disinfectants and antiseptics.  

                    Quantitative nonporous surface test for bactericidal or fungicidal activity in food, industrial, domestic, and institutional areas (Phase 2, Step 2).

## Section Summary: Appendices V–VII

                

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### Summary: Appendices V–VII in Practice

                    

**Appendix V — EPA Safety Labeling**

                    

The EPA assigns antimicrobial products to Toxicity Categories I–IV based on six acute toxicity studies (oral, dermal, inhalation, eye irritation, skin irritation, dermal sensitization). Category I is most hazardous and requires the strongest precautionary language and PPE specifications on labels. GMP facilities must align their SOPs and PPE requirements to the EPA toxicity category of each disinfectant in use.

                    

**Appendix VI — AOAC Protocol Testing**

                    

AOAC methods (Use-Dilution, Sporicidal, Germicidal Spray, Fungicidal, Tuberculocidal) are the standardized U.S. EPA registration tools. They are not GMP validation requirements, but confirming supplier EPA registration (and the underlying AOAC data) is a necessary step in disinfectant selection. Pharma and biotech operations typically adapt AOAC test designs with lower inocula (104 CFU/mL) and quantitative log-reduction criteria against site-specific environmental isolates.

                    

**Appendix VII — EN Tests**

                    

EN tests operate in a three-phase framework (basic suspension → quantitative suspension with BSA → surface tests → field trials) and are the EU regulatory standard under BPR. EN 1276 (bacteria, 5-log/5 min), EN 1650 (fungi, 4-log/15 min), EN 13697 (surface, 4-log bacteria/3-log fungi), and EN 13704 (spores, 3-log/60 min) together cover the full spectrum of disinfection claims.

                    

**The CDMO Challenge**

                    

Facilities supplying both US and EU markets must verify that disinfectant suppliers provide *both* AOAC and EN test data. A mismatch between a claimed efficacy level and the supporting test standard is one of the most common supplier qualification gaps identified during audits.

## Appendix VIII: Large-Scale Gassing or Fogging of Clean Rooms (Section 24.0)

### Overview & Chemical Agents

                        

Vaporization or fogging of disinfectants for large-scale decontamination is being used or considered by many firms in the pharmaceutical industry. A significant amount of published data show the efficacy of this type of disinfectant system against vegetative bacteria, fungi, and bacterial spores.

                        

Chemical agents commonly used for large-scale gassing/fogging include:

                        

                            
- Paraformaldehyde

                            
- Peracetic acid / hydrogen peroxide

                            
- Phenols

                            
- Bleach (sodium hypochlorite)

                            
- Quaternary ammonia

                            
- **Vapor phased hydrogen peroxide (VPHP / VHP)**

                            
- Gaseous chlorine dioxide

                            
- Ozone

                        

                        

                            

#### Critical Principle: Gassing Does Not Replace Cleaning

                            

While gassing and fogging systems can provide excellent destruction of microorganisms present in contaminated areas, **such systems do not clean surfaces**. Therefore, cleaning is considered a mandatory and routine step in addition to gassing or fogging. Residues, particulates, foreign matter, and pyrogens are not removed by any gaseous decontamination process.

## Paraformaldehyde Gassing

                    

p. 63 | Legacy technology — declining use

                

            

            

                

                    

                        

### Mechanism & Historical Use

                        

Paraformaldehyde gas has been used as a large-scale clean room decontamination methodology for many years. It is a white, crystalline powder that, when heated, releases formaldehyde gas — the actual decontaminant. The gas is created inside the room by a pan and heating element, then spread by portable fans set up prior to gas generation.

                        

Paraformaldehyde gas microbial destruction claims through the U.S. EPA are limited to what would be termed a high-level disinfectant, sanitizer, or fungicide. However, sporicidal reduction has been obtained in clean rooms by many firms worldwide.

                        

                            "Paraformaldehyde is an older methodology that has been replaced by most GMP firms with current, more modern chemistries and systems. It leaves concerning residuals on all surfaces (as defined by FDA) and requires the utmost safety concerns for human health. For these reasons, this methodology is declining in use."

## Wet Droplet Fogging

                    

p. 63–64 | Aerosol-based surface coating technology

                

            

            

                

                    

                        

### Technology Description

                        

Wet droplet fogging involves the generation of small liquid droplets (typically **10–25 microns** in diameter) from a chemical agent placed into an airstream by a generator linked to a fogging device. Various fogging devices are placed strategically throughout the room; portable fans circulate the droplets.

                        

Chemical agents commonly used include peracetic acid, hydrogen peroxide, sodium hypochlorite, phenols, and quaternary ammoniums — chosen based on the type of antimicrobial action required.

                        

### Key Operating Parameters

                        

                            
- **Goal:** Lightly coat all surfaces with a thin but constant layer of chemical agent for an extended period

                            
- **Fogging time:** Typically 15 minutes to 1 hour

                            
- **Release time:** 15 minutes to 1 hour, or until all surfaces are dry

                            
- **Efficacy range:** Sanitization to sporicidal, depending on agent selected

                        

                        

This is considered a **wet or aerosol process** rather than a true gaseous process. Wetting of surfaces reduces decontamination times, dry times, and release times. With overuse and without manual cleaning procedures, residues can build up over time and corrosion can occur.

## Vaporized Hydrogen Peroxide (VPHP / VHP)

                    

p. 64 | Gold standard for pharmaceutical gaseous decontamination

                

            

            

                

                    

                        

### Technology & Mechanism

                        

The vapor of hydrogen peroxide is **noncarcinogenic** and breaks down to water and oxygen, eliminating the corrosive residuals inherent with traditional methods such as paraformaldehyde.

                        

VPHP systems generate a low level of vaporized 35% hydrogen peroxide (**250–1,200 ppm**) into manufacturing areas through portable or fixed distribution systems. In the vapor phase, disinfection may require longer times compared to traditional methods, depending on:

                        

                            
- Number of VPHP generators

                            
- Size of the area to be treated

                            
- Required contact and clearance times

                        

                        

The distribution of vapor within the room can be verified with **hydrogen peroxide sensors or chemical indicators**.

                        

### Operational Considerations

                        

                            
- The VPHP process is classified as a **disinfection step, not sterilization**

                            
- Leaks to the external environment and clearance time must be tested and assessed

                            
- Air-handling systems may need to be shut off; doorways and return vents sealed

                            
- **Release times: 2–4 hours**, depending on area size and gassing duration

                        

                        

### Isolator vs. Large-Scale Application

                        

The effectiveness of VPHP has been well documented in isolator operations for many years. However, isolator operations are smaller, sealed, and have evacuation systems that can remove and scrub the volatile gases. The use of VPHP in a large-scale, open manufacturing environment has also been very successful and — unlike UV — is effective in the presence of shadowing.

                        

Conclusive validation studies for large-scale VPHP are specific to the operation and setup. **Each area should be assessed for effectiveness in its own validation study.**

                        

### VPHP in the C&D Program Sequence

                        

                            
- **Before cleaning:** VPHP = sanitization step → must be followed by mechanical wipe/mop

                            
- **After cleaning:** VPHP = final disinfection step → the preferred sequence in most GMP programs

## Gaseous Chlorine Dioxide (ClO₂) & Ozone Gas

                    

p. 64–65 | Emerging alternatives for large-scale decontamination

                

            

            

                

                    

                        

### Gaseous Chlorine Dioxide (ClO₂)

                        

Gaseous chlorine dioxide is another available alternative for gassing. Of the methods discussed, it is most similar to paraformaldehyde or VPHP rather than wet droplet fogging, as it is a true gas product. Application to the area to be decontaminated is accomplished in much the same way as the VPHP gas process. Basic differences between the products relate to corrosion, residual, safety, and setup.

                        

To disinfect a room, gaseous ClO₂ is precipitated into the area via a generator and dispersion heads. These systems have been employed in many industry settings and are now being considered as a possible alternative for GMP operations.

                        

### Ozone Gas

                        

Ozone is made by adding high voltage to oxygen. The system uses a high concentration of ozone gas, integrating a gas generator to emit ozone to the area to be decontaminated. Design specifications for typical systems include:

                        

                            
- **Ozone concentration:** 200 ppm or more

                            
- **Relative humidity:** 80% or more

                            
- **Treatment time:** Determined by area size, inherent bioburden, and obstructions

                        

                        

These systems have been employed in many industry settings and are now being considered as a possible alternative for GMP operations.

## Safety Requirements & Validation Obligations

                    

p. 65 | Non-negotiable principles for any gassing program

                

            

            

                

                    

                        

### Safety Imperative

                        

Whenever chemical agents are used for large-scale gassing or fogging of clean rooms, **safety concerns must be addressed**. All of the agents discussed can result in injury or death of personnel if proper precautions are not taken to ensure the containment of the chemical agent to the intended areas. For many of the agents discussed, residues that are left behind on product-contact surfaces are also a significant concern and must be evaluated.

                        

### Validation Requirement

                        

Although these methods of decontamination are effective, they should **not be used to replace a routine program for cleaning and disinfecting the clean room areas**. If they are used as the standard practice, they should be **validated** to demonstrate their ability to achieve an appropriate level of bioburden reduction. This should be performed taking into consideration the material of construction present in the clean room areas.

                        

### Source Identification

                        

It is also important to consider the source of the organisms, for gassing will only remove what is present and may leave behind moisture, allowing for further proliferation if the causative agents (e.g., leaks, contamination sources) have not been removed from the area.
