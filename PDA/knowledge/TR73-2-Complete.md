The European Union regulation (EU) 2017/745 on medical devices, referred to as MDR, introduces new requirements for manufacturers of medical devices. These new requirements, according to MDR Articles 1(8) and 1(9), subparagraphs 2, also apply to pharmaceutical companies developing and manufacturing combinations of medicinal products and medical devices, including prefilled syringes (PFS), both integral and single integral products. The device part of these PFS must comply with the rules outlined in MDR Annex I: General Safety and Performance Requirements (GSPRs). Evidence of compliance, as described in Article 117, must be provided by requesting an opinion from a Notified Body (NB) appropriately accredited for the issuance of such an opinion (1) (See Section 3.0).

        

PDA Technical Report No. 73-2: Application of Medical Device Regulation Annex I Requirements for Staked Needle Syringes represents the current state of knowledge as to its application. As this is a new process based on a new regulatory requirement, expectations of the NBs and competent authorities may diverge as the process evolves. Ultimately, the responsibility for the entire content of the submission file that is reviewed by the NB lies with the applicant. Accordingly, the intended users of TR-73-2 are applicants, i.e., pharmaceutical companies, their suppliers, and other interested parties.

Moreover, the device part of this single integral (DDC) product (e.g., final prefilled staked needle syringe) must comply with the relevant GSPRs established in MDR Annex I.

        

In order to evaluate whether GSPR compliance can be self-assessed or if it requires a Notified Body Opinion (NBOp), the device part of the PFS must be classified as a "virtual device." MDR Annex VIII lays out the classification rules that determine the level of information and the involvement of a NB that would be required. Annex VIII, Rule 6 refers to surgically invasive devices intended for transient use. Because the device part of a prefilled syringe falls under this description, it is classified as Class IIa or higher, thus requiring a NB to be involved, as stated in MDR Article 117. That Article amends Directive 2001/83/EC Annex I, Section 3.2, point 12 to state that, if the application dossier to the competent authorities does not already include the results of the conformity assessment of the device, a NBOp must be provided.

        

The generation of the required data is a joint effort between the manufacturers of the components, device part, and device and the pharmaceutical company. As the pharmaceutical company (the applicant) is filing both for the NBOp and the marketing authorization, it is ultimately responsible for ensuring that all content and data are "attributable, legible, contemporaneous, original and accurate (ALCOA)" (15). The European Medicines Agency (EMA) provides a comprehensive website, updated regularly, with questions and answers on good manufacturing practices (GMP) and data integrity (8).

        

In summary, applicants who want to place staked needle PFS on the EU market must involve a NB in the assessment of compliance with MDR Annex I GSPRs. The NBOp obtained must be included in the application dossier to the national competent authorities or the EMA.

## **5.2 Chapter I — Risk Management 5.2 I — **

Chapter I of the MDR GSPR list relates primarily to risk management (RM). The pharmaceutical company is required to have an internal RM process that assesses risks associated with their device part in the context of their DDC and its intended use. This may be part of a device RM plan or combined product (i.e., including drug) RM plan. In that case, either the RM standard operating procedure or a description of the process can be shared, for example, as part of an RM file. ICH Quality Guideline Q9: Quality Risk Management provides guidance, as does ISO 14971:2019 Medical Devices — Application of Risk Management to Medical Devices which states:

            "The risk management file need not physically contain all the records and other documents. However, it needs to contain at least references or pointers to all required documentation, so that the manufacturer can assemble the information referenced in the risk management file in a timely manner. [...] The risk management file can be in any form or type of medium. [...] See ISO/TR 24971 for guidance on establishing a risk management file for components and devices that were designed without using ISO 14971" (11).
        

Suppliers of components or device parts can support the pharmaceutical company by providing appropriate documentation, which demonstrates a RM process that communicates risks associated with the respective component(s)/part(s). In the case of an existing RM plan, the supplier may also share this with the pharmaceutical company.

As always, the responsibility of deciding if and how the supportive documents will be used remains with the pharmaceutical company.
