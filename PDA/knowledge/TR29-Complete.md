## 1.0 Introduction |

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

                    

This report should be considered to be a resource to help guide the development or evaluation of a cleaning validation program. It is not intended to establish mandatory standards for cleaning validation. It is intended to be a single-source overview for pharmaceutical manufacturers that complements existing regulatory guidance and other documents referenced in this document.

                    

The reader should also be aware that a specific topic may be discussed in several sections of this Technical Report. Therefore, a more complete perspective may be obtained by considering all relevant sections about a certain topic. Furthermore, while many approaches are presented here, specific approaches utilized for a given cleaning process should be selected based on a good understanding of that process, as well as the appropriateness of the selected practice for that specific situation.

                    

It is not enough to merely say that the practice is mentioned as an acceptable one in PDA Technical Report No. 29; each firm should be prepared to defend why the selected approach is a valid one for its operations.

                    

                        The same goal of a compliant, scientifically sound and practical cleaning validation program should guide all decisions. Where options are given, the rationales for such options are also generally given. Examples are not meant to be prescriptive or limiting; they merely illustrate a certain practice. Actual acceptable practices should not be considered limited by the discussion in this Technical Report.
                    

                    

Sound science based on an understanding of the cleaning and manufacturing processes may lead to other equally acceptable practices. The Task Force that developed this document hopes that the report will be used in this spirit and will not be solely used as a checklist.

                    

Based on an understanding of the unique nature of any individual situation, different approaches or additional issues should also be considered. Actual acceptable practices should not be considered limited by the discussion in this Technical Report.

## 2.0 Glossary of Terms |

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### Glossary Definitions |

                    

#### Core Cleaning Concepts |

                    

                        **Cleaning Validation**
                        

Documented evidence with a high degree of assurance that a cleaning process will result in products meeting their predetermined quality attributes throughout its life cycle.

                    

                    

                        **Cleaning Verification**
                        

A one-time sampling and testing to ensure that specified equipment has been properly cleaned following a specific cleaning event.

                    

                    

                        **Cleaning Process**
                        

A process used to remove any product, process-related material and environmental contaminant introduced into equipment as part of the manufacturing stream.

                    

                    

                        **Cleaning Agent**
                        

The solution or solvent used in the washing step of a cleaning process. Examples: water, organic solvent, commodity chemical diluted in water, and formulated detergent diluted in water.

                    

                    

                        **Cleaning Procedure**
                        

The documentation that assures any product and process-related material introduced into equipment as part of the manufacturing process stream is removed and the equipment is adequately stored.

                    

                    

#### Residue & Contamination |

                    

                        **Soil**
                        

The chemical or microbiological materials left on process equipment after completion of process manufacturing, but before initiation of the cleaning process.

                    

                    

                        **Residue**
                        

Chemical or microbiological material remaining on equipment surfaces after a cleaning process.

                    

                    

                        **Contamination**
                        

An undesired residue or residue level on cleaned equipment surfaces or in a manufactured product.

                    

                    

                        **Worst-Case Process Condition**
                        

A condition or set of conditions encompassing upper and/or lower processing limits and circumstances, within SOPs, which pose the greatest chance of product or process failure compared to ideal conditions (such conditions do not necessarily induce failure).

                    

                    

                        **Worst Case Soil**
                        

A soil that is the most difficult to clean from production equipment based on knowledge generated from laboratory studies, scientific properties, and/or production experience.

                    

                    

#### Acceptance & Limits |

                    

                        **Acceptable Daily Exposure (ADE)**
                        

A dose that is unlikely to cause an adverse effect if an individual is exposed, by any route, at or below this dose every day for a lifetime.

                    

                    

                        **Acceptable Daily Intake (ADI)**
                        

An amount of a substance consumed on a daily basis that is considered at a safe level.

                    

                    

                        **Acceptance Criteria**
                        

Numerical limits, ranges, or other suitable measures for the acceptance of test results.

                    

                    

                        **Acceptance Limit**
                        

The maximum amount of residue allowed in a product, in an analytical sample, or as an amount per surface area.

                    

                    

                        **Limit**
                        

A value for a residue above which a cleaning process would not be acceptable.

                    

                    

#### Time & Equipment |

                    

                        **Clean Hold Time**
                        

The time from the end of the cleaning process until the equipment is used again (which may be product manufacture, autoclaving, or a SIP cycle).

                    

                    

                        **Dirty Hold Time**
                        

The time from the end of product manufacturing until the beginning of the cleaning process (also called "soiled hold time").

                    

                    

                        **Campaign**
                        

Processing of multiple lots or batches of the same product serially in the same equipment.

                    

                    

                        **Changeover / Product Changeover**
                        

The steps taken for switching multiproduct equipment from the manufacture of one product to the manufacture of a different product.

                    

                    

                        **Dedicated Equipment**
                        

Equipment used exclusively for the manufacture of only one drug product, bulk drug substance, or intermediate.

                    

                    

                        **Equipment Train**
                        

The sequence of equipment through which a product is produced or processed.

                    

                    

#### Analytical & Laboratory |

                    

                        **Recovery Study**
                        

A laboratory study combining the sampling method and analytical method to determine the quantitative recovery of a specific residue for a defined surface.

                    

                    

                        **Analyte**
                        

Substance (usually a residue) for which an analysis is being performed.

                    

                    

                        **Blank**
                        

Analytical sample taken to establish background value for the analytical measurement which may be subtracted from an experimental value to determine the "true" value.

                    

                    

                        **Coupon**
                        

A small, generally flat portion of a defined material of construction (such as stainless steel or PTFE) and of a defined surface finish, typically used for laboratory cleaning evaluations and/or recovery studies.

                    

                    

                        **Marker**
                        

Component of a product or cleaning agent used as an analyte to quantitate the total amount of product or cleaning agent present.

                    

                    

#### Mock & Strategy |

                    

                        **Mock Soil**
                        

A soil which is used in place of the manufactured product during a cleaning validation protocol (also called a "surrogate" soil).

                    

                    

                        **Mock Soiling**
                        

A process of soiling equipment for a cleaning validation protocol in which soil is applied to simulate the condition following typical product manufacturing.

                    

                    

                        **Grouping Strategy**
                        

A strategy for establishing similar cleaning processes, usually based on similar products or similar equipment, and to validate the cleaning process based primarily on validation data for a representative of the group.

                    

                    

#### Hazard & Drug-Related |

                    

                        **Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API)**
                        

Any substance or mixture of substances intended to be used in the manufacture of a drug (medicinal) product and when used in the production of a drug, becomes an active ingredient of the drug product (also called "drug substance").

                    

                    

                        **Highly Hazardous Drug Active**
                        

A drug active that can cause serious adverse effects at typical doses. Those adverse effects are generally not related to the main therapeutic activity of the drug, and includes carcinogenicity, mutagenicity, genotoxicity, reproductive hazards, allergenicity, and cytotoxicity.

                    

                    

                        **Degradation**
                        

Breakdown (usually chemical) of material during manufacture (including during and after the cleaning process).

                    

                    

                        **Normal Dose**
                        

The therapeutic dose of a product as given on the approved product labeling.

                    

                    

                        **LD50**
                        

The dose of a material which results in 50% mortality in an animal test.

                    

                    

                        **Investigational Medicinal Product**
                        

A pharmaceutical form of an active substance or placebo being tested or used as a reference in a clinical trial.

## 3.0 Cleaning Process Design and Development

                

This section describes the application of operational parameters and measurements, design of laboratory scale experiments, selection of appropriate test soils, and scale-up for the cleaning of the manufacturing equipment. Additionally, the concept of Design Space — a Quality by Design approach to the development of pharmaceutical processes — is discussed and applied to the development of cleaning processes.

                

The cleaning process requires design and development prior to implementation in a manufacturing plant to ensure the cleaning process and equipment are acceptable for use.

                

The operational parameters that describe the cleaning process include:

                

                    
- Cleaning agent — chemical selected for soil removal

                    
- Concentration — effective and safe dilution range

                    
- Contact time — duration of agent-surface interaction

                    
- Temperature — process and rinse step temperatures

                

                

Factors which affect the cleaning process include product characteristics and product condition. Relevant specifics about the cleaning equipment include automated cleaning pathways, the sequence of manual or automated cleaning steps, and flow rates during each step.

                

Generally, establishment of acceptable cleaning processes follows a standard progression beginning with identification of control variables, cleaning measurements, and performance criteria. Laboratory (scale-down) experimentation, analogous to process characterization experiments, along with specific equipment requirements, may provide data to establish cleaning parameter control ranges.

## 3.3 Design Considerations

                

### 3.3.1 Location of Cleaning — CIP vs. COP

                

Equipment may be cleaned at its installed location, or disassembled and moved to a central location for cleaning.

                

#### 3.3.1.1.1 Clean-in-Place (CIP) Systems

                

Clean-in-Place (CIP) usually refers to an automated system using various tanks and piping to deliver cleaning solution through the equipment to be cleaned. Sprayballs (stationary or rotating) provide coverage and impingement. Used for large equipment such as manufacturing tanks, blenders, fluid bed dryers, reactors, and fermentation tanks.

                

CIP systems may be recirculating or single-pass. Centralized CIP systems provide a single location for handling cleaning agents but complicate segregation. Piping must be sloped continuously to a low point to ensure acceptable draining. Deadlegs must be eliminated.

                

#### 3.3.1.1.2 Solvent Reflux Cleaning

                

For small-molecule API manufacture, cleaning may involve boiling a volatile solvent in the reactor vessel. Solvent vapors rise, condense on cooler surfaces, dissolve residues, and carry dissolved residue back to the boiling solvent. Key issues: residues must be soluble in the solvent, and vapors must contact and condense on all intended surfaces.

                

#### 3.3.1.1.3 Placebo Batches as a Cleaning Method

                

For highly viscous ointments, a placebo run may be used to clean equipment. The placebo must have no detrimental quality impact on the next product. Disadvantages: cost and difficulty of demonstrating effectiveness.

                

#### 3.3.1.2 Out-of-Place Cleaning & COP Systems

                

Clean-Out-of-Place (COP) equipment includes wash tanks for cleaning small parts removed from larger equipment — recirculating baths, dishwasher-type cabinets for drums, filter housings, or hoppers. Placement patterns and disassembly are critical to cleaning success. Manual manipulation makes these procedures more complex than automated in-place cleaning.

                

### 3.3.2 Automated vs. Manual Systems

                

**Manual:** Direct cleaning by trained operator using hand tools. Operator controls parameters. Requires clearly written SOPs, training, and supervision.

                

**Semi-automated:** Intermediate — operator may manually remove gaskets before automated CIP, or use high-pressure spray device.

                

**Automated:** No personnel intervention during cycle (except cycle selection and start/stop). PLC-controlled. Provides consistent, robust monitoring of time, flow rate, pressure, concentration, and temperature. Validation of control systems is critical.

                

### 3.3.3 Soil Evaluation and Categorization

                

Cleaning processes should address the wide variety of potential process soils: manufactured products, degradation products, process aids, solvents, and cleaning agents. Categories of soils should be created and representative soils selected for testing. The final selection should be based on physiochemical properties similarity.

                

**Physical removal mechanisms:** High-pressure spray, high-velocity flow, manual scrubbing, vacuuming. Dependent on solubility, soil amount, and degree of adhesion.

                

**Chemical removal mechanisms:** Solubility, emulsification, wetting, chelation, dispersion, hydrolysis, and oxidation. Multiple steps may be used — e.g., alkaline detergent for solubilization followed by sodium hypochlorite for protein oxidation. Note: aggressive solutions may cause corrosion.

                

**Dirty hold time:** Fresh soils are generally easier to remove than dried soils. Time between soiling and cleaning must be considered in design studies.

                

### 3.3.4 Equipment Considerations

                

Equipment should be free-draining with limited intricate or complex parts. Sanitary designs employ appropriately finished surfaces, lack of crevices, absence of dead legs, and suitable construction materials.

                

Dedicated equipment (single product): concerns limited to cleaning agent residues, degradants, bioburden, and endotoxin. Nondedicated equipment: prevention of active ingredient carryover between products is a major focus. Certain products (beta-lactams, mutagenic APIs) may require segregated or dedicated facilities.

                

Surface finish affects cleanability — rough surfaces provide more area for soil contact and contain crevices difficult for cleaning agents to penetrate. Porous materials (filter bags, membranes) are typically dedicated to a given product.

                

### 3.3.5–3.3.7 Operational, Cleaning Agent, and Product Considerations

                

**Campaigns:** Series of batches of the same product. Minor/in-process cleaning between batches (vacuuming, solvent rinse) does not require separate validation. Full cleaning at end of campaign must account for accumulated soil, dirty hold time, and degradation effects.

                

**Cleaning agent selection:** Should be based on scientific rationale — suitability to remove residues, compatibility with equipment, ease of removal, and low toxicity. Vendors should be qualified like raw material vendors. Water for final rinse should match product grade (WFI for injectables, purified water for orals).

                

**Product risk:** Highly hazardous APIs (allergenic, cytotoxic, mutagenic) require more robust cleaning processes. Route of administration affects acceptable residue limits — injectables and inhalants pose greater risk of cross-contamination.

## 3.4 Cleaning Development Laboratory Experiments

                

Laboratory testing often includes screening combinations of soils and relevant process surfaces. Screening experiments test soil removal capability using representative soils and coupons of relevant surface materials. Cleaning conditions can be selected based on the soil-surface combination encountered in the production equipment.

                

### 3.4.1 Soil Selection

                

The soils should be representative of soils on equipment in the manufacturing plant, including the chemical and physical (dried, baked) nature. Solutions or suspensions of selected soils are coated on coupons representing process contact surfaces and dried to simulate soil conditions prior to testing.

                

Preparation of coupons involves a pre-cleaning procedure to ensure uniform starting conditions. Coupons are dried before spotting with soils. Spotting must be consistent to minimize experimental variability. Fixed drying time simulates the dirty hold time. Non-electro-polished stainless steel coupons with representative or worse surface finish than equipment surfaces may be preferred for worst-case evaluation.

                

### 3.4.2 Parameter Selection

                

Parameters impacting cleaning performance include: nature of product-surface interactions; cleaning agent-soil interactions; time (dirty hold time, each cycle step); cleaning agent concentration; temperature; cleaning action (stagnant, laminar, turbulent flow; pressure); and properties of the cleaning solution (ionic strength, pH, components, viscosity, density).

                

Use of a risk analysis tool such as Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) may assist in prioritizing operational parameters. Single-parameter studies identify significant impacts. These are then followed by Design of Experiments (DOE) to investigate interactions.

                

### 3.4.2.1 Parameter Interactions

                

DOE experiments determine the effect of varying individual parameters on cleanability and indicate interactions. Statistical tools — regression analysis, leverage plots, response surface analysis, interaction profiles — study main and interaction effects. DOE analyses can construct a multi-parameter design space for the cleaning process and establish ranges of operational parameters providing acceptable performance.

                

### 3.4.3 Measurements to Determine Cleaning Effectiveness

                

Cleaning effectiveness may be determined by sampling and analytical methods including: visual inspection; analytical techniques for measuring residues of manufactured product, degradant, cleaning agent, bioburden, and/or endotoxin. These may be online or offline measurements of rinse or swab samples.

                

Using existing knowledge and a risk-based approach, cleaning experiments can be reduced or eliminated — e.g., for transfer of a manufacturing process from one facility to another.

## 3.5 Cleaning Process Scale-Up

                

Following selection of cleaning agents and parameter ranges from historical plant data (if available) and laboratory development work, the cleaning process can be implemented for use on larger-scale manufacturing equipment. Determination of soil and cleaning agent residue removal is generally performed prior to formal cleaning validation protocols. Adjustments to cleaning parameters may be made during scale-up based on plant experience and laboratory development studies.

                

### 3.5.1 Setting Process Controls

                

It is both prudent and consistent with CGMP to establish control ranges for cleaning process operational and performance parameters. Operational parameters include:

                

                    
- Dirty hold time for equipment (time between completion of use and initiation of cleaning)

                    
- Clean hold time for equipment (time between completion of cleaning and next use)

                    
- Flow rate and/or delivery pressure of the cleaning stream (proof of flow for parallel flow paths)

                    
- Cleaning agent concentration

                    
- Duration of each step (by time or volume)

                    
- Temperature of washing solutions and rinses

                    
- Air flow verification during water removal or drying steps

                

                

Alert and/or action levels can be set for each critical parameter. Alert levels are set based on expected variability; action levels are set to permit adjustment before jeopardizing acceptable operation. Both must be within acceptable ranges.

                

Performance parameters may include: final rinse solvent analysis for active ingredients/degradants; final rinse solvent analysis for cleaning agent; final rinse water bioburden; final rinse water endotoxin.

## 3.6 Applying the "Design Space" Concept to Cleaning Processes

                

Design Space is the multidimensional combination and interaction of input variables and process parameters that have been demonstrated to provide assurance of quality. This concept, introduced by ICH Q8, can be applied to cleaning processes.

                

The cleaning design space for a manufacturing facility is defined through a risk- and science-based approach relying on cleaning process knowledge, product/equipment knowledge, regulations, and quality practices. The cleaning design space is represented by the range of each operational parameter that results in acceptable cleaning process performance.

                

                    "The assumption is that any cleaning process that is performed within the space defined by these conditions will be effective, reliable and consistent."
                

                

Many manufacturers want to design one cleaning process for a specific equipment train used regardless of the manufactured product. This may be accomplished by identifying the worst-case soils and defining the design space around cleaning process performance using these soils.

                

Cleaning effectiveness may be influenced by:

                

                    
- Soil type or family

                    
- Nature of the soil on the surface

                    
- Equipment and contact surface type and finish

                    
- Cleaning technology and functional specifications

                

                

Field conditions considered when developing an effective cleaning process: flow rate, cleaning agent concentration, contact time, process temperature, and dirty hold time. Parameters may be categorized based on their level of criticality, with the most critical parameters monitored closely.

## 3.7 Standard Operating Procedures

                

One of the outputs of the design and development of a cleaning process should be a draft Standard Operating Procedure (SOP). That draft SOP should reflect sufficient detail to ensure process consistency. Issues to be considered in the draft SOP:

                

                    
- The maximum allowable hold time for equipment — after use but before cleaning; after cleaning but before reuse, sanitization, or sterilization

                    
- Steps for disassembly of equipment, broken down in a manner that allows all parts to be effectively cleaned

                    
- Critical sites or difficult-to-clean areas requiring special cleaning emphasis or specific inspection

                    
- Cleaning process parameters

                    
- Assignment of responsibility for cleaning of equipment

                    
- Cleaning schedules, and where appropriate, sanitizing schedules

                    
- Removal or obliteration of previous batch identification

                    
- A description in sufficient detail of the methods, equipment, and materials used in cleaning

                    
- Sampling and testing that is part of the routine cleaning process

                    
- Steps for reassembling equipment for storage and subsequent use

                    
- Visual inspection for equipment wear, product residuals, and foreign materials

                    
- Protection of clean equipment from contamination prior to use

                    
- Batch records as appropriate (automated: PLC-stored; manual: documented per process complexity)

## 3.8 Operator Training for the Cleaning Process

                

Operator training is critical. During cycle development, operators should be trained in the requirements of the evolving or existing SOPs. Proper training consists of:

                

                    
1. Understanding the SOP

                    
2. Demonstration of the correct procedure by a trained operator

                    
3. Demonstration of the correct procedure by the trainee

                

                

Operator training for manual cleaning may also include qualification and/or requalification of the trainee by measuring residues on equipment cleaned by the operator. Operator training should be done on a more frequent basis for manual cleaning processes as compared to automated cleaning processes.

                

Training practices may be improved by:

                

                    
- Clearly written, understandable, and detailed SOPs

                    
- Use of checklists to determine all operations are carried out in proper sequence and are documented

                    
- Periodic monitoring of cleaning processes to ensure proper training and continued compliance

                    
- Dedicated or assigned cleaning personnel

                    
- Feedback from operators to modify procedures

                    
- Use of video to demonstrate proper cleaning operations and techniques

                

                

The operators should understand the process of cleaning and the operation of the equipment they are cleaning. In addition, operators should be aware of the cleaning process impact on the quality and safety of the next product manufactured in the same equipment.

## 3.9 Introduction of New Products to a Validated Cleaning System

                

When new products or significantly different raw materials are introduced to the plant, a system must be in place to ensure that the cleaning process will remain effective.

                

Generally, cleaning effectiveness for new products can be tested by performing laboratory experiments using coupons of relevant materials (see Section 3.4). These experiments test both the effectiveness of the proposed cleaning regimen and the relative difficulty of cleaning the new soils compared to soils already introduced to the plant.

                

                    "If the new soils are easier to clean than the most difficult soil already being cleaned, introduction of the new material using existing cleaning procedures can be made with confidence. If the material is more difficult to clean than each of the present soils, some modifications to the current cleaning process may be required, and cleaning validation for the new product is an expectation."
                

                

Key decision framework:

                

                    
- **New soil easier to clean** than current worst case → existing cleaning procedures can be used with confidence; based on risk assessment, determine if confirmatory runs are needed

                    
- **New soil more difficult to clean** → modifications to current cleaning process may be required; cleaning validation for the new product is an expectation

## 4.0 Qualification

            

Qualification is a part of cleaning validation involving the traditional activities of equipment qualification and process qualification. For cleaning validation purposes, equipment qualification focuses on qualifying (or verifying) the equipment used as part of the cleaning process, such as a CIP skid and automated parts washer. For fully manual cleaning operations, such as brushing or scrubbing, there may be no equipment qualification activities. Design qualification has also been considered as another qualification activity, which is addressed in the design and development stage.

            

The emphasis for this section is on process qualification activities. Process qualification involves the runs performed under a protocol designed to demonstrate the consistency of the cleaning process.

            

The traditional approach for cleaning validation has been to focus on the qualification protocols to demonstrate effectiveness and consistency. The lifecycle approach that the industry has been moving toward involves a different approach with a more comprehensive view, with qualification runs being only one of the stages of validation. The lifecycle approach also includes design/development activities and validation maintenance (ongoing controls).

            

                This section covers protocol elements and specific important issues for cleaning validation protocols, including the number of validation runs required, mock soiling for validation runs, worst-case process conditions, and the disposition of equipment/product during validation runs. It also covers grouping approaches for products and equipment as well as important considerations in clean hold time studies.

## 4.1 Protocol Elements

            

Cleaning validation protocols have many of the same elements as process validation protocols. For reasons of clarity, the format of a cleaning validation protocol usually follows the same approach (as appropriate) as used for process validation protocols for a given company.

            

Common elements include (but are not limited to):

            

                
- Purpose

                
- Validation design/strategy

                
- Scope

                
- Responsibilities

                
- Applicable product(s) and equipment

                
- Cleaning procedure and associated documentation

                
- Acceptance Criteria

                
- Training

                
- Requirement for a final report

            

            

Key elements for cleaning validation protocols include **residue limits** (see Section 5.0), **sampling procedures** (see Section 6.0) and **analytical methods** (see Section 7.0).

            

### Documentation Approaches

            

Two approaches are used for documentation of elements:

            

**Referencing approach:** Specification of swab sampling sites can be in the protocol while the rationale for selection of those sites is in another referenced document. This results in more "streamlined" protocols containing only information required for execution.

            

**Inclusive approach:** All relevant details for a given element are included in the protocol, providing greater clarity to those executing the protocol. The approach used should consider the knowledge management systems within a given firm.

## 4.2 Key Protocol Issues

            

The validation protocol is not written and approved until the cleaning process has been designed and developed (see Section 3.0). The execution of the protocol should not begin until the protocol is approved. However, execution of the protocol as an **engineering or practice run** can be helpful in some circumstances (e.g., for activities that are highly complicated or new to those executing the protocol).

            

### 4.2.1 Number of Runs in a Protocol

            

The traditional approach for cleaning validation protocols has been to require an evaluation of three consecutive runs of the cleaning processes. "Consecutive" means that no cleaning events of that same process are skipped without an appropriate rationale.

            

Based on lifecycle approaches to validation, as well as several regulatory documents including the **2011 U.S. FDA process validation guidance**, the newer approach has been to provide a rationale, based on an understanding of the cleaning process, documentation from the design and development phase, and data from sufficiently similar cleaning processes, for a specific number of validation runs required. This might result in *fewer than three runs* or *greater than three runs*.

            

                It should be recognized that the 2011 U.S. FDA process validation guidance does not formally cover cleaning validation. However, a number of principles in that document may be applicable to the validation of cleaning processes.

### 4.2.2 Mock Soiling

            

Ordinarily a cleaning validation run is performed by cleaning on a commercial-scale batch. An alternative approach is to use what is called mock soiling or "artificial soiling" to simulate the nature and condition of the manufactured product on the commercial equipment at the time of initiating the cleaning process.

            

If mock soiling is used, a rationale must be provided for its use as well as why the mock soiling simulates a "realistic" manufacturing situation. A common reason for mock soiling has been to obtain three consecutive cleaning validation runs without being forced to make three commercial-scale batches of the cleaned product.

            

                "Mock soiling" (a process) should be distinguished from a "mock soil" (sometimes called a "surrogate soil"), which is a product which simulates the physicochemical properties of the actual soil.

### 4.2.3 Worst-Case Process Conditions

            

The traditional approach has been to include worst-case process conditions in the three protocol runs. Rationales for worst-case conditions should be given in or referenced in the protocol.

            

Examples of worst-case process conditions include:

            

                
- **Maximum dirty hold time** — longest allowed time from end of manufacture to start of cleaning

                
- **Maximum batches or elapsed time in a campaign**

                
- **Shortest allowed time** for manual cleaning steps

                
- **Lowest allowed temperature** for manual cleaning processes

                
- **Worst-case circuits** for CIP skid selection

            

            

Parameters such as temperature, cleaning agent concentration, flow rates, and process step times for *automated* cleaning processes are generally controlled in a narrow range; challenging these at extremes is generally not appropriate in formal validation runs — instead they can be evaluated in development studies to demonstrate robustness.

            

#### Campaign Length as Worst Case

            

When validated cleaning is only performed at the end of campaign (with no cleaning between batches or only "minor cleaning"), the **maximum number of batches in a campaign** may represent the worst case. The validation protocol should address this by:

            

                
1. Specifying a minimum campaign length for initial validation runs

                
2. Executing additional validation at longer campaign lengths as they occur

                
3. Or addressing campaign length fully in design and development (showing no change in cleaning difficulty regardless of campaign length)

### 4.2.4 Disposition of Products and Equipment during Validation

            

A cleaning process generally only affects the next product manufactured in the cleaned equipment. Therefore, following protocol execution the "cleaned" product may be released following company procedures for product release. That release of product is **independent** of the data obtained for the immediately following cleaning process.

            

There are several approaches for disposition of the equipment following the cleaning process:

            

                
- **Approach 1 (Hold until data):** Do not release the equipment until acceptable data (including meeting all residue criteria) are obtained for that specific validation run. Then release for manufacture of same or another product.

                
- **Approach 2 (Release at risk):** Release the equipment following company procedures at risk for manufacture of the next product. However, that next product cannot be released until acceptable data are obtained. If the validation run fails, the impact on that next manufactured product must be assessed as part of the non-conformance investigation.

            

            

If there are separate validation protocols for equipment items in a train, each equipment item can be released based on its own protocol data — it is not necessary to wait until validation is complete on all equipment items in the train before any item can be released.

## 4.3 Grouping/Family Approach

            

Grouping is a strategy whereby manufactured products and/or equipment are considered together, and a formal protocol is performed on a representative from the group. The representative is usually the worst case among products or equipment in a group. Grouping is also called matrixing, family approach, or bracketing.

            

The rationale for grouping is to generate optimum value from cleaning validation tasks based on a risk approach. One requirement for grouping is that product and equipment be cleaned by the **same cleaning process**.

            

### 4.3.1 Product Grouping

            

Products may be grouped together if they are manufactured on the same or equivalent equipment and cleaned by the same cleaning procedure. Relative cleanability may be assessed by:

            

                
- Selecting the product with the **least-soluble active ingredient** in the cleaning solution

                
- Determining relative difficulty of cleaning using **laboratory coupon studies** (using representative stainless steel surfaces, with roughness same or rougher than actual equipment)

                
- Considering **bioactivity and clinical effects** for representative product selection

            

            

One option is a **surrogate worst-case product** — an artificially constructed product designed to be more difficult to clean than commercial products. Rationale: maintains continuity of worst case if a commercial product is discontinued, and minimizes situations where new worst-case products emerge.

            

Successful cleaning validation of the representative (worst-case) product means the cleaning of the other products in the group is also validated. Options based on risk assessment:

            

                
- Perform a single confirmatory validation run on every other product in the group

                
- Perform full protocols on both the most difficult-to-clean product AND the product with the lowest limit

            

            

### 4.3.2 Equipment Grouping

            

Equipment may be grouped if similar and cleaned by the same procedure. Equipment is designated as identical or similar based on design, mode of operation, and cleanability.

            

                
- **Identical equipment:** A protocol on any combination of identical equipment items is sufficient; no need to validate every item.

                
- **Similar equipment:** Choose worst case, or use bracketing (largest and smallest, representing extremes). Confirmatory validation runs are an option for non-worst-case items.

            

            

**Minor equipment** (utensils, small parts) may be grouped based on complexity, size, and functionality.

            

### 4.3.3 Introduction of New Product or Equipment

            

New products are assessed using the same science and risk-based evaluation process as initial grouping. The new product's relative cleanability vs. the previous worst case and any change in the lowest residue limit dictates the validation requirements:

            

                
- **Easier-to-clean new product:** May require only laboratory/scale-up studies or one confirmatory run

                
- **More difficult-to-clean new product:** Requires full validation of the new worst case

                
- **New identical equipment:** May require only equivalence determination or one confirmatory run

                
- **New similar equipment:** Evaluate whether it represents a new worst case or new bracketing extreme

## 4.4 "Cleaning Verification" Documentation

            

Cleaning verification as used in this Technical Report refers to documentation which says that a **one-off cleaning event** is effective for cleaning equipment so that the equipment can be used for subsequent manufacture of a product.

            

Examples of where cleaning verification might be used:

            

                
- Cleaning after manufacture of a **clinical trial product**

                
- Cleaning after product manufacture where there is a **deviation** (e.g., the dirty hold time is exceeded) that affects a validated cleaning process

            

            

Documentation for cleaning verification is similar to documentation for cleaning validation, **except** that the verification data is specific to one cleaning event. Key differences:

            

                
- The data applies *only* to the one cleaning event (scientifically the data may suggest similar performance, but from a compliance perspective it is not generalized)

                
- Because cleaning verification is typically performed on a unique cleaning event, there may be **limited cleaning design and development** before execution

                
- Common approaches: a cleaning SOP + a cleaning verification protocol, or an SOP defining explicit requirements with a form documenting specific activities, sample positions, etc.

            

            

                It is generally not appropriate to consider three cleaning verification runs as constituting a "validation" especially if the element of appropriate design and development is absent.

### 5.2 The Basis for Quantitative Limits

                    

Limits are usually based on one of the following:

                    

                        
- The medical or pharmacological potency of the drug active

                        
- The toxicity of the residue

                        
- A default value

                    

                    

Different manufacturing and cleaning situations may require different approaches. For example, for in vitro diagnostics, the effect of the residue on the stability or performance of the subsequently manufactured product may provide a better scientific rationale for establishing limits.

                    

Depending on the manufacturer, the expression of calculations may differ because it may combine various steps. It is critical that the units used in the equation be internally consistent; unit conversion factors (e.g., grams to micrograms) may be utilized. Companies may use different terms (or acronyms) for the same concept but still apply the same basic principles.

1. Determine **ARL** (Acceptable Residue Level) — concentration in next product

                            
2. Calculate **MAC** (Maximum Allowable Carryover) — total mass on equipment

                            
3. Derive **SAL** (Surface Area Limit) — mass per cm²

                            
4. Convert SAL to sample limits (swab or rinse)

## Acceptable Concentration of Residue in Next Product (ARL)

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

The first determination is the acceptable level (i.e., concentration) of the target residue in the subsequently manufactured product. For this document, that concentration will be called Acceptable Residue Level (ARL). This is an expression of the maximum concentration of residue allowed in the next product, as determined by medical, pharmacological, safety, stability and/or performance issues.

                    

For chemical residues (such as the drug active or cleaning agent), this concentration is typically given as **µg/g or µg/mL**. For bioburden, this is typically given as **CFU/g or CFU/mL**.

                    

### 5.3.1 ARL Based on Drug Active Dose (Equation 5A)

                    

For drug actives in drug product manufacture, this is typically determined as **one-one thousandth (0.001)** of minimum daily dose of the drug active in a maximum daily dose of the next drug product.

                    

                        [Equation 5A] — ARL from Dose
                        ARL = (MDD × SF) / LDD
                        

                            
- **ARL** = Acceptable Residue Level in next drug product

                            
- **MDD** = Minimum Daily Dose of active in cleaned product

                            
- **SF** = Safety Factor (typically 0.001)

                            
- **LDD** = Largest Daily Dose of next drug product manufactured in same equipment

                        

                    

                    

                        

#### Notes on Equation 5A

                        

                            
- **Note 1:** For API manufacture, LDD = largest daily dose of the *next drug active* (not drug product).

                            
- **Note 2:** MDD may be replaced by single therapeutic dose for daily-administered products. For weekly-administered products, normalize to the same time basis (daily or weekly) before calculating.

                            
- **Note 3:** Some companies express SF as 1000 in the denominator instead of 0.001 in the numerator — mathematically equivalent.

                        

                    

                    

### 5.3.2 ARL Based on Toxicity

                    

Two types of toxicity-based calculations:

                    

                        
- **ADE approach** (Risk-MaPP): for drug actives, intermediates, degradants. Based on NOAEL. Required for highly hazardous drug actives.

                        
- **LD50 approach**: for residues like cleaning agents that do not have a dose.

                    

                    

### 5.3.2.1 ADE Determination (Equation 5B)

                    

A qualified toxicologist estimates the Acceptable Daily Exposure (ADE) based on body weight and adjustment factors:

                    

                        [Equation 5B] — ADE Calculation
                        ADE = (NOAEL × BW) / (UFC × MF × PK)
                        

                            
- **NOAEL** = No Observable Adverse Effect Level

                            
- **BW** = Body weight of patient taking next product

                            
- **UFC** = Composite uncertainty factor (interspecies, intraspecies, subchronic-chronic, LOAEL-NOAEL, database completeness)

                            
- **MF** = Modifying factor (toxicologist judgment)

                            
- **PK** = Pharmacokinetic factor (route of exposure)

                        

                    

                    

                        [Equation 5C] — ARL from ADE
                        ARL = ADE / LDD
                        

                            
- **ADE** = Acceptable Daily Exposure of the residue

                            
- **LDD** = Largest Daily Dose of next drug product

                        

                    

                    

### 5.3.2.2 Toxicity Calculations Based on LD50 (Equations 5D–5F)

                    

                        [Equation 5D] — NOEL from LD50
                        NOEL = (LD50 × BW) / MF1
                        

                            
- **NOEL** = No Observable Effect Level

                            
- **LD50** = 50% lethal dose (mg/kg body weight, by appropriate route)

                            
- **BW** = Body weight of patient taking next product

                            
- **MF1** = Modifying factor(s) — cumulative total generally ≤ 1000

                        

                    

                    

                        [Equation 5E] — SDI from NOEL
                        SDI = NOEL / MF2
                        

                            
- **SDI** = Safe Daily Intake of the residue

                            
- **MF2** = Modifying factor(s) — cumulative total generally ≤ 1000

                        

                    

                    

                        [Equation 5F] — ARL from SDI
                        ARL = SDI / LDD
                    

                    

### 5.3.3 Other ARL Determinations

                    

For genotoxic residues where NOEL values are not available: use the **Threshold of Toxicological Concern (TTC)** principle — SDI established at **1.5 µg/day** (FDA determination for safe levels in foods). Note this may not be appropriate for injectables.

                    

                        [Equation 5G] — ARL for TTC-based SDI
                        ARL = SDI / LDD
                        

                            
- **SDI** = Safe Daily Intake (1.5 µg/day for TTC approach)

                            
- **LDD** = Largest Daily Dose of next drug product

                        

                    

                    

For residues with concern about **deleterious effect on stability or performance** (e.g., in vitro diagnostics), ARL must be determined directly based on spiking studies evaluating effects on stability and/or performance.

## Maximum Allowable Carryover (MAC) & Surface Area Limit (SAL)

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.4 Acceptable Total Carryover — MAC / MACO (Equation 5H)

                    

Once the ARL is determined, the maximum allowable carryover (MAC or MACO) can be calculated. MAC is the **total amount** of a target residue allowed in a batch of the next manufactured product.

                    

It is calculated by multiplying the ARL by the minimum batch size of the next product. MAC is expressed in mass units (µg, mg, or g) for chemical residues.

                    

                        [Equation 5H] — MAC Calculation
                        MAC = ARL × MBS
                        

                            
- **MAC** = Maximum Allowable Carryover

                            
- **ARL** = Acceptable Residue Level in next product (µg/g or µg/mL)

                            
- **MBS** = Minimum Batch Size of next product (g or mL)

                        

                    

                    

                        

#### Important Notes on MAC

                        

                            
- MBS is in **mass units** if ARL is in µg/g, or **volume units** if ARL is in µg/mL.

                            
- For API manufacture: MBS = minimum batch size of the *next drug active* manufactured in the same equipment.

                            
- The MAC is the **total amount allowed on all shared equipment surfaces** between the two products — not per piece of equipment.

                        

                    

                    

### 5.5 Surface Area Limit — SAL (Equation 5I)

                    

Once the MAC is determined, the surface area limit (SAL) can be calculated by dividing the MAC by the total equipment shared surface area. SAL is expressed in mass per surface area (e.g., µg/cm²).

                    

                        [Equation 5I] — SAL Calculation
                        SAL = MAC / SSA
                        

                            
- **SAL** = Surface Area Limit (on shared equipment surfaces)

                            
- **MAC** = Maximum Allowable Carryover

                            
- **SSA** = Shared Surface Area (cm²)

                        

                    

                    

                        

#### Default SAL Value

                        

The most typical default value for the SAL is **4 µg/cm²**. This level is commonly cited as the upper limit for what is considered visually clean.

                        

                            
- If Equation 5I results in a value **above 4 µg/cm²**: use 4 µg/cm² as the SAL.

                            
- If Equation 5I results in a value **below 4 µg/cm²**: use that lower calculated value.

## Limit in Protocol Samples |

                

Swab Limits, Extracted Swab Concentration, Rinse Concentration

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

Once the SAL is determined, the limit in swab or rinse samples can be calculated. Three typical cases are covered.

                    

### 5.6.1 Limit per Swab (Equation 5J)

                    

For swab sampling, one approach is to express the limit as a **mass-per-swab sample** (e.g., µg/swab). The mass-limit-per-swab is determined by multiplying the SAL by the area sampled. Typical swab sampling areas are **25 cm² and 100 cm²**.

                    

                        [Equation 5J] — Limit per Swab
                        Limit per swab = SAL × swabbed area
                        

                            
- **SAL** = Surface Area Limit (µg/cm²)

                            
- **Swabbed area** = Area sampled by a single swab (cm²)

                        

                    

                    

### 5.6.2 Concentration Limit in Extracted Swab Solvent (Equation 5K)

                    

Another approach: express the limit as a **concentration of residue** in a fixed amount of extraction solvent (aqueous or organic). Units: µg/g, µg/mL, or ppm.

                    

                        [Equation 5K] — Swab Extract Concentration Limit
                        Concentration Limit = (SAL × swabbed area) / SEA
                        

                            
- **SAL** = Surface Area Limit (µg/cm²)

                            
- **Swabbed area** = Area sampled (cm²)

                            
- **SEA** = Solvent Extraction Amount (g or mL)

                        

                    

                    

### 5.6.3 Concentration Limit in Rinse Sampling Solution (Equations 5L & 5M)

                    

For rinse sampling, most companies express the limit as a **concentration** in the rinse sampling solution (µg/g, µg/mL, or ppm).

                    

                        [Equation 5L] — Rinse Concentration Limit (general)
                        Concentration Limit = (SAL × Area Sampled by Rinse) / Rinse Sampling Volume
                    

                    

If the **entire equipment train** is rinsed with one rinse solution, then the SSA and the "Area Sampled by Rinse Sampling" are identical. A simplified expression is:

                    

                        [Equation 5M] — Rinse Concentration Limit (simplified, whole equipment)
                        Concentration Limit = MAC / Rinse Sampling Volume
                        

                            
- **MAC** = Maximum Allowable Carryover

                            
- **Rinse Sampling Volume** = Total volume of rinse solution (g or mL)

## Consolidated Expressions & Example Calculations

                

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.7 Consolidated Expressions

                    

While the calculations in Sections 5.3 to 5.6 are presented to explicitly show the steps, it is common for companies to combine several equations to simplify calculations.

                    

For companies setting limits based on a fraction of therapeutic dose, the combined equation for MAC is:

                    

                        [Equation 5N] — MAC Direct (Dose-based)
                        MAC = (MDD × SF × MBS) / LDD
                        

                            
- **MAC** = Maximum Allowable Carryover

                            
- **MDD** = Minimum Daily Dose of cleaned product active

                            
- **SF** = Safety Factor (typically 0.001)

                            
- **MBS** = Minimum Batch Size of next product

                            
- **LDD** = Largest Daily Dose of next drug product

                        

                    

                    

For companies combining Equations 5A, 5H, 5I, and 5K for a swab extract concentration limit directly:

                    

                        [Equation 5O] — Swab Concentration Limit Direct (Dose-based)
                        Concentration Limit = (MDD × SF × MBS × swabbed area) / (LDD × SSA × SEA)
                        

                            
- **MDD** = Minimum Daily Dose of cleaned product active

                            
- **SF** = Safety Factor (typically 0.001)

                            
- **MBS** = Minimum Batch Size of next product

                            
- **LDD** = Largest Daily Dose of next drug product

                            
- **SSA** = Shared Surface Area (cm²)

                            
- **SEA** = Solvent Extraction Amount (mL)

                        

                    

                    

### 5.8 Example Calculations

                    

                        

#### Example 1: MAC Calculation (Equation 5N)

                        

                            **Given:**
                            

                                
- MDD (cleaned product) = 100 mg active

                                
- SF = 0.001

                                
- MBS (next product) = 10 kg = 10,000,000 mg

                                
- LDD (next product) = 800 mg

                            

                        

                        

**Calculation:**

                        

                            MAC = (MDD × SF × MBS) / LDD  

                                = (100 mg × 0.001 × 10,000,000 mg) / 800 mg  

                                = 1,000,000 mg² / 800 mg  

                                = **1,250 mg**
                        

                        

                            MAC = 1,250 mg — this is the total limit for all residues of the active on all shared equipment surfaces between the two products.
                        

                    

                    

                        

#### Example 2: Swab Extract Concentration Limit (Equation 5O)

                        

                            **Given (same products as Example 1, plus):**
                            

                                
- SSA (Shared Surface Area) = 250,000 cm²

                                
- Swabbed area = 100 cm²

                                
- SEA (Solvent Extraction Amount) = 5 mL

                            

                        

                        

**Calculation:**

                        

                            Conc. Limit = (MDD × SF × MBS × swabbed area) / (LDD × SSA × SEA)  

                                = (100 mg × 0.001 × 10,000,000 mg × 100) / (800 mg × 250,000 × 5)  

                                = 100,000,000 / 1,000,000,000  

                                = **0.1 mg/mL (or 100 µg/mL)**
                        

                        

                            Concentration Limit = 0.1 mg/mL = 100 µg/mL — this is the limit in the extracted swab solvent.

## Other Considerations |

                

Multiple Products, Dedicated Equipment, Default Limits, Special Residues

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.9.1 Multiple Next Products

                    

If multiple products could follow a given cleaned product, calculations should be considered for all "subsequently manufactured products," and the resulting **lowest limit** (typically the lowest limit per surface area) should be established. Three options exist:

                    

                        
- **Option 1:** Calculate limits for all possible next products; use the worst-case lowest limit.

                        
- **Option 2:** Restrict order of manufacturing by procedure.

                        
- **Option 3:** Cleaning verification mode — evaluate acceptability for each specific cleaning event based on the immediately following product.

                    

                    

### 5.9.2 Next Product in Verification Approach

                    

In a cleaning verification protocol (especially in development/clinical manufacturing), the next product may not be known at the time of cleaning. Approaches:

                    

                        
- Measure residues after cleaning; hold equipment release until next product is determined, then perform carryover evaluation.

                        
- Establish worst-case values for next product parameters; clean to meet those values; verify actual next product falls within worst-case when determined.

                    

                    

### 5.9.3 Default Limits

                    

Two types of default limits:

                    

                        
- **Type 1:** Default used when it is more stringent than the medically safe calculation.
                            

                                
    - Default ARL for drug products: **10 ppm** (used if calculated ARL > 10 ppm)

                                
    - Default SAL: **4 µg/cm²** (used if calculated SAL > 4 µg/cm²)

                                
    - For API manufacture: common defaults are 50 ppm or 100 ppm for ARL

                            

                        

                        
- **Type 2:** Default used when a medically safe limit cannot be established (e.g., intermediates in API manufacture). Established on risk assessment and process understanding.

                    

                    

### 5.9.4 Use of Different Safety Factors

                    

The safety factor of **0.001** is typically applied regardless of route of administration. A risk assessment may justify more stringent or less stringent factors:

                    

                        
- More stringent: clinical trial materials where dose is not fully established

                        
- Less stringent: short-duration drug products (e.g., cold tablets, ~10 days) — since SF = 0.001 was originally established for chronic administration

                    

                    

### 5.9.5 Different Routes of Administration

                    

If cleaned product and next product have different administration routes (e.g., oral → injectable), perform a risk assessment including evaluation of hazards if oral drug is administered as injectable, or extent of systemic availability.

                    

### 5.9.6 Different Doses for Adults and Children

                    

Calculate ARL for both products using adult doses and using child doses separately. Use the **lower ARL** for subsequent limit calculations. Where one product is dosed only for adults and the next only for children, a risk assessment should be considered.

                    

### 5.9.7 Human and Veterinary Products on Same Equipment

                    

A risk assessment is required, considering species differences and body weight differences.

                    

### 5.9.8 Genotoxic and Highly Hazardous Active Ingredients

                    

Four approaches for genotoxic residues:

                    

                        
- Use TTC value of 1.5 µg/day as SDI (Section 5.3.3)

                        
- Dedicate equipment to that one product

                        
- Perform cleaning validation with toxicology-based limits (Section 5.3.2.2)

                        
- Set limit below limit of detection (with medical risk assessment)

                    

                    

These approaches also apply to: reproductive toxicity hazards, allergenicity, cytotoxicity, mutagenicity.

                    

### 5.9.9 Limits Based on Analytical Detection Limits

                    

Not normally recommended — as analytical methods improve, limits will be driven exceedingly low. The issue is not whether the residue can be measured, but whether it is **medically safe** and does not affect subsequent product quality.

                    

### 5.9.10 Degradation of Active Ingredient

                    

If active ingredient degrades during/after cleaning, the relevant residue to measure is the **degradant**. Two approaches:

                    

                        
- Set limits for the degradant and measure it with an appropriate specific method.

                        
- Set limits for undegraded active based on its dose; measure with a nonspecific method (e.g., TOC); convert measured residue to equivalent undegraded active amount.

                    

                    

### 5.9.11 Limits Not Measurable

                    

If calculated limits fall below analytical detection limits, options include:

                    

                        
- Dedicate equipment to one product

                        
- Modify next product parameters (raise minimum batch size, restrict manufacturing order)

                        
- Modify sampling parameters (larger swab area, smaller solvent volume)

                        
- Lower rinse volume for rinse sampling

                        
- Concentrate the rinse sample (e.g., vacuum evaporation)

                    

                    

### 5.9.12 Limits for Organic Solvents

                    

Options: toxicity calculations; use ICH Q3C (R5) acceptable levels; not setting limits for volatile organic solvents if adequate evaporation conditions are established. Note Q3C technically applies to solvents used in API manufacture, not necessarily cleaning.

                    

### 5.9.13 Dedicated Equipment

                    

For equipment dedicated to one product, active ingredient carryover concern is minimized. Visually clean may be appropriate (per U.S. FDA guidance). However, cleaning validation may still be required for **degradants, cleaning agents, and bioburden**.

                    

Dedicated parts of an equipment train are not considered part of the SSA for active ingredient limits, but may be relevant for cleaning agent limits.

                    

### 5.9.14 Dividing Limits Among Equipment Pieces

                    

MAC is the total amount across all shared surfaces. Options:

                    

                        
- Apply the same SAL to every surface (common approach)

                        
- Apportion MAC differently among equipment pieces, provided total remains at MAC (e.g., SAL = 0.5 µg/cm² for Equipment A, 1.0 for B, 1.5 for C — sum = MAC)

                    

                    

### 5.9.15 Limits for Preferential Transfer

                    

Equipment should be classified into: zones where residue distributes **homogeneously** in next product (blender, granulator), vs. zones with **preferential transfer** to individual dosage units (tablet press, vial filler). For preferential transfer zones, use more stringent limits, or discard an initial portion of the next batch, or dedicate those parts.

                    

### 5.9.17 Products with More Than One Active Ingredient

                    

Options: set limits for all actives and measure each, or determine the worst-case active (lowest limit based on difficulty of cleaning, solubility, concentration) and measure only that.

## Bioburden Limits & Endotoxin Limits |

                

Microbial Carryover Considerations for Cleaning Validation

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.10 Bioburden Limits

                    

In considering bioburden limits following cleaning, it is **not expected** that the cleaning process itself results in sterile equipment. If limits are established for bioburden in a cleaning validation protocol, those limits can be established using carryover calculations (Sections 5.3–5.6).

                    

For **non-sterile manufacture**: starting point is an ARL in CFU/g or CFU/mL based on the bioburden specification of the next product. Since there are sources of bioburden other than the cleaned equipment (e.g., from raw materials), an **adjustment factor** is usually applied to lower the bioburden ARL.

                    

Carryover calculations typically result in SAL values significantly above **10 CFU/cm²** or rinse sampling solution limits significantly above **100 CFU/mL**. A risk assessment should evaluate the nature of the next product (low vs. high water activity, presence of preservatives, bioburden proliferation during clean hold time).

                    

Many companies establish very conservative bioburden limits:

                    

                        
- Surface sampling: **1–2 CFU/cm²**

                        
- Rinse samples (non-sterile): typical purified water limit of **100 CFU/mL**

                    

                    

Even if equipment is **steamed in place or autoclaved** prior to next product, or next product is **sterile-filtered**, it is typically practice to evaluate bioburden to ensure the process is not overly challenged.

                    

For rinse sampling with WFI after subsequent steaming:

                    

                        
- Approach 1: Use typical WFI values (10 CFU/100 mL)

                        
- Approach 2: Use 100 CFU/100 mL or 1,000 CFU/100 mL (rationale: equipment will be subsequently steamed)

                    

                    

Additional consideration: objectionable organisms — not limited to USP specified organisms, but include organisms selected based on product and manufacturing understanding. The degree of identification may be to species level, or just methods to exclude objectionable organisms.

                    

### 5.11 Endotoxin Limits

                    

Endotoxin carryover to the final product is a concern for any product with endotoxin specifications. Common practice:

                    

                        
- Measure endotoxin in the **final rinse water**

                        
- Limits typically set at the WFI limit of 0.25 EU/mL

                    

                    

If the equipment is **depyrogenated by heat**, endotoxin will be deactivated and measurement of endotoxin for cleaned equipment may not be required.

## Visually Clean Criterion |

                

When Can Visual Inspection Replace Analytical Sampling?

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 5.12 Visually Clean Criterion

                    

Visual appearance of production surfaces is a direct measurement that verifies removal of residuals.

                    

The most common use of a visually clean criterion is to **supplement** swab and/or rinse testing — in such cases, it is common practice *not* to establish a quantitative visual limit.

                    

If visual examination is used **without** swab and rinse sampling, it is required to establish a quantitative visual limit for a residue on a specified surface under specified viewing conditions. This methodology is discussed in Section 7.7.3.

                    

                        

#### Conditions for Using Visually Clean WITHOUT Analytical Sampling

                        

                            
1. The quantitative visual limit must be **more stringent than the SAL carryover limit** (Section 5.5).

                            
2. Equipment surfaces must be viewable in the cleaning validation protocol under conditions that are **the same or more stringent** than the viewing conditions established for the quantitative limit.

                            
3. A **second-person verification** in protocol execution should be utilized.

                        

                    

                    

Typical visual limits reported in the literature: **1–4 µg/cm²**. However, this limit depends on conditions such as:

                    

                        
- Nature of the residue (color, contrast)

                        
- Nature of the surface (rough vs. polished)

                        
- Lighting conditions

                        
- Distance of viewing

                        
- Angle of viewing

                        
- Visual acuity of the operator

                    

                    

                        "The requirement for 'visual cleanness' usually applies to equipment surfaces. It is not necessarily a requirement that swabs be visually clean after a surface is swabbed, due to the fact that residue which is not visible on a larger surface may become 'visible' when concentrated on the smaller area of the swab head."

## Sampling |

                

Introduction to sampling strategy and method selection

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.0 Sampling

                    

                        This section covers the key sampling methods used in cleaning validation: visual inspection,
                        Rinse Sampling, Swab Sampling,
                        and Placebo Sampling. In addition, sampling considerations for
                        microbial and endotoxin analysis are discussed, along with
                        Recovery Studies and the training and qualification of samplers.
                    

                    

                        The selection of a sampling method should be based on the nature of the residue, the equipment
                        design, and the intended analytical method. No single method is universally superior; the best
                        approach is often a combination of methods.
                    

                    

                        Visual inspection is the most basic form of sampling and represents the minimum regulatory
                        expectation: the equipment must be visually clean when viewed with the **unaided eye**.
                        Use of magnification aids is considered more stringent.
                    

                    

                        Instrumental surface methods (e.g., ATR-FTIR probe via fiber-optic cable) allow in-situ
                        analysis without removing residue from the surface. These techniques do not require a separate
                        Sampling Recovery study but are limited by probe length and surface
                        geometry — many Worst-Case Locations cannot be reached.

## Rinse Sampling |

                

Grab samples, separate sampling rinses, and special cases

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.1.2 Rinse Sampling

                    

                        Rinse Sampling involves flowing solvent (water, aqueous solution,
                        organic solvent, or mixture) over all relevant equipment surfaces to remove residues, which are
                        then measured in the rinse solvent. Key considerations include solubility, location, timing,
                        and volume.
                    

                    

                        Two main approaches exist: (1) a **"grab" sample** taken from the final portion of
                        the rinse solvent during the final rinse of the cleaning process; (2) a
                        **separate sampling rinse** performed after completion of the process rinse,
                        which may involve filling the equipment with solvent, agitating for homogeneity, and then
                        analyzing a sample.

### 6.1.2.1 Extraction Rinse Sampling for Small Parts

                    

                        Small parts may be sampled by placing them in extraction solvent and agitating or sonicating
                        for a fixed time. For items with an orifice (e.g., filling needles), a fixed volume of
                        sampling solution is passed through the lumen and analyzed. Because the surface area and
                        sampling volume are precisely known, limits can be accurately calculated.
                    

                    

### 6.1.2.2 Solvent Reflux Sampling

                    

                        In organic solvent reflux sampling, volatile organic solvent is heated to vaporize it. The
                        vapors condense on upper parts of the manufacturing equipment, dissolve soluble residues, and
                        carry them back to the reactor. Although the distribution mechanism differs, the principles
                        of rinse sampling still apply.

## Swab & Wipe Sampling |

                

Direct surface sampling for worst-case locations

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.1.3 Swab and Wipe Sampling

                    

                        Swab Sampling and wipe sampling involve wiping a surface with a
                        fibrous material to transfer residue, which is then extracted into a solvent for analysis.
                        Swabs consist of textile (knitted, woven, or nonwoven) attached to a plastic handle. Wipes are
                        larger fibrous materials applied by hand. Cotton balls or pads moved across a surface with
                        forceps are a special case.
                    

                    

                        The selection of swab or wipe requires evaluation of **extractables** and
                        **shedding properties**. Recovery of residues depends on the size and shape of the
                        swab head or wipe, as well as the properties of the swab handle (flexibility, length).
                    

                    

                        In most cases, swabs and wipes are **wetted with a solvent** prior to sampling.
                        The solvent should assist in dissolving the residue and be compatible with the analytical method
                        (e.g., mobile phase for HPLC; water for TOC and conductivity). Multiple swabs or wipes may be
                        used on the same site to increase recovery; additional swabs may be dry or wetted.

## Placebo Sampling |

                

Demonstrating absence of carryover to the next product

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.2 Placebo Sampling

                    

                        Placebo Sampling can be used to detect residues on equipment
                        through the processing of a placebo batch subsequent to the cleaning process. It is used
                        primarily to demonstrate the **lack of carryover to the next product**. The
                        placebo should mimic product attributes.
                    

                    

                        Placebo sampling may also be called **"mock runs"** or **"blank runs"**.
                        In biotechnology, this generally involves processing only with water — which is different from
                        rinse sampling in that the water is processed through the equipment much as the product would be.
                    

                    

                        In the sampling process, the equipment is first cleaned. Following cleaning, a manufacturing
                        process is performed (to the extent feasible) using only a placebo product. The placebo product
                        is then evaluated for residues as for any other cleaning validation sample.
                    

                    

                        Placebo runs can demonstrate actual carryover to the processed material, but if done, are
                        typically used to **complement swab/wipe and/or rinse sampling** rather than
                        replace them. The equipment characteristics also impact the choice of the placebo batch size.
                        Placebo sampling may present **analytical challenges** for measuring residues in a
                        true placebo (excipients may interfere with the analytical method).

Special considerations for bioburden and LAL testing

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.3 Sampling for Microbial and Endotoxin Analysis

                    

                        Sampling for **bioburden** may involve rinse-water sampling and/or swabbing, and
                        may also involve **contact plates**. For swabbing, a sterile solution such as
                        **phosphate-buffered saline (PBS)** should be used. For rinse sampling of large
                        equipment, use of sterile water is generally not practical; purified water or
                        **WFI** is used, and results may be compared to a blank taken from the same
                        use point.
                    

                    

                        Rinse-water sampling for bioburden should use **sterile sample containers**.
                        "Aseptic" sampling technique — much like that used for cleanroom bioburden sampling — is
                        required for any microbial method to avoid external contamination of the sample.
                    

                    

                        Sampling for **endotoxin** is almost always a rinse water sample, preferably
                        with **low endotoxin water**.

## Additional Considerations |

                

SOPs, material compatibility, and sample order

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.4 Additional Considerations

                    

                        It is preferred to have a **separate sampling SOP** (apart from any special
                        instructions in a cleaning validation protocol). This helps prevent **"procedure drift"**
                        — which might occur if the swabbing procedure text is repeated in every protocol. A separate
                        SOP also ensures that the same sampling procedure is used in recovery studies as in protocol
                        execution, simplifying training.
                    

                    

                        The rinse sampling procedure may be the same procedure used for sampling water systems,
                        appropriately modified to cover sampling of process equipment.
                    

                    

                        In selecting sampling techniques, considerations should include:
                    

                    

                        
- Compatibility of sampling materials (vials, swabs, sampling solutions) with each other, with the residue, and with the analytical method.

                        
- Any requirement for cleaning or removing sampling materials from the sampled surface after sampling must be addressed in the design and selection of sampling methods.

                    

                    

                        Finally, in taking samples in a protocol, consideration should be given to the
                        **impact of a given sample on subsequent samples**. This includes the
                        **order** in which samples are taken — considering both the type of sampling
                        method (visual, rinse, swab) and the type of residue (active, cleaning agent, bioburden,
                        endotoxin).

## Sampling Recovery Studies |

                

Swab, rinse, visual, bioburden, and endotoxin recovery

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.5 Sampling Recovery Studies

                    

                        Recovery Studies are generally required to demonstrate that a
                        residue, if present on equipment surfaces, can be adequately measured by the combination of
                        the analytical method and the sampling procedure. These studies provide a scientific basis
                        for utilizing sampling and analytical methods to measure residues. The objective is to
                        establish a **reproducible level of recovery** from equipment surfaces.
                    

                    

                        Recovery studies may be performed as part of analytical method validation or as separate
                        studies. They are laboratory studies involving **coupons** of sampled equipment
                        of different materials of construction (stainless steel, glass, PTFE, EPDM) spiked with
                        residues to be measured.
                    

                    

### 6.5.1 General Considerations

                    

                        Recovery studies may not be required for residues that are known to be **readily
                        soluble** (as defined in USP or Merck Index, used well below the solubility limit,
                        such as NaOH or H₃PO₄ used as cleaning agents), provided they are not reactive with or
                        absorbed into the surface.
                    

                    

                        The amount of material spiked onto coupons should represent an amount equal to what could be
                        present at the residue limit. Spiked levels at extremely low
                        levels may give lower recovery percentages due to analytical method variability at those
                        levels.
                    

                    

                        The spiked residue should represent the same residue present at the end of the cleaning
                        process. In practice, it is common to only spike the active ingredient for active recovery
                        studies, and only spike the cleaning agent for cleaning agent recovery studies. Drying and/or
                        holding times of spiked coupons should be appropriate for the nature of the residue.
                    

                    

                        If the active degrades during cleaning, recovery studies are typically performed by spiking
                        with the active ingredient itself, unless degradation products have significantly different
                        recovery levels or unusual safety/solubility concerns.
                    

                    

                        A **grouping or family approach** may be used for recovery studies — performing
                        one residue study on a surface documented to be equivalent (in terms of percent recovery) to
                        other surfaces. Surfaces constituting less than 1–2% of total equipment surface area may be
                        excluded from formal recovery studies, using the lowest recovery of any other surface type,
                        or the company's minimum acceptable recovery percentage.
                    

                    

### 6.5.2 Swab/Wipe Recovery

                    

                        Coupons are spiked in a controlled manner, allowed to dry, and sampled with the swabbing
                        procedure. The amount recovered is compared to the amount spiked and expressed as
                        percent recovery.
                    

                    

                        Because swabbing is a manual procedure, each person typically performs a recovery study with
                        **three replicates**. It is preferable to have at least **two people**
                        perform swabbing recovery studies for each combination of residue and surface type.
                    

                    

                        Acceptable recovery criteria:
                    

                    

                        
- **≥70%** — required when recovery is used to qualify the sampling method without correcting a limit or analytical result

                        
- **≥50%** — required when the recovery percentage is used to correct a residue limit or analytical result

                        
- **<50%** — requires a written rationale for why that percentage is appropriate

                        
- An **upper limit** for percent recovery should be established (to address recoveries >100%)

                        
- Acceptable variation within one operator: typically **15–30% RSD**

                    

                    

                        Recovery studies are typically performed on a nominal coupon surface area of
                        **25 cm² or 100 cm²**. A range of acceptable surface area (e.g., 25% to 150%
                        of nominal) should be established. If the sampled area varies from nominal, the residue
                        limit should be adjusted based on the actual surface area swabbed.
                    

                    

### 6.5.3 Rinse Recovery

                    

                        Rinse recovery studies demonstrate that residues on a surface would be effectively removed
                        and could be analyzed in the rinse solution. They address the FDA "dirty pot" and
                        "baby/bath water" analogies. Studies are performed on spiked coupons; exact rinsing
                        conditions cannot be fully duplicated in the laboratory, but should be simulated.
                    

                    

                        The ratio of **solvent to sampled surface area** should be the same or lower in
                        the recovery study as compared to the equipment rinsing situation (worst case).
                        Since laboratory rinse sampling studies are generally **not operator dependent**,
                        three replicates by one operator may be adequate.
                    

                    

### 6.5.4 "Recovery" in Visual Inspection

                    

                        This is actually the determination of a quantitative **visual detection limit**.
                        Coupons are spiked at different levels (in μg/cm²) and a panel of trained observers
                        determines the lowest level at which residues are clearly visible. Typical values reported
                        in the literature for a visual detection limit are **1–4 μg/cm²**. A percent
                        recovery is not established; the purpose is to establish the value below which any
                        visually clean surface clearly lies.
                    

                    

### 6.5.5 Recovery for Bioburden and Endotoxin Sampling

                    

                        Percentage recovery studies are **not normally performed** for microbiological
                        sampling. Four reasons:
                    

                    

                        
1. Microbiological tests count **colony forming units (CFU)**, not individual organisms — enumeration is inherently different from chemical quantification.

                        
2. Vegetative organisms will **die or lose viability** when dried on a coupon in a standard recovery procedure.

                        
3. It is unclear which **species** should be used for a recovery study.

                        
4. Bioburden limits are typically set significantly below levels that could cause product quality issues, so even low recovery does not impact safety conclusions.

                    

                    

                        Endotoxin recovery studies from surfaces are also **not ordinarily performed**.
                        Standard endotoxin from LAL test kit suppliers may not be indicative of endogenic endotoxins
                        from a manufacturing process. The cleaning process itself is very effective at physically
                        removing endotoxin along with other manufacturing soils.

## Training & Qualification of Samplers |

                

Swab, rinse, and visual inspection training requirements

            

        

        

            

                
                

                    

### 6.6 Training and Qualification of Samplers

                    

                        **Training** involves the steps taken to assist the prospective sampler in
                        learning the technique. **Qualification** involves the process of "certifying"
                        that the prospective sampler can appropriately sample. For this section, "sampling" and
                        "sampler" also include "inspection" and "inspector" for visual evaluation.
                    

                    

                        **Training always precedes qualification.** At a minimum, training involves:
                    

                    

                        
1. Reading of the sampling procedure

                        
2. Demonstration of the correct procedure by a trained sampler (including rationale for certain practices)

                        
3. Demonstration of correct procedure by the *prospective* sampler

                    

                    

                        Demonstration of technique may also utilize a **visual indicator** on the swabbed
                        surface to help the trainee see the consequences of poor technique.
                    

                    

                        Qualification processes will depend on the type of sampling performed. Qualification may involve:
                    

                    

                        
- Demonstration of correct technique (last step of training), or

                        
- A "test" that challenges the trainee's ability to perform correctly (e.g., swab sampling for a known soil residue level on coupons; visual inspection using an array of coupons where some are soiled and others are not)

                    

                    

                        Qualification may be repeated on a regular basis or upon any retraining. Retraining may be
                        conducted based on suspected operator error, or because an operator has not performed a
                        swabbing event over a certain time frame.
                    

                    

### 6.6.1 Key Issues for Training for Swab Sampling

                    

                        Four keys to consistency in swab sampling training:
                    

                    

                        
1. **Consistency of wetting the swab head**

                        
2. **Consistency of the swabbing motion** (including overlapping strokes)

                        
3. **Consistency in applied pressure**

                        
4. **Consistency in swabbing of the correct surface area**

                    

                    

                        A **fifth factor** for some types (e.g., TOC analysis): preventing external
                        contamination of the swab from volatile organics in the atmosphere around the sampling
                        location.
                    

                    

                        Because swab sampling depends heavily on operator consistency, consideration should be given
                        to have swab samplers **retrained and/or requalified on an established basis**.
                        Retraining and requalification should also be addressed as part of change control for the
                        swabbing procedure, and when swab sampling "operator error" is suspected in the
                        investigation of a nonconforming result.
                    

                    

### 6.6.2 Key Issues for Training for Rinse Sampling

                    

                        The major concern for accuracy in rinse samples is to **prevent contamination of the
                        rinse sample**. Sources of contamination include the sampling port, the environment
                        around the port, and the operator. Prevention steps include:
                    

                    

                        
- Adequately flushing or cleaning the port prior to taking a sample

                        
- Avoiding isopropanol on gloves or for cleaning the port when TOC is the analytical procedure

                    

                    

                        **Timing** is critical for grab sampling from the final CIP rinse. Once process
                        rinsing is complete, there is no way to go back and collect a rinse sample. The last portion
                        of the rinse is typically sampled, but it may be acceptable to sample earlier if it represents
                        a worst case.
                    

                    

                        Since rinse sampling consistency is **less operator dependent**, there may be no
                        need for routine retraining and/or requalification; however, the need should be addressed
                        in change control and when operator error is suspected.

## 7.0 Analytical Methods — Purpose & Context

                

Analytical methods are a critical component of any cleaning validation program. Their purpose is to demonstrate that residues of active ingredients, excipients, cleaning agents, and degradation products have been reduced to acceptable levels after a cleaning process.

                

The selection of an appropriate analytical method depends on the nature of the residue, the stage of cleaning validation (design/development, qualification, or validation maintenance), the sensitivity required relative to acceptance limits, and practical considerations such as availability of instrumentation and speed of analysis.

                

Where existing in-house methods are adequate for the intended purpose, those methods should be used rather than developing new methodologies. This is consistent with sound scientific practice and minimizes unnecessary investment. For example, many companies used the readily oxidizable substances pharmacopeial method for cleaning validation until TOC (Total Organic Carbon) replaced it — at which point companies became familiar and comfortable with the new technology.

                

On the other hand, if a new analytical method is required because existing in-house methods are not adequate, then that new method should be considered. These may be implemented by using contract analytical laboratories or by bringing the new analytical methodology in-house. A decision on bringing the method in-house versus using a contract laboratory may be based on business considerations.

## 7.3 Specific vs. Nonspecific Analytical Methods

                

Specific Analytical Method methods are those which measure a certain residue in the presence of expected interferences. If the target analyte in a validation protocol is the active ingredient, such interferences may include degradation products, related substances, excipients, cleaning agents, and cleaning process by-products.

                

Examples of specific methods include liquid chromatography (including HPLC, UPLC and TLC) and spectrophotometry (including UV Spectrophotometry, visible and infrared). Each of these methods requires the use of an appropriate reference standard.

                

In contrast, Nonspecific analytical methods measure a general property, such as Conductivity or TOC (Total Organic Carbon), which could be due to a variety of analytes or sources.

                

Selection of an analytical method may depend on the nature of the residue as it exists *after* the cleaning process. Only if an active ingredient is not degraded during cleaning does it make sense to use a specific analytical method for that active ingredient. If a cleaning process degrades the active ingredient, a specific method would likely show the residue as nondetectable — yet degradants may still be present. In such a case, use of a specific method for the degradant, or use of a nonspecific method such as TOC, should be considered.

                

It should be recognized that the proper use of a nonspecific analytical method may provide a more robust demonstration of acceptable cleaning, because it may have responses from species other than the target residue — yet those responses must be assumed to be due to the target residue. However, exceeding the residue limit using a nonspecific method provides no information on the nature of the failure.

                

                    "Nothing in this Technical Report should be interpreted as saying that, as a general principle, specific analytical methods should be used in preference to nonspecific analytical methods."
                

                

### 7.3.1 Regulatory Status of Specific and Nonspecific Methods

                

Both specific and nonspecific methods have been found acceptable by regulatory authorities. However, one must be careful not to misuse an analytical method. Specific methods can be misused by failing to recognize degradation of the active ingredient in the cleaning process. Nonspecific methods can be misused by failing to attribute the nonspecific response entirely to the residue of concern.

                

The U.S. FDA guidance states that one should "Determine the specificity and sensitivity of the analytical method used to detect residuals or contaminants." The European PIC/S recommendations state that analytical methods "should be specific for the substance to be assayed." Despite these wordings, nonspecific methods are widely used and accepted by both U.S. FDA and European regulatory authorities.

## 7.4 Most Commonly Used Analytical Techniques

                

The focus of this section is the most commonly used analytical procedures in pharmaceutical cleaning validation. Additional considerations in selecting methods:

                

                    
- Availability of instrumentation

                    
- Speed of analysis

                    
- Specificity of technique

                    
- Sampling limitations (including sampling solvents)

                    
- Detection/quantitation limit

                    
- Linearity of response

                    
- Online adaptability

                    
- Cost

                

                

Most applications involve quantitation over a validated range. However, pass-fail ("go-no go") tests may be used in some situations, such as qualification runs for clinical manufacture, or routine monitoring based on final rinse solvent testing. Pass-fail tests generally do not demonstrate the robustness of the cleaning process unless the pass-fail point is significantly below the desired acceptance limit.

                

### 7.4.1 Liquid Chromatography (LC)

                

LC includes HPLC (High Performance Liquid Chromatography), UPLC (Ultra Performance Liquid Chromatography), and TLC (Thin Layer Chromatography). All these methods involve the separation of components by a chromatography procedure followed by measurement of one or more separated species. For HPLC and UPLC, measurement is typically by ultraviolet (UV) detectors.

                

HPLC and UPLC methods are typically specific methods, widely used for measurement of active ingredients in small-molecule manufacturing (both API and drug product). In many cases, HPLC/UPLC methods have been previously developed as potency assay methods and need only minor modification for residue determination. These modifications confirm that the useful range is suitable and that cleaning process interferences do not interfere with measurement of the active ingredient.

                

HPLC/UPLC may not be suitable for measuring residues of an active ingredient if the active ingredient is degraded in the cleaning process, unless the chromatography conditions allow separation and measurement of degradants of interest.

                

TLC methods may be used for design/development to confirm and characterize degradation of the active, and for investigation to characterize residues.

                

### 7.4.2 UV/Visible Spectrophotometry (UV/Vis)

                

UV Spectrophotometry involves measuring transmission/absorbance of a specified wavelength of light by a solvent solution of the residue. It typically requires a chromophore in the molecule, although it is also possible to modify the residue to produce a chromophore. Because of its simplicity, UV/Vis may be used in the design/development, qualification, and validation maintenance stages as well as for any investigations. UV/Vis has also the possibility of being used in PAT applications for completion of the cleaning steps for small-molecule API manufacturing.

                

### 7.4.3 Total Organic Carbon (TOC)

                

TOC (Total Organic Carbon) is applicable to any residue containing significant amounts of organic carbon. The TOC method is based on oxidizing the carbon present and measuring the carbon dioxide produced. Oxidizing methods include UV, persulfate, and combustion. Techniques for measuring the generated CO₂ include conductivity, membrane-based conductivity, and infrared. Both online and offline applications are possible.

                

For use of TOC, the target residue must have adequate aqueous solubility. The most common way of applying the TOC method is to assume that all residues detected are due to the target residue (worst-case assumption). In manufacturing situations, TOC is commonly used when the target residue (e.g., the active ingredient) is degraded during the cleaning process. However, it may also be used where the active is not degraded — the rationale being ease of analytical method development and the worst-case assumptions inherent in TOC analysis.

                

TOC may be used for all stages of cleaning validation: design/development, qualification, and validation maintenance, as well as for investigations.

                

### 7.4.4 Conductivity

                

Conductivity measurement is a method to detect dissociated ionic substances in water samples. For qualification protocols, conductivity readings are expressed in micro-Siemens/cm (µS/cm); for control and monitoring of the cleaning solution, readings are expressed as milliSiemens/cm (mS/cm). It is often used to measure cleaning agent residues (e.g., caustic or acidic agents) and to control cleaning agent concentration in automated cleaning processes (CIP). Conductivity readings are highly influenced by sample temperature; temperature adjustment, automated temperature compensation, or a conductivity/concentration curve at a specified temperature can standardize measurements.

                

Conductivity is a nonspecific method that correlates linearly (within a defined range) to ion concentration. Analytical instruments are robust and can be used on the manufacturing floor by trained personnel. The method cannot differentiate between different ions. Therefore, as with TOC, all conductivity results above the water baseline should be attributed to the contaminant in question.

                

Conductivity is a good measure of the completion of rinsing and therefore an indirect measure of good cleaning for routine monitoring. Conductivity can also be used for measuring residues of an ionic active ingredient.

                

### 7.4.5 Organoleptic Evaluation

                

"Organoleptic" evaluation includes visual inspection as well as other evaluations such as smell. Visual inspection is a minimum requirement under GMPs for use of equipment for manufacture. It is a nonspecific method — the nature of the residue generally cannot be identified except by further analysis.

                

Training and a detailed documented procedure are required to ensure consistency. Factors affecting what can be seen include: distance, angle, lighting, nature of surface, and inspector's visual acuity. Some equipment surfaces (e.g., piping) are not accessible for visual inspection; optical equipment such as mirrors, remote videoscopes, or borescopes can help.

                

Four types of visual observations may be found: residue, surface anomalies, foreign object, and water. Residue is the main concern constituting a visual failure. Surface anomalies and foreign objects are not visual inspection failures for cleaning validation purposes but must be investigated. Rouge is the most common surface anomaly — a preventive maintenance problem, not a cleaning process problem.

## 7.5 Other Useful Analytical Techniques

                

Below are other techniques which may be useful for various stages of cleaning validation.

                

### 7.5.1 pH

                

pH is a measure of hydrogen ion concentration. It can be used as a monitoring process check, particularly when equipment is stored wet in a preservative solution. pH can also verify qualitatively the presence of the correct cleaning solution and can complement conductivity measurements. However, pH is less useful than conductivity for measuring residues of alkaline or acidic cleaning solutions because pH has a logarithmic relation with hydrogen ion concentration, whereas conductivity has a direct, linear relationship.

                

### 7.5.2 Infrared (IR)

                

This includes both FTIR (Fourier Transform InfraRed) and NIR (Near InfraRed). Most useful in an investigation to identify organic residues. FTIR has also been combined with a fiber-optic probe for direct quantitative measurement of residues on surfaces for qualification protocols.

                

### 7.5.3 Light Microscopy

                

Including Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM). In many cases, conventional light microscopy and SEM can be combined with x-ray diffraction, mass spectrometry, and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR). Microscopic techniques alone may identify the physical nature of a residue but not the chemical nature. Especially valuable in evaluation of residues in an investigation, and for identifying unknown contaminants on new or used equipment.

                

### 7.5.4 Titrations

                

Titration is a simple method often overlooked even though it might provide valuable information. Titrations may be specific (orthophosphate ions) or nonspecific (e.g., for all anionic surfactants). More likely to be used for alkaline or acidic cleaning agent analysis in qualification runs.

                

### 7.5.5 Gravimetric Analysis

                

Useful for design/development studies and qualification runs. Most commonly used for determining residues in small-molecule API synthesis where a larger volume of solvent rinse or solvent reflux is evaporated to dryness.

                

### 7.5.6 ELISA (Enzyme Linked Immunosorbant Assay)

                

An antigen-antibody type reaction involving specific chemicals developed for the residue involved. Use is generally limited to biotechnology and biologics manufacture where it can be used in the design/development stage to confirm degradation of the active ingredient and in any investigations.

                

### 7.5.7 Capillary Zone Electrophoresis (CZE)

                

Also known as capillary electrophoresis (CE). Separates residues by charge and frictional forces in an electrical field. Detection is usually with a fluorescence detector. Applied mostly in the biotechnology industry for active ingredients and degraded active ingredients in design/development and qualification stages as well as in investigations.

                

### 7.5.8 Atomic Absorption (AA) and Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP)

                

Both techniques can be used for measuring metals in solution, where the metal is part of a formulation or for unknown residues such as suspected rouge.

                

### 7.5.9 Ion Mobility Spectrometry (IMS)

                

A type of mass spectrometry which only provides information on the time of flight of the analyzed species. Promoted for its short analysis time (a few minutes). May have more application for routine monitoring and release.

## 7.6 Microbial Test Methods

                

The FDA guidance states that "Control of the bioburden through adequate cleaning and storage of equipment is important to ensure that subsequent sterilization or sanitization procedures achieve the necessary assurance of sterility." The PIC/S recommendations call for validation of cleaning procedures for removal of microbial contaminants. Control of microbial residues — including bioburden and endotoxin — is thus an important part of cleaning validation.

                

Typically, bioburden sampling and analysis is performed during cleaning validation protocols unless there is a documented science- and risk-rationale for omitting it. Rationales for excluding microbiological testing may include: all solvent processing for small-molecule API manufacture, use of a final alcohol rinse for oral dose drug products, use of subsequent sterilization cycles, and/or demonstration of adequate microbial control in sufficiently similar cleaning processes.

                

### 7.6.1 Endotoxin

                

Typically, endotoxin testing is performed for cleaning validation runs if the next product has endotoxin specifications. Endotoxin analytical methods are typically compendial methods. Science- and risk-based rationales for excluding endotoxin testing may include: all solvent processing, use of a validated endotoxin reduction step, and/or demonstration of adequate endotoxin control in sufficiently similar cleaning processes.

                

### 7.6.2 Bioburden

                

Testing of bioburden is done through rinse-water sampling, swab sampling, and contact plate sampling.

                

**Rinse-water sampling** typically involves membrane filtration, placement of the membrane on an appropriate agar, incubation, and a count of CFUs. It provides an overall picture of equipment cleanliness. The biggest weakness is that the full range of the acceptance criteria cannot always be utilized — for example, if 100 mL of rinse water is used with an acceptance criterion of 100 CFU/mL, typical TNTC (Too Numerous To Count) occurs at 300 colonies, limiting the effective acceptance criterion to 3 CFU/mL.

                

**Swab and contact plate** methods directly measure on surfaces. For swab samples, the swab can be desorbed and counted by pour-plate or spread-plate method. Contact plates are directly incubated and enumerated. A concern with both is potentially exposing product contact surfaces to unknown media or buffer from the swab; acceptable removal must be demonstrated. Contact plates also require flat surfaces.

                

Most companies use incubation-based techniques counting CFUs. Such procedures have the disadvantage of only providing a count, not individual cells. Enumeration below 20 CFU is not scientifically well established. Rapid instrumental microbiological procedures are an alternative (see PDA Technical Report 33).

## 7.7 Analytical Method Validation

                

This section focuses on analytical Method Validation for "chemical" residues. Endotoxin methods are typically compendial and do not require formal validation, but require a confirmation of suitability for their application. Approved microbiology lab methods do not require additional method validation.

                

### 7.7.1 General Principles

                

Since one key part of cleaning validation is setting residue limits and then measuring actual residues left on surfaces after cleaning, it is critical that the analytical method be appropriately validated. Method validation is typically accomplished using the criteria in ICH Q2(R1). However, the types of assays listed in ICH Q2 do not explicitly cover cleaning validation methods.

                

One approach is to essentially validate analytical methods like an "Assay" in ICH Q2, establishing accuracy, precision, specificity, linearity and range, with added determination of LOD (Limit of Detection) and LOQ (Limit of Quantitation). LOD/LOQ must be below the acceptance limit for the sample, and ideally significantly below so that the robustness of the cleaning process can be established. Having LOD/LOQ of 10% or less of the residue limit in the analytical sample is ideal (but not always possible).

                

In addition to the ICH Q2 parameters, sample stability as a function of storage conditions (time, temperature, storage vial, etc.) may be evaluated if there is a significant interval between sampling and analysis. Specific methods should address possible interferences from other species, such as cleaning agents.

                

In cases where a nonspecific method is utilized, it is not necessary to compensate for the lack of specificity by "other supporting analytical procedures" as suggested in ICH Q2 — because for cleaning validation purposes, the limit is not a target but a value not to be exceeded. As long as other species contribute to the response in a positive manner, the total measured value should be attributed to the target residue.

                

When performing carryover calculations, analytical methods must be sensitive enough to meet the acceptance criteria. Results should be at or above the LOQ to be reliable. Results between the LOD and LOQ show higher-than-acceptable variation and are typically reported as less than LOQ.

                

For pass/fail methods, analytical method validation is less extensive — accuracy and precision are performed only at the residue limit; linearity and range are not performed. Pass/fail methods do not allow collection of data to support a process capability determination for action or alert levels.

                

Acceptability of variability for accuracy and precision at typical residue levels is much broader than for a typical potency assay. Relative standard deviation (RSD) requirements of 15–20% are typical.

                

### 7.7.2 Compendial Methods

                

Compendial methods do not require separate analytical method validation provided those methods are used within the parameters in the compendia. For example, a compendial method for endotoxin is generally appropriate for measuring endotoxin in final rinse water samples. However, suitability of use should always be addressed.

                

When using TOC in rinse-water samples (a compendial method), additional work should be done to support its applicability if TOC values could be above 500 ppb or a linear range is to be established. The USP TOC method is set up essentially as a pass/fail test, not a quantitative assay — so for cleaning validation purposes, formal method validation as for any other method should be considered.

                

### 7.7.3 Visual Inspection

                

Method validation for visual inspection is the determination of a quantitative "visual limit" where visual examination is the sole sampling/analytical method. If visual examination is used to supplement swab or rinse sampling, such determination of a visual limit is not required.

                

A visual limit under specified viewing conditions can be determined by spiking coupons of equipment surface materials with solutions of the residue at different levels (in µg/cm²) and having a panel of trained observers determine the lowest level at which residues are clearly visible. Appropriate viewing conditions include distance, lighting, and angle.

                

### 7.7.4 Bioburden Methods

                

Approved and qualified microbiological lab procedures do not require additional method validation for use in cleaning validation programs. However, suitability for use of such methods in the presence of cleaning process chemicals should be addressed.

                

### 7.7.5 Transfer to Another Laboratory and Use of Contract Laboratories

                

Other laboratories can be utilized to perform an analytical method for cleaning validation purposes. In such cases, a method transfer protocol should be established and executed to determine that the other laboratory can suitably analyze samples. If a method is developed by a contract laboratory and qualification run samples are analyzed by that same contract laboratory, then no transfer protocol is required.

                

Care should be used in the transfer protocol to first determine whether the measurements between the two laboratories are practically significant before any determination of statistical significance is performed. If an analytical method has been developed and validated previously by the contract laboratory, the pharmaceutical company should review that protocol, the final report, and perform an audit of the contract laboratory.

### 8.2 Critical Parameter Measurement & Alarms

                        

Process parameters and/or events are typically based on a quality risk approach but there may be alarms based on business or safety concerns. In an automated cleaning cycle, alarms may be based on a variety of parameters, such as temperature of the wash and rinse solutions, online analytical results of the recirculating wash solution, pressure at the spray device, flow through various circuits, and online analytical results of the final rinse. These are typically automated alarms, in which a light flashes, a buzzer sounds, or the cleaning process is aborted, with the generation of a failure record.

                        

When using measurement probes for alarm purposes, the device should have appropriate accuracy and should be maintained in current calibration. There may also be other "nonautomated" alarms, in which observations by an operator trigger a response (e.g., visual observation by an operator that a cleaning detergent drum is empty).

                        

There are a variety of approaches to cleaning the equipment on which an alarm occurred. The cause of the alarm should be investigated. This may be done as part of a Corrective and Preventative Action (CAPA) program. One strategy is that on specified alarm conditions, the cleaning cycle may be restarted. For example, if inadequate cleaning agent concentration occurred (as indicated by an alarm on the wash cycle conductivity), the cleaning cycle can be restarted from the beginning after appropriate actions are taken.

                        

This is a conservative approach and ensures a complete cleaning cycle is performed, but care should be taken that alarms are noted and trended to ensure cycle performance is not trending towards being ineffective and to better correct repetitive problems. Alternately, the step in which the alarm occurs may be restarted. This approach strikes a balance between ensuring cycle performance and minimizing cleaning time.

                        

In all cases, it should be ensured that cycles performed during validation are not "best case" due to alarm conditions. For example, if equipment is soiled and during the validation runs of the cleaning cycle, alarms occur that result in multiple additional rinse steps being completed, this cycle may no longer be representative or worst case but may be a best case.

### 8.3 Change Control |

                        

A change control system is critical for ensuring maintenance of the validated state for cleaning processes. The change control system should cover all key parameters and components of the cleaning system to ensure that all changes with a potential to impact maintenance of the validated state are evaluated. This includes not only changes in the cleaning process but also changes in equipment and changes in the manufacturing process that might affect the performance of the validated cleaning process. Quality preapproval and tracking of changes are key requirements for this system.

                        

The change control system should provide for a review of each change by an interdisciplinary team. This should include a review of current validation for the equipment being changed, and depending on the nature of the change, may result in laboratory, pilot scale and/or commercial scale evaluations. Significantly major changes may result in the decision that the new cleaning process requires separate validation as a new process.

                        

There are some important considerations for designing the test plan to verify changes:

                        

                            
- **First:** Control parameters should stay within their validated ranges. If changes are made to extend or widen a validated range, an evaluation should determine the nature and extent of testing necessary to change that range.

                            
- **Second:** The acceptance criteria for analytical methods should remain unchanged from the previous validation unless there is a justified reason for the difference.

                            
- **Third:** Reduced sample sites and/or fewer analytical methods may be appropriate in many cases to confirm validation maintenance based on a change.

### 8.4 Routine Monitoring |

                        

Another tool for ensuring maintenance of the validated state is a risk-based routine monitoring program. A routine monitoring program may provide analytical data to be trended (see Section 8.5 below), such as by Statistical Process Control (SPC). In most cases involving automated processes, the data are provided by the automated equipment itself. For example, data may be generated by the CIP skid on wash-solution conductivity, final rinse conductivity, temperatures, times, flow rates and pressure.

                        

In other cases, separate sampling may be established for data collection, such as rinse analysis by UV/Vis, HPLC, or TOC. Visual examination after each cleaning process is another type of routine monitoring. Visual inspection after each cleaning process typically does not involve disassembly of equipment solely for the purpose of that inspection.

                        

A documented risk-based approach should be used to optimize compliance in an efficient manner. This could include leveraging family or grouping approaches, reduced sample sites and reduced analytical methods. When defining these approaches, the inherent risk associated with a given cleaning process and historical experience/data should be considered.

                        

For example, during initial validation, residues of an active ingredient may be measured via a variety of swab and rinse samples. However, with the proper data analysis, it may be appropriate to measure using only rinse sampling during routine monitoring. However, it may be appropriate for cleaning of highly hazardous drug active ingredients to include more sampling for residues as part of routine monitoring.

### 8.5 Data Trending and Review |

                        

Trending of cleaning cycle performance, analytical data from routine monitoring, and alarms are another recommendation to ensure continued cleaning cycle performance. Data that is trended can be continuous data (such as final rinse water analysis) or discrete data ("yes/no" data such as occurrence of an alarm).

                        

When trending any of these data sets, procedures should be in place to initiate an investigation when adverse trends are observed even if ineffective cleaning cycles have not occurred. Trending of cleaning cycle performance data is important for identifying potential cleaning cycle issues before they result in ineffective cleaning cycles.

                        

For example, a slowly increasing trend in the final rinse analytical result may not be indicative of an ineffective cleaning process. However, such a trend should require an investigation of the cause. In the example given, it may be that the spray device is becoming clogged, in which case it should be cleaned, and appropriate steps should be taken to prevent clogging in the future. On the other hand, it may be a result of a fouled sensor, such as a conductivity sensor.

                        

For data trending, there should be appropriate criteria established for action and/or alert levels. It is advisable to obtain guidance from a statistician to determine the appropriate number of data points necessary to obtain a statistically relevant data set. These values are typically less than any pass/fail acceptance criteria established for the qualification runs.

                        

Statistical process capability studies, based on multiple (e.g., 20–25) data points, may be used to establish action/alert levels. Since such extensive data may not be available for initial commercial manufacture, data from development runs and/or sufficiently similar cleaning processes may be used to establish tentative action/alert levels.

### 8.6 Evaluation of Cumulative Changes |

                        

Review of the cumulative impact of changes on a system should be considered. Such a review may be initiated based on data/events from the cleaning process or may be time-based. One approach is to include a review of cumulative changes for every change control event.

                        

This review should provide evidence that the cleaning cycle continues to meet specified requirements despite multiple small changes, each of which was appropriately approved. It is possible that many minor changes (each deemed to have no impact on the validated state) could have an impact when considered in total.

                        

This review of cumulative changes may involve two approaches:

                        

                            
- **First:** A documented analysis (i.e., review of the changes and the impact these changes will have on other parts of the process) of the changes should be undertaken.

                            
- **Second:** Process performance and alarms should be monitored to ensure continued maintenance of the validated state and system performance.

### 8.7 Training |

                        

Training after the initial qualification runs should be done to help assure maintenance of the validated state. Types of training include:

                        

                            
- **Type 1:** Training on a procedure revised for either clarification or for a cleaning process change.

                            
- **Type 2:** Retraining of a previously trained operator because of suspected operator error.

                            
- **Type 3:** Retraining on a regular basis for manual cleaning processes — this may be done on a regular basis to avoid process "creep".

                            
- **Type 4:** Training of any new (previously untrained) operators.

                        

                        

Training should cover cleaning process operators, sampling personnel, and analytical personnel as applicable.

### 8.8 Periodic Review |

                        

As part of lifecycle validation, it is common practice to perform an overall periodic review of the validation state. The frequency of such a review will depend on a risk assessment. Such a review typically involves a review of data collected as described in Sections 8.1 through 8.7 above. In addition, it typically involves a review of any changed regulations as well as any change in common industry or inspectional practices that might be considered part of current Good Manufacturing Practice. This periodic review should be documented and should include a conclusion as to the validation status of the cleaning process.

                        

Historically, it was considered acceptable to perform periodic revalidation on cleaning processes in lieu of routine monitoring and periodic review. However, the approach of revalidation yields a much less robust picture of the state of control of the cleaning process and may be more resource-intensive. Revalidation as a concept is no longer used by some regulatory agencies because of a preference for a lifecycle validation approach.

                        

Under a lifecycle validation approach, a significant change in a cleaning process involves not the revalidation of the previous process, but rather validation of a new process. Such validation of a new process, however, may rely on data from the old process based on it being sufficiently similar.

### 9.1 Cleaning Validation Master Plans

                        

It is good practice to have a document or documents near the top of the cleaning validation documentation hierarchy that broadly define the expectations for a cleaning validation program. ### 9.2 Documentation for Design/Development

## 10.1 Cleaning Agents |

                

Selection factors, qualification, and CGMP requirements for cleaning agents

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.1.2 Factors in Selection

                    

A number of factors need to be considered when selecting cleaning agents for CGMP applications. These include:

                    

### 10.1.2.1 Broad Spectrum Effectiveness

                    

The cleaning agent should be effective at removing the residues that may range from single components to complex mixtures of various chemistries that constitute a product's active ingredients, excipients, degradants, and other contaminants. A broad-spectrum cleaner may also facilitate more effective grouping strategies.

                    

### 10.1.2.2 Substrate Compatibility

                    

The cleaning agent should be compatible with the various equipment substrate materials, such as stainless steel, polymers, glass, and soft metals.

                    

### 10.1.2.3 Stability and Shelf Life

                    

To ensure consistent performance after transportation and storage, cleaning agent stability and shelf life under those exposure conditions should be considered.

                    

### 10.1.2.4 Analyzability

                    

Cleaning agents should be analyzable and quantifiable down to the acceptance criteria established.

                    

### 10.1.2.5 Disposal

                    

Cleaning agents should meet the local waste water discharge requirements such as limits on pH, phosphates and heavy metals. When organic solvents are used, air emission requirements may need to be considered.

                    

### 10.1.2.6 Safety

                    

Particularly for cleaners used for manual cleaning applications, appropriate personal protective equipment may be required.

                    

### 10.1.2.7 Toxicity

                    

Cleaning agent toxicity is not only important for personnel safety during cleaning, but also is used in determining the residue limit and consequently cleaning process efficiency.

                    

### 10.1.2.8 Rinsability

                    

Cleaning agents should be free-rinsing. Cleaning agents that foam can be difficult to rinse and may also cause pump cavitation in CIP systems and COP washers.

                    

### 10.1.2.9 Quality

                    

Cleaning agents should have a specification, be lot-traceable, and preferably be manufactured using CGMP practices with appropriate change control policies.

## 10.2 Nonproduct Contact Surfaces |

                

Indirect contact surfaces, risk assessment, and validation scope

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.2 Nonproduct Contact Surfaces

                    

Nonproduct contact surfaces may be defined in different ways by manufacturers. For surfaces with no product contact (e.g., floors, walls, outsides of process equipment), there should be cleaning procedures. However, these cleaning processes are generally less critical and do not require cleaning validation. Cleaning for these nonproduct contact surfaces may be repeated in full or in part if the cleaning process results in visible and/or gross levels of residual soils.

                    

There are other nonproduct contact surfaces which may contact the product indirectly, such as by a vector or by an airborne route. These are sometimes called "indirect product contact surfaces". Examples of these types of surfaces might include lyophilizers, equipment used solely to manufacture and transfer buffers, media, and excipients, and stopper bowls.

                    

These indirect product contact surfaces should be included in the cleaning validation program. However, because of the limited impact of these indirect product contact surfaces, requirements for cleaning validation, such as limits, may be different from cleaning validation for direct product contact surfaces. A risk assessment should be utilized to define the requirements, which will depend on the specifics of the manufacturing situation.

                    

For example, for highly hazardous drug active ingredients, cleaning validation of nonproduct contact surfaces may be performed in order to document any potential of airborne transfer to another product as well as for operator safety reasons.

                    

*See Section 10.9 for information related to secondary packaging equipment surfaces.*

## 10.3 Process Analytical Technology (PAT) |

                

Using timely measurements to control and monitor cleaning processes

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.3 Process Analytical Technology

                    

Process Analytical Technology (PAT) is defined by the U.S. FDA as "a system for designing, analyzing, and controlling manufacturing through timely measurements (i.e. during processing) of critical quality and performance attributes of raw and in-process materials and processes, with the goal of ensuring final product quality." The use of a PAT approach may replace traditional validation approaches.

                    

### 10.3.1 Timely Measurements

                    

"Timely measurements" have long been used in cleaning processes to assist in the design of rinse cycle times in automated CIP systems. For example, measuring conductivity of the final rinse as a function of rinse time can determine the minimum time to consistently complete the rinsing process. While such a study in the design phase is appropriate for a PAT application, unless it combines the timely measurement with a feedback mechanism to control the cleaning process during commercial cleaning processes, it would not be considered to be a PAT.

                    

### 10.3.2 PAT for Cleaning Process Control

                    

The more relevant use of PAT for cleaning processes is the use of a timely measurement to define the completion of a cleaning process. In conductivity monitoring: the rinse time is not fixed but variable depending on the time needed to achieve a predetermined conductivity value. The U.S. FDA PAT guidance states: *"Within the PAT framework, a process end point is not a fixed time; rather it is the achievement of the desired material attributes."*

                    

Another example is in the use of organic solvent cleaning in small-molecule API synthesis, where online UV spectroscopy measures the active ingredient in the solvent, and the achievement of a low absorbance value determines process completion.

                    

### 10.3.3 Additional Considerations for Online Measurements

                    

Online methods by themselves do not necessarily constitute PAT. Online measurements (such as UV spectroscopy or conductivity) of a final rinse can be a routine monitoring tool without controlling a process step. Such online measurements, even though they don't control process completion, may be used as a means of cleaning verification after each cleaning event.

## 10.4 Clean Hold Considerations |

                

Storing cleaned equipment safely; microbial control during hold periods

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.4 Clean Hold Considerations

                    

Following cleaning, equipment that is to be reused should be stored in a manner to protect it from contamination during storage. Clean hold time is the time from the end of cleaning until subsequent use of the equipment, which may be product manufacture or may be a steam-in-place (SIP) cycle.

                    

"Clean hold time" is different from "dirty hold time" in that dirty hold time should be evaluated in the basic cleaning validation protocol as a worst-case condition or challenge. Clean hold time may be included as a second part of the basic cleaning validation protocol or may be considered as a separate protocol.

                    

The major concern with the clean hold time is the possibility of recontamination from external sources and the possibility of **microbial proliferation** because the equipment is wet with water during the clean hold period. If the microorganisms that proliferate are Gram-negative bacteria then issues with endotoxin may also arise.

                    

### Storage Strategies

                    

The best procedures are to store cleaned equipment in a **dry state** or in a **solution that inhibits microbial proliferation**. If equipment is stored in a dry state and protected from external contamination (e.g., by sealing the equipment or covering openings with GMP covers), formal studies to demonstrate lack of microbial proliferation may not be necessary.

                    

If stored in an inhibiting solution (such as dilute caustic), the solution should be known to inhibit microbial growth or data should be developed to demonstrate inhibition. Procedures should be in place to adequately remove that inhibiting solution from equipment prior to use.

                    

### When Validated Clean Hold Is Exceeded

                    

If clean hold validation is not performed, or if the validated clean hold time is exceeded, a validated water (usually hot purified water or WFI) flush may be used before sterilization, sanitization, and/or use. After the water flush, bioburden (and optionally endotoxin) levels are measured to ensure that levels would not challenge sterilization or exceed in-process specifications.

                    

Criteria used to determine acceptability after storage include: lack of microbial proliferation, endotoxin level, and visual examination.

## 10.5 New and Used Equipment |

                

Integrating new or used equipment into an established cleaning validation program

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.5.1 New Equipment

                    

When adding new equipment to a cleaning program, key considerations include:

                    

                        
- **10.5.1.1 Cleaning Procedure Development:** If sufficiently similar to existing equipment, design/development may leverage existing knowledge. If not similar, additional cleaning development work is required, as new equipment may present hard-to-clean areas or operating ranges not previously encountered.

                        
- **10.5.1.2 Post-Installation Cleaning:** Following installation, cleaning is required to remove grease, dust, or debris. Effectiveness may be shown using visual inspection, water-break evaluation, white-glove/black-glove test, and/or chemical tests (TOC and conductivity). This cleaning is typically verified, not validated.

                        
- **10.5.1.3 Grouping Impact:** Addition of new equipment may impact established equipment groups. Review grouping/family approach applicability.

                        
- **10.5.1.4 Limit Calculation Impact:** New equipment may increase or decrease product-contact surface area, impacting acceptance limits. Calculations should be reviewed and changes implemented through change control.

                    

                    

### 10.5.2 Used Equipment

                    

All points for new equipment apply. Additionally, equipment history should be obtained. Information of interest includes: type of compound (pharmaceutical or pesticide) and hazards/toxicity of previous compounds. This information is used to determine acceptable cleaning process for the equipment and to set acceptance limits for those compounds for a cleaning verification evaluation.

                    

If little information about previous compounds is available, FTIR analysis of swabbed surfaces may identify potential manufactured products. Additional surface modification steps (descaling, pickling, passivation, reglassing, repolishing) may be taken. The justification should be captured in a documented risk-consideration.

## 10.6 Measurement Systems Analysis (MSA) |

                

Understanding and controlling variation in analytical measurement systems

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.6 Measurement Systems Analysis (MSA)

                    

The purpose of measurement systems analysis is to measure, understand and control the variation caused by measurement systems. By separating out this source of variation, the impact of variation in the parameters being measured can be understood in relation to the cleaning method.

                    

Before beginning an MSA it is extremely important:

                    

                        
- To clearly define the parameters or aspects to be controlled

                        
- To identify tests capable of achieving the measurements needed

                        
- To understand measurement requirements (bias, gage Repeatability and Reproducibility (R&R), accuracy, stability, linearity, etc.)

                    

                    

### 10.6.1 MSA Components

                    

The measurement system variation for continuous data can be broken down into two components: R&R.

                    

                        
- **Repeatability:** Short-term variation of error when successive measurements are made under the same conditions.

                        
- **Reproducibility:** Variation in the average of measurements between operators using the same equipment or between laboratories performing the same assays.

                    

                    

### 10.6.2 Attribute R&R

                    

Attribute R&R is used for discrete data with binary outcomes (pass/fail, good/bad). The focus is on analysis of the ability of the evaluator to detect nonconformance (effectiveness). The probability of false negatives/positives is also calculated.

                    

### 10.6.3 Minimizing Variations

                    

Actions to improve precision of measurements include: regular maintenance and calibration of equipment, updating SOPs, meaningful operator training, mapping/charting the process to identify sources of variation (noise), alternative measurement systems, analytical methods with greater resolution, development of better standards for comparison, and change from manual to automated system.

                    

### 10.6.4 MSA and Cleaning Validation Strategy

                    

MSA is a statistical tool that aids both the analytical methods of detection for carryover as well as the process parameters monitored in the control strategy for the cleaning process. The main goal is always to achieve an acceptable level of control for the system based upon agreed-upon criteria from business and patient safety perspectives. Ideally, the bulk of the variation should come from the items being tested, not the test methods themselves.

## 10.7 Cleaning for API Manufacture | API

                

Special considerations for multi-product intermediate and API manufacturing equipment

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.7 Cleaning for API Manufacture

                    

Equipment for multiproduct intermediate and API manufacturing can be exposed to a large variety of substances and agents (e.g., solvents, reagents, catalysts, cell cultures and processing aids). Detailed written procedures should be established to enable effective and reproducible cleaning of these residues.

                    

For equipment producing later stage intermediates and final APIs, cleaning procedures will need to be developed and validated to remove manufacturing residues including raw materials, un-isolated intermediates, by-products, degradants and the product itself. For equipment used solely for raw materials that are Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS), visual inspection alone may be appropriate if all parts are visually inspectable.

                    

The need for limits for residual organic solvents should be evaluated. For some residual organic solvents that evaporate upon drying, analytical limits may not be necessary. If necessary, limits may be derived from ICH Q3 guidance.

                    

### Equipment Design Considerations

                    

Equipment for API manufacturing is often large, closed, and complex, including significant process piping and large-scale vessels, dryers, condensers, and/or chromatography columns. Design factors of particular importance include:

                    

                        
- Equipment and pipe sloping for drainage

                        
- Elimination or minimization of deadlegs

                        
- Adequate pump size for turbulent flow of cleaning solutions

                        
- Use of spray coverage devices

                    

                    

### Microbiological and Endotoxin Considerations

                    

The harsh chemical environment (pH extremes, organic solvents, high temperature) often associated with small-molecule API manufacture may eliminate microbiological risk. Quality risk assessments should determine the need for microbiological specifications. Equipment cleaning and/or sanitation studies should address microbiological and endotoxin contamination for aqueous-based processing of non-sterile APIs used to manufacture sterile products.

                    

### Post-Validation Monitoring

                    

Cleaning procedures should be monitored at appropriate intervals after validation. It is typical for API equipment cleanliness to be routinely monitored by analytical testing and/or visual examination after each cleaning. This routine monitoring for multipurpose equipment is often accomplished via rinse sampling for large closed equipment.

## 10.8 Topical Drug Products |

                

Limit-setting approaches for systemic vs. limited-availability topical products

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.8 Topical Drug Products

                    

The major issue with topical drug products relates to how limits are set. The approach depends on the systemic availability of the drug active ingredient when applied to the skin.

                    

### 10.8.1 Topical Drug Products with Systemic Availability

                    

Limits are established based on a typical carryover calculation based on a safe level in the next product. In the absence of specific information, it may be assumed as a worst case that the residue is 100% systemically available when in the next product. Minimum dose of cleaned active ingredient and maximum dose of next product should be adjusted based on number of applications per day and amount of product applied per application.

                    

### 10.8.2 Topical Drug Products with No or Limited Systemic Availability

                    

If the therapeutic effect is limited to the skin (e.g., sunscreen), a modification of a traditional carryover calculation may be utilized. The relevant safety concern is what happens if 0.001 of the amount of active applied to a given area appears in the next drug product applied to the same surface area. Limits can be based on 0.001 of the concentration of the active from the cleaned product in the subsequently manufactured drug product.

                    

### 10.8.2.2 Modification Based on Frequency of Application

                    

Carryover calculations should be modified based on frequency of application. If the cleaned product is applied a minimum of once per day and the second product is applied a maximum of three times a day, the limit should be lower by a factor of 3.

                    

### 10.8.2.3 Modification Based on Amount Applied per Surface Area

                    

A second modifying factor is based on the amount applied per surface area. One FTU (fingertip unit) measures approximately 0.5g and covers approximately 286 cm², yielding approximately 1.75 mg/cm².

                    

### 10.8.4 Additional Cleaning Considerations

                    

Topical drug products may present cleaning challenges due to the nature of excipients and high viscosities. In place of a water prerinse, physically removing gross amounts using a plastic scraper or nonwoven wipe may be necessary. If excipients are designed to make the product "waterproof," more stringent conditions (higher temperatures or higher concentrations of cleaning agents) may be required.

## 10.9 Animal Drug Products |

                

Cross-species limit-setting and toxicological assessment

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.9 Animal Drug Products

                    

Cleaning validation for animal drug products is basically the same as for human drug products. The main complication is in setting limits because one product (the cleaned product) may be for one animal species and the next product manufactured in the cleaned equipment may be for a different species.

                    

For example, the cleaned product may be dosed only to horses and the next product dosed only to dogs. In this situation, a toxicological assessment to determine a safe limit should be considered for the effect of the active ingredient of the cleaned product (dosed only to horses) as if it were used for dogs.

                    

All relevant toxicology and safety information should be considered. For example, drugs that may be residues in products for cows may have restrictions not based on toxicity to the cows but because of concerns about safety of the cow's milk. These concerns may also apply to facilities that make both human and animal drugs. Limits based on toxicological evaluations are discussed in Section 5.0.

## 10.10 Packaging Components and Equipment |

                

Primary and secondary packaging: cleaning qualification, endotoxin removal, risk-based approach

            

        

        

            

                

                    

### 10.10.1 Primary Packaging Components

                    

Product contact surfaces of primary packaging closures and containers should be free of materials that could adulterate the drug product. An evaluation of suitability may include considerations of manufacturing process residues, cleaning agents/solvents, particles, bioburden and/or endotoxin.

                    

### 10.10.1.1 Oral Dosage Forms Primary Packaging Components

                    

Cleaning of containers and stoppers used for oral dosage forms is based on a risk analysis. For solid oral dosage forms, cleaning could be limited to removing solid material by blowing compressed filtered air into bottles while inverted. From a microbial perspective, most solid oral drug products will not allow microorganisms to proliferate due to extremely low water activities.

                    

### 10.10.1.2 Parenteral Dosage Forms Primary Packaging Components

                    

Cleaning of containers and stoppers used for parenteral dosage forms is based on risk analysis, and the cleaning levels must be validated at the acceptance criteria. For cleaning processes used as a depyrogenation step, the qualification should demonstrate successful endotoxin removal. The studies are typically performed by applying reconstituted endotoxin solution onto test surfaces, air drying, then measuring endotoxin after cleaning. Data should demonstrate at least a **3-log reduction** in a spiking study.

                    

Container washer qualification should start with a spray coverage test to verify all surfaces are efficiently rinsed. Closure (stopper) cleaning performance qualification should consider removal of lubricant and cleaning agents from the closure manufacturing process, as well as particles.

                    

### 10.10.2 Packaging Equipment

                    

### 10.10.2.1 Primary Packaging Equipment

                    

Primary packaging equipment has direct impact on finished product quality. Examples include oral, topical and aseptic liquid fillers, tablet fillers, tube fillers, blister machines. Cleaning processes and validation practices for primary equipment should not differ from those for direct impact manufacturing equipment. The cleaning of dedicated primary packaging lines may not require validation.

                    

### 10.10.2.2 Secondary Packaging Equipment

                    

Secondary packaging equipment (induction sealers, retorquers, labelers, palletizers) does not have direct impact on product quality but should be designed to allow only minimal inevitable residuals. Once the drug substance is sealed in its primary packaging, the risk of cross-contamination is generally low. Cleaning processes after primary packaging do not require cleaning validation, except where a compromised primary package (broken vial or crushed bottle) might release product. For genotoxic APIs, a dedicated line or validated cleaning step should be considered.
