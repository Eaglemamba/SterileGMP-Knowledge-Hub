# PDA Technical Report No. 66 (2014): Application of Single-Use Systems in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing

## Section 0: Introduction & Glossary (p1-p3)

# Section 0: Introduction & Glossary of Terms

    

    

PDA Technical Report No. 66 (2014) — Application of Single-Use Systems in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing | p1 - p3

    

        

Single-use technology, often described as single-use systems (SUSs) or single-use equipment, has the potential to transform pharmaceutical manufacturing by offering tremendous opportunities to reduce cost, improve flexibility or cycle time, and shorten the time needed to build a manufacturing process for new, lifesaving drugs. This success, however, is very much dependent on how effectively the industry approaches the development and implementation of single-use technology. Ultimately, a new drug can only be successful if it is effective, safe, and available. Traditionally, only a comprehensive understanding of the drug product and manufacturing process can achieve these goals. This remains true as SUS is introduced in place of traditional reusable equipment. Encouraging an open science- and risk-based dialogue during supplier audits and evaluation of SUS supply chains significantly improves an SUS implementation.

        

This document is intended to provide the reader with critical concepts or points to consider when implementing an SUS strategy in a pharmaceutical manufacturing process. These concepts are intended to be valid both for chemically synthesized small molecules and for bioprocesses that produce large-molecule biopharmaceutical products. However, to be truly effective, many of these critical concepts must start with the design, supply chain, manufacturing, and distribution of SUSs themselves, as many inherent quality attributes can impact either the product molecule or its production process. Pharmaceutical manufacturers and single-use technology suppliers have become partners whose success is dependent on the control strategies implemented.

        

This document discusses SUSs that are in either direct or indirect contact with the raw materials, intermediates, and pharmaceutical drug substances or drug products. The document does not intend to discuss disposable items related to laboratory activities, final delivery system to the patient, transfusion bags, packaging, or medical devices.

        

Successful SUS implementation needs a comprehensive approach balancing the product and process goals achieved by using single-use technology. Section 3, Manufacturing Strategy, of this technical report is intended to present an approach that ties together key considerations when evaluating single-use technology. A well-designed manufacturing strategy will address technical, quality, business, and implementation considerations. Each topic has a dedicated section in this technical report, providing a detailed discussion of the associated considerations.

        

Determining the optimal manufacturing strategy involves concepts from many disciplines. An effective evaluation will have a balanced viewpoint, with input from engineering, regulatory, quality, project management, and accounting. Balancing risks and rewards of an SUS over a multiple-use system (MUS) will help determine the most appropriate manufacturing strategy. Thus, a structured science- and risk-based approach is recommended and should be consistent with principles described in ICH Guidelines Q6, Q7, Q8, Q9, Q10, and Q11. Primary goals, when developing any manufacturing strategy, should focus on controlling impacts to patient safety, product availability, and product and process understanding (1–6).

        

Only a formal partnership with an SUS supplier can ensure that quality is as good as or better than what is achieved with traditional systems (e.g., a purchase order is not a partnership). SUS suppliers provide equipment that includes the outsourced, value-added activities that the end user no longer performs. These value-added activities are important for the success of both organizations, and a winning control strategy for SUS has elements in both the supplier’s and the end user’s quality systems.

        

The concepts and recommendations presented in this technical report were developed over several years of discussion within the task force, at PDA workshops, and at other industry meetings. The authors of this technical report recognize that the conversation regarding how best to implement SUSs is just beginning. Ultimately the success of these systems will be determined by the decisions suppliers and end users make during implementation, and the hope is that this report provides a foundation for the industry to build on.

## **4.3 SU Components and Assembly 4.3 **

The primary components for the assembly of an SUS include fittings, tubing, filters, connectors, bags (film), porting (bags), impellers, spargers, valves, sensors, filling needles, pump heads, docking port containers, and outer pouches (dust covers). The individual components do not require environmentally controlled storage rooms; they are stored in sealed bags and must be handled with gloves once opened to avoid contamination during assembly of the SUS. Several of the components can be assembled into a system and supplied presterilized, commonly via gamma irradiation. This is especially convenient for a completed fluid contact pathway of tubing preattached to a bag with all the tubing legs either heat-sealed or terminated with aseptic connectors. These systems are called bag assemblies, and they have tubing sets or tubing manifolds attached to them.

As shown in Figure 4.3-1, fully functional assemblies are often created by connecting components to a set of subassemblies, which in turn forms a functional SUS assembly that is capable of performing specific unit operations.

MUSs are assembled in classified and environmentally monitored facilities, and the equipment interior is cleaned by a clean-in-place (CIP) technique. An SUS must be manufactured and assembled in a clean environment because there is no practical method to clean an SU assembly. Some tubing can be purged with ultrapure air or nitrogen for particle removal during fabrication; however, this is not practical for the assembled SUS. The area where the SUS will be assembled must be prepared in advance using standard clean-room practices. The outside of each component package must be wiped with alcohol before transport into the assembly area. Assembly rooms are typically ISO 7 or 8 (Grade B/C or Grade C/D, respectively), and it is normal to conduct a combination of nonviable particle and microbial sampling of the assembly room. The design of the room is similar to that of pharmaceutical manufacturing suites (i.e., HEPA-filtered air, low returns, smooth surfaces, and airlocks for entry and exit). Staff gowning must be appropriate for an ISO 7 or ISO 8 environment. Regular, consistent, and high-quality cleaning of the assembly room is required to maintain area classification and provide a consistent environment for manufacturing SU solutions. SUS components are manufactured and assembled in controlled environments in order to meet acceptable standards for particle, bioburden, and endotoxin levels. Since there are no established testing standards for an SUS, the end user must rely on the supplier's specifications and control strategies for particles, endotoxins, and bioburden. Suppliers should have quality systems in place to ensure their facilities and procedures meet end-user requirements. It is also critical that quality agreements are established between end users and suppliers to ensure that the suppliers' facilities are adequate to deliver an SUS that meets end-user requirements.

### 4.7.1 Process Connections

Connectors and tube welders are used primarily to connect:

- Components within an SU subassembly

- Multiple subassemblies to create a complete operational assembly

- An assembly to a neighboring unit operation

In addition, connectors are used with tube sealers or disconnectors to disconnect:

- Components (e.g., bags) within an SU subassembly

- A subassembly from a complete operational assembly

### 4.7.1.1 Technological Examples

A wide variety of connectors are commercially available for various user requirements. In general, two-piece connectors consist of two units with mating ends that may be joined to form a connection. Identical mating parts are denoted as "genderless," and nonidentical mating parts are usually denoted as "male" or "female." Although connectors are usually used to create a connection between parts, they may be installed internally within a pipe in order, for example, to open a flow path of a steaming process.

Single-piece insert connectors commonly are variants of standard straight, Y, or T configurations and employ hose barbs that can be inserted into tubing and secured with cable ties or clamps. A variant of a single-piece connector can be made by a specialized overmolding procedure that creates a connection with a uniformly smooth inner bore.

Connectors with integrated valves and resterilizable SIP connectors have the additional functionality of enabling aseptic disconnections and reconnection.

The type of connection technology to be used should be considered when planning an SUS:

- **Tube welder:** A tube welder is an alternative type of connection technology that is designed to make a connection by cutting the two pieces of appropriate thermoplastic tubing to be connected and rapidly heating, aligning, and joining the two ends to form an aseptic, integral weld upon cooling. The sealed area is then separated from the rest of the tube by cutting it away. Silicone tubing is not appropriate for tube sealing, as it is not a thermoplastic.

## 4.9 Storage

    

        

        

Appropriate storage is necessary in order to maintain the quality of process solutions (e.g., buffers and culture media), intermediates, and bulk drug substance until they are ready for further processing. Long-term storage of process intermediates often requires rapid, controlled freezing or cryogenic preservation. The storage bag must be qualified for the specific use, with a focus on maintaining bag integrity at the lower of the storage or freezing temperatures.

        

### 4.9.1 Technological Examples

        

A storage bag with a polyethylene fluid contact layer is normally used to store aqueous-based liquid intermediates. Polyethylene polymers (particularly low-density grades) have broad chemical compatibility and very low reactivity to most intermediates and process fluids. Other materials like fluoropolymers are used to address specific process compatibility concerns.

        

Typical plastics used for freezing applications include poly(tetrafluoroethylene) (PTFE) and poly(ethylene vinyl acetate) (EVA). These materials are particularly good at retaining their mechanical strength at very low freezing temperatures. Specific bag systems have been integrated into commercially available controlled freeze-thaw systems. A gas barrier such as ethylene vinyl alcohol (EVOH) is typically used with EVA to create a multilayered composite film that offers mechanical strength and resistance to gas permeation.

        

For short-term storage, process intermediates in standard bags are normally kept at ambient temperature or in a refrigerator. Long-term storage of process intermediates often requires rapid, controlled freezing or cryogenic preservation (refer to Section 4.10, Freezing). Factors that affect the storage of process intermediates are summarized in Table 4.9.1-1.

        

    

        

## 4.10 Freezing

    

        

        

The end user may use freezing techniques to separate process pools or intermediates and hold them for a short time at the process temperature and conditions or for a longer duration before another production step. For long durations, the end user may freeze the intermediate directly in an SUS that is designed for freezing. Uncontrolled freezing is performed in standard laboratory freezers, purpose-designed blast cells, or nitrogen freezing chambers. Controlled freeze-and-thaw equipment (e.g., a plate freezer) is used to regulate the rate of freezing and thawing of the contents of each SU bag. To maintain the integrity of the bag containing the frozen process intermediates or bulk drug substances, careful attention must be paid to the reusable storage container design and logistics of shipping and handling.

        

### 4.10.1 Technological Examples

        

            
- **Unprotected small-scale bag:** It is common practice to freeze an unprotected bag (less than 20 L) that is not specifically designed for the application. Compared to other technologies, the failure rates of frozen unprotected bags tend to be higher (Table 4.10.1-1🔍).

            
- **Separate bag shell** — The purpose of a bag shell is to maintain the structural integrity of a frozen bag by protecting it from bumps and drops during handling at low temperatures. Bag shells are made of either rigid plastics or rigid foam construction.

            
- **Integrated shell-protected bag** — The shell design includes a tubing and fitting compartment for security and storage at the most common point of failure. Off-the-shelf International Safe Transit Association (ISTA) and ASTM-qualified shipping containers can also be used.

            
- **Controlled freeze-thaw system, bag, frame, and logistics equipment** — A controlled freeze-thaw system uses contact freezing technology to allow for the reproducible freezing of the contents of the bag. Contact freezing plates maintain freezing distances by maintaining the filled bag between two freezing contact plates. In addition, the rate of heat transfer from the bags is increased in a controlled freeze-thaw system, and this decreases the time required for freezing. Thus, a controlled freeze-thaw system is part of an integrated refrigeration system for maintaining the integrity of the bag and the quality of its contents during fill, transport, freezing, and thawing.

        

        

    

        

## 4.11 Filtration

    

        

        

Normal flow filtration (NFF) or direct flow filtration (DFF) devices are used in biopharmaceutical manufacturing to remove contaminants such as particulates, microbial cells, and virus particles. Cross flow filtration (CFF) or tangential flow filtration (TFF) devices are used to reduce the volume of a buffer and/or perform buffer exchange as part of the downstream process.

        

### 4.11.1 Technological Examples

        

CFF devices are commercially available in the following structural formats: flat-sheet cassettes, and hollow fiber cartridges. Currently, only a hollow-fiber cartridge can be classified as an SU filter device. Structurally, there is little difference between SU and MU CFF hollow fiber devices. Functionally, an SU CFF hollow fiber device provides a higher degree of cleanliness and sterility compared to that of a SU flat sheet device. Current flat sheet devices marketed as single-use contain a disposable flow path (e.g., ports, gaskets, and the filter device), which is then assembled into a stainless steel holder that provides the compression and support required for filtration. NFF devices have been commercially available in an SU format for many years, and structurally, they contain a depth filter media or membrane for separation via size exclusion.

        

    

        

## 4.12 Centrifugation

    

        

        

Centrifugation is often used for cell harvest as an alternative to filtration, and SU formats of this technology are emerging for low shear separations, including harvest clarification; cell concentration, wash, and harvest; and cell separation. These systems utilize a gamma-irradiated single-use module or polymeric liner for product contact and, as such, eliminate the need for CIP or manual cleaning and SIP or hot water for injection (WFI) for bioburden sterilization and control of traditional centrifuges. Flow rates vary depending on the manufacture and design of the equipment, ranging from <10 to 100 L per minute at <100X to 8000X G force. Continuous and batch centrifugation technologies are available.

    

    

        

## 4.13 Chromatography

    

        

        

Biomolecules are purified using chromatographic techniques that separate them according to differences in their specific physical or chemical properties. SU formats for various chromatography steps are available.

        

### 4.13.1 Technological Examples

        

Chromatographic media can be divided into two main categories: resin- or bead-based and membrane-based. Membrane-based chromatographic devices are also known as membrane adsorbers, and because they are membrane-based, they share similar physical and structural features with traditional filter devices. In order to produce membrane-based chromatographic devices with specific properties, the membrane is chemically derivatized with ligands.

        

Disposable bead-based chromatographic devices tend to contain the same resins and have the same general physical structure as traditional packed-bed columns that are made of glass and stainless steel. Bead-based chromatographic devices are commercially available as prepacked units made of plastic structural parts. They are useful for product campaigning because they can be used more than once. As with traditional packed-bed columns, sterilization methods such as gamma irradiation or moist heat cannot be used with disposable bead-based chromatographic devices; instead, rigorous chemical sanitization is used. As with multiple-use columns, the preservative solution in the SU device must be flushed before the unit is used. Conventional chromatography and SU chromatography both require large amounts of process fluids like buffers (refer to Section 4.8.1, Fluid Management, and Section 4.17, Transportation).

        

    

        

## 4.14 Drug Product Final Filling

    

        

        

The impact of any failures in final fill operations is high, since further processing cannot reduce quality failures at this stage. Common strategies for reducing the risk of failure include performing filling operations in aseptic environments, use of physical barrier systems to separate operators from the process, and validating the sterility of process components (refer to Section 4.15, Isolators).

        

SU components are widely used in product final filling operations. For example, flexible plastic tubing is used between the filling pump head and needle. Another example is a peristaltic filling system, which employs disposable peristaltic tubing inside the pump head. Both types of tubing are normally replaced between batches.

        

### 4.14.1 Technological Examples

        

An SU dosing system comprises a product hold bag, supply tubing, and a filling needle. These components are assembled in a cleanroom and sterilized using gamma irradiation. The complete assembly is removed from its outer pouch and connected to the filling machine immediately before use. The filling machine may be as simple as a single pump or may contain multiple pumps to achieve commercial production speeds.

        

SU filling systems are controlled volumetrically or gravimetrically.

        

            
- **Volumetric dosing system** — This type of system involves a volumetric system that consists of a peristaltic pump and specialized tubing, within which flows the product to be dispensed. Peristaltic pumps work by mechanically compressing the tubing within the pump head to push a specified volume of fluid through the dosing path. Accurate dosing depends on factors such as the mechanical life of the pump tubing. A positive displacement pump-based system relies on the use of either a rotary-piston or rolling-diaphragm pump design. In both cases, fluid is forced out of the pump by the action of the piston. Accurate dosing depends on the precision of the piston stroke length.

            
- **Gravimetric dosing system** — A gravimetric system is normally used in low-throughput applications. It uses an optical sensor to dose a specified weight of the product based on the calculation of the interior volume of a given length of tubing or glass. The entire product path comprises SU components. The accuracy and precision of the system with aqueous solutions is comparable to that of other dosing systems. The dosing speed and accuracy are directly related to the fluid temperature and viscosity. Dosing time is the speed at which a liquid will flow through the tubing based on gravity; thus, viscosity influences dosing time. Relatively small temperature changes over the course of a filling event can affect product viscosity enough to have a significant effect on the dose volume.

        

        

        

    

    

        

## 4.15 Isolators

    

        

        

If the canister of a traditional alpha-beta (A-B) port is replaced with an SU bag or connector, SU technology can be used for the aseptic transfer of liquids, powders, stoppers, plungers, tools, materials, and waste into and out of isolators or restricted-access barrier systems (RABS). To avoid compromising the sterility of the isolator, SU bags are designed to prevent reuse. Categories of an SU product format include:

        

            
- **Gamma-sterilizable bag with mating A-B connector** — Manufacturers of stoppers use this as packaging for prewashed, siliconized, and sterile ready-to-use stoppers or plungers.

            
- **Gamma-sterilizable bag with mating A-B connector and tubing set** — This bag design includes one or more tubing sets for the transfer of liquids into the isolator or RABS. These are normally available as stand-alone components or as part of a presterilized SUS.

            
- **Autoclavable bag with mating A-B connector** — Manufacturers of stoppers use autoclavable bags as packaging for prewashed, siliconized, and ready-to-sterilize stoppers or plungers. In addition, autoclavable bags are used for in-house stopper processing, tool, and component transfer.

            
- **A-B connector** — A connector is used for liquid transfer. Connectors can be used with either an SUS or an MUS because they can be sterilized by gamma irradiation or autoclaving.

        

        

    

        

## 4.16 Sampling and Laboratory Analysis

    

        

        

Sampling is the process of removing a volume of process fluid from an SU assembly. This sample fluid is then usually submitted for laboratory analysis.

        

### 4.16.1 Technological Examples

        

Techniques for obtaining a sample fluid include:

        

            
- Use of a sampling port with or without the aid of a needle to collect the sample

- Use of a preassembled manifold of sampling bags that may be filled and detached from the assembly one at a time using crimping tools or a tube sealer

            
- Use of an SU sample valve integrated with one or more sample bags

        

        

        

    

    

        

## 4.17 Transportation

    

        

        

Transportation is the movement of process solutions such as buffers, culture media, WFI, and process intermediates either within a facility or between facilities.

        

### 4.17.1 Technological Examples

        

The supply of process solutions in large-volumes bags (≤1000 L) from the point of manufacture to the point of use is a well-established practice involving the following components:

        

            
- A bag designed to fit a rigid-wall outer container

            
- A rigid-wall outer container such as a plastic drum or tote, or a stainless steel bin

            
- Secondary packaging materials (e.g., dunnage) and lids or other mechanical devices to suppress the fluid wave action in the bioprocess bag

        

        

Transportation of process solutions in bags in small volumes (nominally ≤20 L) is an equally well-established process that requires less complex packaging solutions. The exception is the transportation of frozen material, which requires special temperature-resistant materials and processes (e.g., bags, shells and secondary packaging materials, operating procedures, and cold chain logistics; refer to Section 4.11, Freezing).

## 4.18 Sensors

    

        

        

An SU sensor enhances the functionality of an SUS by providing parametric data that allows for manual or automated process control or monitoring. Modern sensors can be directly integrated into SU systems, and traditional sensors equipped with a fluid-path barrier can be used to measure factors such as temperature and pressure. A hybrid sensor uses a combination of a sensor and a transducer. Although sensor and transducer technologies have been available in many industries for a considerable time, the application of SU sensor technology in bioprocessing is relatively recent.

        

### 4.18.1 Technological Examples

        

SU sensor technology is routinely used to measure factors such as temperature, pressure, and conductivity. Recently, advanced sensors for measuring pH, dissolved oxygen, and CO2, as well as other sensors that employ chemical and/or optical technologies, have become available in an SU format. As the use of SU sensors becomes more widespread, challenges such as their compatibility with sterilization methods and the physical design of the SUS are beginning to emerge.

        

The dynamic range, accuracy, and robustness of a particular sensor must be taken into account during sensor evaluation for a particular SUS application. Other factors to consider are the need for a common sensing platform across several unit operations (i.e., from small- to large-scale operations); the impact of employing multiple sensing technologies, each with different quantification attributes (such as range, accuracy, and precision); and assessment of sensor calibration requirements and the ease of performing a post-use verification.

## **5.0/5.1 Qualification and Verification — Introduction 5.0/5.1 — **

### 5.0 Qualification and Verification of Suppliers, Materials, Components, and Completed Assemblies

### 5.1 Introduction

The qualification and verification of suppliers, materials, components, and completed assemblies require attention to several factors. It is important to use a risk-based and science-based approach to qualify both materials and completed assemblies for SUSs in pharmaceutical development and manufacture (18). The assessment of risk must be based on the complexity of the system and its intended use (e.g., product contact versus non-product contact, upstream versus downstream use, short-term contact versus long-term storage). See PDA's Technical Report No. 54 on Quality Risk Management for more information (7). In addition, scientific principles must be applied to 1) identify and monitor extractable materials from films and other components; 2) investigate potential interactions with product CQAs or significant process parameters like cell culture media; and 3) assess the effects of assembly processes such as welding, fusing, mechanical stress, and sterilization (e.g., gamma irradiation) on the materials during manufacturing. In assessing the suitability of the SUS with respect to potential leachables in the final product, extractables may be considered as potential in-process leachables. Finally, the design and functional integrity of the completed SUS must be verified and maintained by appropriately trained end users via suitable processes for construction, packaging, shipping, and deployment.

The supplier of the SUS must provide sufficient data to qualify the materials and components used to assemble the final SUS. In this regard, the end user may use the documents from the supplier to meet most of the verification criteria required to make sure that the SUS is fit for the intended application and purpose. This requires a thorough qualification of the supplier by the end user to ascertain an acceptable supplier quality system, documentation, and an appropriate level of technical capability that extends all the way to the original sources of materials to the supplier. In addition, the suppliers must be able to provide documented evidence of performance claims for off-the-shelf component designs for the SUS. A supplier quality agreement must underpin any custom designs that the end user may need from the supplier.

The first step in a science- and risk-based approach is to identify and assess the risks associated with using an SUS. The end user must identify that the SUS supplier's approach to the design and selection of materials of construction includes components and materials such as:

- Filters

- Sensors

- Polymers

- Lubricants

- Slip agents

Supplier quality, manufacturing and analytical capabilities should also be evaluated. The end user must fully qualify and audit a number of potential suppliers before embarking on extensive studies of specific materials because it is preferable to have more than one supplier for the SUS.

It is important for the end user to have access to controlled documentation of the qualification studies conducted on the materials and components of the assembled SUS. Such documentation may include:

- Batch numbers traceable to the raw materials

- Product shelf life

- Certificate of quality

- Certificate of integrity

- Endotoxin certification (to required and specific endotoxin level)

- Animal-free certification

- Sterilization certification (if sterility is claimed)

A robust change control communication and assessment process between the end user and the supplier (including sub-suppliers) must be established to monitor any changes in processing conditions, equipment, analytical methods, manufacturing site, resins, films, additives, and more. If the supplier does not have an appropriate assessment process in place, the end user may have to perform independent testing. After performing material and component qualification of the assembled products, a functional verification of the SUS should be performed according to the following:

- **Risk assessment** — To confirm that the assembled products are fit for purpose

- **Process verification and validation** — To confirm that the completed SUS functions within process constraints, including repetitive use or extended storage (if required)

- **Safety** — To make sure that the SUS conforms to safety requirements as a pressure vessel (especially for larger volumes) or for containment in the event of rupture or leak

If the SUS assembly includes SU sensors, calibration and additional verification documentation must be supplied.

## **5.7 Bovine Spongiform Encephalopathy (BSE)/Transmissible Spongiform Encephalopathy (TSE) Concerns 5.7 BSE/ TSE**

SUSs have low-risk BSE/TSE concerns and they do not contain high-risk animal-derived components. Certain mold release and slip agents and plastics resins (e.g., polypropylene or polycarbonate) may contain trace levels of additives (e.g., calcium stearate), which are manufactured from bovine tallow and have low BSE/TSE risk. Tallow is a lubricant sometimes used in pelletizing operations for bulk resin or as a mold release agent for the manufacture of SU components. Tallow is produced by treating beef fat with steam to create a mixture of fats. The fat (e.g., glyceryl tristearate) is then boiled in a sodium hydroxide solution, resulting in a mixture of sodium stearate and glycerin.

Tallow-derived additives are not considered as specified BSE risk materials according to the current revision of the U.S. Code of Federal Regulations, Title 21 Part 189.5 (47). Furthermore, the CPMP's "Note for Minimizing the Risk of Transmitting Animal Spongiform Encephalopathies via Human and Veterinary Medicinal Products" (EMA 410/01 rev 3), gives specific consideration to tallow derivatives (48). It states that they are unlikely to be infectious due to the rigorous processing steps (e.g., transesterification, or hydrolysis, at not less than 200°C under pressure for not less than twenty minutes) used during their manufacture. A BSE/TSE risk assessment should be completed and periodically updated to reflect whether an SUS supplier is using components in the fluid pathway that were fabricated from plastic resins containing bovine-derived additives at trace levels.

## **5.8 The Quality Systems of the End User and Supplier 5.8 **

It is important for the end user to have instituted a robust quality system to receive materials, quarantine unreleased and rejected materials, inspect those materials, and release them to production. Since the testing and release of an SUS can vary widely from end user to end user, a complete and careful documentation in the form of a purchase specification and inspection plan should accompany any off-the-shelf or customized SUS requisition. A supplier quality agreement is another useful way to manage the requirements and expectations of both supplier and end user.

## **5.9 Quality Management for Single-Use System Implementation 5.9 **

### **5.9.1 Determination of Expiration Date and Shelf Life 5.9.1 **

The SUS supplier is expected to provide estimates for expiration dates and the shelf life of the SUS according to validation of maintenance of sterility tests and key functional properties. The expiration date is normally set post-sterilization. Any extension of the shelf life of the SUS must be supported by pertinent data. Real-time aging studies provide the best data to ensure that both the materials and the functional integrity of a sterile barrier system do not degrade with time. Accelerated studies may be a suitable substitute if real-time aging studies cannot be performed. The ASTM F1980–07(2007) Standard Guide for Accelerated Aging of Sterile Barrier Systems for Medical Devices is a widely used test for accelerated aging of the plastic components of SUSs (49).

The term Ready-To-Use is a marketing term often used to describe the benefits of single-use technology or SUS. This designation has no regulatory or scientific basis supporting suitability for use, and end users are responsible for evaluating and determining whether appropriate quality requirements have been met for their application.

## **5.10 Sterilization 5.10 **

### **5.10.1 Irradiation Sterilization 5.10.1 **

SUSs are especially suitable to sterilization by irradiation because of the relative transparency of plastics to ionizing radiation, which allows complex systems to be sterilized without the risk of spreading the sterilizing agent throughout the fluid path. Irradiation may be used to sterilize plastics or SUS assemblies that may be unsuitable for steam sterilization. The practice of sterilizing via irradiation is well established in the medical device industry with transferable standards that can be applied to SU process equipment (51). Although no specific industry standard has been developed for the sterilization of SUSs, the industry and regulatory communities have adopted the ANSI/AAMI/ISO 11137 standard, *Sterilization of Health Care Products—Radiation*, and the closely related AAMI Technical Information Report TIR 33:2005, *Substantiation of a Selected Sterilization Dose—Method VD max* (51,52). Adaptation and implementation of these standards to the implementation of SUS have been discussed in the literature (53). The most common alternative methods mentioned in the standards are the dose substantiation methods (i.e., the verification dose max, or "VD max," methods).

In practice, a typical audit involves the selection of a single-use product (SUP) as representative of the SUS. A supplier quality audit must consider how the product family and the representative product design were derived. In cases where testing the representative product would be impractical (e.g., due to size and complexity), a portion of the sample item may be used with mathematical scaling between the actual product and the sample item. A supplier quality audit must include an understanding of the mathematical scaling technique. Since the standards require that a supplier must perform quarterly dose audits for each product family, the end-user quality audit of the SU supplier must ascertain that there is a procedure in place for the supplier to conduct quarterly dose auditing. Validation normally involves the determination of an average bioburden level in a representative master system, assessment of radiation resistance by sterility testing of the fluid path of the master system after a low "verification" dose (about 8 to 10 kGy), and calculation of a suitable dose to provide a sterility assurance level or a probability of nonsterility of 10-6 for the model product and its variants. The supplier and the end user should review the applicability of validation via master system sterilization to the end user's SUS configuration.

## **5.11 Integrity 5.11 **

### **5.11.1 Testing an SUS for Leaks 5.11.1 SUS **

The requirements for testing an SUS will depend on its intended use. The retention of structural integrity (i.e., containers do not leak) is the main concern and is a key concern in process-contact applications. For low-risk applications (e.g., sample and buffer bags), an understanding of supplier testing, performance, and training in appropriate handling procedures may be sufficient.

SUSs are increasingly used for the production of vaccines, and the egress of a live virus could have serious consequences; therefore, specific testing of individual systems is recommended.

When an SUS is used for holding media, the potential for microbial ingress that may compromise sterility becomes a significant concern. Attempts have been made to assess the maximum size of a defect that will compromise the effectiveness of a flexible bag (56). This is a complex undertaking because it depends on factors such as the surface tension of the liquid in the bag, time, temperature, and the method of measurement. In addition, the permeability of individual components (e.g., tubing and films) may differ according to the test agent (e.g., gas or dye) used.

The structural integrity of the SUS should be carefully evaluated in a collaborative manner by the end user and the supplier. Post-shipment/transport testing to evaluate the method of packaging the SUS will need to be incorporated. To test for the integrity of the fluid path during qualification—including tubing manifolds and connectors—a suggested method is to internally pressurize tubing with air and hold it under water. Provided that testing will not compromise the sterility or cleanliness of the equipment, manifolds can be inspected by manipulation to check for air bubbles at both tubing connector engagements and along the entire length of tubing. For upstream applications, the user should confirm the supplier's claims during verification and accept materials based on their documentation thereafter. A special case may need to be made for customized SUS. Demands are generally stricter for product containers; therefore, end users may wish to ask the suppliers what testing is feasible for larger containers at their sites versus the end user's site (after installation).

To test a bag container or a SUS for leakage, a suggested method (depending on the size of the SUS) is to fill the plastic bag or SUS with water inside the bin, drum, or tank and check for leaks at maximum volume. If the location of the leak is not apparent, fill the plastic container with water outside of the bin, drum, or tank on a flat, absorbent surface such as cardboard or paper. Locate the leak by identifying wet spots on the absorbent surface corresponding to a position on the container. Using the mapped location of the leak on the bag, examine the bin, drum, or tank for sharp points that may have caused the leak. Examine the leak under magnification to determine whether the leakage was caused by puncture, tear, creasing, pinhole, or another means. Track leak locations to help with root cause analysis of failure modes (e.g. handling, structural/design considerations, and material failure).

If the end user determines that a leak was caused by a manufacturing defect, the failed sample should be returned to the supplier for quality investigation and corrective action. The returned sample should include full information regarding the conditions of use and location of the leak. Suppliers should maintain records of system failures and their root causes for further analysis.

## **6.0 Business Drivers for the Adoption of Single-Use Systems 6.0 **

This section provides a detailed description of the key business drivers that inform the evaluation and implementation of single-use versus traditional MUSs. The business drivers for the introduction of an SUS are many, varied, and unique to each company's individual situation and business model. Several factors must be considered for a successful analysis. These factors include:

- Supplier-demonstrated technical and quality capabilities

- Product sustainability

- Guarantee of business continuity

- The business model of the company considering an SUS

- Process design

- The product and its potential market volume

- Available facilities

- Anticipated location

Each company will approach SUS implementation with a different set of objectives developed from business drivers that are unique to a specific business model.

SUS implementation confers advantages such as reduced capital investment, flexible operation, faster changeover, and the application of lean manufacturing principles. Since a company's individual situation and business model will affect the role of pertinent business drivers, it is critical to start the evaluation with an understanding of the business in question. For example, the main concern of a contract manufacturing company (CMO) may be the elimination of cross-contamination in a multiple-client, multiple-product facility. A stand-alone manufacturer with a facility dedicated to the production of a single product may consider a hybrid approach of an SUS and MUS to reduce SU variable costs yet allow for the expansion of production capacity at peak periods. An effective implementation of an SUS would help the company to meet important business goals in addition to cost savings.

## **6.1 Evaluation of Business Drivers 6.1 **

The decision to implement an SUS tends to be driven by economic incentives such as 1) potential elimination of cleaning and sterilization processes; 2) reduction of dedicated equipment or changeover time; and 3) increased flexibility and capacity of the manufacturing facility. The end user must consider both tangible cost savings and less quantitative benefits such as low risks of cross contamination. A cost-of-goods-manufactured (COGM) analysis can be used to support claims of economic savings. Other tools, such as risk assessment, value-stream mapping, CO2 footprint, green manufacturing, and value-added analysis, can provide invaluable data to inform a business decision on whether to implement SUS or not.

## **6.2 Lifecycle Approach 6.2 **

The importance of specific business drivers will depend on the needs of each individual company. The scope of the project may be as narrow as using an SUS to boost the capacity of a single process step or to eliminate cleaning steps. Such a tactical comparison depends on technological, process compatibility, and economic factors. Conversely, the project scope could be a corporate-wide initiative to evaluate the feasibility of SUS implementation for an entire production process or part of an overall lifecycle evaluation for a product or facility. A lifecycle approach involves an evaluation of the costs incurred throughout the lifetime of the project in order to provide a complete economic basis for making decisions. Costs for evaluation may include the initial capital investments, production and quality overheads, and decommissioning. The lifecycle approach incorporates time and risk factors as relevant strategies are applied, and this leads to a more comprehensive economic evaluation.

For instance, the forecast for a new product may include an initial launch phase with a relatively uncertain production demand followed by a full-scale production phase with options for capacity expansion or additional products. In such a situation, operational flexibility becomes important to the business case because of the uncertainty in product demand. Such flexibility comes with an increase in capital investment for traditional MUSs, whereas the implementation of an SUS tends to reduce the cost of operational flexibility because it is less reliant on investment for utilities and equipment installation. An SUS is less costly to modify or expand to a new facility when a change in product lifecycle occurs.

## **6.3 Opportunity Cost 6.3 **

Opportunity cost is defined as the value of the next-best choice available when choosing between several mutually exclusive options. These costs are often excluded from estimates of fixed operation costs because they can be difficult for comparative analyses in the overall decision process. For example, deciding to expand a facility may result in forgoing the opportunity to invest in other projects, contract batch production at a CMO, or maintain cash reserves. Opportunities can be difficult to compare; hence, opportunity cost calculations between competing projects may help identify the best path forward.

## **6.4 Cost of Quality 6.4 **

The cost of quality can be defined as any cost that would not have been expended if quality were perfect. The inherent advantages of an SUS may lead to reductions in the cost of quality for the end user. In order for an SUS to be used in a manufacturing process, the end user must be able to demonstrate that it is fit for purpose and meets applicable quality and regulatory requirements. SUS suppliers may take advantage of automation, economies of scale, and lean manufacturing to improve consistency and reduce the cost of manufacturing. This arrangement makes the end user dependent on the supplier's quality system. Therefore, it is critical for the quality system of the supplier to be robust enough to manage subsuppliers (e.g., resin and component suppliers). In addition, the quality system of the supplier must be able to monitor and detect the unique failure modes associated with SUS (e.g., bag breakage, variations in the profile of extractables and leachables, and effects on cell growth). It is the responsibility of the end user to ensure that the SUS meets applicable quality standards. Any tests conducted by the end user to ascertain process compatibility due to changes made by the supplier may be included in cost-of-quality estimates.

## **6.8 Investment Costs 6.8 **

### 6.8.1 Process Equipment

The first step in evaluating an SU project is normally an equipment-to-equipment comparison of SUS versus MUS direct investment cost. Reduced demand for cleaning and sterilization allow for simplifications in piping and instrumentation, which leads to a reduction in the investment cost for SUSs. While this will give an estimate of whether the technology is economically feasible for the project, Table 6.8.1-1🔍 shows additional factors for evaluating investment costs that should be considered for an SUS project (59).

### 6.8.2 Utility Equipment

The effect of utility systems on the investment costs should be explored because it may be possible to remove entire system groups from the scope (e.g., clean steam, CIP stations) or achieve enough reduction in capacity to obtain cost reductions (WFI for rinsing, kill systems). The need to inactivate solid waste may increase the investment costs of an SUS, while the absence of a CIP in an SUS design may lead to a reduction in the generation of wastewater.

### 6.8.3 Indirect Equipment Costs

The estimates for engineering and installation costs (e.g., design, commissioning, and qualification and validation) are normally calculated as a percentage of the equipment costs. One of the advantages of an SUS is that reductions in piping and instrumentation may decrease the number of both installed hardware and communication I/O points in the automation system. However, an SUS may incur a higher engineering percentage cost compared to similar MUS designs because of lower initial investment costs and the cost of validating leachables and extractables for the SUS. The installation percentage is lower for an SUS than an MUS. A similar approach can be used to determine the percentage costs for construction and project management (60).

### 6.8.4 Facility

An SUS uses stackable rectangular containers, which results in a smaller footprint compared to MUS. The absence of a CIP and washing/sterilization function further reduces the space requirements of SUS. However, inefficient movement of containers, installation, and dismantling of bags can easily waste the space saved by an SUS. In addition, proper storage and handling of an SUS is very important, since any damage might result in a lost batch or contamination. The transport route of an SUS may increase compared to that of a hard-piped system, and an SUS requires space to store process waste prior to final disposal. Storage space for SUS is typically greater than that for traditional MUS because of the need to manage SUS inventory and associated reusable storage containers. The main contributions to variable operating costs are summarized in Table 6.8.4-1🔍 (61).

### 6.8.5 Materials

Often, input materials (e.g., SUS materials prefilled with hydrated or powdered media and buffer components and chromatography gels) may be independent of SUS or MUS technology (62).

### 6.8.6 Consumables

The best way to determine the component cost of consumables is from the specific SUS process design under consideration because there can be a significant discrepancy between the costs of a supplier's standard configuration and that of the specific assembly required for a particular process step. Whenever possible, using the supplier's standard configurations would be the most cost-effective way to determine the cost of consumables. Designing the SUS with backup systems such as redundant tubing, filters, and connectors will increase the costs per batch of that SUS. However, if these backup systems are necessary, they must be reflected in the final cost analysis of the project.

It is possible to minimize SUS overdesign via a thorough understanding of the real risks determined in a process risk assessment such as a failure mode and effects analysis (FMEA) (7). It is also important to get a feel for the actual cost of the SUS once specific connectors, filters, and tubing sets are added to the design. Since it may be possible to obtain discounts with large-volume orders from prospective suppliers, it is necessary to understand SUS configurations and how they can be customized to meet individual SUS process requirements. It would be advisable to incorporate a sensitivity analysis for the purchase cost of an SUS because final configurations and prices can be difficult to predict. In general, the sensitivity analysis should be extended to any variable with uncertain cost estimates (63).

### 6.8.7 Utilities

The reductions in cleaning and sterilization associated with an SUS leads to a decrease in the cost of utilities, especially for clean water generation, CIP, and SIP systems. It is important to use carefully verified cost data since large volumes may be involved in these processes. The cost of water for injection is significantly lower for large on-site systems than for commercially supplied WFI. It may be calculated from system depreciation, labor, and energy costs.

### 6.8.8 Waste

The cost of inactivating solid waste can be significant depending on the degree of containment required. The solid waste may be 1) inactivated on-site, which will lead to increased load on kill autoclaves; or 2) transported off-site for incineration or disposal at a landfill. Both approaches would incur transport and disposal fees. A third approach involves chemical inactivation. The disposal strategy for each SUS must comply with the laws and requirements of the municipal authority in charge (64–66).

## **6.9 QC/QA and Cost of Quality 6.9 QC/QA **

Since some variable costs (e.g., analyses, preparing batch documentation, and batch release) are rarely affected by SUS implementation, the baseline costs of MU and SU systems tend to be similar. Batch review and investigation efforts may decrease and result in additional savings because SUS implementation results in reduced cleaning, SIP, and changeover activities. However, certain quality oversight activities (e.g., quality agreement, supplier audits, SUS documentation review, subsupplier audits, and QC-receiving inspection) may increase with an SUS implementation. The operation of several quality activities within the supplier's quality system rather than that of the end user may make process investigations more complex, and this additional effort should be taken into account.

## **7.6 Implementation S6: Testing and Validation 7.6 S6**

During this stage, methods and documentation must be developed for equipment SOPs, maintenance, qualification and process validation, HSE processes, and personnel training. Some documents (e.g., the validation master plan) may require updates at this stage to maintain compliance.

## 7.6.1 Delivery ()

The PEP must cover the delivery process, including responsibilities of the supplier and the end user. Transport logistics are sometimes complex, and the procedure by which the equipment will be safely shipped (transported) and unloaded from its delivery vehicle must be considered. Some suppliers do not take unloading into account, and the end user may need to take particular precautions here or ensure that the supplier contractually takes responsibility. There may be specific non-GxP issues to resolve such as documentation (delivery notes), inspection for inventory or damage, and insurance or the removal and appropriate disposal of crates and other packaging materials.

## 7.6.2 Installation ()

The PEP will also cover all aspects of the startup process, including post-delivery storage, handling prior to installation unpacking, and the responsibilities of the supplier and the end user in a more general manner. Transferring the equipment to its place of use can be problematic if equipment dimensions or facility access has not been reviewed in advance. Preparing the area, particularly in cases where the SUS equipment will be used in manufacturing operations, may require specific training. The overall strategy and approach will depend on the nature of the SUS and the level of risk associated with this process (Section 7.4.1). In some cases, non-GxP documentation may be generated or supplied and work permits (e.g., fire safety) secured during the installation processes (e.g., removals).

## 7.6.3 Qualification ()

Qualification of an SUS should include lifecycle testing that reproduces the most impactful steps of the system's lifecycle (e.g., sterilization, transport, freezing/thawing) followed by a verification of the key functionalities and performance attributes (e.g., integrity testing). The lifecycle testing must be designed to cover the intended working space. Please refer to Sections 5 and 7.7 for a discussion of the SUS qualification process and supplier qualification, respectively.

## 7.6.4 Training ()

Training is a GMP requirement and an integral part of SUS technical implementation. The goal of any training program should be to provide specific knowledge and understanding that will support successful operations. Although, the specific training needs are normally described in the user requirements, other training requirements (e.g., for project management and risk assessment) may be critical for the successful implementation of the project. Training programs for SUS should include concepts and principles of operation that might be unique to SUS and unfamiliar to MUS operators.

## 7.6.5 Training Workflow ()

Guidelines for a training workflow are as follows:

- Examine each part of the entire SUS PEP individually and identify the functional areas concerned.

- Identify the training needs in each functional area and assign stakeholders.

- Assemble training requirements in a training matrix (example in Section 10, Appendix 3).

- Determine the scope of each training module. Some subjects may require more than one level of presentation and may require periodic retraining.

- Make sure that a trainee assessment or evaluation process is included at the end of the training module; this is a GMP requirement.

- Take the resources as well as the subject and complexity of the training requirement into account when planning training procedures.

- Some training modules may be delivered by competent team members or operators who are familiar with SUS implementation. Other training modules may be delivered by suppliers.

- Add completed training modules to the individual training records of each stakeholder.

## 7.6.6 An Example of SUS Training (SUS )

Factors that may contribute to the leakage of bags are:

- Poor handling during deployment

- Mechanical stress on films or welds

- Accidental puncture or stressing of the SUS during handling and setup

Incidences of bag leakage may be reduced through the following interventions:

- Changing storage bin and bag design, if appropriate

- Optimizing the method of deployment (i.e., installation)

- Providing operational training

- Providing bag-handling awareness training

- Implementing a control strategy (e.g., remedial procedures by the supplier)

- Monitoring location and type of bag damage for trending

The in-house training program should be based on operational parameters and operator feedback. Operators should be educated and engaged about:

- Working areas and surfaces

- Bag installation and filling

- Prior root causes for failure

- Leakage history and trends

- Root-cause analyses

Additional details on training are provided in Section 6.

## 7.6.7 Safety ()

Health and safety issues involving the control of both recognized and unidentified hazards in order to achieve an acceptable level of risk in the workplace are the responsibility of all stakeholders. Ensuring safety in the workplace as required by local laws can be best addressed by preparing an HSE plan.

## 7.6.8 Preparation of a Health, Safety, and Environment Plan (HSE )

The HSE plan addresses health, safety, and environmental concerns throughout the SUS project. It should be prepared at an early stage and incorporated into the user requirements. Published examples of an HSE plan (although not related to SU technology) can be found in the literature. The HSE plan lists stakeholders and those responsible for performing specific HSE activities. These activities may range from mapping and analyzing the impact of HSE to developing and implementing process improvements. A workflow for developing an HSE plan may consist of the following steps:

- **Screen** — Quick clarification of any HSE aspects related to the project

- **Define** — Overall evaluation of significant HSE concerns during the project lifecycle

- **Plan** — Final HSE plan with stakeholders, activities, responsibilities, and deadlines

The screening and defining activities involve the complete evaluation of the project from an HSE point of view in order to determine HSE concerns that require immediate action. Some of the HSE concerns for a typical SUS project are presented here.

## 7.6.9 Health and Safety Issues Related to SUS (SUS HSE )

SUSs often consist of plastic components that have low resistance to physical stress, pressure, and temperature extremes. A typical setup includes a range of support equipment (e.g., pumps, agitators, scales) that requires manual transportation, installation, and dismantling. This generates a unique set of HSE concerns, as presented in the following lists.

### Safety

- Tubing ruptures at eye level

- Bag ruptures at eye level

- Spills and slippage

- Emergency stops for moving parts

- Automatic restarts after power cuts

- Tubing on floor that may result in tripping

- Loose parts (e.g., media containers, peristaltic pumps) that can fall

- Mobile containers over feet or hands, mobile containers squeezed in doorways

- Mobile containers' finish, corners, and handles

- Connections failing for compressed air and other gases (e.g., CO2 and nitrogen)

- Retention of infectious material

- Failure in connecting to CIP/SIP equipment

- Position of emergency stop during filling operations may be out of sight from dispensing vessel

### Health

- Ergonomics for installation and removal of SUS components (e.g., installing bags, filters)

- Ergonomics for handling bags that contain liquid

- Stress of communicating during filling operations that may be out of sight from dispensing vessel

- Lifting heavy or cumbersome objects

- Insufficient work space and surfaces

## 7.7 Implementation S7: Launch ()

The PEP leads to an initial procurement of equipment for the SUS project in addition to setting out a secure supply chain for continuous operation of the SUS. Initial supplier evaluations are part of the PEP preparation in S4 (Section 7.4.10). In the following paragraphs, these first steps are consolidated with a detailed evaluation of the appropriate tools for the routine management of suppliers.

## 7.7.1 Management of Materials ()

Since continuous sourcing of supplies is critical to keeping the SUS in a productive state, it is important to maintain an efficient routine supplier management (e.g., technical diligence, quality audits, quality and supply agreements, and purchasing, logistical, and waste-handling strategies) to create a robust support for SUS manufacturing operations.

## 7.7.2 Routine or Operational Procurement ()

Routine procurement for SUS (e.g., single-use bioreactors or chromatography systems) requires a supplier qualification program for each of the SUS suppliers included in the PEP. Whenever the risk and complexity of the component in question is considered significant, the qualification program should be implemented using a risk-management approach. Some of the factors to consider are as follows:

- The end user may have to adopt a different approach to the procurement of integrated SUS equipment versus routine SUS components (Sections 5.2, 5.4, 5.5, 6.12.1, 6.14, 7.5.7, and 7.6.2).

- A backup policy of sourcing the same component from at least two different vendors should be adopted, and the suitability for the backup to be interchangeable with the original must be defined.

- The end user must evaluate and audit the supplier's manufacturing capabilities and supply chain to determine the supplier's ability to increase output on demand.

- The relationship between the end user and the supplier should be as transparent as possible in order to enhance the unimpeded operation of the SUS.

- The quality systems of the supplier should be examined as part of a quality audit process, and quality and supply agreements should be implemented (Section 7.7.4).

- The supplier should be provided with a detailed description of the manufacturing process of the SUS, film quality, biocompatibility, and testing (e.g., bioburden monitoring of the SUS), particulates, extractables and leachables, and testing of leakages.

- A contract (supply or manufacturing agreement) describing the commercial conditions that relate to the supply chain, guarantee of delivery, and transport quality should be implemented.

- Both the supplier and the end user must institute robust change-control processes in order to prevent ad hoc modifications to the SUS.

- Time lines from the design to arrival of both MUS and SUS parts should be monitored.

- Evaluation models (preferably at the end user's site) that use mock-ups of preliminary SUS designs (available for training and simulations) should be tested. Testing is an important risk-mitigating procedure. This process may have been started as part of the SUS strategy (Section 7.2.3).

- The end user should adhere to green and sustainable manufacturing policies throughout the entire supply chain and evaluate waste disposal for carbon footprint reduction.

## 7.7.3 Technical Diligence ()

A QbD approach is the best way to qualify suppliers for SUSs. It is based on ICH principles (ICH Q8) and it embraces the complementary concepts of quality-risk management and pharmaceutical quality-system management (as detailed in ICH Q9 and Q10), process validation and verification, and stakeholder management (Sections 7.2.1, 7.2.2 and 7.2.3). These approaches lay the foundation for quality-driven processes built on a science- and risk-based approach and understanding. This is the case not just for the process for which the SUS will be used but also for the SU technology that will be deployed, thus supporting patient safety and product availability. The QbD approach allows for the identification and management of critical sources of variability in which control strategies can be used to maintain a state of control and facilitate the application of continuous improvement throughout the SUS product lifecycle.

Technical diligence can be started at S4 (Section 7.4). Based on established principles, it involves a carefully documented visit to the supplier's site. The outcome should be an appropriate and measured understanding of all the technical issues pertaining to the SUS manufacturing process, readiness for installation, and operation at the end user's facility. Technical diligence should be considered an important step in the supplier qualification process and a complement to the quality audit process. ## 7.7.4 Quality and Technical Agreements ()

Quality and technical agreements clarify the functional aspects of a business relationship by delineating the responsibilities of quality, regulatory, and production personnel within each company in order to enhance communication and prevent ambiguity as referred to in Section 6. A quality agreement should not be confused with a business, supply, or manufacturing agreement. Several templates for APIs are available for chemical and biological products, and the end user may adapt them to an SUS from the Bulk Pharmaceuticals Task Force (Society of Chemical Manufacturers and Affiliates or SOCMA) or the International Pharmaceutical Excipients Council (IPEC).

## 7.7.5 Logistics and Storage ()

The storage and warehouse facilities required for the efficient operation of the SUS should be defined in the PEP. In general, the amount of pallet space required may be calculated as a function of consumption and the dimensions of the shipping container. A risk-based approach may be used to allocate storage space for critical consumables with limited or no alternative suppliers, while standard consumables can be sourced as and when they are needed. Value-stream mapping (a lean manufacturing technique used to analyze the flow of materials and information required to bring a product or service to the user) can be used to identify improvements in lead time or to identify inefficient operations. For example, a value-stream map can show where excess materials are accumulating. Some of the factors to consider are:

- High-risk consumables

- Standard consumables

- Storage policy

- Pallet space

- Frequency of delivery to consumers

- Environmental storage conditions monitoring and control (a GMP requirement)

### 9.11 Handover and Operation Support

The project is handed over to the customer in three steps: 1) ready to run, 2) handover, and 3) final handover. Final handover is the last part of the project.

The project is handed over to the customer with the participation of the SEC project manager, relevant SEC and/or contract project engineers, and representatives from the customer of his or her own choosing.

If the customer wishes to put the area into use before the handover has taken place, the SEC project manager shall ensure that a physical examination of the area takes place together with the customer or the user and that an agreement on operation and maintenance responsibilities is made.

Prior to final handover, deviation lists will be resolved by a cleanup team.

Warranties from third-party vendors, suppliers, and subcontractors are handed over to Company X.
