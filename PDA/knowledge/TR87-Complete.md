## 1.0 Introduction

  

    
    

      

### 1.0 Introduction

      

Glass has long been one of the first materials fabricated by humankind to contain valuables—from perfumes, oils, and potions to the most technologically advanced. Glass has many properties that make it an appropriate packaging material for parenteral drugs, including:

      

        
- Rigidity and durability

        
- Stability and compatibility with filling and shipping processes

        
- Ease of cleaning and sterilization

        
- Gas impermeability and maintenance of container-closure integrity

        
- Compatibility with a wide range of drug products

        
- Optical transparency and clean appearance

        
- Ease of drug product inspection

      

      

Like other packaging materials, glass is not always the appropriate packaging material. Not all glass types are the same in composition and properties. Based on the glass type, some drug formulations can react with glass, particularly those with a high pH or certain buffers.

      

Beyond this consideration is the fact that glass can break. Glass breakage can result in processing downtime, an increased number of production interventions, injury, increased particulate load, and loss of product. Ultimately, patient safety can be put at risk due to a loss of container-closure integrity (possibly including sterility breaches), lost or improper doses, or injury from glass shards and particulates.

      

The resistance of glass to breakage is less a property of the material, as glass is inherently strong, but more about the condition of its surface. For glass to break, both a stress and a flaw must be present at the same place and at the same moment. As will be explained in this technical report, stress, whether from an applied load, impact, or thermal shock, is generally temporary and the glass container will retain its initial integrity and strength. Flaws, however, are permanent once induced.

      

As glass is handled through its manufacturing processes and subsequent pharmaceutical processes, it is vulnerable to surface damage. Best handling practices can preserve the inherent strength of the glass, whereas poor glass handling practices can significantly reduce the practical strength of glass containers. Surface damage induced early in the manufacturing process may manifest itself as failure later in the process or in the field as the container is subjected to subsequent stresses.

### 1.1 Purpose and Scope

      

The purpose of this technical report is to provide a technical understanding of glass, its strength, and its limitations, and to provide guidance in best handling practices for glass vials throughout the pharmaceutical process—receipt, storage, preparation, filling, stoppering, freeze-drying, and capping. This document focuses on glass vials, where surface damage may occur, and where excess stresses may be applied during processing and handling. Many of the principles discussed here can be applied to the handling of glass syringes, cartridges, ampoules, and other glass container types.

      

This report also reviews typical pharmaceutical glass handling processes and identifies areas of concern. Points to consider are presented for line, material, and equipment enhancements as well as guidance and practical approaches for using procedural controls to better employ existing equipment.

## 4.1 — Risk Management Overview

    

        

            

### 4.0 Risk Assessments

            

An effective risk management process can reduce glass handling hazards during initial process development and design and also when evaluating process changes. Further, risk management can be used to discover the root cause of glass breakage events and make informed decisions during investigations. A risk management program can support regulatory discussions and inquiries, ultimately providing regulators with higher confidence in the pharmaceutical manufacturer's capabilities to address these types of events.

            

The scope of the risk management program, specific to glass handling, should include the full lifecycle of the glass container including the manufacture and shipment from the supplier to filling, packaging, and distribution to the customer. There are several helpful documents available to support risk assessment activities, such as ISO documents, ICH guidelines, and PDA technical reports.

            

Risk assessment comprises identification of potential risks, analysis of the probability of occurrence, evaluation of potential impact of the risk, and prioritization for action. Risk assessments can be quantitative or qualitative in nature. A quantitative assessment, in which a numerical value or range of values describing the level of risk, may be used. Or, a qualitative assessment, using relative categories such as "high," "medium," and "low," may be used to characterize the risks. In either case, the definition and rationale for each category should be clearly defined.

            

A risk assessment is performed by a cross-functional team that includes the appropriate SMEs and/or responsible persons to assure accurate analysis. Multiple data sources are used to support the assessment including, among other material, process maps, procedures, manufacturing floor experience, frequency data, deviation and complaint history, and detection capability. These inputs ensure the accuracy of the risk analysis, so they must be robust.

## 4.2 — Risk Controls and Communication

    

        

            

### Risk Controls and Communication

            

Subsequent to risk assessment, risk controls are considered and applied where appropriate to eliminate or reduce risks identified by the risk assessment. The amount of risk control (i.e., risk reduction or elimination) should be proportional to the significance of the risk and should be assessed based on a benefit-to-risk analysis.

            

The resulting risks, mitigating activities, and level of risk acceptance must be effectively communicated. Risk communication and review requires appropriate documentation as well as communication of the risk decisions to the persons authorized to accept the risk and direct response strategies for risk mitigation and follow-up (e.g., functional experts and area or operations heads). Risk response activities need to be reviewed periodically to confirm ongoing compliance effectiveness and proactively reevaluated when new process, equipment, or industry changes influence or alter the underlying risk profile.

## 4.3 FMEA — Failure Mode and Effects Analysis

    

        

            

### FMEA — Overview and Method

            

One effective method used to assess the risk inherent in a process specific to glass handling is the Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA). This method is widely used by many industries, including the pharmaceutical industry. Although its original and intended use had been to proactively identify unacceptable risks prior to failures occurring, the FMEA has also been used successfully in a reactive fashion to evaluate processes for most likely root causes of failures after they occur.

            

The FMEA method is based on the theory that the risk of any particular failure is a composite of the severity, frequency, and probability of the detection of that failure. Cross-functional team participants, with a diverse knowledge of the process, compile an exhaustive list of potential failures and, taking into account the known attributes of the process, thoroughly analyze each one. This output is then evaluated to ascertain if the risk is acceptable or unacceptable. Mitigation activities are identified and completed for those risks deemed unacceptable.

## 4.4 FMEA — When and What to Assess

    

        

            

### FMEA — Timing, Scope, and Data Sources

            

An FMEA should be performed during the initial design and development of the product. Thus, critical elements of the container design can be identified to ensure product quality including, but not limited to, considerations for processing steps such as filling, blast freezing, lyophilization, and final storage conditions. The glass breakage FMEA may be performed in conjunction with a FMEA for other types of potential failures. This can confirm the original FMEA and provide a benchmark risk assessment for production. An FMEA should be performed during evaluations of changes to the process as a specific step of process change control. The existing FMEA should be modified and used to ascertain if risk will change due to the planned process change. Any increase in risk due to the planned change should be mitigated prior to implementation. A periodic review should be completed to confirm ongoing effectiveness of controlling risk levels and identify opportunities for further mitigation through development of new technology or advanced procedures.

            

The specific glass-handling hazards to address as part of an FMEA should include glass particles or fragments that could enter a container from a glass breakage event, and/or events or risks resulting in breakage or a cracked container. The scope of the FMEA should cover as much of the lifecycle of the glass container as possible, including supplier manufacturing and shipment as well as receipt, filling and packaging, and final distribution.

            

During the risk analysis, potential failure modes specific to the glass-handling hazards are identified and the potential causes and current controls are captured. The risk associated with specific failure scenarios is estimated using a risk priority number (RPN). An RPN is determined for each scenario based on the severity of the failure effect, the frequency of occurrence of the potential cause, and the detection and mitigation (prevention) capability that exists based on the controls currently in place. The definition and rationale for rankings used to complete each element of the RPN should be clearly outlined and agreed upon by the FMEA team.

            

Multiple sources of information are employed to determine the RPN. Process maps and procedures that outline the relevant process steps, and the equipment associated with them, are used to ensure that all relevant areas are captured in the evaluation. In addition, and importantly, observations on the manufacturing line are conducted as part of a complete process review of the manufacturing floor or location of the process and equipment. Pictures and videos gathered, either during the process review or from previous efforts, are useful for evaluating the severity, frequency, and detection of each failure mode. Specific glass-breakage frequencies, locations, and root causes, as well as investigation history, are used to further inform the FMEA. Visual inspection capabilities throughout the process should be outlined to provide information relative to detection.

            

Finally, the definition and rationale for rankings used to assess each criterion of the RPN should be clearly outlined and documented.

## 4.5 RPN — Post-Assessment Actions

    

        

            

### Risk Acceptance, Mitigation, and Documentation

            

After all risks are identified and assigned an RPN, each risk is evaluated to determine the appropriate level of mitigation or acceptance of risk and prioritized as to which potential failure modes should be addressed first. The level of mitigation should be proportionate to the level of risk. The rationale for the level of mitigation and/or acceptance of risk should be clearly documented and agreed upon by all FMEA participants and stakeholders.

            

The FMEA results, participants, ranking rationale, and supporting data should be appropriately documented to support future investigations, process change or periodic review, and regulatory discussions. In addition, the results of the FMEA should be communicated to the appropriate stakeholders to ensure mitigation actions are taken and tracked to closure.

## 5.0 Glass Vial Handling Process

      

The glass-handling process begins at the glass vial manufacturer and continues through the final packaging at the pharmaceutical company and shipment to their customer. Numerous transfers take place within that process. Considerations for glass handling must be taken into account for the transfers between process steps.

      

Some points to consider in transfers to and from the warehouse include ensuring the hand truck or lift wheels are in good condition, the operators are properly trained, the facility floor is in good condition with no potholes or deep cracks, and the floor of the transition from room to room is level.

      

Once vials are in the filling process, many types of transfers occur such as tray loading (flipping) or unloading onto the washer loader. Ensuring the proper fit for off-loading trays and the proper alignment of the transfer trays will mitigate the risk of damage to the vials. During transfer, "pushing" on glassware during the transfer can create excessive back pressure, which should be avoided. Any damaged vials should be discarded promptly throughout the process.

      

Caution should be taken to ensure suitable materials of construction are used for transfers on the filling line for belts, guide rails, dead plates, screw feeds, and star wheels.

## 5.1 Packaging for Transport and Storage

      

The glass-handling process begins at the glass vial manufacturer, where the pack-out configuration must be specified to minimize the risk of damage or breakage during transit or shipment from a supplier to a pharmaceutical firm. These configurations should be standardized for each container type and size: shrink-wrapped module type with glass-to-glass contact or a tray with or without cell partitions or separators. The number of containers that are held in a module or tray and their arrangement is typically driven by the infeed of the pharmaceutical processing equipment. The packaging format is also sized to maximize pallet space. Where modules are used, ensure proper packing and shrink-wrapping of the module (e.g., vial alignment, tightness of shrink-wrap) and avoid flexing and module-to-module impact during handling which may cause glass-to-glass abrasive damage.

      

The remaining aspect of the pack-out configuration should include a review of such factors as: the set-back of the load from the edge of the pallet; module-stacking configuration; need for corner protection and straps; need for slip sheets between layers; method of securing the load to the pallet; and total height of the pallet. Double-stacking of pallets, especially during transit, should be avoided.

## 5.2 Integrated Line with Multiple Transfer Points

      

To ensure the pharmaceutical processing equipment is designed appropriately, collaboration with machine manufacturers is recommended. Where equipment from different machine manufacturers is used, ensure that each machine is properly integrated into the line. An appropriate risk assessment should be conducted during the machine design and line integration. Typical transfer points at the pharmaceutical company that should be evaluated include belt transfers, guide rails, dead plates, screw feeds, star wheels, and pick-and-place devices.

      

Vials can be automatically or manually pushed from a tray onto an accumulation table, where they are transferred to an infeed screw. To ensure the proper feeding of vials into the screw, proper spacing between vials, which is determined by belt speed before the screw, is important. The number of vials that back up prior to the screw should be kept to a minimum to reduce glass-to-glass contact between vials that could potentially cause damage.

      

Machine designers endeavor to remove potential negative impacts to the vial; however, not all risks can be removed. With the transport of vials, abrasion to the body is always a risk; therefore, proper materials should be used to reduce friction on the bottom, heel, and body of the vial. The greatest risk in vial damage comes during the transition from infeed screw to star wheel or from star wheel to star wheel (or inspection turret). Proper timing of the star wheels is critical, as is precision machining of size-change parts and low-backlash gearing or clutches. If the vial is not handled smoothly, with care taken to reduce impact forces, the vial body may experience excessive stress, which can cause breakage, cracks, or other damage.

      

A risk assessment should be conducted to properly design the process and materials for collecting downed vials that are ejected from the line if they may be reintroduced into the line. The assessment should determine the appropriate drop height, the amount of cushion or padding required, the frequency of emptying the eject bin, and the level of inspection required prior to reintroducing the vials to the line. The edges of the eject bin should be rounded to avoid sharp edges that could damage the vials.

## 5.2.1 Accumulators

      

Accumulators are used between segments of a filling line where the throughput speeds of the specific units of equipment may vary or be intermittent, or where one unit may be more likely to experience periods of down time (e.g., after depyrogenation and before filling, or after stoppering but before capping). Accumulators are also used for loading and off-loading trays at the beginning, during transfers, or at the end of line.

      

Accumulators may be of several types, including rotary tables, push (back pressure), and bi-flow.

      

### 5.2.1.1 Key Parts of the Process and Potential Impact on the Containers

      

The accumulation of vials often leads to abrasion damage to the glass surface. This happens especially where large numbers of vials are tightly gathered and the vials continue to revolve and rotate against one another, or where one or more vials become trapped in an eddy of the flow and remain for a long period of time. This type of damage is typically evidenced by circumferential scuff or scratch rings. These rings are usually observed at contact areas just below the shoulder and above the heel of the vial. While every surface discontinuity affects glass strength, the significance of these, relative to the potential for container failure, should be evaluated through the process described in Section 5.4.

      

The type of damage may occur wherever rotary tables are used, but it is most prevalent when these tables are used immediately after depyrogenation. Depyrogenation dehydrates the glass surface, eliminating its natural lubricity and exacerbating friction damage. In addition to damaging the glass surface, visible and subvisible glass particulates may be generated in the process.

      

### 5.2.1.2 Points to Consider

      

As good general practice, the table should be properly sized for the planned line speeds and large enough so that it is not loaded to more than one-third of its capacity. Additionally, directional flow control devices should be engineered to prevent eddies (opposite circular flows) in the flow of vials.

      

Guides and rails should be constructed of suitable, nonabrasive plastic materials. Transfers onto the table should prevent any impact, either glass-to-glass or glass-to-metal, especially at the heel of the vial. To facilitate this, the turntable itself should be slightly lower than the dead plate or belt from which the vials are being received. This difference, however, must be small enough that it does not cause vials to tip over while still allowing for a smooth transfer.

      

Monitoring and controlling the vial accumulators can mitigate the risk of damage to the glass surface, and limiting the number of vials allowed to accumulate on the table to a practical minimum is recommended.

      

Throughput speeds should be as consistent as possible for each segment of the line, reducing the need for accumulation. When this is not possible, the line should be regulated to minimize accumulation. Accordingly, the movement of vials on the table should also stop if the line itself is stopped. This will preclude excessive accumulation of vials onto the table.

      

If turntables or accumulation tables must be used, they should be designed to minimize the momentum of the containers (e.g., low speed settings) in order to reduce the level of forces between containers, thus reducing the occurrence of abrasions in the glass and generation of the glass particles.

## 5.3 Vial Washing

      

In parenteral packaging, glass vials are typically washed prior to being sterilized for filling. Vial washing is conducted in order to ensure removal of such materials as bacterial toxins, chemical substances, and particulates so that the product meets expectations of identity, purity, quality, and safety.

      

### 5.3.1 Washing Machines

      

Washing machines are available in various designs, the most common being linear tunnel and rotary. Vials are washed with either a diving spray nozzle or a stationary, nonpenetrating spray nozzle. When using diving spray nozzles, where a needle enters the vial and sprays the vial from the bottom up, there is a risk of needle strikes on the bottom of the vial, the inside wall, or the finish of the vial that could result in a bent needle, glass damage, and particulate generation (glass chips). The risk of vial strikes increases when multiple vials must be aligned with an array of needles for simultaneous washing. The process parameters, including needle depth and centering, must be adapted for varying vial sizes or designs. Needles should be routinely inspected for straightness to prevent needle strikes. Some newer washing machines can be equipped with needle strike alarms to aid in risk management.

      

For washers that use buckets to hold the vials, inverted or double-stacked vials (two vials within the same bucket) are vulnerable to breakage due to needle strikes. Automated systems that detect and eject upside-down vials or detect double-stacked vials can be incorporated to mitigate this risk. Manual intervention procedures to address vial orientation issues may be needed for some washing systems.

      

For rotary washers, water jet pressure should be moderated to ensure vials are not forcibly ejected from the grippers by an excessive jet stream.

      

In limited applications, batch washers are used to wash vials one tray or module at a time. Loading plates and corresponding washing needles must be matched to the respective tray configuration and vial size to avoid glass damage or breakage during loading. A visual check and preventive maintenance plan should be in place to identify bent or damaged needles. Care should be taken during loading and unloading to prevent glass damage or breakage.

## 5.4 Dry Heat Sterilization and Depyrogenation

      

Dry heat sterilization and depyrogenation of the vials occurs immediately after washing and is usually accomplished using dry heat. Dry heat ovens may take the form of tunnels or batch ovens. The ovens often serve as the transfer portals from the preparation area into the aseptic area. Vials are usually sterilized at ≥ 160 °C for up to two hours and depyrogenated at approximately 325 °C for at least thirty minutes, but the times and temperatures can vary depending on the specific process requirements.

      

These two processes may increase the coefficient of friction (COF) of the glass surface and make the vials more susceptible to abrasion. This is of particular concern if the vials will be subjected to glass-to-glass or glass-to-metal contact, such as on a rotary accumulation table. This type of friction damage is typically evidenced by scuff rings or scratches on the vials. This damage is usually observed just below the shoulder and above the heel of the vials. Though small and relatively minor in appearance, scratches and scuffs can potentially contribute to breakage under sufficient mechanical or thermal stresses further downstream. In addition to damaging the glass surface, visible and subvisible glass particulates may also be generated in the process. The longer the time the vials are subjected to high temperatures, the more profound the increase of the COF. In a case where vials are in contact with one another, one vial may bond with the surface of another vial, causing them to stick together. When these vials come apart, distinct vertical marks (stuck marks) will be apparent. These marks may act as stress concentrators and fracture origins as the vials are subjected to subsequent loads during downstream processing or handling.

      

### 5.4.1 Continuous Convection Tunnel

      

After the air-drying process, the glass vials (either in single file or in rows) pass onto a conveyor belt over a dead plate. Back pressure from the continuous loading of the belt pushes the material onto the conveyor.

      

Ideally, the conveyer belt should be operated in a "pull mode" and run at a slightly higher speed than the feed rate from the washer in order to maintain a small space (e.g., ~1 mm) between the rows of vials. This transition is sometimes supported by a pushing device to complete the transfer from the washer to depyrogenation tunnel.

      

Maintaining a minimal downward transition of the vials is recommended to reduce the probability of damaging the heel region.

      

Vials are then moved through the convection tunnel on the conveyor at a predetermined rate. Tunnels typically include three heat zones: a preheat zone, a hot zone (where the glassware is exposed to sufficient temperature for sufficient time to effect sterilization/depyrogenation), and a cooling zone. Depyrogenation guidelines are presented in PDA Technical Report No. 3.

      

The vials are then pushed out of the tunnel, using slight back pressure, across a dead plate, and onto the next stage of the process by an accumulator.

      

### 5.4.2 Points to Consider

      

As best practice, containers should be dry prior to exposure to depyrogenation temperatures (Zone 2), as residual moisture may attack the interior surface of the glass causing etching or glass corrosion.

      

In the event downstream processes come to a stop, it should also halt additional vials from entering the tunnel to prevent overloading, while allowing the in-process batch to complete the validated depyrogenation cycle.

      

Viewing windows are recommended so the entire depyrogenation area can be observed for evidence of overcrowding, fallen or broken containers.

      

Although the glass structure is not impacted directly by the temperatures used for depyrogenation, leaving the containers in the tunnel beyond the validated depyrogenation process time is not recommended.

      

The temperatures achieved during depyrogenation remove the protective water skin from the containers, leaving them more vulnerable to surface damage, such as scratches.

      

### 5.4.3 Batch Depyrogenation Oven

      

Another method for dry heat sterilization and depyrogenation is the use of a depyrogenation oven, which allows for processing of smaller rather than continuous processing. Trays of vials are loaded into the oven on racks and depyrogenated according to a validated cycle. The trays are typically unloaded under a Grade A (ISO 5) unidirectional airflow and then transported to the vial-filling line. Due to the absence of the protective water skin, consideration should be given during loading, unloading, and transport to limit movement of the glass vials within the stainless-steel trays to prevent damage or breakage.

## 5.5 Vial Filling

      

Drug product is added to vials at a filling station. Vials are transferred to the filling station after washing and depyrogenation by a conveyor belt, with a star wheel or by a gripper that picks up the vial by the neck. Vials are filled with a filling needle by either a fixed nozzle or a diving nozzle apparatus. Once filled, the vials move along the line to the stoppering station where they are stoppered either by direct placement (where the stopper is picked up by vacuum) or by a rotating wheel.

      

### 5.5.1 Impacts

      

Vials transported by means of a conveyor belt experience glass-to-glass contact as they move along the line, which can result in abrasions to the glass. Vials that are transported by a star wheel or by gripper have glass-to-glass contact only at the vial gate, where they accumulate, and where damage can occur. For filling lines that use grippers (i.e., pick and place) to move vials, the gripper configuration and the amount of pressure exerted on the vial are critical and should be compatible with the vial neck size. Ideally, grippers should be constructed of plastic rather than metal.

      

Needle installation and alignment should be verified at set-up. A needle strike may occur when a filling needle is misaligned with the vial being filled; the needle strikes the top of the vial and can chip the vial and/or bend the needle. Needles can also become bent when striking vials that have offset finishes. Damage in the lip region of the vial can go unnoticed through inspection as the stopper and seal can mask the damage. Regular inspection during the production batch should assess misalignment of and damage to filling needles.

      

During weight checks, vials can be damaged or broken when transferred from the filling line to the weigh station. Vacuum-assisted vial grippers can reduce the incidence of damage but can also be impacted by particulate matter if the vials are not adequately clean.

      

### 5.5.2 Points to Consider

      

The filling process can be improved to further reduce damage to the vials by replacing the metal with plastic throughout the line. Damage to the vial due to needle strikes can be reduced by changing the stainless-steel filling needles to disposable plastic. In contrast to the impact from a metal needle, a plastic filling needle would fail or bend before the vial would break.

## 5.7 Lyophilization

  

    

      

### 5.7 Lyophilization

      

Lyophilization, or freeze-drying, is a process through which water is removed from the liquid solution by freezing the product, subliming the ice, and desorbing residual water to low levels. Vials are filled with liquid product and stoppers are partially seated into the vials on the filling line prior to accumulation for the lyophilizer-loading operation. The vials are loaded onto the freeze-dryer shelves, either manually using bottomless trays, or via an automated mechanical loading system. Upon reaching the specified end conditions of the process, the stoppers are fully seated into the vials with the hydraulic shelf-stoppering system. Vials are then unloaded from the chamber. Manipulation of the vials during the loading, stoppering, and unloading processes, in addition to the freeze-drying process itself, can all be potential sources of glass vial handling issues.

    

    

      

### 5.7 —

      

        

### 5.7.1 Loading and Unloading

      

During the traying, transfer, loading, and unloading process, glass-to-glass and glass-to-metal interactions occur similar to other vial manipulation operations, such as filling and capping. Vials are typically conveyed by sliding across stainless-steel surfaces and then nested with other vials in a close-packed configuration, experiencing forces applied from surrounding vials during movement. Vials could be accumulated onto individual trays and transferred manually to the freeze-dryer or transferred by automated loading systems. Typical automated material-handling systems transfer and load the entire lyophilizer shelf, row by row, or by some other indexing method. The tooling used to manipulate these vials should be compatible with the vial size and type. Material-handling methods with significant glass-to-glass and glass-to-metal contact can lead to small scratches, checks, or cracks, creating a weakened region in the glass that can potentially lead to breakage at that location when stresses are exerted.

      

Over time and repeated use, cleaning, and sterilization, wear on the bottom surface of the tray can become evident and warping of the transfer ring may occur, leading to "pinched" vials when nested. In turn, the prominence of glass defects can increase the risk of container failure due to increased friction and limited mobility.

### 5.7.2 Lyophilization Process Concerns

      

The potentially significant stress imposed on the glass from the expansion of product during the freezing and drying steps is a concern. As the product freezes, crystals form and the product can expand, often upward from the bottom of the vial, but also outwardly and down from the product surface toward the bottom. The extent of this effect will vary depending on the specific product formulation. Although this expansion would not initiate glass breakage by itself, the risk to vial breakage increases with the presence of a defect, such as a blemish due to scratches in the vial from glass-to-glass and glass-to-metal abrasion. Vial design, especially in the heel area, may also have an impact on breakage during expansion. With a large fill volume, internal forces are increased significantly. Examples of glass fractures and lensing can occur during the freeze-drying process. Other product effects can also impose stress on the container.

### 5.7.3 Seating the Stopper

      

Final seating of the stopper occurs within the lyophilizer chamber at the end of the lyophilization process, when the shelves are either lowered or raised to fully insert the partially seated stoppers. The suspended shelves most often are driven by a hydraulic ram. Pressure exerted by the hydraulic system is set initially for the most demanding need, such as 2 cc or 3 cc vials with a 13 mm finish. Such small vials have greater density across the shelf surface area and require the greatest pressure (force per unit area) to fully seat the stoppers. In order to apply a uniform pressure to each vial across the shelves, the shelves should be level and planar. If there is any significant variation to pressure distribution, the force on the vials can be localized significantly in one area, which could lead to glass breakage.

      

One area to assess is the container-to-container height variation; stoppering forces will be concentrated to any vials that may be slightly taller than the surrounding vials. The difference in vial-stopper height, in some instances, has been sufficient to result in broken vials. When a partial shelf-load of vials left at the end of the filling operation are being placed on the lowest shelf, there must be a sufficient number of vials, and they must be evenly positioned across the shelf to accommodate the pressure evenly. Failing to evenly distribute the vials across the shelves can lead to cocked shelves and distortion of the shelf suspension hardware. In addition, for container-closure systems with a poor stopper fit or misplaced stopper, cocked closures upon stoppering can lead to excessive force and, potentially, broken vials.

      

Some breakage observed in a lyophilizer may not be due to improper handling but may be attributed to vial nonconformities such as leaners, rocker or uneven bottoms, or an improperly designed or formed bottom radius.

### 5.7.4 Points to Consider

      

Once risks for glass handling defects and breakage have been assessed, based on material-handling conditions and variables for the specific product and container configuration, there are systems that can be implemented to reduce the risk. The material of construction on contact surfaces, such as guide rails on the filling line during material-handling operations, has an impact. The condition of the shelves, conveyors, trays, and rings should be flat, level, and smooth, and monitored on a predetermined schedule for adequate maintenance. Vial pushers, indexers, and other vial manipulators should be compatible with the system. Using plastics for vial contact surfaces and eliminating pinch points can help alleviate glass-to-glass or glass-to-metal friction (stickiness). The container-closure industry is making strides in decreasing the risk of vial defects; for example, preloaded vials, already indexed into tubs, can limit glass-to-glass and glass-to-metal interaction.

      

The height of the container-closure system should have an allowable tolerance range specified to prevent significant variation across a shelf and unequal forces. Thermal stresses should be limited, where possible, during the lyophilization process. Product expansion behavior during the solidification process should be known and potentially limited, by limiting fill height in the container, altering the ratio of crystallizing to noncrystallizing components, or considering alternate freezing methods, such as controlled nucleation, which initiates nucleation of ice from the liquid surface.

## 5.8 Capping

  

    

      

### 5.8 Capping

      

Vial capping is the last step in the aseptic process to fully seal a parenteral vial. The primary seal of a parenteral vial packaging system is the interface of the elastomeric stopper flange and the land sealing surface on the crown of the vial. Capping is the process of sealing the vial by compressing the flange of a rubber stopper against the top surface of the glass vial, forming the primary seal, and then crimping an aluminum ferrule around the stopper and vial finish. Sufficient vertical load force is necessary to optimally compress the stopper, achieve sufficient seal tightness, and assure container-closure integrity. A side load may also be imparted when the metal is crimped.

### 5.8.1 Impacts

      

Regardless of the sealing method, the stress created in the glass from these loads of an optimized capping process (< 130 N force) should be well below that where breakage would be expected. Breakage, however, is sometimes observed at the capper. Glass failure at capping is likely the manifestation of significant surface damage caused to the vial earlier in the process.

      

Improper set-up of the capper or excessive vial variability (e.g., offset necks, bent necks, large necks, leaners, rocker bottoms, laps (folds) in the neck) could cause the neck to come in contact with the sealing rail or disc, causing surface damage to the neck of the vial that may propagate into fractures. Transfer of vials into and out of the capper usually consists of a combination of belts, screw feeders, and star wheels.

### 5.8.2 Points to Consider

## 5.9 Terminal Sterilization

  

    

      

### 5.9 Terminal Sterilization

      

Terminal sterilization is performed once the vial is stoppered and capped to assure the sterility of the product. Moist heat, which is applied in the sterilizer (oven) by spraying hot water on the product, is the method used for parenterals in vials. The process uses pressure, temperature, and time parameters. The loading pattern of the units in the chamber should be uniform to ensure proper heat distribution.

      

Vials that are in contact with one another in the sterilizer may bond with the surface of another vial, causing them to stick together. When these vials come apart, the stuck marks may act as stress concentrators. Breakage of vials during terminal sterilization is most likely due to an upstream incident. The practical strength of the glass may have been reduced, thus reducing the burst pressure of the vials.

### 5.10 External Washing

      

Transitions that convey the glass containers into and out of the external washing unit operation (e.g., star wheels, screw feeds) must be aligned and synchronized. ## 5.11 Inspection

  

    

      

### 5.11 Inspection

      

Filled containers should be inspected individually for contamination or other defects. Inspections can be carried out using three methods: manual, semi-automated, or automated. The sorting of accepted or rejected vials should follow the recommendations outlined in this section.

    

    

      

### 5.11

      

  

  
  

    

      

### 5.11.1 Manual Inspection

      

Manual inspection consists of a human inspector handling the vials directly. Typically, an inspector manipulates a vial under a calibrated light source and in front of both white and black backgrounds to enable proper inspection. During the inspection process (as with all manual processes), proper and careful handling should be employed to avoid damaging the vials. The accept-or-reject decision is based on the inspector's knowledge and training.

### 5.11.2 Semi-Automated Inspection

      

Semi-automated inspection also uses a human operator to make accept-or-reject decisions; however, the vial handling is done with automation. Typical semi-automated inspection machines include a roller conveyor for vial transportation and spinning of the vial, while also providing proper lighting and mirrors that allow the inspector a full view of the container. Handling the vials within a semi-automated inspection machine should limit glass-to-glass contact and minimize impact during transitions.

### 5.11.3 Automated Inspection

      

Automated inspection can be performed using both sensor-based and camera-based systems and, depending on the process, can incorporate different handling designs. Appropriate, container-specific change parts and set-up are necessary to ensure proper handling, inspection, and prevention of additional damage. For example, the base attachments and spindle caps on machines using inspection turrets are designed in such a way that the vial is held in place during the spin process to prevent the vial from being thrown from the turret ("spin-out"). Vials are sorted depending on the results of the inspection process. For ejected vials, limit glass-to-glass contact and minimize impacts during transitions as the ejected vials may be reinspected manually or reintroduced into the automated inspection process.

### 5.11.5 Points to Consider

      

Two upgrades are recommended to improve the handling of glass vials during the inspection process: puck-handling and direct-spin system. With puck-handling, after the vial is fed through an infeed screw and star wheel, it is placed on a specialized puck. This puck is then transported throughout the machine carrying the vial. The advantage of this system is the reduced interaction between machine and glass. The machine passes the puck from the star wheel to an inspection turret and back to the star wheel without any additional forces exerted on the glass vial.

      

The direct-spin system provides an individual servo motor for each location on the pre-spin or inspection turret. That allows increased control of the spin speeds of the vial, optimizing the machine for a larger range in containers and reducing the risk of vial "spin-out".

## 6.3 Outer Surface Treatments or Coatings

### 6.3 Outer Surface Treatments or Coatings

Glass containers may be subject to abrasion during conveyance on production lines and during transportation, resulting in scratching and bump checks that may adversely affect their mechanical resistance. Strengthening the outer surface and/or applying a lubricating layer can mitigate possible damage.

The COF between bare glass surfaces is very high. Vials are typically sterilized by dry heat depyrogenation prior to entering the filling line, which removes water and most lubricious organics and produces a surface with a COF ranging from 0.9 to 1.0. Under these conditions, glass-to-glass contact should be minimized. To mitigate particle generation and any subsequent damage, glass may be coated to reduce the COF.

In some molded-glass containers, the exterior surfaces may receive up to two coatings to reduce damage and particle production. The first is applied to the container exterior as tin chloride prior to annealing, forming a thin, tin-oxide film. The second coating is applied after annealing and is organic. The tin-oxide coating helps improve the adhesion of the organic to the glass surface, and the combination improves the mechanical scratch- and damage-resistance of the container's outer surface.

For tubular containers, a low-COF polymer chemical-composition coating can be applied to the surface. These types of exterior coatings can persist through the pharmaceutical processes, such as washing and depyrogenation, and maintain a low COF (< 0.5 under a 30 N load).

## 8.1 Coefficient of Thermal Expansion (CTE) |

  

    

      

### 8.1 Coefficient of Thermal Expansion

      

Most materials expand and contract upon heating and cooling, respectively. The extent of expansion is dependent on the specific glass chemical composition. This expansion can be measured using techniques such as push-rod dilatometry and is expressed as the change in length per temperature unit, otherwise known as the coefficient of linear thermal expansion (CTE). The CTE for a glass is generally reported as mean value over a specific temperature range. The higher the CTE, the more the material will expand when heated and, conversely, the more it will shrink when cooled. ASTM E228 and ISO 7991 provide guidance on measuring the CTE of glass.

      

The CTE is also a main factor in determining the resistance of glass in a flawless, nonstrengthened state to sudden temperature changes. Increasing resistance to thermal shock is correlated with decreasing CTE.

      

Soda-lime silicate glass with a wall thickness of 0.5 mm can withstand a maximum rapid temperature difference of approximately 110 °C without cracking. Standard borosilicate glass of the same thickness can endure greater temperature differences. Borosilicate glasses having a CTE of 5.0 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ and 3.3 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ with a wall thickness of 0.5 mm can withstand approximately 220 °C and 340 °C rapid temperature differences, respectively, and are generally more resistant to thermal shock than soda-lime silicate glass.

      

Due to the fact that glass has poor thermal conductivity, a thinner wall thickness is recommended for thermal resistance while a thicker wall will withstand greater mechanical loads. These calculations are theoretical, however, and refer to glass in a flawless state. In practice, glass is never in a flawless state; therefore, the presence of surface flaws may be more significant than the CTE alone in determining thermal shock resistance.

## 8.2 Stress Inside the Glass |

  

    

      

### 8.2.1 Melting and Solidification Behavior of Glass

      

The transition of a solid crystal to molten liquid occurs instantaneously at fixed melting temperatures, Tm. In contrast, solid glass does not possess a melting temperature. Instead, the viscosity of glass changes continuously as a function of temperature when going from the solid state to the liquid state. This behavior can be illustrated using the Volume-Temperature (V-T) diagram.

      

In order to form a typical glass, a molten liquid must be cooled below Tm without crystallizing, thereby becoming a supercooled liquid. The point at which the slope of the V-T curve deviates from the supercooled liquid line marks one boundary of the "glass transformation range." The intersection point of lines extrapolated from the solid and liquid sections of the glass V-T curve is one definition of the glass transition temperature, Tg.

      

The Tg of a glass is dependent on chemical composition and is of practical importance in glassmaking. The Tg lies between the annealing point and the strain point. The annealing point is approximately located at the upper temperature boundary of the glass transformation range and can be broadly defined as the temperature at which a majority of residual stress within a glass is dissipated within several minutes. The strain point is approximately located at the lower temperature boundary of the glass transformation and can be broadly defined as the temperature at which a majority of residual stress within a glass is dissipated within several hours. In addition, the strain point marks a limit for structural rearrangement of the glass. Knowledge of these temperature points are used to design annealing schedules that remove a majority of forming-induced stress within a glass article.

### 8.2.2 Physical Definition of Stress

      

Physical stress (σ) can be defined as the force (F) per unit area of a material element (A). There are two important types of stress in the pharmaceutical packaging handling process: tensile stress and compressive stress. If the atomic positions are changed by an external force (tensile or compressive force), the atoms will attempt to return to their original, optimal distance by counteracting the external force.

### 8.2.2.1 Thermal Stress During Container Manufacturing

      

During the formation of a container, the raw material is melted, and a tube is drawn and cut into lengths. The glass has a low, even stress distribution at this point in the process. Heat must be applied again to the glass tube when forming a container. Forming the container employs temperatures of 1000 °C or greater, which causes stress at the bottom and at the opening of the container while the rest of the tubing remains cool.

      

While heating up one part of a glass tube, the atoms being heated increase their oscillation. As the heated part is cooled down quickly after the forming process, the atoms do not have sufficient time to rearrange to their former structure. Rather, they are stuck in the position they occupied at that time, and both tensile and compressive stress is created.

      

In order to restore the glass to a low-stress state, the entire vial is annealed by heating to about 20 °C above Tg for some period of time and then slowly cooled in a strictly controlled process in an annealing lehr (oven). After the annealing process is complete, a majority of forming-induced stress in the glass should be reduced to an acceptable level. In this manner, introducing thermal stress into the glass and releasing it again does not lead to an accumulation of stress and weakening of the glass. However, if this stress is not released properly, the risk of the glass breaking at the zone of tensile stress is greater.

      

The amount of residual stress can be determined by polariscopic measurement devices.

      

Except for the sealing of ampoules, residual thermal stress cannot be introduced in the filling process as the temperature never exceeds the Tg of the glass.

      

Like the tubular process, stress is introduced into molded vials or bottles during the forming process. A gob of molten glass is dropped into a mold and formed via a press-and-blow process or blow-and-blow process. The vials must undergo an annealing process with controlled cooling to relieve the stress.

      

If the temperature difference at a certain spot on the glass exceeds the particular thermal shock parameter of that glass, however, micro-cracks can result. Thermal shock occurs when a thermal gradient causes different parts of an object to expand by different amounts. This phenomenon can occur where cold metal meets hot glass. For example, residual water from the washing process can cool the bottom of the vial in the depyrogenation oven while the glass is heated from the outside.

      

During depyrogenation, the containers are typically heated to about 350 °C in order to reduce the risk of possible pyrogen contamination. As this temperature range is below the typical Tg temperatures of glasses used in pharmaceutical packaging, this process poses no risk in terms of thermal stresses. It does, however, facilitate the introduction of scratches that decrease the mechanical strength.

### 8.2.2.1.1 Exposure to Cold Temperatures

      

Glass will be exposed to temperatures of approximately -40 °C to -80 °C during lyophilization and of -150 °C to -196 °C for cold storage, including cryopreservation. The lower the CTE, the more stable the glass is in terms of thermal shock resistance, but the rate of change must be extremely high for thermal shock breakage.

      

Use of glass with lower CTEs may reduce the breakage risk due to rapid rate of change; however, surface defects still play the major role in glass breakage. Breakage during exposure to cold temperatures are primarily due to flaws present in the glass and the outward pressure of the internal materials, not thermal stress. The fewer defects there are on the bottom area and area of drug contact, the less likely the glass will break. Vial breakage during the lyophilization process is usually due to the mechanical stress from the expanding solution rather than thermal stress.

## 8.3 Mechanical Strength of Glass |

  

    

      

### 8.3.1 Ductile versus Brittle Materials

      

Along with thermal stress, physical load can introduce a great amount of stress onto glass. Breakage will occur if the physical load is greater than the practical strength of the container.

      

Materials are generally differentiated into two failure mode categories: ductile and brittle. Ductile materials, like metals (and some polymer materials), deform under tensile stress; that is, they undergo a plastic deformation (changing their shape) rather than breaking immediately. During plastic deformation, the molecules in the material are able to move. This provides the material's ability to bend before breaking.

      

Brittle materials break before the molecules in the material can move sufficiently to support bending. The molecules are literally torn apart in place. Brittle materials, such as glasses, are unable to undergo plastic deformation and therefore, break abruptly.

### 8.3.2 Practical Strength

      

For brittle materials like glass, practical strength depends on such parameters as geometric aspects of the glass piece, flaws inside and on the surface of the glass, residual stress inside the glass, and the interior and exterior mechanical loads on the glass. An equation to determine practical strength for brittle materials, developed by A. A. Griffith:

      

        **Griffith Fracture Criterion**
        

```
K = σ × Y × √c

Where:
  K  = Stress Intensity Factor
       (amplification of tensile stress at flaw tip)
  σ  = Applied stress
       (from external force or internal pressure)
  Y  = Geometric Factor
       (location and shape of the defect)
  √c = Critical Dimension
       (square root of defect depth/size)
```

        

Breakage occurs when K exceeds the fracture toughness (KIC) of the glass.

      

      

The stress (σ) applied to the glass can result from an impact with a material harder or equally as hard as glass (e.g., metal). Stress can also come from inside the container, such as when internal pressure is built up during lyophilization, from vertical pressure (e.g., during crimping), or from side pressure (e.g., a jammed screw feed).

      

The geometric factor (Y) takes into consideration the flaw geometries, including the relative location of the flaw (through thickness crack, edge/surface flaw, corner flaw, surface flaw shapes).

      

The critical dimension (c) describes the geometric aspects of the defect — the larger the radius of the flaw tip, the more the glass is weakened. A very deep flaw will lower the glass strength much more than a surface scratch.

      

Ultimately, breakage only occurs if both the stress and the flaw or stress riser are present in tandem, and the combined phenomena exceed the practical strength of the particular piece of glass.

### 8.3.3 Tensile and Compressive Stress

      

Glass always breaks at the point of tensile stress location. When compressive stress is exerted from one side, it always induces tensile stress in certain other places, but these areas of tensile stress are not always clearly detectable.

      

(a) A downward force (e.g., capper) creates tensile stress in the outer surface of the neck and heel and compressive stress in the side wall.

      

(b) A side or squeeze-force (e.g., a backed-up conveyor or screw feed) creates tensile stress on the interior surface at the exterior load location and exterior surface 90 degrees from load.

### 8.3.4 Surface Condition and its Impact on Practical Strength

      

During the lifecycle of a glass container, it passes through a number of handling steps. Any of these steps can create varying degrees of surface discontinuities, like scratches or even microfissures in the glass, that may decrease the functional strength of that particular glass container. Only in combination with a specific stress on the glass, however, does breakage occur.

      

Break force, strength, or failure probability measurements can be used to determine the extent that surface discontinuities and physical impact result in decreased practical strength. In certain cases, the effects of different handling processes can be determined by these measurements.

## 8.4 Adsorption Layer on the Glass |

  

    

      

### 8.4 Adsorption Layer on the Glass

      

When glass is freshly heated or cleaned, the surface reacts with adsorbates from ambient air (e.g., water, hydrocarbons) that then saturate the surface. Initially, the water is only adsorbed, and a monolayer is developed. After a few weeks, however, the water diffuses deeper into the surface. This adsorption layer acts like a cover on the glass and protects it from scratches, and the more developed layer can better protect the container from scratches. This protective layer can be removed by heating the glass (e.g., depyrogenation oven); a monolayer will evaporate more quickly under dry air/heat conditions. As the kinetics of reestablishing the protective layer are very slow; submerging the glass in water will not accelerate the reaction.

      

This phenomenon of building up and evaporating the protective layer is equally valid for all glasses. This also infers that scratches and sticking behavior is a matter of glass handling rather than the composition of glass itself.

      

The water layer not only protects the glass from scratches, but it also plays a significant role as a lubricant, decreasing the friction between glass containers or between glass and metal. This property becomes especially important during the filling process as it can limit the number of scratches that the containers may receive, potentially allowing an increase in the speed of the filling line.

## 8.5 Chemical Strengthening |

  

    

      

### 8.5 Chemical Strengthening

      

Chemical strengthening by ion exchange creates a residual compression layer on the inner and outer glass vial surfaces that resists the applied tensile stresses required to propagate flaws. It can also hinder minor to moderate surface damage, blocking the routes along which cracks could otherwise propagate and, thereby, prevent breakage. Chemical strengthening can be performed on aluminosilicate, borosilicate, or soda-lime silicate glass containers with sufficient available sodium ions to increase their mechanical strength. Because the glass tubing is heated in the container formation process, any chemical process must be performed on the final container, not the glass tubing.

      

To avoid thermal shock, the empty glass containers are gradually heated to the bath temperature before immersion in a potassium melt (e.g., KNO₃) where an ion exchange reaction takes place. In this reaction, sodium ions (Na⁺) from the near-surface regions are exchanged for potassium ions (K⁺). Potassium ions are larger in diameter than sodium ions (K⁺'s radius is roughly 0.38 Å larger than that of Na⁺), leading to compressive forces that let the glass withstand higher external loads.

      

At the end of the designated immersion time, the containers are removed from the bath, allowed to cool, undergo thorough salt-removal process, and are then dried.

      

This surface compressive stress creates an additional barrier to prevent the formation and propagation of checks as well as suppressing the creation of visible glass particles. Chemical strengthening also improves the overall robustness of the vial, reducing the probability of breakage from high-stress loading and particulate generation from fragmentation.

      

Applying an additional protective coating to the outer surface of the glass container will lower its COF and minimize surface damage during frictive sliding contact.

## 9.0 Appendix II: Investigations

      

Production personnel must be aware when a failure occurs. Any failure should be immediately investigated; this may include conducting a risk assessment to determine the cause and the corrective action steps to be taken. These steps should be detailed in a standard operating procedure (SOP) and the corrective actions taken should be documented. Production personnel should be well trained in proper glass handling, conducting initial-failure assessments, sample collection, and sample preparation for fracture analysis.

      

Certain risks must be well understood when glass damage or breakage is observed. Risks go beyond the immediate failure of a broken or damaged container, potentially including subsequent failure of additional containers and possible glass particulate contamination in adjacent containers on the line. Steps should be taken to mitigate any risk by isolating and evaluating adjacent vials before batch release. If glass fragments are observed on or near the line, it is crucial that all the other remnants be found and collected. The objective is to ensure that no vials—chipped, damaged, or containing glass particles—continue to be processed that pose a potential risk to subsequent steps or harm to the patient.

      

In order for breakage to occur there must be a:

      

        
- Force applied to the item to generate stress in the glass

        
- Flaw to act as the origin of the fracture

      

      

The general aim of fracture analysis is to identify the:

      

        
- Type and magnitude of force that was applied to the container

        
- Type and severity of the defect at the fracture origin

        
- Cause of the breakage, whether due to:
          

            
    - Defective container that failed at normally expected levels of applied force

            
    - Excessive force applied to a commercial-quality container

### 9.1 Initial Investigation

      

Initial evaluations by production personnel can often help identify the root cause(s) of the failure. Observations can include the breakage pattern, line location, and relative size and number of fragments; these comments can provide important clues as to the cause of the breakage, for example:

      

        
- A chip observed near or around the filler, or on a belt or accumulator just beyond the filler, often indicates a filling needle strike on the mouth of the vial.

        
- Numerous small fragments or a complex fracture pattern observed often indicates the breakage was due to unusual excessive forces on the container; small fragments could also occur when a primary fragment (such as a chip) is subsequently crushed by another applied load. The excessive force may be due to misaligned transfer mechanisms, a pinch point, a mispositioned container, or an out-of-spec container.

        
- A simple fracture pattern or just a few large fragments observed indicates a low-energy break, possibly a manifestation of a significant flaw or damage that occurred upstream.

      

      

Sometimes, the only corrective action needed is a simple line adjustment and a determination that the correct change parts are in place. Other times, more extensive corrective actions may be needed.

      

Performing a detailed analysis of the breakage using fractography (fracture analysis) may be the best way to determine or confirm the exact cause of a failure. A qualified fractographer can identify the point of failure origin, providing clues to the type of surface defect and the cause, including the material at contact. Fractography can also estimate the force that caused the failure. Fractography requires that all, or as many of the fragments as possible, be collected for evaluation along with the portion of the sample containing the fracture origin. As much as possible, fragments from different individual vials should not be comingled; they should be treated carefully so as not to create secondary defects on the broken glass.

### 9.2 Root Cause Analysis

      

Proper analytics and root-cause determination can help identify and address systemic failures in the glass handling process. If no trained fractographer is onsite, there are fractography services available to outsource analysis of breakage samples.

      

In cases where the breakage is witnessed or the cause is known, fracture analysis may provide further information regarding the mechanics of the failure. However, if the cause of fracture is not known, before adjustments are made to the line handling, it is very important to perform a thorough fracture analysis to identify the exact cause of failure.

      

        
- Simple breakage patterns with a few large fragments sometimes suggest a significant flaw in the container; the source of the flaw needs to be identified in order to be corrected.

        
- Complex breakage patterns with many small fragments suggest a high-energy break due to excessive applied stresses.

      

      

To mitigate future incidents of glass breakage, root cause analysis may include:

      

        
- **Fracture Analysis** – Conducted by a qualified fractographer

        
- **Supplier Evaluation** – Review for atypical levels of incoming defects, dimensional conformity, etc.

        
- **Incoming Supplies Inspection** – Review alignment with supplier testing procedures and criteria

        
- **Equipment and Handling** – Assess areas prone to breakage, including glass-to-glass contact, glass-to-metal contact, high back-pressure zones, transitions, and others; high-speed cameras are often used to assess glass breakage sources on the line

        
- **Line Clearance Procedures** – Remove all open containers that could be impacted during a glass breakage event

        
- **Training** – Assess employee training and ensure that operations staff are trained to understand the criticality of their response to glass breakage

        
- **Metrics** – Review glass breakage frequency and locations of impacted lots

        
- **Visual Inspection** – Review visual inspection process and defect rates

        
- **Packaging and Distribution** – Review downstream processing for potential issues

### 9.3 Corrective and Preventive Actions

      

Corrective and preventive actions (CAPA) should be defined as part of the glass breakage investigation. A list of typical CAPAs would include:

      

        
- Collaborate with supplier to eliminate the source of incoming defects and ensure high-quality supply

        
- Remediate sources of glass breakage found on the line, upstream and/or downstream

        
- Install active monitoring through a walk-down of the line or continuous operator-monitoring

        
- Enhance line-clearance procedures to eliminate the potential impact of glass-breakage events

        
- Install shielding to limit the impact of glass-breakage events

        
- Train operators, supervisors, and all staff to reinforce the importance of proper glass handling and the appropriate response to glass-breakage events
