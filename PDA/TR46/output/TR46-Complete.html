1.0 Introduction

This guidance document complements the information provided in PDA Technical Report No. 39 (Revised 2021): Guidance for Temperature-Controlled Medicinal Products — Maintaining the Quality of Temperature-Sensitive Medicinal Products through the Transportation Environment by going beyond manufacturer-focused issues and addressing issues specifically encountered within the "last mile" of the distribution of medicinal products and devices. This revision updates the original 2009 technical report to account for expanded regulations, the development and implementation of newer, more advanced technologies and supply chains that have become more extensive and complex.

1.1 Purpose and Scope

"Last mile" distribution is an often-used logistical term that refers to the transport of product from the manufacturing facility to the end user. For the purposes of this technical report, last mile is defined as the final transfer of a drug from the manufacturer or wholesaler to the end user which could be either the patient or the entity (e.g., pharmacy, hospital) that provides the drug to the patient.

Transfers earlier in the distribution process involve organizations with greater knowledge of the product and the resources to ensure carefully controlled conditions of storage and transport compared to those available to the end user. As such, PDA Technical Report No. 46 (Revised 2024): Last Mile: Guidance for Good Distribution Practices for Pharmaceutical Products to End User provides recommendations and tools based on current best practices to ensure that product quality is maintained until the final handoff.

TR46 vs TR39 定位關係

TR39（2021年修訂）聚焦的是製造商至批發商之間的溫控運輸，涵蓋大型冷鏈基礎設施、驗証性研究（qualification studies）與運輸容器測試，是供應鏈的「主幹道」管控。

TR46（2024年修訂）則接棒TR39，處理批發商或製造商到最終患者/藥局/醫院這最後一段路。這段配送的特徵是：

  • 組織GDP知識薄弱（藥局人員、快遞員、甚至患者本人）
  • 溫度暴露風險高（最後一段往往無溫控車輛）
  • 管制鏈鬆散（監控設備不足、紙本記錄主導）

兩份技術報告合用，才能涵蓋完整的藥品供應鏈溫控管理。

最後一哩的快遞業類比

「最後一哩」源自電信與物流產業。以快遞業為例：

  • 主幹網絡（製造商→區域配送中心）：效率高、溫控穩定、全程追蹤
  • 最後一哩（配送中心→家門口）：最難控制——受交通、天氣、配送員素質、電梯故障等影響

藥品配送亦然：大型物流商的倉庫到配送卡車管控精良，但藥品離開藥局窗口、交到患者手中的這一段，往往沒有冷藏袋、沒有溫度記錄，是品質風險最高的環節。

2024年修訂新增重點

相較於2009年原版，此次修訂主要更新：

  • 法規更新：序列化（Serialization）、DSCSA（美國藥品供應鏈安全法）、FMD（歐盟藥品偽造指令）
  • 新技術：區塊鏈（Blockchain）追溯、無人機（drone）配送、IoT感測器
  • 地區擴展：納入非洲、中國、東南亞等新興市場的最後一哩挑戰
  • 直接配送到患者（DTP）：特別針對細胞與基因治療（CGT）產品的居家輸注服務

台灣實務：最後一哩的挑戰

台灣藥品配送最後一哩面臨獨特挑戰：

  • 冷藏鏈斷點：許多社區藥局無符合規格的冷藏設備，患者取藥後以一般購物袋攜帶
  • 偏遠地區：離島（金門、澎湖）與山區部落運輸時間長，溫度累積暴露顯著
  • 居家護理：生物製劑（胰島素、生長激素）居家注射用藥，患者冰箱溫度管理參差不齊
  • 電商藥局：網路購藥興起，常溫快遞運送需要冷媒包但規範尚不明確

TR46提供的框架可直接應用於台灣健保藥品配送SOP的制訂與稽核。

Glossary Terms (A–Z)

ALCOA+
An acronym standing for Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate ("ALCOA") and Complete, Consistent, Enduring and Available ("+"). It is a set of characteristics that all data must meet to ensure data integrity. Adherence to ALCOA+ principles is the basis for Good Documentation Practice.
Corrective Action, Preventive Action CAPA
Action to eliminate the cause of a detected nonconformity or other undesirable situation. Corrective action is taken to prevent recurrence, whereas preventive action is taken to prevent occurrence.
Customer
In distribution, the customer is the trading partner or reseller; in direct-to-consumer situations, the customer is the end user who is either the patient taking the product or the person administering the product to the patient such as a physician.
Data Integrity
The degree to which data are complete, consistent, accurate, trustworthy, reliable and that these characteristics of the data are maintained throughout the data life cycle.
Downstream
Refers to the demand side of the supply chain that consists of one or more companies or individuals who participate in the flow of pharmaceuticals moving from the manufacturer to the end user.
Good Distribution Practices GDP
Defined as the part of quality assurance that ensures the quality of the pharmaceutical product is maintained by means of adequate control of the numerous activities that occur during the distribution process.
Good Manufacturing Practices GMP
Best practices in manufacturing of pharmaceuticals or biopharmaceuticals, from a regulatory standpoint, regarded as the minimum current good manufacturing practice for methods, facilities, and controls used for the manufacture, processing, packaging, or holding of a drug to ensure it meets requirements of safety, identity, strength, quality and purity.
Last Mile
A multidimensional, cross-industry term that defines a point in the supply chain where the product or service directly faces the customer, end user, or patient.
Mean Kinetic Temperature MKT
The single calculated temperature at which the total amount of degradation over a particular period is equal to the sum of the individual degradations that would occur at various temperatures. MKT may be considered as an isothermal storage temperature that simulates the nonisothermal effects of storage temperature variation. It is not a simple arithmetic mean.
Metadata
Data that describe the attributes of other data and provide context and meaning. Typically describe the structure, data elements, inter-relationships, and other characteristics of data (e.g., audit trails). Metadata also permit data to be attributable to an individual or, if automatically generated, to the original data source.
Original Record
Data as the file or format in which it was originally generated, preserving the integrity (accuracy, completeness, content and meaning) of the record — e.g., original paper record of manual observation, or electronic raw data file from a computerized system.
Pedigree, e-Pedigree
A pedigree is a record containing information regarding each transaction resulting in a change of ownership of a pharmaceutical, from sale by a manufacturer, through acquisition and sale by a wholesaler, until final sale to a pharmacy or person furnishing, administering, or dispensing the pharmaceutical. An e-Pedigree is the electronic form of this record.
Phase Change Material PCM
A physical material that stores and releases thermal energy when freezing or melting. A PCM releases energy when freezing (latent heat energy) and absorbs energy when melting.
Radio Frequency Identification RFID
An automatic technique for identifying objects using radio frequency transmissions. An RFID system generally consists of a tag, reader, antenna, and software. Tags comprise a semiconductor chip with memory, processing capability and a transmitter connected to an antenna.
Reverse Logistics
The process of planning, implementing, and controlling the efficient, cost-effective flow of raw materials, in-process inventory, finished goods and related information from the point of consumption to the point of origin for the purpose of recapturing value or disposal.
Shipping Lane
An established route of transportation between a specific point of origin to a specific destination. More defined routes will consistently use the same combination and types of transportation vehicles and transfer procedures, but this may not be observed through all shipping lanes throughout the last mile.
Supply Chain
The chain of events or process which includes all storage and movement of raw materials, work-in-process inventory, and finished goods from point-of-origin to point-of-consumption.

ALCOA+ 逐字解析

ALCOA+是FDA數據完整性稽查的核心框架，在GDP配送記錄管理中同等重要：

  • Attributable（可歸屬）：誰在何時做的記錄？必須可追溯到個人
  • Legible（清晰可讀）：溫度計錄單手寫必須清楚，不可塗改
  • Contemporaneous（即時）：溫度異常發生時立即記錄，不可事後補記
  • Original（原始）：保留原始數據，不可只保留摘要
  • Accurate（準確）：溫度記錄器經過校正，數值可信
  • +：Complete（完整）、Consistent（一致）、Enduring（持久）、Available（可取得）

違反ALCOA+常見例子：配送員事後補填溫度日誌（違反C）；用修正液塗改記錄（違反O）。

CAPA 在配送偏差中的應用

糾正措施（CA）：針對已發生的偏差，消除根本原因。例：某批次冷藏藥品溫度超標，CA = 立即隔離批次 + 根因調查 + 更換冰排配方。

預防措施（PA）：防止類似問題再次發生。例：夏季高溫季節主動縮短配送時間窗口、要求使用更高規格的保溫箱。

在最後一哩GDP稽查中，稽查員最常查CAPA的有效性：措施是否真正解決了根本原因，還是只是表面的「再訓練人員」。

Upstream vs. Downstream 方向辨析

製造商 → 批發商 → 藥局/醫院 → 患者 ← Last Mile →

Upstream（上游）：從患者方向看回去，是製造商/原料供應商側。供應側。

Downstream（下游）：從製造商方向看出去，是需求側，最終到患者。

TR46的「Customer」定義靈活：B2B情境下，客戶是交易夥伴（批發商）；DTC（直接到消費者）情境下，客戶是患者或施藥的醫師。這個雙重定義直接影響GDP文件管理對象的設計。

MKT（平均動力學溫度）深度解說

MKT 不是算術平均溫度，而是用Arrhenius方程計算的「降解等效溫度」。其意義是：在MKT這個單一溫度下連續儲存，藥品降解量等同於在實際波動溫度歷程中的降解量。

計算公式：

T_MKT = (ΔH/R) / [-ln((e^(-ΔH/RT₁) + e^(-ΔH/RT₂) + ... + e^(-ΔH/RTₙ)) / n)]

其中：
  ΔH = 活化能（Activation Energy），藥品降解反應的能量閾值
       USP 預設值 ≈ 83.144 kJ/mol（20 kcal/mol）
  R  = 氣體常數 8.314 J/(mol·K)
  Tᵢ = 第 i 個時間點的溫度（單位：Kelvin）
  n  = 溫度測量點數

實務意涵：若某批次藥品在配送途中有2小時暴露在38°C環境（夏日無空調廂型車），MKT計算後可能等效於在28°C下儲存更長時間。透過MKT可客觀評估是否超出允許範圍，而非武斷地以單一峰值溫度判斷批次命運。

Metadata 與 Original Record 的法規重要性

FDA數據完整性警告信中，最常見的違規是刪除或覆蓋原始電子數據，只保留人工篩選後的「乾淨」記錄。

Metadata例子（配送場景）：溫度記錄器的稽核軌跡（audit trail）——誰在何時設定了記錄器、是否有人修改過時間戳記。這些元數據與溫度數值本身同等重要。

Original Record要求：若使用電子溫度記錄器，原始數據必須保留原始格式（如.pdf或廠商專屬格式），不可只保留匯出的Excel摘要，因為Excel版本無法證明數據未被竄改。

Pedigree/e-Pedigree：藥品追溯的法規演進

Pedigree（藥品所有權鏈記錄）是現代藥品序列化法規的前身：

  • 美國：DSCSA（Drug Supply Chain Security Act，2013年）要求2023年起全面序列化，取代早期的Pedigree概念，實現逐件追溯（unit-level tracing）
  • 歐盟：FMD（Falsified Medicines Directive，2019年生效）要求藥品唯一識別碼與防偽裝置
  • 台灣：衛福部食藥署推動藥品追蹤追溯系統，逐步要求藥廠上傳產品序號至國家資料庫

e-Pedigree是區塊鏈（Blockchain）應用於藥品供應鏈的理想場景：每次所有權移轉即寫入不可竄改的分散式帳本。

相變材料（PCM）的物理原理與應用

PCM利用物質固液相變時的潛熱（Latent Heat）儲放大量熱能，維持特定溫度平台：

  • 結冰冰排（0°C）：一般冰袋，適合2–8°C保冷；缺點是可能造成凍傷（藥品直接接觸）
  • 乾冰（-78.5°C）：固態CO₂，用於深冷運輸（如mRNA疫苗、CGT產品）；需注意CO₂揮發的密閉空間危險
  • 特製PCM（2–8°C）：專門配方的鹽水溶液或有機材料，相變點精確設計在2–8°C，不會造成凍傷，是高端藥品冷鏈保溫箱的主流選擇
  • 特製PCM（15–25°C）：用於「室溫」藥品的夏季保護，防止過熱

PCM的核心優勢：在相變過程中，即使環境溫度大幅波動，箱內溫度幾乎保持恆定，比一般隔熱保溫更穩定。

逆向物流（Reverse Logistics）的GDP意涵

在藥品供應鏈中，逆向物流涵蓋以下場景：

  • 藥品召回（Recall）：已流出的藥品從藥局/醫院/患者端回收，是最複雜的逆向物流場景，須追蹤到每個序號
  • 到期品退回：藥局將近效期或過期藥品退回批發商，須確保逆向鏈的GDP文件完整
  • 冷鏈中斷後的退貨：溫度偏差（excursion）發生後，受影響批次需退回並進行處置決策（disposition），逆向物流記錄是品質決策的依據
  • 患者退藥：直接配送（DTC/DTP）場景中，患者退回未使用藥品的處理流程

台灣藥事法對藥品回收有嚴格分級（自願召回/強制回收），逆向物流的GDP文件是回收完成報告的核心證據。

Shipping Lane 配送路線管理

每條Shipping Lane（配送航線/路線）在GDP框架下應被獨立驗証（Qualification）：

  • 夏季 vs. 冬季條件：同一路線在不同季節的溫度風險完全不同
  • 最後一哩的特殊性：主幹物流路線有標準化的溫控貨車，但最後一哩（城市快遞、機車外送、步行取藥）往往沒有統一交通工具，每種模式需分別評估
  • Lane Qualification（路線驗証）：使用溫度記錄器進行實地配送測試，記錄最壞情境（worst-case）下的溫度分佈，作為配送容器設計的依據

縮寫速查：TR46稽查最常見詞彙

稽查員在TR46框架下進行GDP稽查時，以下縮寫出現頻率最高：

  • MKT：溫度偏差處理決策的核心計算工具，稽查員會要求查看MKT計算記錄與原始溫度數據
  • CAPA：偏差調查結束後的必要產出，無CAPA等同於承認系統失效
  • ALCOA+：稽查任何配送記錄（紙本或電子）的判斷準則
  • GDP：TR46的整體合規框架，需與GMP接軌但有配送特定要求
  • DTP/DTC：細胞治療、個人化醫療時代的新型配送模式，監管關注度快速上升

DSCSA vs. FMD：兩大序列化體系的比較

兩套法規都要求藥品唯一識別，但執行細節不同：

  • DSCSA（美國）：要求製造商、批發商、藥局三層追蹤，系統互通採用GS1標準，最終目標是建立全國藥品電子追蹤系統（EPCIS共享）
  • FMD（歐盟）：著重在藥局端的「點確認」（point-of-dispensing verification），藥局每次調劑前必須掃碼驗証並解封防偽裝置
  • 台灣藥品追蹤：參考DSCSA/FMD架構，目前要求特定管制藥品與高風險品項上傳序號至食藥署系統

e-Pedigree是Pedigree的電子化形式，DSCSA與FMD事實上已取代傳統Pedigree概念，但TR46保留此術語以涵蓋未實施序列化法規地區的追蹤需求。

自我測驗：術語辨別

閱讀以下場景，判斷涉及哪個TR46術語：

  1. 配送員將胰島素放在室溫摩托車後座配送2小時，到達藥局後如何評估藥品是否可用？（答：MKT計算）
  2. 藥局要求退回過期血液製品至批發商，整個退貨流程的GDP文件要求是？（答：Reverse Logistics + ALCOA+）
  3. 政府要求查驗某批次藥品從製造商到患者的完整移轉記錄，使用哪個追蹤機制？（答：Pedigree/e-Pedigree 或序列化）
  4. 保溫箱內的藍色凝膠包在整個配送過程中維持2–8°C，這是什麼材料？（答：PCM，相變點設計在2–8°C）

3.0 Pharmaceutical Supply Chains

The pharmaceutical supply chain has a high level of complexity as it varies regionally and in the number of stakeholders including but not limited to manufacturers, distribution centers, wholesalers, points of dispensing, and points of administration. In addition, many countries have public and private pharmaceutical supply chains managed by governmental organizations and wholesale distributors respectively.

Before a patient receives a pharmaceutical product, distribution routes should be appropriately managed in the supply chain to maintain product safety, integrity, quality, and efficacy. Therefore, the roles and responsibilities of supply chain partners and the design of the supply chain should be clearly determined to manage the environmental conditions, regulations, and product requirements for delivery to patients.

每往下一層，供應鏈管控能力系統性下降：

  • GDP知識：製造商100% → 批發商80% → 藥局/診所40% → 患者<5%
  • 設備條件：工業級冷藏倉庫 → 商用冷藏車 → 家用冰箱（溫度不穩定）

3.1 Distribution in Supply Chains

Distribution of medicinal products in the supply chain can be divided into internal distribution and external distribution. Internal distribution can be considered as the transport of a commercial product within a pharmaceutical manufacturer's supply chain; that is, all transports from the manufacturing facility to the packaging facility to the warehouse to a distribution center. During internal distribution, the marketing authorization holder (MAH) is the owner of the goods at all times.

An internal distribution network with direct distribution to the customer (also called direct sales) is subject to review by inspectorates as part of the manufacturer's good manufacturing practices (GMP) and good distribution practices (GDP) authorization. Internal distribution is typically called the "first mile" and direct distribution from the manufacturer to a point of administration (e.g., hospital) is referred to as the "last mile." In some unique circumstances, manufacturers may ship directly to the patient such items as test kits and orphan drugs, for example, in compliance with local laws and regulations.

External distribution, on the other hand, is product that is no longer owned by the manufacturer. It covers the transport of commercial products through various steps in the customer's supply chain — transport from a pharmaceutical manufacturer's distribution center to commercial customers (including wholesalers, retailers, and buying groups like non-governmental organizations (NGO)), clinical facilities, or directly to the patient. External distribution is normally not part of a product filing, and it can be called "middle mile" as well as last mile to the end user the patient.

Moving through the supply chain, product volumes decrease from product-pallet shipments in the first mile to single packs in the last mile. Meanwhile, the mix of products typically varies in a shipment from one or a few product(s) in the first mile to multiple or more than a hundred products in the middle mile to one or a few products in the last mile. The distance and duration of shipments typically decrease from thousands of miles and multiple transportation days by air and ocean in the first mile to a few kilometers and hours or minutes in the last mile. Exceptions do exist, however; for example, the last mile can take days in rural and remote areas where transportation by foot or via rivers by boat is the only option.

Within the external distribution network, there may be two supply chains for pharmaceuticals — public and private. Public supply chains are owned by the government and focus on essential medicines for the population, like vaccines and products for the prevention or treatment of malaria and HIV. Very often NGOs donate products to public supply chains to support the population in low-income and/or developing countries. The public supply chains typically comprise central, regional, and district government distribution centers, public pharmacies, health centers, and public hospitals. Private supply chains typically comprise wholesalers, pharmacies, clinics, and healthcare professionals authorized to dispense medicinal products to the public. The external distribution network is overseen by inspectorates as part of the wholesaler's GDP authorization.

  • 私人供應鏈（批發商主導）：商業藥品，透過批發商→藥局/診所→患者，由GDP批發授權管控

兩套體系的最後一哩均面臨相同挑戰，但公共供應鏈在低收入國家的基礎設施落差更顯著。

MAH（Marketing Authorization Holder）在配送中的責任邊界

MAH在內部配送期間始終擁有產品所有權，責任最重。一旦轉移至外部配送，所有權轉移，但MAH仍需確保：

  • 授權有資質的批發商承接外部配送
  • 建立技術協議（Technical Agreement）規定儲存/運輸條件
  • MAH可在某些國家同時持有批發配送授權，直接管理中間哩和最後一哩

3.3 Supply Chains in Europe

Most pharmaceuticals in the European Union (EU) are distributed via private supply chains, while the public supply chains are used primarily to distribute vaccines to vaccine centers for vaccination programs. In addition, NGOs buy pharmaceutical products from manufacturers for donation programs that they then provide to public supply chains on other continents like Africa and Asia.

The Guidelines of 5 November 2013 on Good Distribution Practice of Medicinal Products for Human Use (2013/C 343/01) are the most important GDP guidelines in the EU: they are in force in all EU member states and in several other countries as well. The GDP guidelines of the non-EU member states such as Russia and the United Kingdom share many similarities with the EU GDP guidelines.

The EU GDP guidelines require a wholesaler to be licensed to distribute an approved product into the region in which the wholesaler will sell that product. While there are many actors in the supply chain, in the EU:

  • ~60% of all medicinal products sold are distributed to pharmacies through pharmaceutical wholesale distributors
  • ~33% are distributed by the manufacturer directly to hospitals
  • ~8% are distributed directly to pharmacies

Pharmaceutical full-line wholesaling activity consists of the purchase, warehousing, storage, order preparation, and delivery of medicines. Full-line wholesalers carry and distribute a complete range and depth of products. They have the capability to deliver all medicines in their geographical area of activity typically on the same day or within 24 hours on working days. They also provide working capital, extend financing services, and fund stock and receivables of pharmacies and healthcare professionals.

In most EU member states, pharmaceutical full-line wholesalers respond to Public Service Obligations (PSO) or carry a Public Service Function as charged by Article 81 of Directive 2004/27/EC:

"The holder of a marketing authorization for a medicinal product and the distributors of the said medicinal product actually placed on the market in a Member State shall, within the limits of their responsibilities, ensure appropriate and continued supplies of that medicinal product to pharmacies and persons authorized to supply medicinal products so that the needs of patients in the Member State in question are covered." — Article 81, Directive 2004/27/EC

Apart from this provision, many EU member states (e.g., Belgium, France, Germany, Greece, Italy, Slovenia, Spain, Portugal) have appended a separate Public Service Obligation on wholesale distributors to guarantee that certain products be made available across the region through permanently available buffer stocks. One of the minimum requirements for wholesalers of medicinal products to obtain their distribution authorization (as set by Article 84 of Directive 2001/83/EC) is compliance with the principles and guidelines of GDP.

溫度類別詳解：從液氮到常溫

供應鏈溫度管理的五個層級，冷鏈設備需求與成本依序遞減：

  • 深冷（-196°C）：液氮容器（Dewar flask），用於細胞治療產品。最後一哩的超難挑戰——患者端幾乎不可能具備液氮設施，通常需要在醫院完成給藥。
  • 冷凍（-25 ~ -15°C）：工業/商用冷凍庫、冷凍車。mRNA疫苗（輝瑞/莫德納）的超低溫（-70°C/-20°C）要求在COVID-19期間凸顯了最後一哩挑戰。
  • 冷藏（2~8°C）：最常見的冷鏈需求。胰島素患者每日自注射，家用冰箱是最後一哩的主要儲存設備，溫度波動風險最高。
  • 受控常溫（15~25°C）：看似容易，實則夏季台灣室溫常超過28-33°C，需特別注意車輛/物流中心溫控。
  • 常溫（≤30°C）：一般藥品，但仍需避光、防潮。

Qualified Person for Distribution (QPD)：EU GDP的關鍵角色

類似製造端的Qualified Person（QP），EU GDP要求批發商指定一名具備GDP知識與經驗的合格責任人（QPD），負責：

  • 確保批發活動符合EU GDP指南
  • 監督質量管理體系的建立與維護
  • 偏差、投訴及召回的最終責任人
  • 代表公司接受稽查機關查核

QPD的存在確保批發配送端有一個「有名有姓」的GDP責任人——這是最後一哩問題中最缺乏的元素。

EU FMD（Falsified Medicines Directive）序列化要求

EU Directive 2011/62/EU（防仿冒藥品指令）自2019年起要求：

  • 唯一識別碼（Unique Identifier）：每個藥品包裝的二維碼包含：產品代碼、序列號、批號、有效期
  • 防拆封裝置（Anti-tampering Device）：包裝一旦開啟即可視覺識別
  • 核實義務：藥局在配藥前必須掃描驗證序列號（連接歐盟醫藥核實系統EMVS）

這是EU最後一哩GDP的重要工具——確保到達患者手中的藥品未被偽造或掉包。台灣目前尚無全面強制藥品序列化，但已在規劃推動。

Public Service Obligation：為什麼批發商要有「義務」？

純粹市場機制下，批發商只會服務利潤高的城市地區，偏遠地區或小量需求的藥品可能面臨缺藥。PSO要求批發商：

  • 覆蓋指定地理區域的所有藥品需求（不論利潤）
  • 維持「緩衝庫存」（buffer stocks）確保供應連續性
  • 通常在同日或24小時內送達

這在本質上是一種「公共服務交叉補貼」機制，確保最後一哩不因商業考量而斷鏈。

3.4 Supply Chains in Africa

Pharmaceutical supply chains in Africa present a unique set of challenges that differ substantially from those in high-income regions. While significant progress has been made in strengthening medicines regulatory systems across the continent, a combination of infrastructural limitations, regulatory capacity gaps, and the prevalence of informal distribution networks continues to undermine product quality and patient safety at the last mile.

Key Structural Challenges

Infrastructure limitations represent the first and most immediate challenge. Reliable electricity supply is inconsistent in many sub-Saharan regions, with frequent power outages disrupting cold chain continuity. Road infrastructure in rural areas limits access for conventional refrigerated vehicles, and alternative last-mile delivery modes — including motorcycles, bicycles, and river transport — offer minimal temperature control.

Regulatory and enforcement capacity varies widely between and within countries. While several national medicines regulatory authorities (NMRAs) have made notable progress — for example, Tanzania's TFDA (now TMDA), Ghana's FDA, and Nigeria's NAFDAC — enforcement at the sub-national and community level remains inconsistent. The capacity to conduct routine GDP inspections of wholesalers, pharmacies, and informal outlets is constrained by limited budgets and personnel.

Informal markets constitute a significant share of pharmaceutical distribution in many African countries. Unlicensed vendors, open-air markets, and unregulated retail outlets provide access to medicines for populations that lack proximity to formal healthcare facilities. However, these channels carry significant risks: storage conditions are uncontrolled, product provenance is opaque, and the proportion of substandard and falsified (SF) medicines is substantially higher than in formal channels. The WHO has estimated that up to 1 in 10 medicinal products in low- and middle-income countries is substandard or falsified.

WHO Prequalification Programme

The WHO Prequalification (PQ) Programme plays a critical role in assuring the quality of medicines supplied to African public health programs, particularly for HIV/AIDS, malaria, tuberculosis, and vaccine programs funded by international donors such as PEPFAR, the Global Fund, and GAVI. Medicines that receive WHO PQ status have been evaluated against internationally accepted standards for quality, safety, and efficacy, and manufacturers are subject to ongoing surveillance inspections.

WHO PQ is particularly significant because many African NMRAs lack the capacity to independently evaluate complex biologics or generic medicines. By relying on WHO PQ as a proxy for quality assurance, national procurement agencies and NGOs can more confidently source medicines that meet international standards, reducing the risk of procuring substandard products even when local regulatory oversight is limited.

挑戰一：基礎設施（Infrastructure）

非洲最後一哩的基礎設施缺口在全球最為嚴峻：

  • 電力不穩：撒哈拉以南非洲平均每天停電4-8小時，冷藏倉庫溫度波動成為常態。柴油發電機是常見備援，但燃料成本高且供應不穩定。

  • 替代配送模式：摩托車（motorcycles）、自行車、獨木舟是農村最後一哩的實際配送方式。WHO的EPI（擴大免疫規劃）針對這些場景設計了適合的疫苗包裝規格。

挑戰二：監管執法能力（Regulatory Enforcement）

非洲各國監管能力差異懸殊：

  • NMRAs能力不足：許多國家的國家藥品監管機構（NMRA）僅有不到20名員工，難以對全國批發商、零售藥局進行系統性GDP稽核
  • 非正規市場管控空白：露天市場和無牌販售者在許多國家處於監管真空，執法行動偶發且缺乏持續性
  • 進展中的合作：非洲藥品監管協調（AMRH）計畫與非洲藥品局（AMA，2023年成立）正在推動監管能力建設和協調工作

挑戰三：非正規市場與仿冒藥（Informal Market & SF Medicines）

非洲仿冒/劣質藥品問題在全球最嚴重：

  • 規模：WHO估計低中收入國家每10份藥品中有1份為劣質或偽造（Substandard & Falsified, SF）
  • 高風險品類：抗瘧疾藥（青蒿素類）、抗生素、抗結核藥是偽造最多的品類，直接導致治療失敗和抗藥性
  • 近端原因：正規渠道藥品價格不可及（affordability）、供應短缺是患者轉向非正規市場的主因

3.5 Supply Chains in Brazil

In Brazil, wholesalers, logistic service companies, distributors, pharmacies, clinics, and hospitals must be licensed to distribute, store, commercialize, and administrate an approved product, according to the Portaria CVS 1/2020 as shown in Figure 3.5-1. The MAH (Marketing Authorization Holder) may engage a third-party company for the distribution or logistics services, according to Resolution of the Collegiate Board (RDC) n° 234/2018, in which case the contracted company is jointly and severally liable with the contracting company for the technical and operational aspects of the inherent activity.

GDP is regulated by RDC n° 430/2020 (modified by RDC n° 653/2022) which requires suitable and adequate procedures, premises, installations, and equipment that will ensure proper storage and distribution of medicinal products. This requirement includes:

  • Temperature-mapping of distribution routes
  • Temperature-monitoring during product distribution
  • Shipping qualification for all medicines (not only temperature-sensitive products), employing a risk-based approach for product quality

Humidity monitoring during the last mile is not considered necessary as properly chosen container closure systems protect against any humidity excursions which might be experienced. However, Brazilian regulation does require humidity monitoring unless a risk assessment conducted by manufacturers and wholesalers determined there is no product impact prior to last mile. Overall, product distribution within the temperature and humidity approved in the regulatory application is an expectation, and the range of allowed temperature and humidity excursions must be technically justified, supported by data, and documented in a risk analysis.

The MAH of a medicinal product must ensure appropriate and continued supply of that medicinal product. In the event of a temporary supply discontinuation, a notification to health authorities is required, according to RDC n° 18/2014.

The main challenge in last-mile transport via road, air, and water, is the size of Brazil as the mode of transport is executed by a logistic operator who subcontracts trucks, flights, and boats.

3.6 Supply Chains in the United States

In the United States, there is little difference between the public and private supply chains as public-health efforts normally use private supply chains to achieve their goals. Most prescription drugs travel through a complex system that includes large primary wholesalers, smaller secondary wholesalers, and a small number of specialty wholesalers. In addition to purchasing overstock from manufacturers and primary wholesalers, secondary wholesalers buy and sell drugs among themselves, as well as selling them to pharmacies. Thus, prescription drug products may undergo multiple handoffs within a supply chain.

In the Code of Federal Regulations (CFR), the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) defines wholesale distribution as the "distribution of prescription drugs to persons other than a consumer or patient…" (Part 205.3(f)) and defines a wholesale distributor as "anyone engaged in wholesale distribution of prescription drugs…" (Part 205.3(g)). Additionally, the CFR defines an authorized distributor as "a distributor with whom a manufacturer has established an ongoing relationship to distribute such manufacturer's products" (Part 203.3(b)). Wholesalers must have a method to ensure that shipments of prescription drugs are only sold and delivered to licensed entities.

Neither 21 CFR 203 nor 21 CFR 205 addresses packaging and shipping requirements for temperature-controlled products. However, U.S. Pharmacopeia (USP) General Chapter <659> Packaging and Storage Requirements and General Chapter <1079> Risk and Mitigation Strategies for the Storage and Transportation of Finished Drug Products are very clear in their guidance for the storage, distribution, monitoring, calibration, training and qualification of "cold chain" equipment.

USP <659> and USP <1079> — Cold Chain Guidance

USP <659>, revised in 2020, gives specific guidance on packaging, temperature, storage, and distribution requirements.

Also revised in 2020, USP <1079> provides specific responsibilities, not just for manufacturers but for all last-mile participants, including wholesalers, distributors, pharmacies, compounding pharmacies, hospitals, healthcare providers, brokers, and logistics service providers. It addresses the responsibilities for:

  • Storage, transport, supplier qualification
  • Quality agreements, excursion handling, training
  • Calibration, monitoring systems, staffing
  • Temperature mapping and shipping package qualification
USP <1079> on Distribution & Shipment:
"Shippers and distributors are to follow the proper storage and shipping requirements as indicated by the manufacturer… The manufacturers and distributors should work together to establish proper distribution and product-handling requirements for the purpose of ensuring appropriate product maintenance in transit."
USP <1079> on Temperature Challenges:
"Storage temperature ranges may not be indicative of the allowable tolerances during shipping. Articles labeled for special storage conditions (between 2 °C and 8 °C) vary widely in their tolerance of short-term exposure to heat and cold."

Manufacturers ship refrigerated pharmaceuticals in a variety of ways, ranging from protective packaging or temperature-controlled trailers to no temperature protection or control at all. The decision depends on the stability data a manufacturer provides, which may not be included in product labeling.

Shipments from wholesalers are made via private fleet (vehicles owned by the wholesaler), commercial carriers, and local courier services. Typically, the duration of these shipments from the wholesaler's distribution centers to the point of dispensing facilities ranges from several hours to multiple days.

USP 章節定位：指引 vs. 法規

USP 的 General Chapter 分兩大類：

  • 編號 <1000 的章節（如 <659>）：具有強制性（mandatory）——只要藥品在 USP 收載範圍內，必須符合
  • 編號 ≥1000 的章節（如 <1079>）：僅為指引性（informational）——不具法律強制效力，但被監管機構和業界廣泛採納作為最佳實務標準

因此 USP <1079> 雖非法規，卻是 FDA 查廠和行業標準的實際基準，GAP 存在時可能引發 FDA 483 觀察意見。

郵購藥局（Mail-Order Pharmacy）最後一哩：溫控灰色地帶

美國郵購藥局占處方藥市場約 30%，其最後一哩面臨獨特挑戰：

  • 包材選擇：通常使用常溫隔熱袋 + 冰袋（gel pack），可維持 2–8°C 24–72 小時
  • 暴露時間：包裹送達後若患者不在家，可能在信箱/門口暴露數小時，超出設計保護範圍
  • 責任歸屬爭議：製造商、批發商、郵購藥局、快遞公司，誰對溫度偏差負責？——美國目前無統一答案，USP <1079> 主張各方應有質量協議（Quality Agreement）明確規定
  • 患者意識不足：患者常不了解需立即存入冰箱，FDA 已收到越來越多患者投訴

監管趨勢：Florida、Georgia、Oklahoma 等州已開始強化批發商/藥局的配送合規要求。

3.6 (cont.) Wholesale Distributor Accreditation & State Licensing

The National Association of Boards of Pharmacy (NABP) Drug Distributor Accreditation program (formerly the Verified-Accreditation Wholesale Distributors / VAWD program) is designed to protect the U.S. prescription drug and medical device supply chain. The accreditation process changed in January 2022 and no longer requires on-site visits. This program involves a self-conducted supply chain inspection of a facility's procedures and practices.

Nearly half of the states require NABP Drug Distributor Accreditation for distributors who are licensed to distribute in their state, whether or not they have facilities in their state, while some states, such as Florida, have a more rigorous licensing process that also involves passing state-certification exams.

Other states, such as Georgia and Oklahoma, have started to review their regulatory requirements related to the distribution of finished drug products. Public awareness has also increased in patient claims of improper handling of temperature-sensitive drug products within the last mile that might have adversely impacted patients.

Without specific regulatory requirements, wholesale distributors have only the manufacturer's instructions to ensure they are providing adequate, appropriate, and proper packaging and shipping temperature ranges to maintain product integrity, stability, and potency.

In the absence of specific packaging requirements or stability data provided by a manufacturer, the distributor should use qualified packaging or transportation to maintain product-storage labeling requirements for the duration of the shipping time from the removal from the distributor's temperature-controlled environment to delivery at their customer's location.

If available, manufacturers should be encouraged to provide specific packaging requirements or stability data that supports shipping within parameters outside of storage-labeling requirements, since this would reduce environmental packaging waste and costs that are passed on to consumers. In either case, the distributor is responsible for having data showing that the package and shipping method ensures the product's temperature will stay within the storage-label requirement or specified time and temperature parameters.

3.7 Supply Chains in Canada

Canada has two main levels of regulating bodies for medicines: Health Canada is the federal regulator, and the Colleges of Pharmacy are the provincial regulators for pharmacies. Health Canada oversees the activities of manufacturing, importation, testing, packaging, labeling, distribution, and wholesaling and, thus, regularly audits and licenses wholesalers.

Each of the ten provinces and three territories in Canada have their respective College of Pharmacy. Hospitals fall under the College of Pharmacy, and they are funded by their respective provincial government. Pharmacies and clinics, private or public, obtain their licenses from the College of Pharmacy of their respective province.

The Canadian Food and Drug Act (CFDA) and Food and Drug Regulations (FDR) are the two main legal documents that govern the drug, medical device, natural health product, and cosmetics industries. The FDR and the Guidelines for Environmental Control of Drugs during Storage and Transportation are the Canadian equivalent of the EU GDP. Health Canada refers to these documents when auditing any licensed owner of a drug establishment.

Canada is the second largest country in the world, having an area of almost 10 million km², with a relatively small population of 38 million people. Almost 80% of the population is situated in the south of the country, close to the USA–Canada border, which occupies only about 30% of the total area of the country.

Wholesalers package, pick up, store, transport, and deliver medicines to more than 11,000 pharmacies and hospitals across the country. Transportation of pharmaceutical products can be challenging due to long distances and seasonal temperatures that range from -40 °C to 50 °C, especially when products travel from the south to the north. Products need robust environmental control and protection. In some areas, like in the far north, only one carrier operates — making it imperative to assess the route, understand carrier capabilities, and account for the handoff between carriers.

Each building used to store drugs must be indicated on the drug establishment license (DEL) warehouse annex. When using contracted third parties (3PL), it is the wholesaler's responsibility to ensure that the 3PL buildings are indicated on the DEL warehouse annex and that drugs are stored according to label conditions.

Packaging and packaging configurations are the responsibility of the DEL owner, who is responsible for designing and validating the packaging used to ship products and for qualifying each route according to a protocol. The executed protocol and documentation may be presented to Health Canada during an audit.

Considering the difficult Canadian geography, long distances, and extremes in weather, manufacturers are encouraged to share allowable temperature excursions with the wholesaler. Unfortunately, this information is difficult to obtain even when it is part of the quality agreement signed between the wholesaler and the product manufacturer.

3.8 Supply Chains in China

China had approximately 134,000 pharmaceutical distributors in 2021 with a market size of 2 trillion renminbi (RMB), equivalent to approximately 300 billion USD. These distributors sell most pharmaceuticals to China hospitals. The Central Pharmacy Department in a hospital is responsible for the purchase and dispensing of pharmaceutical products including inpatient, outpatient, and emergency pharmacies. Hospital sales still account for more than 60% of drug sales.

A typical manufacturer uses provincial distributors to cover hospital channels, a central purchase department to provide for chain pharmacies, and an ecommerce platform to supply directly to the patient. Transaction partners are required to exchange "first transaction documents" — business licenses, drug transaction licenses, and drug sales permits — before a transaction takes place.

A distributor is not necessarily bound to limit distribution to within a certain region, but the local administration that issues the distribution license will be held responsible for any conduct contrary to the regulations. Therefore, it is almost impossible for a distributor to open a distribution center in another region, since no authority would issue a license for a distributor not registered in that region.

The pharmaceutical supply chain in China can be divided into the following three steps:

  1. National distribution from manufacturers to wholesalers
  2. Local dispatch from wholesalers to hospitals/pharmacies
  3. Last-mile distribution from pharmacies to patients

Long-haul distribution is usually carried out with the support of 3PL companies, while the dispatch to hospitals is usually handled by the wholesalers themselves, using their own transportation teams who also serve (partly) as the stocking staff of hospital pharmacies. The complexity of such a delivery system makes it almost impossible for manufacturers to replace wholesalers with 3PL companies to provide direct sales to hospitals.

Retail pharmacies can dispatch prescription drug (Rx) products to homes with their own third-party courier services "if the Rx product is to treat chronic diseases and the prescription has been verified by certified pharmacists." Specialty pharmacies, or direct-to-patient pharmacies, serve as the main channel to patients with home delivery services for specialty drugs.

Two critical factors impact the last mile in China: National Health Insurance and the fast-growing eCommerce channel. Online sales for drugs grew by 37.6% in 2022 over 2021, with a seven-year compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 48.4%, accounting for 2.8% of the total drug sales.

The Online-to-Offline (O2O) platform model allows patients to select a product from listed retail pharmacies and have it delivered within 30 minutes. In 2021, the top 10 wholesalers accounted for 65% of the market with total revenue of 1.3 trillion RMB (~194 billion USD).

In summary, the supply chain in China is shifting from hospitals to retail pharmacies, and online pharmacies are growing at a faster rate than offline retail pharmacies.

4.0 Pharmaceutical Products

The pharmaceutical supply chain distributes different kinds of products to the patient via the last mile. Product characteristics and stability impact how these products should be handled along the chain; understanding these product properties is therefore important.

4.1 Product Characteristics and Stability

The chemical and physical properties of a drug substance and the drug product formulation determine its product stability and shelf life. During this shelf life, pharmaceutical products like vaccines, tablets, creams, and injectables are exposed to hazards that may impact their quality, efficacy, safety, and aesthetics. These hazards include:

  • Intrinsic factors: molecular structure of the drug itself
  • Environmental factors: temperature, pH, buffer species, ionic strength, light, oxygen, moisture, additives, excipients

Temperature is one of the primary hazards that affects drug stability and shelf life. Storage and shipping temperatures should stay within the allowed and filed conditions established during stability-testing programs that are typically run at the beginning of the product lifecycle. These studies determine the impact of temperature, moisture (relative humidity), light, shock, vibration, drop, and pressure on the product in its primary marketed packaging.

The formation of degradation products and decrease in label claim or potency are measured using stability-indicating methods to the end of the product shelf life. During these stability studies, the product is stored under strictly controlled conditions in climate chambers at, for example, 5 ± 3 °C and 25 ± 2 °C. Samples are drawn from the controlled temperature chambers for testing at regular intervals. From these stability studies, the product shelf life and temperature conditions during manufacture, packaging, distribution, and storage are established.

4.2 Application of Product Stability Budget and Mean Kinetic Temperature

Products requiring storage at defined temperatures may experience exposure to temperatures outside of the defined range during filling, labeling, inspection, packaging, shipping, and distribution including to the end user. Some examples of real-world scenarios:

  • Refrigerated truck: Even though a truck has reached the correct set temperature, the temperature may rise temporarily in warm environments or fall in cool environments as the doors are opened for loading and unloading.
  • Hospital/pharmacy refrigerator failure: The refrigeration unit used to store temperature-sensitive pharmaceuticals accidentally stops cooling overnight or during the weekend.
  • Pharmacy dispensing: A product is removed from the refrigerator to place a patient label and check suitability for dispensing.
  • Mail-order delivery: A controlled room temperature product is mailed to a patient's home and remains in a mailbox for several hours, exposed to ambient summer or winter temperatures.

USP <1079> states that deviations or variances in exposure to temperatures outside of the product's label range should be addressed with the manufacturer or MAH to determine if the exposure is acceptable and if the product may be used.

The total allowable time outside the storage condition, based on stability studies, will be defined as the product's Time Out of Refrigeration (TOR) or Time Out of Storage (TOS) and will be used to support excursion temperatures outside the storage conditions. The total TOR/TOS found to be acceptable is divided by site departments to cover the total time required for each stage in manufacturing, shipping, distribution, and use.

Manufacturers are reluctant to provide expanded standard stability information to other parties, since the same allowable excursions could be used multiple times by those possessing the product without the knowledge of the manufacturer. A better approach would be to use transportation statements in labeling, for example:

"Store at 2–8 ºC; excursions to 25 ºC during transport not to exceed 24 hours"
"Store at 15–25 °C; excursions to 2–32 °C during transport not to exceed 24 hours"

Such statements must be supported by stability data and approved by competent health authorities.

Drug manufacturers can facilitate the use of their temperature-controlled products by: providing a wider range of label storage temperatures (e.g., 2–15 °C rather than 2–8 °C), providing separate long-term storage and transport temperatures, and/or using Mean Kinetic Temperature (MKT).

Using the calculated MKT for a drug product is appropriate to approximate the effects of storage-temperature variations that may occur during product transport in the absence of product-specific kinetic data. MKT calculations for each portion of the distribution process may be used to estimate the impact of temperature excursions on the quality of the drug product.

  • Brief segments (e.g., transport): use MKT in conjunction with upper and lower temperature limits from the manufacturer.
  • Longer segments (e.g., storage): calculating the MKT of only the excursion is preferable to using the MKT of the entire segment.

Each successive owner of the material — manufacturer, wholesaler, pharmacy, and patient — can, at best, be responsible only for their particular segment, including the storage and transport temperatures and the interpretation of the resulting partial MKT values.

The manufacturer of a drug product has a unique responsibility, since it is the manufacturer who performs the stability studies to determine the expiration dating of the drug. For this reason, when questions arise during the distribution process about temperature excursions, they are best raised with the manufacturer.

The misalignment between logistics needs and stability data can be resolved if the logistics group openly communicates the stability needs to the research and development team that executes the stability studies. Comprehensive stability data is essential for managing temperature excursions that are likely to occur during transit.

  • 批發商無法科學評估一次偏差是否「用掉」多少預算
  • 患者更不知道自家冰箱溫度是否符合要求

比喻：MKT 如同「溫度加權平均分」

MKT 不是普通算術平均溫度，而是考量高溫對反應速率的指數加速效果後的「等效傷害溫度」。例如：產品在 25°C 暴露 1 小時，對穩定性的傷害遠大於在 8°C 暴露 1 小時——MKT 反映的正是這種非線性累積效應。

台灣類比：就像基測/學測不是每科分數直接加總，而是依科目難度加權計算——MKT 依溫度對降解的加速程度加權計算。

Services and Temperature Controlled Systems — 服務與溫控系統

5.0 Services and Temperature Controlled Systems

Within supply chain management, suppliers and receivers of goods may use services and temperature-controlled systems from other entities to support the storage and transport of pharmaceutical products. When temperature-sensitive products need to be shipped, additional services may be needed to protect and monitor the goods.

5.1 Contract Management

When services or outsource activities are applied in the supply chain, contract management should be applied according to the applicable country's rules. The quality system should extend to the control and review of any outsourced activities related to the procurement, holding, supply, or export of medicinal products, and all of these processes should incorporate quality risk management.

There must be a written contract between the contract-giver and the contract-acceptor that clearly establishes the duties of each party. From a regulatory perspective, a technical or quality agreement is expected that includes additional details for deviation management and change control. The contract-givers are responsible for auditing the contract-acceptor to verify the agreed-upon activities.

The contract-acceptor should not pass any of the work entrusted to them to a third party without the contract-giver's prior evaluation, approval, and an audit of the third party. The contract-acceptor must forward any information that can influence the quality of the product(s) to the contract-giver in accordance with the requirements of the contract.

5.2 Storage

5.2.1 Storage Facilities

Storage facilities should be secure and structurally sound to allow safe storage and handling of medicinal products. Management and operation of the facility should include but is not limited to:

  • Access to the storage facilities should be restricted to authorized staff.
  • Premises should be provided with adequate lighting to enable all operations to be carried out accurately and safely.
  • Appropriate cleaning equipment and cleaning products should be chosen so as not to present a source of contamination.
  • Any products withdrawn, recalled, expired, or suspected of falsification should be segregated from saleable stock (physically or through an equivalent electronic system).
  • Medicinal products should be handled and stored to prevent spillage, breakage, contamination, and mix-ups.
  • Stock should be rotated according to the "First Expired, First Out" (FEFO) principle. Regular stock inventories should be performed, and stock irregularities should be investigated.

5.2.2 Environmental Control

The storage of pharmaceutical products is critical for the last mile. Temperature and humidity requirements must be maintained during storage, regardless of the type of storage unit used (e.g., deep freezer, freezer, refrigerator, walk-in cooler or freezer, storage rooms).

Three essential conditions must be met: temperature and humidity maintenance, distribution, and monitoring. Temperature and humidity must be maintained during regular operation and should consider traffic movement (door opening) and defrost cycles where applicable.

Storage units should be mapped (thermal mapping) to ensure that all locations inside do not deviate from the required temperature range. Temperature- and humidity-mapping should be conducted when the unit is empty. Additional studies might be required based on geographic location, atmosphere, and local regulations in combination with partial or full loads, as product placement may affect air movement.

Once temperature-mapping is executed, the next step is analyzing the thermal mapping data to identify the locations with the lowest and highest values where temperature and humidity sensors can be positioned for permanent monitoring.

In some cases, temperature probes are placed inside a container with liquid to provide a more accurate product temperature rather than air temperature, which can fluctuate and trigger an alarm unnecessarily. A contingency plan must be in place in the event of a power outage or mechanical failure. Standup freezers and refrigerators can only maintain the proper temperature for a few hours during failures and are not typically designed with significant insulation properties.

The medication provider (e.g., pharmacist) must communicate to end users the importance of proper storage conditions. Refrigerators used at home often cool the interior space with cold air from the freezer section. Placing products near the vents supplying cold air may expose products to freezing temperatures.

FEFO vs. FIFO — 為什麼藥品用 FEFO？

FIFO（First In, First Out）：先進先出，以進庫時間排序，適合非到期品管理。

FEFO（First Expired, First Out）：先到期先出，以到期日排序。藥品採用 FEFO 的原因：同批產品可能有不同到期日，或補貨時有更短效期的產品進入。FEFO 確保近效期產品優先出貨，避免過期損耗。

台灣藥事法規亦要求藥品庫存管理採行 FEFO 原則。

Thermal Mapping（溫度圖譜）三步驟

  1. 空載量測：空倉庫/冰箱時進行，找出溫度最高點與最低點（通常靠近門口、頂部、出風口）
  2. 滿載/半載量測：產品擺放影響氣流，需驗證不同載量下的溫度分布
  3. 探頭定位：將永久監控感測器安裝在最高溫與最低溫位置（worst-case positions）

家用冰箱特別注意：靠近出風口的位置溫度可能低於 0°C，導致原本要求 2–8°C 儲存的藥品結凍失效。藥師有義務告知患者正確儲放位置。

5.3

Packaging — 溫控包裝：被動式、主動式與永續性

5.3.1 被動式熱防護 | 5.3.2 主動式熱防護 | 5.3.3 永續包裝

5.3 Packaging

The diversity of environmental conditions encountered in the last mile makes this segment the most challenging of the whole distribution chain. For this reason, the qualification of packaging requires a deep analysis of potential environmental and handling conditions that will be experienced until the product is delivered to end users. PDA Technical Reports No. 64, No. 72, and TR 39 detail all the processes for qualifying a thermal solution.

The most crucial step is to design proper testing conditions before starting the qualification process. Any scenarios that the products will encounter must be taken into consideration. If the product will be delivered by car or truck, with or without active heating and cooling, or with couriers or parcels, the conditions inside the packaging, the number of stops, and how the products are tendered to end users need to be known in the development of testing temperature profiles and handling procedures.

Data collection is crucial in the establishment of qualification procedures. Using dummy boxes to collect outside temperatures and measure handling conditions (e.g., vibration, orientation) during shipments is a very effective method for understanding the shipping environment. In the case of parcel shipments, thermal shipping containers can be flipped multiple times.

Earlier in the supply chain, pharmaceutical products are transported in larger quantities (pallet size, multiple-counts packages). For the last mile, products are shipped in much smaller quantities for final deliveries to pharmacies, clinics, nursing homes, and directly to patients. These shipments will experience more diversified conditions than in their homogeneous distribution.

5.3.1 Packaging with Passive Thermal Functionality

Packaging with passive thermal functionality uses a combination of insulation and refrigerants, such as water-based gel packs, phase-change materials (PCM), dry ice, or liquid nitrogen to ensure that medicinal products are distributed under defined temperature conditions. Containers must be qualified.

A prequalified passive-thermal solution can be acceptable as long as the ambient temperature for which it was qualified and the payload used for the qualification is similar to the product to be shipped. Universal pack-outs do not require seasonal or regional adjustments and maximize operational efficiency but usually are more expensive and weigh more.

Factors related to home deliveries that include additional transport hours and time packages are left outside or in mailboxes must be considered in the final design of the thermal-packaging solution.

5.3.2 Packaging with Active Thermal Functionality

Several different systems with active thermal packaging are available, equipped with active refrigeration or heating capabilities using calibrated and temperature-control devices. The refrigeration or heating used to keep the products under proper temperature conditions can be mechanical, chemical, or electric and typically is designed to be reused multiple times.

Contrary to previous segments of the supply chain, active-thermal containers used in last-mile delivery are much smaller and accommodate a small number of units. They require a power source to maintain temperature and offer the advantage of being resettable — the temperature set point can be adjusted as needed.

5.3.3 Sustainability

Sustainability considerations in thermal packaging include:

  • Single use versus reuse of packaging components
  • PCM recovery rates and recycling logistics
  • Growing trends of expanded polystyrene (EPS) bans in various jurisdictions
  • Development of biodegradable packaging alternatives (e.g., mushroom foam, recycled paper insulation)
  • Carbon footprint of packaging materials vs. temperature performance trade-offs
  • Theft deterrence and damage prevention as part of holistic packaging design

PCM（相變材料）選型關鍵

相變材料（Phase-Change Material）在相變（固→液或液→固）時吸收或釋放大量潛熱，從而維持穩定溫度。常見規格：

  • +5°C PCM：適用 2–8°C 冷藏藥品（防止過熱）
  • 0°C PCM（水/冰）：成本最低，但需注意結凍風險
  • -21°C PCM：適用凍存藥品

  • 紙基隔熱材（如蜂窩紙板）熱性能通常不如 EPS，需要更厚材料
  • 生物可降解包裝（如菌菇泡棉）目前確效資料仍不足
  • PCM 回收需要建立逆向物流（reverse logistics）體系

最後一哩包裝確效的特殊挑戰

最後一哩配送的包裝確效比幹線運輸更複雜，因為：

  • 包裝倒置：快遞包裹可能被翻轉多次，PCM 位置改變影響溫控
  • 等待時間不確定：包裹可能在門口或信箱等候數小時
  • 環境溫度多變：同一條路線，夏季 38°C 與冬季 -10°C 的確效條件截然不同

推薦做法：使用 dummy box（擬真包裝）進行 live shipment testing，並在最惡劣季節條件下驗證。

Transportation — 運輸方式：公路、無人機、郵購藥局

5.4 Transportation

5.4.1 Road Transportation

Road transportation is the most common mode of pharmaceutical last-mile delivery globally. Vehicles used for pharmaceutical transport — ranging from large refrigerated trucks to smaller vans and motorcycles — must be qualified for the intended purpose. Key considerations include:

  • Vehicle temperature qualification: the vehicle compartment must be mapped to confirm uniform temperature distribution, accounting for door openings, defrost cycles, and ambient extremes.
  • Handoff points: each transfer from one vehicle or carrier to another represents a temperature risk event that must be managed with defined procedures and documentation.
  • Route qualification: the entire route, including expected transit time, seasonal temperature profiles, and number of delivery stops, must be incorporated into the packaging and vehicle qualification.
  • Driver training: personnel handling pharmaceutical products must understand storage requirements, how to handle temperature excursions, and how to operate temperature-monitoring equipment.

5.4.2 Drones

Drone delivery is an emerging last-mile transportation mode with potential advantages for remote and underserved areas. Key aspects include:

  • Payload limitations: most commercial delivery drones can carry only 1–5 kg, restricting them to small parcel deliveries (e.g., insulin pens, EpiPens, blood products for point-of-care).
  • Flight range: current technology supports approximately 10–30 km per flight, making drones suitable for localized distribution from a hub to a defined catchment area.
  • Weather window: precipitation, high winds, and extreme temperatures restrict safe drone operation, creating delivery reliability challenges.
  • Regulatory status: in the United States, the FAA regulates drone operations; in Europe, EASA. Beyond-visual-line-of-sight (BVLOS) operations — necessary for practical pharmaceutical delivery — require special waivers or approvals that are still being developed in most jurisdictions.
  • Temperature control during flight: airborne packages are exposed to altitude-related temperature changes, wind chill, and solar radiation. Passive packaging must be specifically qualified for these conditions.

Pilot programs exist in Ghana (Zipline for blood products and vaccines), Rwanda, the USA (Wing, Amazon), and Switzerland. Regulatory frameworks continue to evolve.

5.4.3 Mail Service Pharmacy

Mail service pharmacy (MSP), also known as mail-order pharmacy, has grown substantially, particularly for chronic disease medications. Key last-mile challenges include:

  • Uncontrolled delivery environment: packages may sit in an unheated or unventilated mailbox or on a doorstep for hours after delivery.
  • Seasonal extremes: summer temperatures inside a residential mailbox can exceed 60°C; winter temperatures in northern climates can reach -30°C or lower, posing freezing risks for biologics.
  • Carrier variability: national postal services, private couriers, and regional carriers apply different handling standards; no single GDP standard applies uniformly.
  • Regulatory requirements: in the USA, state pharmacy boards regulate MSP operations; product labeling must be appropriate for the expected conditions of shipment and home storage. Pharmacists retain professional responsibility for the integrity of dispensed products.
  • Patient education: patients must be instructed to retrieve packages promptly and inspect for visible damage or evidence of temperature excursion before use.

Temperature Monitoring — 溫度監控：記錄器、指示劑與風險方法

5.5 Temperature Monitoring

5.5.1 Data Loggers

Data loggers are electronic devices that continuously record temperature (and in some models, humidity, light, and shock) at programmable intervals throughout a shipment. Key features include:

  • Downloadable history: data can be downloaded via USB, Bluetooth, or NFC after shipment completion, providing a full time-temperature record for regulatory documentation.
  • Programmable alarm thresholds: loggers can be set to alarm upon exceeding defined upper or lower temperature limits or upon accumulating a defined time out of range.
  • Calibration requirements: data loggers must be calibrated to a traceable standard at defined intervals to ensure measurement accuracy.
  • GxP compliance: for regulated shipments, data loggers must meet 21 CFR Part 11 or equivalent electronic records requirements (audit trail, data integrity).
  • Placement: loggers should be placed at the product level inside the shipping container — not attached to the outside of the container where temperatures differ from the product microenvironment.

Real-time data loggers with cellular or satellite connectivity allow remote temperature monitoring, enabling intervention before a shipment reaches its destination if an excursion is detected.

5.5.2 Temperature Indicators (TTI)

Time-temperature indicators (TTI) are low-cost, disposable devices that provide a visual indication of whether a product has been exposed to unacceptable temperature conditions. Types include:

  • Threshold indicators: change color or display a symbol if temperature exceeds a defined threshold for any duration (e.g., above 8°C indicator).
  • Cumulative TTI: integrate time and temperature exposure, changing irreversibly once a defined cumulative thermal dose (expressed as equivalent time at a reference temperature) is exceeded.

TTIs are widely used as:

  • Point-of-use consumer indicators on vaccine vials (the WHO vaccine vial monitor, or VVM, is a well-known example)
  • Supplementary indicators on outer packaging for visual triage at receiving
  • Cost-effective monitoring tools for high-volume, low-cost shipments where full data loggers are not economically justified

Limitations of TTI: they do not provide downloadable historical data, and threshold indicators do not account for cumulative exposure. They are not sufficient for regulatory documentation requiring a full time-temperature record.

5.5.3 Risk-Based Approach to Temperature Monitoring

A risk-based approach to temperature monitoring recognizes that not all shipments require the same level of monitoring. Factors to consider when selecting monitoring tools include:

  • Product sensitivity: biologics and vaccines are typically more sensitive than small-molecule tablets; higher sensitivity warrants more rigorous monitoring.
  • Shipment value: high-value specialty products justify the cost of real-time data loggers; low-cost generic drugs may be adequately monitored with TTIs.
  • Route characteristics: well-characterized, validated routes with historical excursion data may require less intensive monitoring than novel routes.
  • Regulatory requirements: some regions or product types mandate specific monitoring technologies.
  • Chain of custody complexity: shipments passing through multiple handoffs or carriers benefit from continuous monitoring to assign responsibility for any excursion.

5.6 Security

Security considerations in last-mile pharmaceutical delivery encompass multiple dimensions, including physical security, product authenticity, and chain-of-custody integrity. The last mile presents unique security vulnerabilities because:

  • Products move from large, secured distribution centers into smaller vehicles, courier bags, and ultimately to individual home addresses — environments with far less controlled access.
  • The diversity of delivery personnel (professional couriers, gig-economy drivers, postal workers) varies in awareness of pharmaceutical handling requirements.
  • High-value specialty drugs (e.g., oncology biologics, controlled substances) are particularly attractive targets for theft.
  • Counterfeit or falsified medicines can enter the supply chain at distribution and dispensing points, especially in markets with fragmented distribution structures.

Security measures applicable to last-mile pharmaceutical logistics include:

  • Serialization and track-and-trace: regulatory requirements in the EU (FMD — Falsified Medicines Directive), the USA (DSCSA — Drug Supply Chain Security Act), and China mandate unique product identifiers and electronic verification at the dispensing point, preventing the dispensing of falsified or recalled products.
  • Tamper-evident packaging: outer cartons and secondary packaging should include tamper-evident features (seals, labels, or destructible closure systems) so that patients can visually confirm the product has not been opened.
  • Chain-of-custody documentation: each transfer of pharmaceutical product must be documented, with signatures, timestamps, and temperature data, to enable retrospective investigation in the event of a security incident or quality concern.
  • GPS tracking: high-value shipments are increasingly tracked using GPS devices embedded in the shipping container, enabling real-time location monitoring and geofence-based alerts.
  • Controlled substance handling: additional regulatory requirements (e.g., DEA schedules in the USA, Narcotic Drugs Act in Taiwan) impose stricter chain-of-custody, storage, and documentation requirements for controlled substances delivered via mail or courier.
  • Patient identity verification: for specialty pharmacy and controlled substance deliveries, recipient identity verification (e.g., signature, ID check) is required at the point of delivery.

In summary, security is an integral component of pharmaceutical supply chain design, requiring both physical controls and information system safeguards. As pharmaceutical e-commerce and direct-to-patient delivery models grow, the security framework must evolve proportionally.

6.0 Data and Knowledge Management

Data is critical to manage and prove that products are delivered with the right quantity and quality at the right time and under the right conditions. Data can be product information, transport and storage conditions, track-and-trace or traceability data, serialization numbers, and other information such as supply-chain disruptions related to traffic jams, weather conditions, and inventory levels.

Data can be static or dynamic. Static data does not change after being recorded and is a fixed data set, whereas dynamic data may change after it is recorded and must be continually updated (e.g., temperature data, location track-and-trace data of shipments). Sections 6.1–6.1.3 describe the management of data in relation to GDP and good documentation practices (GDocP), including the ALCOA+ data-integrity principles.

  • 靜態數據（Static Data）：記錄後不再更改——批號、產品代碼、序列號、製造日期、有效期限。

6.1 Good Documentation Practices and Data Integrity

Since 2015, regulatory bodies like the EMA, FDA, WHO, and Medicines & Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) have had an enhanced interest in ensuring data integrity. Each has published data integrity guides to promote the transformation of the pharmaceutical industry to an elevated data integrity level. Initially, the main focus was on testing laboratories and manufacturing, but the guidelines also currently apply to distribution to ensure the quality, efficacy, and safety of the product is maintained according to local requirements.

6.1.1 Data Governance

Data governance encompasses the people, processes, and technology arrangements required to achieve data integrity. Assuring data integrity requires appropriate quality and risk management systems, including adherence to GDocP and local regulations, such as General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in Europe, that:

  • Address data ownership, access, and accountability for processes and risk management throughout the entire data lifecycle
  • Consider an adequate design, operation, and monitoring of processes and systems
  • Include controls over intentional and unintentional breaches of data integrity

Data-governance design and controls must enable the detection of errors, lapses, and omissions of results and data during the data lifecycle. Controls can include procedural, organizational, and functional (technical) controls.

The data governance system must include enough detail to allow accurate and complete reporting and interpretation of all data and must ensure data integrity. This system should include, but is not limited to, the following elements:

  • Validated computerized systems that are fit for their intended use, with special attention to any system used to create, process, and store data
  • Systems and procedures in place to ensure that records are reliable, complete, and accurate; these systems and procedures must also require that all test results that could affect the quality, safety, or efficacy of a drug are reported, reviewed, and assessed appropriately
  • Organization and storage of data in a way that is interpretable and traceable to the execution and purpose of distribution procedures
  • Controls in place to ensure that test data are not deleted and that changes to records are documented and justified, where required (e.g., audit trails should be enabled and reviewed)
  • Data retained in its original format; original records (or true copies), including electronic records, are subject to review by qualified personnel

Risk management is an essential component of the data governance system. A risk-based approach to data management ensures that adequate resources are allocated and that control strategies for the assurance of the data integrity are commensurate with their potential impact on patient safety, product quality, and compliance with regulations. Where risks are identified, appropriate measures or safeguards should be implemented to ensure the integrity of critical data and records.

6.1.2 Good Documentation Practices

According to the FDA and other inspectorates, ensuring data integrity requires that data be attributable, legible, contemporaneous, original, accurate, complete, consistent, enduring, and available (ALCOA+). These general principles of GDocP apply to the management of records throughout its lifecycle, regardless of the medium (i.e., paper or electronic records), from the time data is first generated and any modifications made thereafter.

The EU GDP provides guidance in Section 3.3.1 on how to manage data:

  • Data should only be entered into the computerised system or amended by persons authorised to do so.
  • Data should be secured by physical or electronic means and protected against accidental or unauthorised modifications.
  • Stored data should be checked periodically for accessibility.
  • Data should be protected by backing up at regular intervals. Back-up data should be retained for the period stated in national legislation but at least five years at a separate and secure location.
  • Procedures to be followed if the system fails or breaks down should be defined. This should include systems for the restoration of data.

The WHO guidance differs slightly from the EU GDP in the retention of the data, indicating that "all monitoring records should be kept for a minimum of the shelf-life of the product distributed plus one year, or as required by the national legislation." However, national legislation may differ from either of the WHO or EU GDP guidances; for example the United Kingdom requires a two-year retention period.

Each paper and/or electronic record should be secured against modifications and/or deletions to the end of the retention time as defined by the applicable regulations. Therefore, a paper record should be traceable and stored in a secured environment (e.g., locked, fire-proof cabinet), whereas an electronic record should be secured in a computerized system which employs system access (segregation of duties), vulnerability scanning, security firewalls, antivirus applications, and security patches.

In addition, risk assessment of the computerized system should provide requirements toward data business continuity management and recovery plan, data integrity, system weaknesses against human interactions, data manipulation, confidentiality, data privacy, and so on. Cloud computing services are to be treated as a contracted service, with the risks identified and addressed, and the responsibilities of both parties clearly outlined in a suitable contract or service agreement. If storing electronic records in a PDF format, a risk assessment should be completed on the editability of the document.

6.1.3 Data Integrity and Quality Management

There should be written procedure for a data-integrity and data-security assessment that identifies data integrity and data security risks and ensures that suitable procedural or technical controls have been applied. Any detected or potential data integrity or security breach must be documented as a deviation and investigated through the identification of the root cause and the determination of the resulting corrective and preventive action (CAPA) plan. Some examples of data integrity breaches include:

  • Missing or untraceable data entries or archived paper documents
  • Missing a proof of delivery document
  • Missing temperature data logger
  • Data accidently or intentionally omitted or destroyed
  • Deficient electronic data backup or archive
  • Incorrect calculations impacting decisions
  • Unjustified alterations or manipulation of data (e.g., backdating)
  • Wrong access role used (e.g., administrator role used for routine tasks)
  • Data review not completed on a signed off report
  • Failure to comply with the ALCOA+ principles
  • Loss of data during data transfer or due to system failure
  • Sharing of user accounts or passwords

If the data-integrity breach may have an impact on product quality and/or patient safety, the violation must be escalated to management and communicated to customers (as applicable). An assessment of the breach must be conducted and the relevant regulatory or health authorities (e.g., country, local) must be notified, if required by local regulations. More information can be found in the FDA 21 CFR Part 11 and EU GMP Annex 11.

6.2 Temperature Management

Temperature is a critical process parameter that must be monitored and controlled in the supply chain during manufacturing, storage, and transportation to ensure that pharmaceutical products comply with the critical quality attributes to the end of their shelf life. To secure temperature control of pharmaceutical products in the supply chain, five basic principles should be applied by each supply-chain stakeholder:

  1. Temperature specifications are set for products, processes, and systems.
  2. Temperature is controlled in an environment within the intended range using qualified active and/or passive system(s).
  3. Temperature is measured by calibrated sensors at hot or cold spot location(s) based on temperature-mapping studies.
  4. Temperature data is collected and securely stored in readable and retrievable electronic and/or paper format according to ALCOA+ data integrity principles.
  5. Temperature excursions are investigated and product dispositions are executed according to current GDP and good pharmacy practices.

In event of temperature excursions, the product impact assessment and product disposition should be based on the generated data. However, additional non-GxP data (metadata) can be used by the business to gain a better understanding of the circumstances of the temperature excursion and to determine CAPAs for continuous improvement. In the end, temperature data storage, whether on paper or on a third-party client's cloud solution, needs to comply with all relevant regulatory requirements for security and data integrity. This will ensure that all collected and recorded data is complete, consistent, and accurate throughout the data lifecycle.

6.3 Other Critical Process Parameters

In addition to verifying the shipment and receipt of products, monitoring devices may record temperature at other points in the supply chain. Other possible enhancements that may prove useful include detecting the opening and closing of packing containers and providing a geofence. A geofence is a virtual perimeter for a real-world geographic area; it could be dynamically generated or matched to a predefined set of boundaries. The degree to which these devices may replace or supplement temperature monitors and recorders remains to be determined.

In the last mile, finished products are protected against humidity by the primary packaging and against light by the primary/secondary packaging. Therefore, removal of secondary packaging is not recommended as light may impact product quality, safety, and effectiveness. If a product is moved from a hot, humid environment to a cold environment, that product may get wet due to condensation which may impact readability of a batch number or expiry date.

In the case of a mail-order pharmacy, most shipments use water-based cool bags, packs, or bricks (refrigerated or frozen) in "thermal pack-outs." These bags may leak on occasion due to initial physical damage done while frozen (e.g., dropped, impacted); these leaks will generate a significant amount of water in the secondary packaging. It is important to use a sealed bag to protect both the pharmaceutical product as well as the pharmacy prescription label. The use of dunnage is also advised to prevent movement inside the outer package. Air pillows are not recommended because their volume will decrease significantly at lower temperature and will not provide significant protection.

6.4 Serialization

Serialization is the assigning of a unique serial number to each saleable unit of every prescription product. It provides the product's origin, and other information in the form of a barcode or two-dimensional (2D) data matrix. This also serves as a unit's tracking or traceability system through the entire supply chain—from production, through distribution, to the point of dispensing to the patient. Serialization of pharmaceutical products has been introduced in recent years in many countries to protect the patient against counterfeit and falsified drugs. Counterfeit drugs can prove ineffective, harmful and, in some cases, fatal. Falsified medicines are often disguised as authentic medicines but may contain ingredients of poor or toxic quality or in the wrong dosage.

6.4.1 Serialization in the European Union

To address the threat of falsified medicines in the EU, Directive 2011/62/EU and its Delegated Regulation 2016/161 include measures aimed at preventing the entry of falsified medicinal products into the legal supply chain. The Directive introduces harmonized EU measures to fight medicine falsifications; it ensures that medicines are safe and that the trade in medicines is rigorously controlled including:

  • Obligatory safety features—a unique identifier and an antitampering device on the outer packaging of all prescription medicines
  • A common, EU-wide logo to identify legal online pharmacies
  • Tougher rules on the import of active pharmaceutical ingredients
  • Strengthened record-keeping requirements for wholesale distributors

Since 2019, the EU has required manufacturers of prescription medicines to apply a 2D barcode for the serial numbers, which must be unique and must be randomly generated. In addition to the serial number, the data matrix code contains the product code, batch number, expiration date, and (if necessary) national reimbursement number.

Once the manufacturer has sealed the medicine boxes with a tamper-evident seal and affixed the data matrix, the serial numbers are uploaded in the central database (EU hub) and downloaded to the databases of the member state where the boxes are being sent. It is possible for a consignment of medicines to go to another country, but that triggers an Intermarket Transaction. If the target market of a medicine changes, the batch's serial number is sent via the EU hub to the database of the correct country.

Directive 2011/62/EU obliges pharmacists and healthcare professionals who supply medicines to the public to check that the tamper-evident seal of the medicine packages was not tampered with and, then to deregister the unique serial number on the medicine packaging before dispensing it to a patient. In addition, the unique serial numbers in the EU hub can be traced to all national medicines repository systems in which the package could be dispensed.

Wholesale distributors must verify all packs of medicines they buy from other wholesalers as well as any packs returned to them. They must decommission products they export outside the EU and at the request of member states for other entities (Article 23) who are not themselves connected to the national medicines verification system in their country.

The pharmacist decommissions each pack in the central database by scanning the 2D code. If the pharmacist receives an alert indicating that the serial number does not exist or has already been decommissioned, or if the tamper-evident seal has been tampered with, the pack must be quarantined by the pharmacist until the case is resolved. If a potentially falsified medicine is identified, the pharmacist must report it to the National Competent Authority.

6.4.2 Serialization in the United States

Section 913 of the Food and Drug Administration Amendments Act of 2007 (FDAAA; Public Law 110-85) amended the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act to create "Sec. 505D. Pharmaceutical Security." This section requires the U.S. Secretary of Health and Human Services to develop standards as well as "identify and validate effective technologies for the purpose of securing the drug supply chain against counterfeit, diverted, subpotent, substandard, adulterated, misbranded, or expired drugs."

In March 2010, FDA published the Guidance for Industry: Standards for Securing the Drug Supply Chain - Standardized Numerical Identification for Prescription Drug Packages for identifying package-level standardized numerical identifiers (SNI) as an initial step toward facilitating other measures for securing the drug supply chain. Although the FDA did not specify a particular means of incorporating SNIs onto the unit package at that time, the SNI was compatible with existing machine-readable forms of data carriers, such as the 2D matrix barcode and radio frequency identification (RFID).

While RFIDs and barcodes can carry the same product identification and serial number data, the RFID has the advantage of being read remotely. Regardless of the data carrier, when the product ID and serial number are linked, the supply chain will be further secured, down to the last handoff to the patient through authentication of the product and its pedigree via a database that contains the appropriate manufacturer, distributor, and pharmacy transaction data.

The Drug Supply Chain Security Act (DSCSA), Title II of the DQSA, requires that every prescription drug package and homogeneous case have its unique identifier in both machine- and human-readable forms. The Act outlines steps to achieve interoperable and electronic tracing; this means that, in addition to label serialization, a data system would need to store product traceability information showing the chain of custody. These regulations require all trading partners in the pharmaceutical supply chain—manufacturers, wholesalers, and dispensers—to use secure, interoperable electronic systems and processes to uniquely serialize each individual drug package in the supply chain and trace their distribution.

The Pharmaceutical Distribution Security Alliance (PDSA) is an organization of stakeholders committed to safeguarding patients through better security of the drug supply chain. The PDSA is working with the FDA to develop a single national uniform system for product serialization and traceability with a goal toward unit-level interoperability. Numerous other organizations, such as the Health Distribution Alliance and International Coalition of Medicines Regulatory Authorities, are all working toward similar goals for serialization.

Pedigree requirements currently vary from state to state and where unit serialization may be used, it is seldom linked to a pedigree. While many pedigrees are paper-based, there are various types of electronic pedigrees currently being used, including the GS1 Drug Pedigree Messaging Standard.

6.5 Technologies

6.5.1 Radio Frequency Identification Tag

RFID refers to an automated data-capture system that uses low-power radio waves to communicate between two components: tags and readers. Readers are devices with antennas that emit radio waves and receive signals back from the tags. Tags use radio waves to communicate their information to readers; they can be passive or active. Passive tags are powered by the reader and do not have a battery whereas active tags are powered by batteries.

In the last mile, RFID technologies are used for inventory control, tracking and tracing products, verification of shipments and receipt of products, or finished product authentication. RFID tags may also record temperature within the supply chain or detect the opening and closing of packing containers. The degree to which these devices may replace or supplement temperature-monitors and recorders remains to be determined.

Adverse events associated with RFID application for medicines have not yet been reported or identified. Studies showed that the nonthermal effects of RFID radiation remain undetectable in biopharmaceutical products, even after unusually long exposure durations. An ideal RFID read environment would be free of metals and liquids, would enforce an air gap between each object's tag, would allow the RFID tags to be flat in a standardized orientation to the antenna, and would be in free air.

6.5.3 Blockchain

A blockchain is a tamper-proof, distributed database that stores blocks of information for cryptographically bound transactions via peer-to-peer networks. It is an emerging technology that is expected to open up a promising future for the pharmaceutical industry in counterfeit drug prevention, drug distribution, tracking and tracing, safety and security, and temperature control among other functions.

The architecture of a blockchain enables users to share validated and updated ledgers for every transaction. Nodes receive copies of each transaction and validate them, synchronizing this information throughout the network. As such, the need for a central validating authority is eliminated. The connections between transactions in separate blocks are linked, which gave rise to the term "blockchain."

6.5.4 Machine Learning

Machine learning (ML) is a subset of artificial intelligence (AI) that enables systems to learn and improve from experience without being explicitly programmed. In the context of pharmaceutical supply chains and last-mile delivery, ML algorithms can analyze large datasets from multiple sources—including historical temperature records, route data, weather patterns, and shipment outcomes—to identify patterns, predict future events, and detect anomalies that may not be apparent to human operators.

Applications include predictive maintenance of temperature-controlled equipment, demand forecasting for inventory optimization, and anomaly detection in temperature profiles that may indicate sensor malfunction or packaging failure. ML-driven risk scoring can help logistics providers prioritize interventions for high-risk shipments before temperature excursions occur.

6.6 Management of Deviations and Complaints

A robust system for managing deviations and complaints is essential in last-mile pharmaceutical distribution. Any event that departs from established procedures or specifications—including temperature excursions, delivery failures, packaging damage, serialization verification failures, and security breaches—must be documented as a deviation and subjected to root-cause analysis and CAPA.

Customer complaints related to product quality, delivery conditions, or missing documentation must be investigated promptly. All complaints should be categorized by severity, with those potentially affecting product quality or patient safety escalated to senior management and, where required by local regulation, reported to the relevant competent authority. Complaint records must be retained in accordance with applicable data retention requirements and reviewed periodically for trend analysis.

The deviation and complaint management system should be linked to the overall quality management system, with periodic review of trends to identify systemic issues and drive continuous improvement in last-mile operations.

6.7 Performance Management

Performance management in last-mile pharmaceutical distribution requires the establishment of key performance indicators (KPIs) that are measurable, actionable, and aligned with quality and regulatory requirements. KPIs should cover the full scope of last-mile operations, including delivery performance, temperature control, serialization compliance, complaint resolution, and deviation management.

Regular review of KPIs by senior management enables early identification of systemic issues and facilitates continuous improvement. Performance data should be trended over time and benchmarked against regulatory expectations and industry standards. Where KPI targets are not met, root-cause analysis and CAPA should be initiated through the deviation management system.

Performance management data should also be used to evaluate the performance of third-party logistics providers and other supply-chain partners through periodic business reviews and quality agreements.

6.8 Last-Mile Education

Education and training are fundamental components of a quality last-mile distribution program. All personnel involved in last-mile operations—including warehouse staff, couriers, pharmacists, and healthcare professionals who receive or dispense temperature-sensitive products—must receive appropriate training on GDP requirements, cold chain handling, serialization procedures, and deviation reporting.

Training programs should be risk-based and tailored to each role. Training effectiveness should be verified through assessments and observed competency checks, and records of training must be retained. Where last-mile delivery involves direct contact with patients (e.g., home delivery of specialty medicines), couriers should also be trained to handle situations in which the patient is not available to receive the product and to document delivery attempts in accordance with quality procedures.

For healthcare professionals who receive cold-chain products at clinical sites, education should include correct storage upon receipt, procedures for reporting damaged or suspect packaging, and the importance of maintaining the cold chain up to the point of administration.

6.9 Good Distribution Practice Information for Healthcare Professionals

Healthcare professionals (HCPs) play a critical role at the end of the pharmaceutical supply chain and must be equipped with the information necessary to maintain product quality from receipt through to administration. GDP guidance for HCPs should address: verification of product integrity upon receipt (checking seals, serialization codes, and temperature records); correct storage procedures at the point of care; documentation requirements for receipt and use of cold-chain products; and escalation pathways for reporting suspected product quality issues to pharmacovigilance systems and regulatory authorities.

Healthcare organizations should ensure that GDP-relevant information is integrated into their standard operating procedures and that staff receive regular training updates, particularly when new products with special storage requirements are introduced.

6.10 Information to the Patient/Recipient

In direct-to-patient distribution models, patients and caregivers are the final custodians of pharmaceutical products and must receive adequate information to ensure that product quality is maintained from delivery through to administration or use. This is particularly critical for temperature-sensitive products such as insulin, biological medicines, and vaccines.

Information provided to patients should include: correct storage conditions after receipt (e.g., refrigerator, not freezer; protected from light); the importance of storing the product in its original packaging until use; actions to take if the product has been exposed to incorrect conditions (e.g., left in a warm mailbox); how to identify damaged or suspect products; and contact information for reporting concerns to the dispensing pharmacy or healthcare provider.

Patient education materials should be written in plain language appropriate to the target audience and should be available in the relevant local language(s). For patients with limited health literacy or those who may have difficulty handling technology, simplified instructions and direct support channels should be provided.

7.1 Inventory Management

7.1.1 Rotating versus Stockpiling

An important criterion for the cost of stockpiles is if they are rotating or static.

Emergency stockpiles for crisis situations: Product shelf life varies by the type of product. Stockpiles can be partially dynamic, but also require holding stocks that do not rotate if they are dedicated to a specific emergency or to crisis situations and therefore need to be held as part of a static stockpiling approach (e.g., stockpiling of masks prior to the COVID-19 pandemic or, more recently, iodine tablets). If stocks are not managed on a rotating basis with "normal" inventory, there is a high risk of expiration. This necessitates their disposal, generating unnecessary additional costs, creating a complete loss of the initial investment, and triggering a negative environmental impact. A static stockpile, with no stock rotation, might therefore result in a large volume of expired stock that needs to be replenished, causing additional costs for their creation, maintenance, and the turnover of the stockpile. Who covers the cost of expired products and who pays for their disposal should be defined by contract.

Increasing buffer stocks for critical or essential medicines: This could be integrated via rotation into pharmaceutical full-line wholesaler/full-service healthcare distributor rolling stocks thereby reducing the risk of products expiring and could be carried out on a FEFO basis, i.e., dynamic stockpiling. Naturally, the shelf life of products differs according to the type of product; however, buffer stocks can be organized in a way that the products are continuously used and replenished. In that case, the length of storage, expiry date of products, and potential safe disposal of unwanted medicines need to be considered. Increased buffer stocks held at the wholesale level need to be purchased from the pharmaceutical industry, which increases the capital costs. This is one of the main reasons, other than available warehouse space, why the establishment of larger stockpiles of more than two weeks at the level of pharmaceutical full-line wholesalers and full-service healthcare distributors is highly challenging. This can be attributed to insufficient margins, while also holding a prefinancing function for the healthcare supply chain.

7.1.2 Organizational and Administrative Reflections

Additional administrative and logistics costs may apply to storage, based on the conditions and to whom the safety stocks should be delivered. Costs are higher the more frequently the list of products to be stocked changes because it requires a greater effort to administer the stocks. A decision should also be made as to what condition the emergency stocks will be supplied or released to the market, how they should be rotated (e.g., FEFO), and how the rationalization or allocation system would be organized with respect to market deliveries to ensure equitable access.

7.1 庫存管理 — 解析

FIFO vs. FEFO：藥品應選哪一個？

FIFO（First In First Out，先進先出）：按入庫時間順序出庫，適用於保質期均一的商品（如一般食品）。

FEFO（First Expired First Out，先效期先出）：按最早到期日順序出庫，無論入庫時間先後。

藥品為什麼必須用 FEFO？同一批次到貨的藥品，剩餘效期可能因補貨批次不同而有差異。FEFO 確保近效期產品優先出庫，最大限度減少過期浪費。GDP 要求倉儲流程採用 FEFO 原則（TR46 Section 7.1.1）。

囤積（Stockpiling）的風險：COVID-19 教訓

COVID-19 疫情初期，各國政府和醫院大量囤積口罩、消毒用品及部分藥品（如碘化鉀片）。靜態囤積（Static Stockpiling）的後果：

  • 效期浪費：囤積品若未與正常庫存輪換，大量到期銷毀
  • 資金凍結：採購成本全額損失，無殘值回收
  • 供應鏈扭曲：搶購造成正常配送斷鏈，患者無法取得所需藥品
  • 環境負擔：大批次銷毀帶來額外廢棄物處理成本

動態輪換（Dynamic Stockpiling / FEFO）則可將緩衝庫存融入日常供應流，有效避免上述問題。

安全庫存量（Safety Stock）計算思考

基本公式：

Safety Stock = Z × σ(Lead Time) × 日均消耗量

其中 Z 為安全係數（服務水準對應的標準差倍數，如 95% 服務水準 → Z = 1.65）。

最後一哩特別考量：最後一哩配送因天氣、交通、海關等變數，Lead Time 波動比一般批發配送更大。因此安全係數需適度提高，並定期以歷史配送資料重新校正。

思考題：若某冷藏藥品平均日銷量 50 單位，Lead Time 標準差 2 天，目標服務水準 98%（Z≈2.05），安全庫存應設多少？

7.3 Waste Stream for Packaging Components and Drug Product

Sections 7.3–7.5 provide a non-exhaustive overview of various global directives and waste stream management for packaging components used to distribute temperature-sensitive products in the last mile. Packaging components, including ice bricks, boxes, and isolated containers, may generate waste for each container shipped (e.g., 20 kg per container). Depending on the time period, thousands of waste materials can be generated, hence the importance of studying the reuse or recycling for this business.

7.3.1 Recycling and Regulations

Most national and supranational entities have legislative guidelines in effect governing the proper post-transit disposal of pharmaceutical products and devices. Some also cover the packaging materials used during transport. Currently, the "extended producer responsibility" or Extended Product Responsibility (EPR) program, has been instituted in several countries. The concept behind EPR implies that everyone along the product chain shares responsibility for the environmental impact of packaging. That includes the upstream considerations of material selection and manufacturing processes to the downstream effects of use, waste management, and disposal. However, this approach largely sidesteps the issue of who is directly responsible for the waste and disposal of post-consumer packaging. Some corporations have instituted their own EPR programs, but those are generally confined to the return of specific product components (e.g., printer or copier ink cartridges).

European Union—Directive 94/62/EC on packaging and packaging waste forms the basis of the EU's position toward packaging issues; this directive was amended by Directive 2004/12/EC to include states newly admitted to the EU. The EU GDP does not directly address the impact of packaging on the environment, however, the EU has adopted "extended producer responsibility" as its public policy. This requires packaging producers to be fiscally and physically responsible for their products at the post-consumer stage of their lifecycle. Packaging waste can be "taken back" or managed through reuse, recycling, or remanufacturing. Optionally, the responsibility may be delegated to a third party to act as a producer-responsibility organization. Producers pay certain fees and taxes to governmental and nonprofit entities to cover the reclamation and disposal costs. For example, the Green Dot Program operates in 20 EU member countries where a green dot on a product's packaging indicates it complies with packaging regulations. Manufacturers purchase licenses to participate in the Green Dot program, which collects and recycles or otherwise disposes of the packaging on behalf of the license-holders. The EU's EPR program has recently expanded to automobiles and electronics in many member states.

United States—In the United States, the sole federal EPR program involves the take-back of rechargeable (nickel-cadmium) batteries, in which the producers of the batteries instituted a national take-back program to avoid variable individual state legislation. Most shipments of pharmaceuticals to the consumer, particularly biologicals, use EPS/foam packaging, which usually winds up in landfills. The Alliance of Foam Packaging Recyclers has posted a resource document on their website listing available recycling/reclamation sites in the United States for EPS packaging. At the present, a "true" national EPR public policy and program remains an unmet need.

Japan—Japan's packaging directives are part of the environmental Japanese Industrial Standards, which take their cue from JIS Q 0064: Guide for the Inclusion of Environmental Aspects in Product Standards.

Brazil—Resolution SMA 45/2015 establishes the Terms of Commitment for the Reverse Logistics of Household Medicines for Human Use, Expired or Disused, and their Packaging. Key obligations include: (1) consumers must dispose expired/disused medicines at designated pharmacy collection points; (2) pharmacies as collection points must maintain approved dispensing containers at their own expense; (3) distributors must collect from primary storage points (pharmacies) to secondary storage points using the same delivery vehicles; and (4) manufacturers/importers must arrange transport from secondary storage points to final treatment and environmentally appropriate disposal sites at their own expense.

7.3.2 Reverse Logistics

Reverse logistics encompasses all operations related to the upstream movement of products and materials. It is the process of moving goods from their typical destination for the purpose of capturing value or for proper disposal. Remanufacturing and refurbishing activities also may be included in the definition of reverse logistics. Two types of reverse logistics can be considered in the pharmaceutical environment:

Closed-Loop Transit: Many wholesalers in Europe and the United States use this system. Most of these companies use their own trucks to deliver to the end users, where they can then pick up old packaging and put it back into the distribution channel or recycle the components.

Return of Packaging: Some manufacturers and distributors employ this method, wherein a prepaid return freight sticker is attached or included with the original shipment to the end user. Once the end user removes the medicinal product, the package can be sent back to the distributor or manufacturer. This method is also used to return expired or mishandled medicinal products.

  • 品質評估：每批回收需檢查完整性、溫控記錄、有效期
  • 文件記錄：GMP/GDP 要求完整追蹤，包括回收原因、數量、處置方式
  • 低規模效益：回收量少且不穩定，無法享有正向配送的規模優勢
  • 拆解人力：冷鏈包材（PCM 冰袋、VIP 隔熱板）需人工分類才能回收

PCM 冰袋回收 vs. 一次性銷毀：永續性辯證

製造商回收再用方案：

  • 優點：降低每次使用成本、減少材料廢棄
  • 挑戰：需建立回收物流網絡、清潔 SOP、再驗證程序；回收率需達 60–70% 以上才划算

一次性銷毀方案：

  • 優點：操作簡單、無回收追蹤成本、避免交叉污染風險
  • 缺點：大量 EPS/PCM 廢棄物；不符合 EU Green Deal 精神；長期成本高

結論：高價值、高使用頻率的配送路線（如固定航線的生物製劑）更適合回收再用；低頻次、長途最後一哩配送則一次性方案更具現實性。

7.4 Establishing Lowest-Cost Temperature-Control Solution for Shipment

Freight is an important cost factor to be considered when establishing a solution for temperature-controlled shipment. Some examples of other factors that play a role in the decision are the mode of transportation, the type and weight of effective thermal packaging, the amount of time spent in transport, and the environmental impact of the packaging.

There are different kinds of isolated passive containers available, which may be made of EPS, extruded polystyrene (XPS), polyurethane (PUR), or vacuum insulated panels (VIP), and refrigerants made of water and/or phase-change material (PCM). Passive thermal solutions composed of VIPs and PCM are typically more expensive than more traditional solutions of EPS boxes and water-based refrigerants. Traditional solutions are usually physically larger and heavier for the same quantity of product to be shipped, which results in higher freight costs. The freight costs are generally greater than the temperature-control solution costs, but this solution can significantly impact the total cost of shipment.

Air freight is generally more expensive than ocean freight or road freight. Transport by road in refrigerated trucks is typically less expensive than shipping passive-thermal solutions by air, but the time it takes to reach the destination and the ability to plug the truck into an electrical power supply when the active cooling unit on the truck is turned off (e.g., on a ferry) are necessary considerations.

Even though air freight is the most expensive mode, sometimes it is the best solution or may be the only solution when considering the time available for shipment or accessibility of the destination, for instance, island locations or other difficult-to-reach areas without passable roads. Passive-thermal containers are generally less expensive than the active containers shipped by air, but their performance time is limited, and they may not provide sufficient protection before reaching the destination.

Both passive and active transport equipment is designed for a defined amount of time under specific anticipated external temperature conditions as used in the qualification studies. It is important to consider these factors when evaluating the suitability of such packaging for its intended use, as well as knowing the routing specifics, risks associated with critical control points in the process, and the overall transit time from origin to destination.

Reuse is another way to reduce the cost of passive-thermal solutions. The reuse process should be qualified and have a documented quality control process for the returned solutions, including cleaning and inspections to ensure the used system will provide acceptable performance for its intended use.

7.4 最低成本溫控方案 — 決策工具

溫控包裝全生命週期總擁有成本（TCO）公式

TCO = 採購成本（Purchase Cost）
    + 驗證成本（Qualification: IQ/OQ/PQ）
    + 操作成本（充電 PCM / 充填冰袋 / 人工預冷）
    + 廢棄成本（Disposal: 銷毀費用 + 環保稅）
    - 回收殘值（Salvage Value: 若回收再用）

補充：
  運費 = 重量 × 運費率（被動方案因重量大，運費可能高於包材本身）
  總配送成本 = TCO（包材）+ 運費 + 失敗成本概率 × 損失金額

實務上，被動式 VIP+PCM 方案雖採購成本高，但因體積小、重量輕，運費節省往往能覆蓋價差，反而比傳統 EPS+水冰盒更具總成本優勢。

被動 vs. 主動：關鍵選擇因素

  • 被動（Passive）：低操作複雜性、無電力依賴；但保護時間有限，超過設計窗口即失效。驗證需針對最惡劣溫度情境（Summer/Winter Profile）。
  • 主動（Active）：持續維持溫度、適合長途；但依賴電力（需備用電源計畫）、設備重、維護複雜。
  • 黃金法則：包材性能窗口必須覆蓋「最長預期運輸時間 + 緩衝時間（通常 +12h 或 +25%）」。

7.5 Cost of Failure

Establishing a robust shipment process with a qualified solution is an investment made to avoid losses due to quality issues. The cost associated with damage, recalls, returned goods, and rejection due to temperature-control failure is high. The cost can be even higher if the damage is to patient safety and health because of a failure in temperature-monitoring and appropriate quality controls. Any of these circumstances can damage the company brand and image.

Mistakes in purchase orders, invoices, selecting materials picking and separation, address and customers should be avoided. The result of mistakes impacts products and may lead to rejection due to limited or absence of supporting stability data. Insuring high-value shipments because of the high risks associated is common.

7.5.1 Business Contingency Plan

The goal of risk-based planning is to focus on preparing the organization for a multitude of risks, including man-made and natural disasters. Some points to consider when developing an emergency plan are:

  • Prioritize the operations and responsibilities to guide a disaster response in advance
  • Consider alternative locations for operations such as call centers, offices, warehouses, product processing, transportation operations, or equipment
  • Back up data and IT infrastructure in a remote location outside the anticipated disaster impact zone
  • Consider ways to reroute transport operations and protect or remove a product that is in an impact area
  • Prioritize key activities and responsibilities and designate personnel who will be responsible for these activities if regular personnel are unavailable
  • Plan for the cascading effects of shifting responsibilities if multiple persons are unavailable, and reorganize remaining personnel in response to the disaster
  • Establish local-level, site-specific business continuity plans tailored to individual business centers and individual customers and products, as needed
  • Prevent man-made disasters to the greatest extent practical based on the situation (the Customs-Trade Partnership Against Terrorism guidelines produced by the U.S. Customs and Border Protection provides an example of resources to guide this effort)
  • Implement reporting systems and tools, alarms, and other automated monitoring systems to speed notification of an incident and enable a timely response
  • Establish a response plan and designate a responsible person to coordinate appropriate intervention activities
  • Apply the principles of quality assurance to monitor, measure, and continuously improve disaster-intervention results

8.0 Summary

The intent of this technical report is to convey the importance of proper information, training, and communication between handoffs as vital to maintaining medicinal product integrity in the pharmaceutical supply chain, especially in the last mile. Knowledge of the challenges encountered along the last mile allows for improvements in future guidance documents, products, and services to the temperature-controlled supply chain community.

The last mile of delivering temperature-controlled pharmaceutical products has many challenges to manage including, but not limited to, the complexity of pharmaceutical supply chain networks, various pharmaceutical product characteristics and temperature sensitivities, weather conditions, sourcing of services and temperature-controlled solutions, use of new technologies and communication platforms, developments in new regulations, execution of cost-effective and sustainable measures, and education in product handling. Sharing information about conditions and practices for the storage and transport of product across all supply chain partners is critical to maintaining product quality, applying appropriate measures, and preventing temperature excursions in the supply chain, including the last mile. When all supply-chain partners involved collaborate closely, the last-mile partners can deliver products on time, to the right place, with the right quantity and quality to the end user.

8.0 TR46 核心信息結構化回顧

TR46 最後一哩 GDP：5 個關鍵控制點

  1. 產品知識與穩定性預算（Sections 2–3）：在設計配送方案前，必須確認產品的溫度敏感性、效期穩定性預算（Stability Budget），並據此選擇合適的包材和運輸方式。
  2. 包裝與設備驗證（Section 4）：被動/主動溫控方案需通過基於最惡劣條件的 IQ/OQ/PQ 驗證，並定期複驗。資格認證記錄是法規查核的關鍵文件。
  3. 監控、偏差管理與追蹤（Sections 5–6）：溫度監控器選型、資料完整性（ALCOA+）、偏差調查流程（根本原因分析 + CAPA）、及供應鏈各環節的移交文件是維持合規的核心。
  4. 教育訓練與利益相關方協作（Section 6.8–6.10）：從製造商到患者的完整教育矩陣，確保每個移交點（Handoff Point）的人員都了解自身責任，是最後一哩溫鏈完整性的最後防線。
  5. 成本管理與永續性（Section 7）：FEFO 庫存輪換、TCO 導向的包材決策、EPR 法規合規、BCP 設計——成本優化必須在不損害產品品質的前提下進行。

TR46 的核心訊息（一句話）

"When all supply-chain partners involved collaborate closely, the last-mile partners can deliver products on time, to the right place, with the right quantity and quality to the end user."

最後一哩的成功，不依靠單一環節的完美執行，而依靠整條供應鏈的協同合作——從製造商、批發商、物流服務提供者、藥師到患者，每個角色都是溫鏈的守護者。

TR46 與其他 PDA TR 的關係

  • PDA TR39（溫控運輸）：TR46 引用 TR39 作為溫控藥品運輸的基礎指引；TR46 聚焦最後一哩的特殊挑戰
  • PDA TR52（GDP）：TR46 為 TR52 GDP 原則在最後一哩的具體應用
  • PDA TR58（風險管理）：TR46 的風險評估框架參照 TR58，需結合 ICH Q9(R1)
  • PDA TR64/TR72（主動/被動系統驗證）：TR46 Section 4 的包材驗證直接引用這兩份 TR 的方法論
