QS-1: What should the environmental monitoring frequency be in an
isolator?
Recommendation
A risk-based approach should be employed to determine the frequency of surface monitoring in an
isolator as well as the frequency and duration of airborne monitoring. 1he approach is similar to that
used for conventional filling lines and cleanrooms, although the results of the risk assessment may be
different.
In some cases, the design of the isolator (e.g., gloveless isolators) may not allow the performance of
some airborne monitoring methods, such as settling plates. Therefore, less intrusive methods may be
advantageous.
Surface sampling of the isolator interior and equipment in the isolator (including gloves) should be
conducted at the end of the production batch or campaign, but prior to decontaminating or opening
the isolator. Surface sampling may also occur after an intervention or activity that requires decon­
tamination of the isolator prior to resuming production.
Airborne monitoring should start prior to any aseptic processing activities performed in the decon­
taminated isolator, including any equipment or parts setup being performed after decontamination.
Total particulate monitoring should be continuous over the duration of critical activities (i.e., when
product or product contact surfaces are exposed). Airborne viables should be sampled in a manner
that provides a representative record of air purity for the duration of the run while not introducing
added risk to product.
Trending of environmental monitoring results should be anal d regularly to assess contamination
yze
control performance.
Rationale
Although the interior of an isolator is decontaminated by a chemical agent, monitoring throughout
the run is necessary to demonstrate continuing contamination control. This is particularly important
as an isolator may allow for extended runs beyond those normally associated with more traditional
lines. Monitoring frequency and duration must be carefully considered to optimize benefit while risk
is managed. 1he contamination risk introduced by required sampling interventions, as well as for m
sample media, must be assessed.
Sampling of gloves and other surfaces at the end of the production run will provide adequate evi­
dence of contamination while not introducing risk to product whether or not media is introduced
(e.g., use of swabs wetted with buffer solution).
For airborne viables, sampling methodology should be selected from various alternatives (e.g., settling
plates, active air samplers) to ensure adequately characterized air purity while limiting the necessary
number and type of interventions. The daily or batch-wise monitoring of air is considered to be an
adequate level of contamination control during campaign production/batch filling over several d .
ays
Modern particulate-monitoring systems allow for continuous airborne monitoring for total particu­
late without introducing risk to product. Such systems should be employed wherever possible.
1he FDA guidance for industry states that surface sampling should be performed at the conclusion of pro­
cessing to avoid direct contact with sterile or decontaminated surfaces during processing. Also, PDA Points
to Considerfo r Aseptic Processing states there is a potential risk for contamination of products, components,
and product contact surfaces by media residue. Therefore, surface sampling should be performed at the
completion of the aseptic process followed by a defined deaning procedure.

QS-3: Should environmental monitoring excursions in isolators be treated
differently compared to excursions in conventional filling lines?
Recommendation
Regarding batch disposition, environmental monitoring excursions in an isolator must be treated the
same as excursions on a more traditional line. A deviation investigation should take into account all
relevant information pertaining to the risk that product may be contaminated, and an appropriate
decision made as to batch release.
Regarding system performance, environmental monitoring excursions in an isolator may raise deeper
concerns b ond those for a more traditional line. Because an isolator is specifically designed and oper­
ey
ated to ensure environmental control and eliminate contamination, the likelihood of environmental
monitoring excursions should be significantly reduced. Therefore, environmental monitoring excur­
a
sions that are detected in isolators are of heightened concern and should be investigted at a more
detailed systemic level, taking into account the structure and function of the isolator itself, as well as the
more traditional routes of contamination.
Rationale
An environmental excursion in a critical zone, as for an isolator, requires an investigation regardless
of the technology employed. Environmental monitoring is a critical element of the sterility assur­
ance program and an important measure associated with control in dean areas. Monitoring provides
meaningful information about the quality of the environment and helps identify routes of contami­
nation which, in turn, allows for corrective and preventative actions to ensure product safety.
Isolators are designed and qualified to separate product from sources of contamination. Any contam­
ination detected inside an isolator must be considered a system failure and trigger respective investi­
gation and corrective action. These include, but are not limited to, effectiveness of decontamination,
air flow and pressurization, isolator integrity, glove integrity, and interventions.
If an isolator system failure is the root cause for the environmental recovery, then broader potential
product impact (i.e., prior batches) may need to be considered, as well as a more complex corrective
and preventive action.

Rationale
The objectives of isolator STUs are to provide a clean, controlled area for activities and functions
required to perform the aseptic process or to operate the main production isolator, and to prevent
contamination of the main production isolator while those functions and activities are performed.
Environmental monitoring provides information about and evidence that the cleanliness of the
STUs is being maintained and that contamination is controlled.
A risk-based environmental monitoring program that considers the aspects of the design and function
of the STUs can better ensure that the program will be effective without adding risk to the perfor­
mance of the STUs, the aseptic process in the main production isolator, or the sterility of the product.
The STUs may be smaller in size than the main production isolator. The size and configuration of the
STUs may affect the use of standard environmental monitoring methods. The blockage of airflow from
the placement of environmental monitoring items may affect the performance of the STU. Some STUs
may not provide the level of access needed to place or change environmental monitoring materials.
The operations in STUs may vary from the level of criticality of operations or functions performed
in the main production isolators. The environmental monitoring programs associated with the STUs
need not necessarily be the same as for the main production isolator based on the criticality of those
functions and the design of the STU.
Sterile product is not usually exposed in the STU and interventions may not be as frequent or
complex as those performed in the main production isolator. However, because of the functions that
occur in the STU and the proximity of the STU to the outside environment, environmental moni­
toring of the STU may provide valuable contamination control information, as well as information
related to the condition and performance of the STU.

QS-S: What factors should be taken into consideration when transferring
environmental monitoring plates into and out of an isolator?
Recommendation
In general, rigorous procedures and precautions should be employed when transferring any sterile
item into a critical (i.e., aseptic) zone. Transfer into an isolator, however, typically requires special
procedures due to the isolator's built-in access restrictions.
Where the use of settling plates has been determined to be valuable, th may be contained within
ey
a sterile outer bag and moved from a transfer isolator through a rapid transfer port into a previously
decontaminated isolator. Care should be taken to ensure that the media is not contaminated with
microorganisms or chemical disinfectants during the transfer and handling.
Alternativel plates within a nonsterile bag may be placed in the main isolator (or an external air
y,
lock) prior to decontamination. In this case, the bag is hung so that all external surfaces are decon­
taminated effectively. The bag material must be selected to avoid penetration by the decontaminat­
ing agent to preserve the growth promotion capability of the media. If a media additive is used to
neutralize the decontaminant, its effectiveness in preserving media function must be demonstrated.
At the end of the batch, plates must be secured appropriately (e.g., in a sterilized bag) before th are removed
ey
from the isolator to avoid any risk of false positive results from contact with the surrounding environment.
Rationale
The design, confined space, and restrictive access of the isolator present unique challenges for the
effective transfer of media plates into and out of the isolator. Improper handling and transfer may
result in false positive or false negative results.
When it is necessary to use plates of nutrient media in an isolator, it is important that the transfer of
the plates into and out of the isolator results in neither contamination of the media nor of the isolator.

Q6-1: How should indirect product contact surfaces be sterilized or
decontaminated?
Recommendation
Equipment surfaces like stopper bowls and tracks are considered indirect product contact surfaces
because they contact sterile components. These surfaces should not contaminate or pose an unac­
ceptable risk of contaminating sterile product. To eliminate microbiological contamination, indirect
product contact surfaces should be treated to achieve and maintain a state that does not contaminate
the sterile components. Sterilization or decontamination of the surface to levels that will not pose an
unacceptable risk of sterile product contamination can be used to achieve this state.
Proper isolator design, aseptic practices, aseptic technique, cleaning, decontamination, sterilization,
and other precautions should be used to prevent microbiological contamination of indirect product
contact surfaces during equipment transfer, handling, and assembly.
The predominant method for sterilization of indirect product contact surfaces outside of the isola­
tor is moist heat/autoclave sterilization, although dry heat sterilization or irradiation may also be
appropriate. Additionally, or as an alternative, the decontamination of these indirect product contact
surfaces in the isolator can be largely achieved through or during the isolator decontamination pro­
cess by methods such as VHP.
The approach for treating, transferring, handling, and installing indirect product contact equipment
depends on the design of the isolator and facility. A risk assessment should be used to determine an effec­
tive approach for treatment of the indirect product contact equipment given those design considerations.
The assessment should consider the risk of contamination from the handling of parts during re­
moval and assembly prior to decontamination, the potential bioburden levels on the surfaces prior to
decontamination, and the removal of decontamination chemicals. The selected approach must have
documented justification to ensure that contamination risk is minimized and an acceptable aseptic
manufacturing environment within the isolator is achieved prior to GMP manufacturing.
The handling of indirect product contact parts during transfer and assembly should not introduce
excessive bioburden or other conditions that adversely affect the decontamination process.
The aseptic environment within the isolator must be established prior to manufacturing. The ap­
proaches for handling indirect product contact equipment should consider the efficacy and limi­
tation of the validated VHP decontamination process and risks associated with the removal and
assembly of the equipment within specific isolator installations.
The following are some approaches that might be explored:
Approach 1: Equipment is sterilized and aseptically transferred directly into isolator through
Grade A/ISO 5 environment
• The indirect product contact equipment is removed after use and cleaned using a qualified
cleaning process. Afterwards, the equipment will be wrapped and sterilized using a qualified
moist heat/autoclave sterilization process. [Note: In some cases, the equipment may not be
wrapped.]
• The sterilized and wrapped equipment is aseptically transferred into the decontaminated isolator
directly using a transfer section or port connected to an autoclave.
• Precautions should be taken to prevent excessive moisture and/or heat in the isolator as a result
of transferring the equipment from the autoclave.
• Once in the isolator, proper aseptic techniques should be used to unwrap and assemble the
equipment as necessary.

surfaces.
• Aseptic manipulations and interventions related to the handling of the sterilized equipment
should be within the scope of the aseptic process simulation.

Approach 4: Equipment is periodically sterilized and decontaminated during the isolator decon­
taminati.on cycle
• Where it is not possible or practical to remove, sterilize, and transfer equipment into the isolator (e.g.,
large equipment), the equipment should remain assembled, be cleaned using a validated cleaning
process,* and be decontaminated in place inside the isolator using a validated decontamination
process (e.g., VHP). The frequency for large equipment sterilization should be risk based.
• The validation of the decontamination process should include assurance that the indirect
product contact surfaces have been rendered incapable of contaminating sterile surfaces or
components.
• Additional precautions should be taken as part of the cleaning and handling process to ensure
that oily residues or other substances that can reduce the effectiveness of the decontamination

precautions should be taken to limit their exposure to potential sources of microbiological
contamination during maintenance, handling, and use.
* The deaning process should be qualified to remove substances that inhibit the effectiveness of the

Rationale
Microbiological contamination of indirect product contact parts, such as stoppers, plugs, or closures,
will pose an unacceptable risk of contaminating those components that contact sterile product.
Therefore, these surfaces must be rendered incapable of contaminating product contact components.
Approach 1 will provide a good level of assurance that the indirect product contact equipment
surfaces have been rendered sterile; moist heat is the primary sterilization modality. However,
precautions are necessary because the transfer of the equipment into the isolator may pose a risk to
the isolator environment by elevating the temperature and increasing the amount of moisture. As­
sembling the equipment aseptically may also pose a risk to maintaining the equipment surfaces in a
noncontaminated state and added manipulations through the isolator gloves could lead to increased
risk of damage.
Approach 2 will provide a good level of assurance that the indirect product contact surfaces have
been rendered sterile; moist heat is the primary sterilization modality and decontamination is a
secondary treatment. However, precautions are necessary because the transfer of the sterile wrapped
equipment into the open isolator should not damage the wrapping to the extent that the sterile
equipment surfaces are compromised.

important because contamination by skin-borne particles, grease, oily substances, or other substances
may negatively impact the effectiveness of the decontamination process
Approach 3 will provide a good level of assurance that the indirect product contact surfaces have
been rendered sterile; moist heat is the primary sterilization modality and decontamination is a
secondary treatment. Precautions are necessary to ensure that the outer wraps are not damaged,
disrupted, or placed in a condition that compromises the sterility of wrapped, sterilized equipment
or the effectiveness of the decontamination process.

in a noncontaminated state, and added manipulations through the isolator gloves could lead to
increased risk of damage. Introduction of wrapping material prior to decontamination will impact
the cycle parameters required for decontamination with materials that absorb hydrogen peroxide and
impact airflow pattern.

Q6-2: Should a test for packaging material integrity of load items be carried
out with respect to potential for VHP ingress?
Recommendation
Packaging integrity should be qualified for items being decontaminated and transferred for subse­
quent processing (e.g., decontamination chamber). When items are placed in heat-sealed breather
bags, it is important to validate that package integrity is not compromised during the heat-sealing
process.
For VHP processes in isolators, the integrity of the packaging materials to prevent ingress, and their
compatibility with VHP, should be tested prior to use. If a robust materials compatibility evaluation
has been conducted, routine testing during the process should not be required.
Whether or not packaging material should be tested post-decontamination would depend on the
decontamination process. Specifically, if a pressure differential is created across the exterior/interior
of the package, it could potentially impart a force that creates integrity concerns.
Rationale
If packaging is not integral, sterility could be compromised because decontamination gasses could
reach the inside of the package and impact materials.

Q6-4: How should tubs of sterilized syringes or vials be decontaminated and
transferred into the isolator?
Recommendation
Surface decontamination for ready-to-use items, like nested vials or syringes in tubs, has become
more common in aseptic processing in recent years. These cubs are packed in a single-or double-liner
for transport. For the tubs to be transferred into the isolator via pass-through with VHP or electronic
beam (E-beam), the outside packaging (liners) are removed.
For a VHP airlock, the tubs are positioned inside the airlock and then decontaminated on the outer
surface using VHP. The VHP airlock is a batch unit for the safe transfer of tubs into a filling-line iso­
lator; with this method, a 6 log reduction of surface contamination and a cycle time ofless than 30
minutes can be reached. For prefilled pharmaceutical products that are sensitive to VHP, it is recom­
mended that precautions be taken to avoid penetration of VHP inside the cub.
If the outside surfaces of the tubs are decontaminated by £-beam, they should be positioned in front
of the £-beam and, then, automatically transferred inside the £-beam where the outer surface of the
tubs are decontaminated with beta electrons. The E-beam is a fast and continuous decontamination
system to transfer the tubs inside a filling-line isolator; with this system, a 6 log reduction (25 kGy)
and capacity of six tubs per minute can be reached.
Alternate methods for transferring presterilized and bagged materials that do not involve VHP
decontamination or E-beam may be considered. Where alternate methods are considered a risk­
based assessment should be performed to determine their capability and effectiveness. Any alternate
method must be qualified and validated.
Rationale
Both the VHP airlock and E-beam technologies can be used and validated as an efficient outside de­
contamination process for tubs. The E-beam process has two advantages: it is continuous, as opposed
to using a batch process, and it circumvents VHP penetrating the insides of vials and other types of
containers. VHP airlock and E-beam technologies are currently the only automated technologies
chat can be validated and those chat are considered fully GMP-compliant.
