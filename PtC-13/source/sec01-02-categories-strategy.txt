Topic 1. ATMP Material Categories  
Q 1.1. Which ATMP material categories exist and how are they defined? 
There is no health authority guideline that clearly defines ATMP material categories nor an aligned 
definition across countries or regions. The material categories listed in Table 1.1-1 reflect existing 
regulatory guidance relevant to ATMPs and common industry vernacular. 
The term “critical raw material” is used by European Union (EU) health authorities to describe materials 
critical for product quality in their Guidelines on Good Manufacturing Practice Specific to Advanced 
Therapy Medicinal Products (1). This guideline does not provide a clear definition of a “critical raw 
material,” however, but lists a few examples such as sera, growth factors, enzymes (such as trypsin), 
cytokines, and gene-editing reagents). ATMP sponsors may define the criticality of raw materials based 
on their specific products and internal procedures. 
The term “material” is a general term used to describe manufacturing ingredients, raw materials (e.g., 
starting and ancillary materials, excipients, primary packaging) consumed in the process that may or may 
not be present in the final drug product (2). The material categories given in Table 1.1-1, as well as 
“critical raw materials,” fall under “materials.”  
The term “animal-derived raw material” or “animal-derived material” is widely used, but its definition 
has not yet been globally aligned by health authorities. PDA Technical Report No. 83: Virus 
Contamination in Biomanufacturing: Risk Mitigation, Preparedness, and Response and European 
Pharmacopoeia, Chapter 5.2.12: Raw Materials for the Production of Cell-Based and Gene Therapy 
Medicinal Products, provide definitions (3, 4), which the authors consolidated into the following: 
Primary Animal-Derived Materials 
Any material derived directly from animal sources  
Examples 
•

•

•

Topic 2. Defining an ATMP Material Strategy 
Q 2.1. What are key considerations when defining an ATMP material strategy? 
Defining an ATMP material strategy means to constantly maintain a balance between imperfections 
regarding material quality and supply standards and potential fast-track commercialization (e.g., clinical 
Phase 2 as a pivotal trial). This requires a risk-based approach that is both comprehensive and phase-
appropriate and considers all aspects of material quality, including but not limited to manufacturer 
controls, contamination risk, and potential for process-related impurities. The objective is to always strive 
to maximize knowledge about the materials used at all stages. Focusing on the following key 
considerations has shown to be helpful.  
Human- and Animal-Derived Materials 
For many ATMPs (e.g., cell-based therapies), the use of animal-/human-derived materials is unavoidable 
during early clinical trials, and health authorities accept these materials if safety requirements for 
adventitious agents (e.g., microorganisms, viruses, prions) are met. However, sponsors are advised to 
work on replacing these materials in parallel with the product development, as they often pose a risk to 
eventual commercial manufacturing in terms of supply chain, batch-to-batch variability, and transmissible 
agents. It is important to note though that for some applications, no chemically defined replacements for 
animal-/human-derived materials have yet been found (e.g., human serum used for stem cell 
differentiation) as the development of these replacements requires extensive development activities.  
Research Use Only Materials 
Non-GMP material grades, such as research use only (RUO), can be acceptable for early clinical trials 
when higher-grade material is not openly available, but RUO material should be replaced with higher 
grades for later stages if possible. A potential replacement strategy could be to evaluate higher grades, 
once available, for their compatibility with the manufacturing process in parallel with the ongoing clinical 
manufacturing. Health authorities, such as EMA, may require companies to follow cGMP or GMP 
principles for starting materials and/or critical materials, and excipients for clinical phases and 
commercial manufacturing (5).  
Proprietary Materials 
Certain key ATMP materials can be proprietary (e.g., cryoprotectants used as excipients, cell culture 
media), which complicates regulatory filing since sponsors do not have access to critical information 
associated with the intellectual property for the material (e.g., manufacturing method, full ingredients 
list). However, these materials are often not replaceable, and sponsors must find ways to mitigate the risk, 
for example, by submitting a Letter of Authorization to the FDA to access a supplier Master File.

Chain of Identity/Chain of Custody 
Manufacturers should have traceability systems in place to avoid material mix-ups and cross-
contamination, as these could have severe consequences for patient safety. Especially for cell-based 
therapies, manufacturers must work with suppliers to ensure the chain of identity for human donor 
material in the entire supply chain (see Topic 4).
